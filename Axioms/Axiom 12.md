# Axiom XII: Human Capacities Exist Only in Practice. What Persons Do Not Do, They Lose the Ability to Do. Institutions That Absorb Human Functions Do Not Merely Replace Them — They Destroy Them.

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- Within living memory, across every domain of human life simultaneously, an identical pattern has played out: an institution offered to do something people used to do for themselves and each other; people accepted; the capacity atrophied; the dependency became structural. Medicine, food, governance, education, conflict resolution, care of the dying, care of children, the building and repair of shelter — in each case the same sequence, the same outcome
- Most adults in the United States cannot grow meaningful quantities of food. Most cannot build or substantially repair a structure. Most cannot attend to serious illness or death at home. Most cannot resolve a significant conflict with another person without recourse to institutional intermediaries. Most have not participated in a governing decision in any domain that actually mattered. These are not marginal failures — they are the systematic elimination of capacities that were universal among human beings for the entirety of our history as a species until approximately two generations ago
- The pattern accelerates: each generation inherits the diminished capacity of the one before it as the baseline; what was experienced as loss by one generation is experienced as nature by the next; what was nature for one generation becomes invisible to the one after; within two or three generations a capacity that took centuries to develop can vanish so completely that its absence is not felt as absence but as the way things simply are
- The political stakes are specific: a population that has lost the capacity for self-governance — not merely the habit but the actual practical knowledge of how to govern themselves at the local scale, how to resolve disputes without courts, how to provide for themselves without supply chains that circle the globe — is a population that cannot do what the present moment requires; atomized dependence is not only a personal impoverishment but a civilizational vulnerability

---

## What the axiom claims

- **Capacities are not possessions** — they cannot be stored and retrieved; they are living achievements that exist only in their exercise; the person who has not grown food cannot grow food; the person who has never resolved a conflict without a lawyer cannot resolve one without a lawyer; the knowledge does not go dormant and wait — it goes extinct
- **Institutions that absorb human functions do not merely replace them — they destroy them** — this is the strong claim; not that institutions are bad substitutes for human capacity, but that the substitution itself eliminates the capacity; once the institution absorbs the function, the people who would otherwise have exercised and transmitted the capacity stop doing so; a generation later it is gone
- **The offer feels like progress** — this is what makes the mechanism so effective; the institution is often genuinely more efficient, more reliable, more expert than the individual or community it replaces; acceptance is rational in the short term; the cost is paid later, by people who were not present for the decision
- **Dependency becomes total and then extractive** — once a capacity is lost, the institution that absorbed it holds a monopoly not in the economic sense but in a deeper sense: there is no alternative; the institution can extract whatever it wants — in fees, in compliance, in deference, in the surrender of judgment — because those who depend on it have no other means; this is not necessarily the result of anyone's plan; it is the logical conclusion of the substitution mechanism
- **The Society is not exempt** — every division, every college, every governance structure the Society builds is subject to the same logic; every time the Society does something for its members rather than through them and with them, it risks destroying the capacity it was built to restore; the measure of the Society's success is not what it provides but what its members can do

## The philosophical case: capacities require practice

**What needs to be established:**
- That the capacity-practice relationship is not a contingent feature of how humans are organized but a deep feature of what capacities are
- That this is not merely a psychological observation but a claim about the nature of practical knowledge

**Key thinkers:**
- Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics*, Books II and VI — the concept of *phronesis* (practical wisdom) as the paradigm case; practical wisdom cannot be transmitted through instruction; it is developed only through sustained practice in real situations with real stakes; the person who has read about practical wisdom does not have it; "we become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts"; what is true of virtue is true of every practical capacity — the doing is what constitutes the having
- Ryle, *The Concept of Mind* (1949) — the distinction between knowing-that (propositional knowledge, transmissible through instruction) and knowing-how (practical knowledge, acquired only through practice); the category error of treating practical knowledge as if it were propositional; you cannot learn to swim by reading about swimming; the practical knowledge required for genuine self-governance, for growing food, for resolving conflict is knowing-how — it cannot be transmitted institutionally and cannot be retained without practice
- Polanyi, *The Tacit Dimension* (1966) — "we know more than we can tell"; tacit knowledge is the knowledge embedded in skilled practice that cannot be fully articulated; master craftspeople cannot fully explain what they know because significant parts of their knowledge exist in the trained perception and response of their bodies and minds rather than in propositions they can state; tacit knowledge is transmitted through apprenticeship — through sustained proximity to practice — and is lost when the practice stops; institutions that replace skilled practice with standardized procedures eliminate tacit knowledge at the point of elimination, not gradually

**The developmental evidence:**
- Vygotsky, *Mind in Society* (1978) — the zone of proximal development; what a person can do with support today, they can do alone tomorrow — but only if they actually practice doing it; a scaffold that never comes down does not enable development; it prevents it; the person who is always helped with something never develops the capacity to do it; institutions that always help produce people who always need help

## Illich: counterproductivity and radical monopoly

**What needs to be established:**
- That institutions pass a threshold at which they begin to produce the opposite of their stated purpose
- That the most significant form of institutional harm is not bad service but the elimination of the alternative

**Ivan Illich** is the thinker who developed the fullest account of the mechanism the axiom describes. His three major works each document a different domain:

- *Deschooling Society* (1971) — schools have achieved a "radical monopoly" on learning; they do not merely provide education — they have colonized the concept of learning such that learning that occurs outside schools is not recognized as learning; the result is not a better-educated population but a population that has lost the capacity for self-directed learning and regards any knowledge not credentialed by an institution as suspect; school past a certain scale produces people who cannot learn without school — the opposite of its stated purpose
- *Medical Nemesis* (1975) — medicine has achieved a radical monopoly on health; the concept of "iatrogenesis" — illness caused by medical intervention; Illich distinguishes clinical iatrogenesis (direct harm from treatment), social iatrogenesis (the medicalization of ordinary human experience — birth, death, pain, grief, anxiety — that transfers management of these to professionals and destroys the community capacity to manage them), and cultural iatrogenesis (the destruction of the will and ability of people to manage their own health and suffering); medicine past a certain scale produces a population that cannot manage illness, pain, or death — the opposite of its stated purpose
- *Tools for Conviviality* (1973) — the general theory; Illich distinguishes convivial tools (tools that enhance and extend human capacity, that remain under the control of the person using them) from industrial tools (tools that require and reinforce institutional dependency, that replace human capacity rather than extending it); the bicycle vs. the car; the hand tool vs. the machine; the question for any tool is whether using it develops or destroys the user's own capacity; the concept of "counterproductivity threshold" — every institution reaches a scale beyond which it produces more harm than good; identifying and not crossing that threshold is the design challenge

**The radical monopoly:**
- Illich's most important concept: an institution achieves a radical monopoly when it not only provides a service but eliminates the alternative; a car does not merely provide transportation — at sufficient scale, it destroys the conditions for walking, cycling, and public transit; a school does not merely provide education — at sufficient scale, it destroys the legitimacy and practice of learning outside it; a hospital does not merely provide care — at sufficient scale, it destroys the capacity and expectation for care at home and in community
- The radical monopoly is more powerful than an economic monopoly because it operates on the capacity side rather than the supply side; economic monopolies can in principle be broken by restoring competition; radical monopolies cannot be broken by restoring competition because the capacity that would use the alternative no longer exists

## The pattern across domains

**What needs to be established:**
- That the mechanism is not domain-specific but a general feature of the relationship between institutions and human capacities
- That the pattern across domains has operated simultaneously and within living memory

**Food:**
- The capacity to grow, preserve, and prepare food from primary ingredients was universal until approximately two generations ago; it is now held by a small minority; the industrial food system has not merely competed with home food production — it has eliminated the alternative by eliminating the knowledge, the land access, the time, and the social contexts in which that knowledge was transmitted; the people who cannot grow food do not experience this as deprivation because they have never grown food and have no concept of what the capacity involves; Berry, *The Unsettling of America* (1977): "The knowledge and skills that made the farm work came with the farm family... when the family leaves... that knowledge goes with them"

**Medicine and care:**
- Birth, death, and serious illness were managed at home by communities, families, and local healers for the entirety of human history until the early twentieth century; the medicalization of these transitions has not improved outcomes in all respects (mortality from childbirth and many conditions has dramatically improved) but has destroyed the community capacity to accompany the dying, to birth at home, to manage illness without professional intermediation; what is lost is not merely the practical skill but the cultural framework within which these transitions were held — the understanding of what death is, what birth is, what pain means — that existed only in the practice of being present to them

**Governance:**
- The capacity for local self-governance — the New England town meeting, the commons management systems Ostrom documented, the neighborhood-scale political organization that Tocqueville observed — has been progressively absorbed by state and federal systems; what remains is the formal ritual of electoral participation in systems over which ordinary people have negligible influence; the capacity for genuine self-governance at the local scale — knowing how to make collective decisions, how to resolve disputes, how to enforce agreements without external authority — has atrophied in the same way and for the same reason; Scott, *Seeing Like a State* (1998): the destruction of *metis* — local, practical, situated knowledge of how to govern specific places and communities — by state systems that require legibility and standardization

**Conflict resolution:**
- The legal system was designed to handle conflicts that communities could not resolve; it has expanded to the point where the expectation of community-based conflict resolution has largely disappeared; people sue each other over matters that previous generations would have resolved between themselves or through community intermediaries; the legal system has not merely provided a better alternative to community resolution — it has eliminated the alternative by eliminating the norm that conflict is something communities manage themselves and by producing a population that genuinely cannot resolve significant conflict without legal intermediation

**Education:**
- The capacity for self-directed learning — for acquiring what one needs to know through direct engagement with the world, through apprenticeship, through reading, through community transmission — was the universal human mode of learning until very recently; schooling has achieved Illich's radical monopoly: knowledge acquired outside institutional credentialing is not recognized; the population has largely lost the ability to learn without institutional structure because the expectation and practice of self-directed learning has been replaced, not supplemented

## The two-generation problem

- The Working Axioms state the mechanism precisely: "Within two generations a capacity that took centuries to develop can vanish so completely that its absence feels like nature rather than loss"
- The first generation experiences the substitution as choice — they remember doing the thing the institution now does; they made the decision to accept the institutional substitute; the loss is visible to them as loss
- The second generation inherits the institutional arrangement as given; they did not make the choice; they have no memory of the alternative; the absence of the capacity is not experienced as absence but as the way things are
- The third generation does not know what it does not know; the capacity has disappeared so completely that there is no framework in which its absence could be experienced as loss; its recovery would require not merely relearning a skill but reconstructing the entire cultural framework within which the skill was embedded, transmitted, and valued
- This is the timescale at which the current crisis operates: the simultaneous, rapid absorption of capacities across every domain of life has happened within two to three generations; there is no living memory, in most domains, of what the alternative looked like; its recovery is correspondingly more difficult

---

## The dependency mechanism

**How dependency becomes total:**
- When a capacity is lost, there is no alternative to the institution that absorbed it — not because the institution has prevented competition but because the capacity to use an alternative has been destroyed
- At this point the institution holds leverage that is not primarily economic; it is existential; those who depend on it for food, for medicine, for governance, for conflict resolution cannot withdraw; they have no other means
- The institution can at this point extract whatever it wants: high prices, compliance with rules that serve the institution rather than those it serves, deference to institutional authority even when that authority is wrong, the surrender of judgment in domains where the person no longer trusts their own judgment because they have never been permitted to exercise it
- This is the logic the Working Axioms describe: "the dependency becomes total. And then the institution can extract whatever it wants because there is no alternative — not because anyone planned it that way, but because the logic of substitution, followed honestly to its conclusion, always arrives there"
- The extraction does not require malice; it is the natural behavior of any institution that has achieved a radical monopoly; the logic of self-interest, operating through normal institutional mechanisms, produces extraction because extraction is possible and resistance is not

## Tocqueville revisited: soft despotism as political form

- Tocqueville's account of soft despotism in *Democracy in America* is the political expression of the same mechanism; as the state absorbs more functions that citizens once performed themselves, the citizens lose both the practice and the expectation of self-governance; the state becomes "an immense and tutelary power" that provides for their needs, manages their affairs, and "renders the exercise of the free agency of man useless and irregular"; the citizen is left with the formal apparatus of democracy while having lost the capacity for genuine self-governance
- This connects to Axiom X: the absorption of functions by institutions is one of the mechanisms by which power concentrates; each function absorbed is a domain in which citizens have lost the capacity to do without the institution; as capacity diminishes, power concentrates in those institutions that hold the radical monopoly on what was once widely distributed practice
- The two axioms together describe the mechanism: Axiom X identifies that power concentrates; Axiom XII identifies one of the primary mechanisms — the absorption of functions and the destruction of the alternative capacity — through which power concentrates without anyone necessarily intending it

---

## What the Society is built on

- The Society's purpose — forming fully oriented persons capable of genuine self-governance — cannot be achieved if the Society's institutions absorb the functions through which those capacities develop
- The measure the Working Axioms specify is exact: not what the Society provides but what its members can do — individually and together — because they are part of it; a Society whose members cannot grow food because the Food Division grows food for them has failed; a Society whose members cannot resolve conflict because the governance structures handle conflict for them has failed; a Society whose members cannot make genuine collective decisions because the leadership makes decisions for them has failed
- The design principle: the Society's institutions should be organized around building and maintaining capacity rather than absorbing and replacing it; the Food Division teaches and practices food production with members, not for them; the governance structures train members in the practice of self-governance, not in the practice of delegating governance to others
- The self-referential warning: the Society will face constant pressure to be more efficient — to have experts handle things, to have structures absorb functions that members find difficult or time-consuming; each such absorption will be rational in the short term; the long-term cost is precisely what the axiom describes; the Society must resist this pressure not occasionally but as a structural commitment, because the logic of substitution is powerful and the capacity losses are irreversible on short timescales

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom VII** (community is constitutive of the person) — the capacities that community requires — care, governance, conflict resolution, food production, the transmission of knowledge — are precisely the capacities that institutions have absorbed; the destruction of community and the institutional absorption of community functions are two descriptions of the same process; you cannot build genuine community while simultaneously having institutions absorb the functions that community exists to perform
- **Axiom V** (no generation creates what it receives) — practical capacities are part of what each generation receives and is obligated to pass on; the loss of capacities across generations is a form of inheritance failure; the intergenerational obligation the axiom describes extends to the transmission of practical knowledge and the maintenance of the conditions in which that knowledge can be transmitted
- **Axiom X** (power concentrates) — the absorption of functions by institutions is a primary mechanism of power concentration; every function absorbed is a domain in which those who depend on the institution have lost the capacity to manage without it; the resulting dependency is a form of power that does not require coercion because it does not permit exit

---

## The positions we reject

- The view that institutional efficiency is sufficient justification for absorption of function — the efficiency is real and often genuine in the short term; the axiom's claim is about the long-term cost of that efficiency; trading capacity for efficiency is a transaction with consequences that those who make it cannot fully anticipate and cannot reverse
- The view that lost capacities can be restored by providing instruction — instruction restores knowing-that, not knowing-how; the practical knowledge embedded in sustained practice cannot be re-acquired through courses, workshops, or reading; restoration requires the rebuilding of the conditions in which sustained practice occurs, including the social contexts, the transmission relationships, and the cultural expectations that make practice normal rather than exceptional
- Technological solutionism — the view that new tools can substitute for lost human capacities; this is precisely the mechanism the axiom describes; each technological substitution is a further absorption of function; the accumulation of substitutions produces the dependency the axiom names
- The view that specialization and division of labor make broad human capacity obsolete — specialization is productive within domains; it becomes a civilizational vulnerability when it eliminates the practical capacities that resilient communities require; a community in which everyone is specialized and no one can grow food, build shelter, resolve conflict, or govern themselves is a community whose survival depends entirely on the continued functioning of the systems of supply and governance that have absorbed those functions; the fragility this creates is not a theoretical concern
- The nostalgic reading — the view that the axiom counsels a return to pre-institutional arrangements; the axiom is not against institutions; it is against institutions that absorb functions and destroy the alternative capacity; institutions that extend and support human capacity without eliminating it are exactly what the axiom endorses
