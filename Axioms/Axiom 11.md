# Axiom XI: Persons Are Not Identical to Their Worst Moments

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The United States incarcerates more people per capita than any nation on earth. The logic embedded in American criminal punishment — mandatory minimums, life sentences without parole, the collateral consequences of conviction that follow a person into every subsequent domain of life — is largely the logic of permanent definition: you did this, therefore this is what you are, and what you are will not change. The evidence consistently contradicts this assumption. The assumption persists because it is not primarily held as an empirical claim but as a moral position: some acts are so serious that transformation is beside the point
- The sex offender registry. The permanent loss of voting rights in many states. The employment and housing barriers that follow conviction for decades. The criminal record that surfaces in background checks for the rest of a person's life. These are not proportional consequences of specific acts — they are mechanisms for ensuring that certain acts define a person permanently, regardless of what subsequently happens. The axiom names this as a factual error before it is a moral one
- The internet has created a new form of this error with broader application: the permanent, searchable, algorithmically surfaced record of every public statement, every documented moment, every worst moment that has been captured and indexed. Before the internet, social memory was limited; communities could enact forgiveness and renewal in practice because the mechanisms of permanent documentation did not exist. The infrastructure now exists to ensure that no worst moment is ever actually past — it is always immediately retrievable, always presentable as the most relevant fact about a person. This is a structural change in the conditions under which the axiom operates, not a change in what the axiom says
- Public culture, across the political spectrum, has developed forms of permanent definition that differ in their targets and their rhetorical style but share the underlying premise: this person did or said or believed something that tells us what they fundamentally are, and what they are does not change. The specific mechanics differ — one form relies on criminal records and state systems of punishment; another relies on public exposure and social exclusion; a third relies on ideological categorization — but all proceed from the same foundational error about what persons are
- The axiom is not primarily about forgiveness, though it grounds it. It is about accuracy — about getting right what persons are

---

## What the axiom claims

- Human beings are not fixed — the self is not a static thing that merely accumulates actions and then holds them permanently; it is a living process, shaped continuously by experience, relationship, reflection, and choice; this is an observable feature of what persons are, not a moral preference
- **Persons demonstrably change** — capacities dormant for decades emerge; patterns that seemed permanent dissolve; the person who caused serious harm is not, in any meaningful sense, identical to the person they become if genuine work has been done; the evidence for this is abundant in the psychology of development, in the neuroscience of the brain, and in the documented outcomes of restorative processes
- **Transformation is possible but not automatic** — this is the essential qualification; the axiom is not the claim that everyone changes, or that all harm is automatically forgiven, or that accountability is dispensable; transformation must be earned through honest acknowledgment of what was done, through repair where possible, through demonstrated change over time that others can witness and verify; the possibility is a feature of human nature; whether any particular transformation has occurred is a question that requires evidence
- **Communities that treat persons as permanently defined by their worst moments are making a factual error** — not only a moral one; they are mistaking a moment for a person, a history for an identity, a capacity for a fixed thing; this error has costs that extend beyond the individual person: the community loses what the transformed person might have contributed; the moral culture calcifies around a false premise about human nature
- The accountability that follows from this is more demanding, not less — it requires genuine reckoning rather than symbolic punishment; it asks more of the person who caused harm than permanent punishment does, because it requires them to actually change rather than merely to serve time

## The philosophical case: what persons are

**What needs to be established:**
- That the person is not a fixed entity whose identity is determined by their past acts
- That personal identity over time is a genuine philosophical problem whose best available answers support the axiom's claim

**Key thinkers:**
- Parfit, *Reasons and Persons* (1984), Part III — the analysis of personal identity over time; Parfit argues that personal identity consists in psychological continuity and connectedness — overlapping chains of memory, intention, belief, and desire — rather than in a separately existing self that persists unchanged through time; on Parfit's view, the person who committed a serious act twenty years ago is connected to but not strictly identical to the person today; the chains of psychological continuity have been partially replaced; this has direct implications for moral responsibility: responsibility attaches to the degree of psychological continuity between the person now and the person then, which diminishes over time, especially when genuine transformation has occurred
- Ricoeur, *Oneself as Another* (1992) — the distinction between *idem* identity (sameness — being the same substance over time) and *ipse* identity (selfhood — the self that keeps its commitments, tells its story, holds itself accountable); persons have both: they are continuous in a biological and psychological sense, but their selfhood is a narrative achievement — the story they tell and live of who they are. Transformation is possible because the narrative of the self can be genuinely rewritten — not in the sense of falsifying the past, but in the sense of genuinely becoming someone whose relationship to the past act is one of having done it and having changed, rather than still being the person who would do it
- MacIntyre, *After Virtue* (1981) — the narrative self; we understand who we are through the stories of which we are a part; those stories are not finished while we are alive; a person in the middle of their story is not yet fully defined by any moment within it; to treat a person as permanently defined by a past moment is to declare their story over when it is not
- Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics*, Books II and X — character is formed through habit; virtue is not a fixed endowment but an achievement of sustained practice; the person who is intemperate can become temperate through sustained effort and the right community; character is not destiny — it is the accumulation of choices whose direction can be changed; Aristotle is more guarded about this than some readings suggest (he thinks character becomes fixed through habit over time), but the essential point is that character is formed, not given, and forming can proceed in different directions

**The Lockean complication:**
- Locke, *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689), Book II Chapter 27 — consciousness and personal identity; the person is constituted by the continuity of consciousness, specifically memory; "as far as this consciousness can be extended backwards to any past action or thought, so far reaches the identity of that person"; this grounds a form of moral accountability — you are responsible for what you remember doing — but it also implies that if the consciousness has genuinely changed, the relation to the past act has changed; Locke's account is contested but useful here: it ties identity and responsibility to psychological continuity in a way that leaves room for the axiom's claim

## The empirical case: persons demonstrably change

**What needs to be established:**
- That the philosophical claim is backed by observable evidence about what actually happens to persons over time
- That the premise of permanence — "people don't change" — is empirically false as a general claim

**Neuroplasticity:**
- The brain is not fixed after early development; it continues to form new connections, to reorganize in response to experience, to change in structure through sustained practice; what neuroscience has established under the term "neuroplasticity" is the physical basis for what common observation has always shown: people change their habits, their emotional responses, their behavioral patterns when sustained effort is made in an appropriate environment
- The specific mechanisms: the prefrontal cortex — the seat of executive function, impulse control, and moral reasoning — continues to develop into the mid-twenties and is subject to ongoing change through sustained practice; chronic stress, trauma, and deprivation cause measurable structural changes that diminish capacity; safety, relationship, and sustained effort produce measurable structural changes that expand it

**The psychology of transformation:**
- Gilligan, *Violence: Reflections on a National Epidemic* (1996) — the psychology of those who commit serious harm; the consistent finding that severe violence is almost always preceded by profound shame and humiliation, and that the violence is an attempt to replace the feeling of being nothing with the feeling of being powerful; the implication: transformation requires addressing the underlying shame, not simply punishing the violence; punishment that adds humiliation to an already shame-driven psychology tends to entrench rather than transform
- The longitudinal evidence on desistance from crime — the criminological research on why people stop offending; the most consistent finding is that desistance is associated with changes in identity (seeing oneself as something other than an offender), stable social bonds (a relationship, a community, meaningful work), and age; these are not the things that retributive punishment produces; they are what transformation requires

**The restorative evidence:**
- The evidence from restorative justice programs is consistent: they produce lower reoffense rates than conventional punishment in comparable populations; they produce higher rates of satisfaction with the process among those harmed; they require more from those who caused harm — not passive time-serving but active engagement with what they did and who they harmed; they treat transformation as the goal and structure the process accordingly

## The conditions of genuine transformation

**What needs to be established:**
- That "transformation is possible but not automatic" has specific content — what genuine transformation requires
- That the conditions distinguish real change from performed change, genuine accountability from mere compliance with punishment

**The conditions:**
- **Honest acknowledgment** — not admission in the legal sense (which is strategic) but genuine recognition of what was done, to whom, and at what cost; this requires encountering the reality of the harm, not managing the legal consequences of it; communities that allow persons who caused harm to manage the situation from a distance — to issue statements, to settle, to perform remorse without genuine encounter with those harmed — have not created the conditions for honest acknowledgment
- **Repair where possible** — what was damaged cannot always be restored, but the effort to repair what can be repaired is part of what transformation requires; this distinguishes genuine accountability from the passive suffering of punishment; repair is active; it requires ongoing engagement with those harmed; it is accountable to whether it is actually experienced as repair
- **Demonstrated change over time** — transformation is not a claim that can be assessed at a single moment; it is a pattern, observable across time, in circumstances that test it; the person who says they have changed has only begun to demonstrate it; the demonstration is the pattern of subsequent choices under pressure
- **Witnessed by others** — transformation is a relational achievement; it does not occur in isolation; the community that witnesses the process of change is also the community that verifies it; the person who claims transformation without subjecting that claim to the scrutiny of those who can observe and test it has made an unverifiable claim that cannot bear the weight it needs to bear

**The distinction from punishment:**
- Punishment in the retributive sense asks: what does this person deserve to suffer in proportion to what they did? The answer it produces is: they should suffer an equivalent amount, for the equivalent duration, and then be released with the matter settled
- The accountability the axiom grounds asks: what happened, to whom, at what cost? What would repair look like? What needs to change so that it doesn't happen again? What has actually changed in this person? These questions require more of the person who caused harm, not less — and they are oriented toward the future rather than toward a backward-looking accounting of deserved suffering

## The cost of treating persons as fixed

**What needs to be established:**
- That the error of treating persons as permanently defined by worst moments has costs beyond the injustice to the individual
- That these costs fall on the community and on the moral culture itself

**For the person denied renewal:**
- The structural barriers to reintegration after conviction — loss of voting rights, employment discrimination, housing discrimination, social stigma, permanent public record — do not merely record a past; they actively prevent the future; they create conditions in which the person cannot demonstrate change because they are not permitted to participate in the normal social contexts in which change is demonstrated and witnessed
- The psychology of permanent definition: when a person is treated as permanently identical to their worst moment, they have no identity to inhabit other than the one assigned to them by that moment; permanent labeling is not neutral — it shapes identity in the direction of the label; this is the finding of labeling theory in criminology, and it is the opposite of what the conditions for genuine transformation require

**For the community:**
- The community that excludes persons who have genuinely transformed loses what they might contribute; in a society where rehabilitation is possible and does occur, the permanent exclusion of those who have caused harm represents a loss of human capacity at scale
- The community that practices permanent exclusion has also created a social environment in which persons who are in the process of transformation — who are doing the difficult work of change — have no path forward that others can see; this reduces the incentive for transformation by eliminating its social legibility

**For the moral culture:**
- A community organized around permanent definition has adopted a false premise about human nature and built its accountability practices on that false premise; this corrupts moral judgment in specific ways: it encourages the attribution of permanent character from single acts, it discourages the kind of sustained moral attention that would be required to actually witness transformation, it produces a moral culture in which punishment is a substitute for the more demanding work of genuine accountability
- Stevenson, *Just Mercy* (2014): "Each of us is more than the worst thing we've ever done." This is both a moral claim and a factual one. The moral culture that cannot hold this is a culture that has accepted a diminished account of what persons are

## Restorative justice: the structural application

**What needs to be established:**
- That the axiom is not only a philosophical position but grounds specific practices of accountability
- That restorative justice represents the most developed institutional embodiment of what the axiom requires in practice

**Key thinkers:**
- Zehr, *Changing Lenses: A New Focus for Crime and Justice* (1990) — the founding text of restorative justice theory; the distinction between retributive justice (crime as violation of the state's rules, the question being what the offender deserves to suffer) and restorative justice (crime as violation of persons and relationships, the question being what needs to be healed); the central insight: retributive justice centers the state and the offender; restorative justice centers those harmed and the relationship between those who caused harm and those they harmed
- Braithwaite, *Crime, Shame and Reintegration* (1989) — the distinction between stigmatizing shame (shame that permanently marks the person as deviant, excludes them from the community, and entrenches the offending identity) and reintegrative shame (shame that expresses the community's disapproval of the act while affirming the person's membership in the community and providing a pathway back); stigmatizing shame is correlated with higher reoffense; reintegrative shame is correlated with genuine accountability; the structure of accountability determines whether shame becomes a mechanism of transformation or entrenchment
- Pranis, *The Little Book of Circle Processes* (2005) — the circle practice; the specific form of restorative accountability in which those who caused harm, those harmed, and the community gather in a structured process of genuine encounter; the conditions that make genuine acknowledgment possible; what it means to actually witness transformation rather than managing the administrative consequences of harm

**The evidence:**
- Meta-analyses of restorative justice outcomes consistently show: higher rates of victim satisfaction with the process; higher rates of offender accountability for the harm caused (as opposed to gaming the legal process); lower reoffense rates in comparable populations; better outcomes on measures of victim trauma than conventional adversarial processes
- The evidence does not show that restorative justice is appropriate in all cases or produces perfect outcomes; it shows that the premises on which it is built — that persons can change, that genuine accountability is more demanding than punishment, that those harmed are best served by encounter rather than exclusion — are empirically more productive than their alternatives

## What the Society is built on

- The Society will face situations in which members cause serious harm to other members — harm that cannot be addressed by pretending it did not happen or by informal management that protects the community's peace at the cost of those harmed
- The axiom grounds how the Society approaches those situations: not retributively (what does this person deserve to suffer?) but restoratively (what happened, to whom, at what cost, and what would repair look like?)
- This requires structural investment: processes for genuine encounter between those who caused harm and those harmed; community capacity to witness and support both; clarity about what genuine accountability looks like; willingness to do the harder and slower work of transformation rather than the simpler work of exclusion
- The axiom does not prevent exclusion — some harms may require removing a person from the community for the safety of others; the axiom governs how even that decision is made and what it is understood to mean; exclusion is a protective measure, not a verdict about the permanent character of the excluded person
- The Society's commitment to building genuine community (Axiom VII) is what makes the axiom practically possible: genuine transformation requires genuine relationship; a community of genuine proximity and mutual knowledge is the condition in which transformation can be witnessed and verified; the axiom's conditions cannot be met in a community of strangers

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom IV** (human dignity is equal, sacred, and inviolable) — the most direct connection; dignity cannot be lost through behavior — this is what the axiom means by "inviolable"; the person who has caused the most serious harm retains their dignity; that dignity is the ground on which transformation remains possible; and it is the ground on which the community's obligation to create conditions for transformation rests; the two axioms together describe both the unconditional worth of the person and the possibility of genuine change
- **Axiom VII** (community is constitutive of the person) — persons are formed through relationship and community; if the self is a living process shaped by experience and relationship, then the conditions of transformation are social; a person placed in conditions of isolation, stigma, and permanent exclusion is placed in conditions that shape the self in specific directions; a person placed in conditions of genuine community, genuine accountability, and genuine witness is placed in conditions that make transformation possible; the two axioms together describe what transformation requires
- **Axiom III** (moral blindness) — the axiom about moral blindness describes the structural conditions under which ordinary people cause serious harm without full moral awareness; this matters for how the Society understands those who cause harm within it; the same structural analysis that explains how good people sustain serious harm at scale applies to harm within communities; understanding the conditions that produced the harm is part of what genuine accountability requires

---

## The positions we reject

- Permanent definition by worst moments — the premise of retributive systems, permanent exclusion, and stigmatizing accountability; this is the factual error the axiom names; it is wrong about what persons are
- The view that transformation is automatic or costless — the opposite error; the axiom explicitly requires that transformation be earned, demonstrated, and witnessed; "people can change" is not a license for avoiding accountability or for treating claimed remorse as equivalent to demonstrated change; transformation is more demanding than punishment, not less
- The view that the interests of those harmed are best served by permanent exclusion of those who harmed them — restorative justice research and the experience of those who have gone through restorative processes consistently shows that many (not all) of those harmed want genuine accountability and genuine encounter more than they want the person who harmed them to suffer; the retributive framework often serves the state's interest in processing cases rather than the actual interests of those harmed
- The sentimentalism that collapses the distinction between claim and evidence — "people can change" is not a sufficient basis for treating any specific person as transformed; the distinction between possibility and actuality matters; the conditions and evidence for transformation are specific and must be met; treating the general possibility as evidence of specific transformation in any given case is a failure of the epistemic seriousness the axiom requires
- The institutionalization of permanent stigma — sex offender registries, permanent criminal records, collateral consequences that follow indefinitely — as a response to serious harm; these encode the premise the axiom rejects and produce the consequences the axiom predicts: persons who cannot demonstrate change because they are not permitted to participate in the contexts where change is demonstrated and witnessed
