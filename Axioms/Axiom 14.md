# Axiom XIV: Markets Are Not Natural. They Are Made.

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The most politically consequential claim in contemporary public life is not made in political speeches. It is made in the form of a description: that the economy is like weather — a force that operates according to laws that exist outside human choice, to which political arrangements must adapt, against which political intervention is futile or counterproductive, and whose outcomes, however unequal, reflect natural processes rather than political decisions. This claim places the specific arrangements of the current economic order — the distribution of income and wealth, the terms of employment, the pricing of goods, the ownership of productive resources — beyond democratic accountability by making them appear as natural as gravity
- Thatcher's formulation was explicit: "There is no alternative." The claim is not that current arrangements are the best available but that they are the only possible ones — that the economy's requirements are facts to be accommodated rather than choices to be made. This is a political claim of extraordinary power precisely because it presents itself as not being a political claim at all
- The specific arrangements that this naturalism protects: the taxation of capital gains at lower rates than labor income — a choice that systematically transfers wealth from workers to holders of capital; the legal classification of gig workers as contractors rather than employees — a choice that eliminates their access to labor protections their predecessors held; the extension of intellectual property terms to seventy years beyond the author's death — a choice that converts what was a limited monopoly designed to incentivize creation into a permanent rent stream for the heirs of creators and the corporations that acquired their rights; the lax antitrust enforcement of the past forty years — a choice that permitted the concentration of economic power the axiom connects to Axiom X. None of these are natural. Each was made by someone, serves someone's interests, and could be made differently
- The present moment is one in which the naturalism is under pressure from multiple directions simultaneously: from the left, which has articulated with increasing clarity that specific economic arrangements serve specific interests; from the right, which has identified (if not always accurately) specific economic arrangements as hostile to specific communities; and from the reality of the polycrisis, which has made visible how thoroughly the economy depends on political choices about what to protect, what to sacrifice, and who bears the costs of disruption. The naturalism is weakening. This is the moment to be clear about what it was concealing

---

## What the axiom claims

- **Economic arrangements are political arrangements** — wages, prices, distributions of income and wealth, the terms on which people participate in economic life: none of these are the neutral output of natural laws; they are the outcome of specific rules — about property, contract, labor, money, corporate structure — that were made by someone, serve someone's interests, and could be made differently
- **The appearance of naturalness is itself constructed** — it was produced simultaneously with the market itself, because the market's legitimacy depended on it appearing to exist outside human choice; the mathematics of economics produces the appearance of natural law; the technical vocabulary of the field insulates its political premises from political scrutiny; this is not primarily a conspiracy — it is what happens when a discipline's assumptions become invisible to its practitioners
- **"What presents itself as neutral is always serving someone's interests"** — the political function of economic naturalism is to place existing arrangements beyond contestation; those who benefit most from existing arrangements have the strongest interest in their appearing natural; the appearance of naturalness is therefore not evenly distributed — it attaches most reliably to arrangements that favor the powerful
- **The economy is a set of rules that can be examined, contested, and replaced** — this is the practical conclusion; democratic accountability applies to economic arrangements as much as to any other domain of collective choice; the question is not whether to make economic choices but who makes them, through what process, and toward what ends
- **Alternative constructions are not deviations from nature** — the Society's internal economic arrangements (kin currency, commons, mutual credit) are not utopian departures from natural economic law; they are a different set of construction choices made in the service of different values; this is what every economy is; the difference is transparency about the choice

## The historical case: how markets were constructed

**What needs to be established:**
- That the modern market economy was not a natural evolution from previous arrangements but was violently imposed through specific political mechanisms
- That the state did not merely fail to interfere with the market's natural development but was the primary instrument of its construction

**Key thinker:**
- Polanyi, *The Great Transformation: The Political and Economic Origins of Our Time* (1944) — the foundational work; Polanyi's central argument is that the self-regulating market — the market in which land, labor, and money are treated as commodities subject to the laws of supply and demand — was not a natural evolution but a deliberate political construction that required more continuous state intervention than any previous economic arrangement in human history; it was constructed in England primarily between 1795 and 1834, through specific legislative acts (the repeal of the Speenhamland system, the New Poor Law, the series of Parliamentary Enclosures) that dismantled the existing social protections against market forces and forced people into market participation by eliminating the alternatives; the "naturalness" of the market was ideologically constructed simultaneously with the market itself, because the market's claim to be beyond political interference depended on it appearing to be a natural phenomenon
- The concept of **fictitious commodities**: Polanyi identifies land, labor, and money as fictitious commodities — things that are treated as if they were produced for sale in markets but are not; land is nature; labor is human life; money is a political instrument; treating them as commodities produces specific pathologies when market logic is applied to them without limit; the enclosures converted land from a commons with complex use rights into a commodity; the factory system converted human life into a unit of productive input; the gold standard converted money into a commodity subject to supply and demand
- The **double movement**: Polanyi observed that the expansion of the self-regulating market has consistently generated a counter-movement — spontaneous protective responses from societies threatened by the destruction of their social fabric; labor unions, social insurance, public education, land reform — these are not interventions in an otherwise natural market but the natural social response to a market that threatens the conditions of human life; the conflict between market expansion and social protection is the constitutive tension of modern political economy

**The specific construction mechanisms:**

- **Enclosure of the commons** — the Parliamentary Enclosures of the 17th through 19th centuries converted common land (land to which peasants held use rights under customary law) into private property; approximately seven million acres were enclosed between 1760 and 1820 alone; the people who had been able to subsist through common land use — gathering, grazing, gleaning — were forced into wage labor because their non-market survival options were legislatively eliminated; enclosure was not the natural extension of private property but a massive state-sponsored transfer of wealth from common use to private ownership. Marx, *Capital*, Part VIII: "The expropriation of the agricultural population from the land is the basis of the whole process" — primitive accumulation as the historical foundation on which the market economy rests

- **Corporate personhood and limited liability** — the legal person of the corporation — an entity with rights but without a body that can be imprisoned, with liability limited to its assets rather than the personal assets of its shareholders — was not natural; it was invented through specific legislation in the mid-19th century (the UK Companies Act of 1844 and the Limited Liability Act of 1855; the US 14th Amendment's gradual judicial expansion to include corporations); it enabled the accumulation of capital at scales previously impossible and transferred the risk of large-scale enterprise from investors to workers, creditors, and affected communities; every feature of corporate law — what a corporation may do, what it owes to shareholders vs. stakeholders, whether it has constitutional rights — is a political choice

- **Currency and central banking** — money is not neutral; how it is created, who creates it, what it can be used to buy, what happens when it loses or gains value — these are political questions with political answers; the gold standard, Bretton Woods, floating exchange rates, quantitative easing — each was a specific political choice with specific beneficiaries and specific losers; the institution of central banking, the rules governing fractional reserve lending, the decision about whose debts are forgiven and whose are enforced — these are not technical matters outside politics; they are the core of political economy

- **The dismantling of alternatives** — the market's apparent naturalness required the elimination of the alternatives through which people had met their needs outside market participation: guild systems that regulated entry and conditions of trade; mutual aid networks that provided insurance, credit, and support outside the market; common land that provided subsistence; the enclosures, the Poor Laws, the legislative attacks on combination and guild practice — these were not the clearing away of obstacles to natural market development; they were the destruction of non-market alternatives that would have allowed people to partially exit the market

## Naturalization as political strategy

**What needs to be established:**
- That the presentation of political arrangements as natural is a recurring and identifiable political strategy, not an innocent mistake
- That economic naturalism in particular performs specific political functions that serve specific interests

**Key thinkers:**
- Gramsci, *Prison Notebooks* (written 1929-1935) — the concept of hegemony; the dominant class maintains power not only through coercion but through making its values, interests, and arrangements appear to be common sense — the natural order of things that no reasonable person would question; when economic arrangements appear natural, they cannot be contested; challenging them appears not as politics but as irrationality, ignorance, or dangerous utopianism; the naturalization of economic arrangements is a form of political power that operates by preventing the question from being asked at all
- The mathematical formalism of economics: supply and demand curves, production possibility frontiers, utility functions — these produce the appearance of scientific description rather than political analysis; the political choices embedded in the models (what counts as a cost, whose preferences count, what institutional arrangements are assumed) are invisible within the mathematical formalism; what looks like physics is always also politics; the technical vocabulary of the field insulates its premises from ordinary political scrutiny by requiring specialized training to access
- Social Darwinism as a historical case: the specific deployment of evolutionary biology to naturalize competitive capitalism in the late 19th century; the claim that market competition was the social expression of natural selection; that the outcomes of market competition therefore reflected natural fitness rather than political arrangements; this was not science — it was political argument using scientific vocabulary; it served specific interests with remarkable effectiveness; contemporary economic naturalism operates through different vocabulary but the same structure

## Specific contemporary constructions

**What is political that presents itself as natural:**

- **Capital vs. labor taxation** — in the United States, income from capital (dividends, capital gains) is taxed at significantly lower rates than income from labor; this is a political choice made through the tax code that systematically transfers income from workers to holders of capital; it is justified on the grounds that capital taxation reduces investment (an empirical claim that is contested) and presented as if it reflects natural economic logic rather than a political choice about whose income deserves preferential treatment

- **The employee/contractor distinction** — the legal category that determines whether a worker receives minimum wage protection, overtime pay, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation, and access to employer-sponsored health benefits; the deliberate reclassification of workers as independent contractors — most visibly in the gig economy but across many sectors — is a political choice that eliminates protections that workers previously held; it is presented as the natural outcome of new forms of work rather than a deliberate transfer of risk and cost from employers to workers

- **Intellectual property** — patents and copyright are state-granted monopolies; their existence, scope, and term lengths are political choices; the extension of copyright terms from 28 years (the original US term) to the current life-of-author-plus-70-years was achieved through specific lobbying by specific industries (primarily Disney) and represents a specific political choice about who captures the economic value of creative and intellectual work; the extension of patent scope to cover living organisms, business methods, and software was a specific judicial and legislative choice whose primary beneficiaries are pharmaceutical companies and technology corporations

- **Antitrust enforcement** — the degree to which the state enforces antitrust law determines the concentration of economic power; the past forty years of lax enforcement — grounded in the Chicago School's redefinition of antitrust around "consumer welfare" (narrowly interpreted as short-term price effects) — permitted the concentration of economic power in technology, finance, agriculture, healthcare, and media that the current moment presents; this was a specific intellectual and political project with specific funders and specific beneficiaries

## What the state actually built: the myth of the entrepreneur

**Key thinker:**
- Mazzucato, *The Entrepreneurial State: Debunking Public vs. Private Sector Myths* (2013) — the systematic documentation of the state's role in the innovations that market ideology attributes to private entrepreneurship; the internet (ARPANET); GPS (Department of Defense); the touchscreen (CIA-funded research); Siri (DARPA funding); the basic research underlying virtually every pharmaceutical drug in the past half-century (NIH); the specific finding: private entrepreneurs consistently invest in the late stages of innovation — the commercialization of technologies whose basic research was publicly funded — and then capture the returns; the risks are socialized; the rewards are privatized; the appearance that markets produce innovation while government merely regulates or obstructs it is a description of the stage the camera is pointed at, not the full process

## Alternative constructions: the diversity of human economic arrangements

**What needs to be established:**
- That the current economic arrangement is not the only possible one and has not been the universal human arrangement
- That successful alternatives exist at multiple scales and have existed across history

**Key thinkers:**
- Graeber and Wengrow, *The Dawn of Everything: A New History of Humanity* (2021) — the demolition of the standard narrative in which human societies evolved through fixed stages from primitive communism through feudalism to capitalism; the actual record shows extraordinary diversity in human economic, political, and social arrangements; many societies that encountered market arrangements deliberately rejected them; "the economy" in its modern sense — a separate sphere of human life governed by market logic — is a specific historical construction, not the natural expression of human nature
- Polanyi again — the "always-embedded" economy: in all societies prior to the modern market economy, economic activity was embedded in social relations; people produced and exchanged within webs of obligation, reciprocity, and redistribution that were simultaneously economic, social, and political; the separation of the economy into a distinct sphere governed by its own logic was the specific achievement (and pathology) of the modern market economy
- Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (1990) — the documented success of commons governance arrangements for managing shared resources; demonstrating that the binary choice between privatization and state control is false; community governance represents a third arrangement whose existence the naturalism of private property markets tends to deny
- The cooperative economy: Mondragón in the Basque Country; cooperative networks in Emilia-Romagna; the solidarity economy tradition in Latin America; these are not marginal experiments but substantial successful economies organized on different principles; their existence is the practical refutation of TINA

## What the Society is built on

- The Working Axioms state it directly: "The kin currency, the commons, the mutual credit system — these are not utopian deviations from natural economic arrangements. They are a different set of construction choices, made deliberately, in the service of different values."
- The Society's internal economy is a demonstration of the axiom: that it is possible to construct economic arrangements according to different values than those encoded in the surrounding market economy; that these arrangements are not departures from natural economic law but different political choices about what to value, who holds claims, and how exchange is organized
- The specific construction choices the Society makes — whatever form the kin currency, the commons governance, and the mutual credit system take — embody specific values: reciprocity over commodity exchange, common ownership over private appropriation, credit grounded in mutual obligation rather than collateral, production for use rather than production for sale at profit; these are not more utopian than market arrangements; they are differently constructed, serving different ends
- **"Every economy is constructed. The only question is who constructs it, for whom, and toward what ends. We construct ours explicitly, accountably, and in the open."** — this is the practical conclusion of the axiom; the Society's economic arrangements should be transparent about what they are (construction choices), what they value (the goods articulated in the telos and the other axioms), and who has voice in making and revising them (all members, through legitimate governance)

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom IX** (legitimacy is distinct from legality) — the legal arrangements that constitute the market economy — property law, contract law, corporate law, labor law — are legally valid; the axiom's question is whether they are legitimate; the legitimacy test (genuine consent, service to those subject to it, accountability to them) applies to economic arrangements as much as to political ones; the current distribution of economic power does not survive the legitimacy test
- **Axiom X** (power concentrates) — the construction choices embedded in market arrangements systematically favor the accumulation of economic power; the tax treatment of capital versus labor, the legal architecture of the corporation, the intellectual property regime — all are construction choices that have made power concentration easier and its contestation harder; Axiom X and Axiom XIV together describe both the tendency (concentration) and the mechanism (constructed arrangements that enable it)
- **Axiom V** (no generation creates what it receives) — the wealth held by the current generation was built on public infrastructure, publicly funded research, the labor of previous generations, the natural resources that no one created; the market economy's attribution of this accumulated inheritance to individual achievement is the economic expression of the error Axiom V identifies; wealth that was received cannot be claimed as fully earned, and arrangements that treat it as such are construction choices that could be made differently

## The positions we reject

- Economic naturalism — the view that market outcomes reflect natural laws rather than political choices; the foundational error the axiom names; its political function is to place existing arrangements beyond democratic accountability; its empirical status is false — markets are constructed and their outcomes reflect the specific construction choices that have been made
- TINA (There Is No Alternative) — the specific political deployment of economic naturalism; the claim that current arrangements are the only possible ones, not merely the currently chosen ones; contradicted by the historical record of diverse economic arrangements and the contemporary existence of successful alternative constructions
- The public/private binary — the view that the only choices are between state control and private market provision; Ostrom's work on commons governance, the cooperative economy, and the Society's own construction demonstrate that this binary is false; the relevant question is not public vs. private but what values the arrangement serves and who has voice in its governance
- Market fundamentalism — the view that market outcomes are always preferable to political ones because markets aggregate preferences efficiently; this assumes that existing preferences (formed in existing conditions), existing distributions of purchasing power, and existing market structures produce outcomes worth aggregating; all of these are political choices whose effects are then presented as natural preferences
- The apolitical reading of economics — the view that economic analysis is a technical discipline separate from political argument; all economic analysis makes choices about what to count as a cost, whose welfare to include, what institutional arrangements to assume, and what time horizons to use; these are political choices; the technical formalism of economics makes them invisible, not absent
- The view that alternative arrangements are unworkable by nature — the claim that non-market arrangements necessarily fail because they violate natural economic laws; contradicted by the historical and contemporary evidence for successful commons governance, cooperative enterprise, and mutual aid networks at substantial scale
