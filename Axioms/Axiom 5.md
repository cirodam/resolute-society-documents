# Axiom V: No Generation Creates What It Receives

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The dominant framework of property and ownership treats the earth's resources, its land, its living systems, and its atmosphere as things that can be privately held, accumulated, and depleted without obligation to those who come after — a premise the axiom directly challenges
- Climate change as the clearest contemporary case: the stable atmosphere was a commons received from prior generations; the present generation is depleting it in ways that will profoundly constrain every generation that follows; but the frameworks of private property and national sovereignty have no mechanism for accounting for this, because they do not recognize the prior claim of those who will inherit what we leave
- Land: the value of land is almost entirely socially produced — location, proximity to infrastructure, legal protection, the labor of surrounding communities — yet is privately captured by holders who did nothing to create it. Henry George identified this as the central economic injustice of the nineteenth century; it remains unresolved
- The concentration of wealth: the vast accumulations in the hands of the wealthiest people are not primarily the product of their own genius or labor; they are the product of accumulated social investment, public infrastructure, educated labor forces, legal and financial frameworks built over generations, and natural resources none of them created — yet the framework of private ownership attributes these accumulations entirely to the individual holder
- Intellectual property: the enclosure of the intellectual commons — the privatization of knowledge, culture, genetic sequences, seeds — proceeds on the premise that the holder created what they hold; in most cases they received it, modified it marginally, and claimed ownership of the whole
- The privatization of public goods: institutions built over generations by collective investment — universities, hospitals, public utilities, scientific knowledge bases — are converted to private ownership; the current generation receives the benefit of that accumulated investment and then sells it
- The self-made mythology: the ideology of individual achievement that attributes outcomes primarily to individual effort and talent, ignoring the vast inheritance of infrastructure, knowledge, cultural capital, legal systems, and social trust that all achievement builds on; this mythology is the psychological foundation of the ownership premise the axiom challenges

---

## What the axiom claims

- The inheritance of the earth — its land, its living systems, its atmosphere, its accumulated knowledge and institutions — arrives before any living person and persists beyond them
- It was not made by those who hold it now; it was received from processes older than our species and from generations who themselves received it
- **No one has earned it** — the claim to ownership that might justify unlimited use and disposal is absent; the standard ground for ownership (creation, labor, earning) does not apply to what was simply received
- **Therefore no one owns it** in the full proprietary sense — not that property relations are illegitimate as such, but that the unlimited ownership premise has no foundation when applied to the received inheritance; what was not earned cannot be owned without remainder and without obligation
- **We are inhabitants and recipients, not authors and proprietors** — a fundamental reframing of our relationship to the natural and social world; we are passing through; we did not build the stage; we are not entitled to burn it when we leave
- The temporal structure: the inheritance arrives before us, persists beyond us, and makes us possible; our tenure in it is brief; our obligation to it is real

## The philosophical case: what grounds property, and what it cannot ground

**What needs to be established:**
- That the standard philosophical grounds for property rights do not apply to what was simply received
- That those grounds, honestly examined, already contain the limits the axiom asserts

**Key thinkers:**
- Locke, *Second Treatise of Government* (1689), Chapter V — the labor theory of property: you own what you mix your labor with; the earth is given in common, but labor creates legitimate private claims by adding value. Two things in Locke are usually ignored: first, the Lockean proviso — appropriation is legitimate only "where there is enough and as good left for others"; second, the intergenerational dimension — Locke says nothing about the right to deplete what future persons will need. The axiom builds on Locke's own premises: if the proviso is real, current arrangements already violate it; if labor grounds property, the inheritance (which no one labored to create) cannot be privately owned on Lockean grounds
- Paine, *Agrarian Justice* (1797) — the most direct ancestor of this axiom; Paine distinguishes between the earth in its natural state (common inheritance of humanity) and the improvements made to it (legitimate private property): "Men did not make the earth... It is the value of the improvement only, and not the earth itself, that is individual property." Every landowner owes a ground-rent to the community for private appropriation of the common inheritance; Paine proposes a national fund — paid to every person on reaching adulthood — as compensation for the loss of access to what was everyone's. The argument is not redistribution but restitution
- George, *Progress and Poverty* (1879) — the distinction between the value of improvements (produced by the owner's effort, legitimately private) and the value of land itself (produced entirely by the surrounding community and social development, unearned by the landowner); the land value tax as the mechanism for returning unearned increment to the community; George's insight: the prosperity of a growing city is captured almost entirely by landowners who did nothing to produce it, while the workers and builders who created the prosperity pay rent for the privilege of accessing what their own effort made valuable

**Question to settle:** The axiom says "what no one has earned, no one owns." This needs to be distinguished from two adjacent claims: that private property is illegitimate in general (the axiom does not claim this), and that no one deserves anything for their effort or contribution (the axiom does not claim this either). The target is specifically the appropriation of the unearned — land, natural resources, the atmosphere, accumulated social inheritance — without corresponding obligation. The course should clarify this distinction to prevent misreading the axiom as a blanket rejection of property.

## The philosophical case: intergenerational obligation

**What needs to be established:**
- That future persons have genuine claims on the inheritance — not as individuals we can identify, but as the continuation of the human community into which the inheritance passes
- That the current generation's relationship to the inheritance is custodial, not proprietary

**Key thinkers:**
- Burke, *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) — the partnership of generations: "Society is indeed a contract... a partnership not only between those who are living, but between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born." Burke's conservatism draws very different political conclusions from this premise than the axiom does — Burke uses it to defend inherited institutions against revolutionary change; the axiom uses it to challenge the unlimited appropriation of inherited wealth. But the intergenerational framing itself is exactly right: the living are one party in an ongoing compact that includes the dead who built what we received and the unborn who will inherit what we leave
- Rawls, *A Theory of Justice* (1971), Section 44 — the just savings principle: behind the veil of ignorance, extended to include uncertainty about which generation one belongs to, parties would require each generation to save enough for the next; no generation is entitled to consume the entire capital stock — natural or social — and leave nothing. Rawls acknowledges this is one of the more difficult parts of his theory; the principle is sound even if its precise content is uncertain
- Parfit, *Reasons and Persons* (1984), Part IV — the non-identity problem: the specific persons who will exist in the future depend on choices made now; how can we wrong future persons who would not exist but for the very choices that harm them? This is a genuine philosophical puzzle with no fully satisfying solution. The axiom sidesteps it: the obligation is not framed as an obligation to specific future persons but as an obligation of stewardship — we are recipients of an inheritance and are obligated to pass it on, regardless of who specifically will receive it. The obligation runs to the inheritance itself and to the continuation of human community, not to identifiable future individuals
- The Haudenosaunee seventh generation principle — decisions should be made considering their impact seven generations into the future; this is not merely an ethical intuition but a governance practice; it operationalizes the custodial relationship the axiom describes and has governed successful communities for centuries

**The custodial framing:**
- Recipients of an inheritance have obligations that owners do not: to maintain what was received, to pass it on in at least as good condition, and to account for their stewardship
- This does not mean the inheritance cannot be used — it means it cannot be consumed without replacement; it cannot be permanently damaged for present gain; it cannot be treated as if those who come after have no claim on it
- The Society's relationship to the commons it builds — its land, its physical infrastructure, its knowledge, its institutions — is custodial in exactly this sense; what members build together is held in trust for those who will receive it

## The commons: against the tragedy

**What needs to be established:**
- That the "tragedy of the commons" — Hardin's claim that shared resources are inevitably depleted by individual self-interest — is empirically false as a universal claim
- That successful governance of commons is possible and has been practiced
- That the framing of the commons as inherently tragic is itself ideological — a way of making the enclosure of common inheritance appear inevitable

**Key thinkers:**
- Hardin, "The Tragedy of the Commons," *Science* (1968) — the argument: individual rational actors will always over-exploit shared resources because the benefit of use accrues to the individual while the cost of depletion is shared; the commons are therefore inevitably depleted. The paper is enormously influential and almost entirely wrong as an empirical claim about actual commons
- Ostrom, *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action* (1990) — the empirical refutation; Ostrom studied commons governance systems around the world — Swiss grazing commons, Japanese forests, Spanish irrigation systems, Maine lobster fisheries — and found that communities consistently develop effective governance arrangements for shared resources without either privatization or state control. The tragedy is not inherent to commons; it is specific to the particular institutional arrangement of open access without governance. The Nobel Prize in Economics (2009) for demonstrating something that generations of economists had assumed impossible: people can and do cooperate to manage shared resources sustainably across generations
- The design principles Ostrom identifies for successful commons governance include: clear definition of who has rights, matching rules to local conditions, collective choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution mechanisms, and recognition of rights to organize. These are achievable. They are not exceptional. They describe how most successful communities have managed shared resources for most of human history

**The ideological function of the tragedy narrative:**
- Hardin's "tragedy of the commons" has been used to justify the enclosure of actual commons — the privatization of public land, the conversion of communal resources into private property — on the grounds that commons governance is impossible
- This is backwards: the historical commons were not tragedies; they were governed successfully for centuries before enclosure; the tragedy is what happened after enclosure, to the people who depended on the commons and were excluded from it
- The narrative serves specific interests: those who benefit from enclosure have an interest in making it appear inevitable and beneficial; the "tragedy" framing does that work

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom VI** (living systems have integrity independent of human valuation) — the companion axiom; Axiom V establishes that we don't own the earth in the proprietary sense; Axiom VI establishes that what we don't own has worth we are obligated to respect; together they ground the Society's relationship to the natural world
- **Axiom XII** (institutions that absorb human functions destroy capacities) — the same intergenerational theme; both axioms are about what we pass on; Axiom V focuses on the material and natural inheritance; Axiom XII focuses on the human capacities that are part of what each generation receives and must pass on
- **Axiom XIII** (solutions of one generation become costs of the next) — the same temporal framing from the other direction; Axiom V asks what we are obligated to preserve and pass on; Axiom XIII asks what we are obligated not to leave behind

## The positions we reject

- Absolute property rights — the view that private ownership of natural resources is unlimited and unconditional; even Locke's own proviso undermines this; the axiom goes further: the standard ground for property (creation, labor, earning) simply does not apply to what was received
- The self-made mythology — the ideology that individual wealth and achievement are primarily the product of individual effort and talent; this ignores the inherited infrastructure of knowledge, institutions, legal systems, and social capital that all achievement builds on; it is not a description of how achievement works but a political claim about who deserves what
- Discounting the future — the standard economic practice of applying discount rates to future costs and benefits that render future persons' interests nearly weightless; if future persons have genuine claims on the inheritance, then their interests cannot be mathematically diminished simply because they are temporally distant
- The view that depletion is acceptable if compensated — the premise that consuming natural capital is acceptable if economic capital is produced in exchange; this assumes fungibility between natural and economic capital that does not exist; some things that are depleted cannot be replaced at any price
- The tragedy of the commons as inevitable — Hardin's claim that commons governance is impossible; Ostrom's empirical work refutes this; the tragedy is not inherent to commons but produced by specific institutional arrangements; the axiom is the premise on which the Society's commons governance rests
- Technological salvation — the view that future generations will solve the problems we create with technology we have not yet developed; this is a bet made with someone else's stake; it also assumes future generations will receive the intellectual and material inheritance necessary to develop those solutions, which is precisely what depletion threatens
