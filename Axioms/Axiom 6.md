# Axiom VI: The Living Systems of the Earth Have Integrity That Exists Independently of Human Valuation

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The dominant framework for thinking about the natural world treats it as a stock of resources awaiting human appropriation — valuable insofar as it is useful, inert insofar as it is not; the axiom names this as a philosophical error with catastrophic consequences
- The "natural capital" and "ecosystem services" movement within environmentalism — a well-intentioned attempt to protect nature by translating its worth into economic terms — has conceded the foundational premise: that nature's value is contingent on human assessment and commensurable with other economic values. Once that premise is granted, protection always becomes a question of whether the economic value of nature exceeds the economic value of its destruction. The axiom rejects the premise before the calculation begins
- We are in the sixth mass extinction in the history of life on earth. We are its cause. The rate of species loss is estimated at 100 to 1,000 times the background rate. Entire evolutionary lineages representing hundreds of millions of years of distinct biological history are being permanently eliminated — not as an accident, but as the predictable outcome of a framework that assigns worth only where humans have assigned it
- Industrial agriculture's relationship to soil: topsoil that took thousands of years to form is being depleted in decades; the microbial communities that make soil alive and productive — communities of extraordinary complexity that science has barely begun to describe — are treated as inert substrate. The logic of the axiom makes this recognizable as the wrong it is; the logic of natural capital cannot, because the economic value of intact soil microbiology is too diffuse and long-term to survive discount rates
- Ocean acidification, freshwater depletion, deforestation, the collapse of insect populations: in each case, the same premise produces the same outcome — living systems with no human advocate and no economic valuation are treated as if they have no worth
- The specific timescale of the crisis: the living systems being destroyed evolved over hundreds of millions of years; they cannot be recreated. The decisions being made now are permanent in any humanly meaningful sense of that word

---

## What the axiom claims

- Living systems have integrity — a wholeness, a logic, a set of relationships — that exists whether or not any human generation recognizes or values it
- They are not resources awaiting use; they are not assets to be managed; they are communities of extraordinary complexity whose worth does not depend on human assessment
- We did not create these systems and cannot recreate them once destroyed — irreversibility is not a practical limitation but a fact about what kind of thing living systems are
- We are not their managers; we are their inhabitants — biological creatures embedded in living processes we depend on absolutely and cannot exit; the pretense of standing above nature as its manager is not just philosophically wrong but is the pretense that has made the current crisis possible
- The timescale changes the moral weight: the earth is four and a half billion years old; what we call civilization is three hundred years old; we are passing through something vastly older and larger than ourselves
- This reverses the burden of justification: the default is non-interference; the obligation falls on those who would extract, develop, or destroy — to demonstrate that what will be lost can be restored, that the use is worth the irreversible cost, and that those who come after have been considered

## The philosophical case: intrinsic value in living systems

**What needs to be established:**
- That living systems have worth that does not derive from human preference, use, or recognition
- That this is a genuine philosophical claim about what is real, not merely a rhetorical expression of preference for nature

**Key thinkers:**
- Leopold, *A Sand County Almanac* (1949) — the Land Ethic: "A thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community. It is wrong when it tends otherwise." The extension of ethical community beyond persons to the land community — not as metaphor but as a genuine moral claim. Leopold's argument is biographical as much as philosophical: the Land Ethic emerges from sustained attention to actual ecosystems, from learning to read what a place is saying, from the recognition that the systems one has learned to understand have their own logic that is not human logic
- Rolston, *Environmental Ethics: Duties to and Values in the Natural World* (1988) — the most rigorous philosophical defense of intrinsic value in nature; the distinction between instrumental value (value as means) and intrinsic value (value in itself); the argument that wild organisms value their own lives — they work to sustain themselves, respond to threats, pursue their own ends — and that this constitutes a genuine kind of value independent of human recognition; ecosystems have "systemic value" — they are the generators of the individual values they contain
- Naess, "The Shallow and the Deep, Long-Range Ecology Movement" (1973) — the distinction between shallow ecology (protecting nature for human benefit) and deep ecology (recognizing the intrinsic worth of non-human life and ecosystems regardless of utility to humans); biocentric equality — the principle that all living things have equal intrinsic worth, though the practical implications of this for action are context-dependent; Naess is careful to distinguish the philosophical claim from the tactical question of how it is applied
- Taylor, *Respect for Nature* (1986) — a systematic biocentric ethics; each living organism has a good of its own — a set of conditions under which it flourishes — and this constitutes an intrinsic value that generates genuine obligations

**Question to settle:** The strongest philosophical objection to intrinsic value in nature is that value requires a valuer — something that can have preferences, experience satisfaction or suffering, take an interest in outcomes. If that's right, then only sentient beings can have intrinsic value, and ecosystems have value only derivatively, as the habitat of sentient beings. Rolston's response is that organisms pursue their own ends without having preferences in the sentient sense; the question of whether this constitutes "valuing" is partly terminological and partly substantive. The course should engage this honestly: the axiom's claim that living systems have worth independently of human valuation is more philosophically contentious than the claims of Axioms I-V, and that contentiousness is part of what needs to be examined.

## The embedded argument: inhabitants, not managers

**What needs to be established:**
- That the management model of the human relationship to nature rests on a false premise — that we stand above or outside the systems we would manage
- That we are in fact embedded within living systems in ways that make the management pretense both philosophically wrong and practically dangerous

**Key thinkers:**
- Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants* (2013) — the grammar of animacy; in the Potawatomi language, living beings are grammatically animate — addressed as "kin" rather than "it"; what changes in our perception and our relationship when a living being is "it" rather than someone? The English grammar of nature — rivers, trees, soils as "it" — encodes the very premise the axiom rejects; Kimmerer's argument is that indigenous languages encoded a different and more accurate relationship. This is not sentimentality; it is a claim about what kind of relationship with living systems is sustainable and what kind destroys what it touches
- Abram, *The Spell of the Sensuous: Perception and Language in a More-Than-Human World* (1996) — the phenomenological argument; human perception, language, and consciousness emerged through relationship with the animate earth; the capacity for abstract symbolic thought (literacy, in particular) has been purchased at the cost of sensory and perceptual relationship with the living world; the ecological crisis is inseparable from a perceptual crisis — a civilizational forgetting of the embeddedness that has always been our actual condition
- Jackson, *Becoming Native to This Place* (1994) — the critique of the "eyes-to-acres" ratio in agriculture; the imbalance between human managerial attention and the complexity of the systems being managed; the argument for agriculture that works within natural systems rather than overriding them; becoming native — developing the deep place-based knowledge that sustainable inhabitation requires — as a generational project

**The management premise and its consequences:**
- The management model assumes that we have adequate knowledge of the systems we manage — that we can intervene, optimize, and correct; the actual complexity of living systems makes this assumption false in ways that are well documented
- The rebound effect: every major technological intervention in natural systems has produced unintended consequences that required further intervention; the logic of management escalates commitment to the premise even as evidence of its failure accumulates
- The precondition for treating a system as manageable is simplifying it — reducing its complexity to what the manager can comprehend and control; simplification of living systems is itself the primary form of damage; the managed forest is not a forest; the managed fishery is not an ocean; the managed field is not land
- We cannot exit the systems we damage: a factory can close; its externalities remain. The humans who degrade a watershed continue to depend on it. This is what "embedded" means — not that we are one part of nature among many (though we are), but that we cannot relocate outside the consequences of our actions

## Deep time and the timescale argument

**What needs to be established:**
- That the temporal scale on which living systems operate is so much larger than human civilization that claims of ownership and management are revealed as absurd
- That irreversibility, understood in the context of deep time, means something different and more serious than it typically does in human planning

**The argument:**
- The earth is approximately 4.5 billion years old. Life has existed for approximately 3.8 billion years. The first land plants appeared 470 million years ago. The first forests, 385 million years ago. The soil ecosystems that make agriculture possible took tens of millions of years to develop. The tropical forest ecosystems being cleared today took 50-100 million years to evolve their current diversity
- What we call civilization is approximately 6,000 years old. Industrial civilization is approximately 300 years old. The period of intensive ecological destruction — the "Great Acceleration" in which human impacts on the earth system began to compound exponentially — is approximately 70 years old
- When a forest is cleared, a species goes extinct, an aquifer is depleted, a coral reef acidifies past the point of recovery — the loss is not a setback that time will heal. On any human timescale, it is permanent. On geological timescale, recovery is possible — but only after spans of time that make the entire history of human civilization invisible
- "Irreversible" in the context of living systems means: not recoverable within any timeframe that matters for human purposes; not recoverable within any timeframe that matters for the purposes of the millions of species currently alive; potentially recoverable only on timescales measured in millions of years, after which what emerges will be different, not what was lost

**Key thinkers:**
- McPhee, *Basin and Range* (1981) and the Annals of the Former World series — the literary rendering of deep time; the imaginative work required to actually feel the temporal scale rather than merely knowing it intellectually; McPhee's geology writing is one of the most effective attempts to make deep time humanly real
- Wilson, *The Diversity of Life* (1992) — the evolutionary timescale of biodiversity; each species represents a distinct evolutionary lineage, often millions of years old; extinction is not the loss of a type but the permanent end of an experiment in being; recovery from mass extinction events in the geological record took approximately 10 million years

## The reversal of burden: non-interference as default

**What needs to be established:**
- That the current default — development and extraction until proven harmful — is not a neutral starting point but a specific philosophical choice with specific beneficiaries
- That the axiom's reversal — non-interference as default; burden on those who would destroy — is philosophically grounded, not merely a policy preference

**The argument:**
- Every legal and regulatory system embodies a default: who has to make the case? Environmental regulation in most jurisdictions places the burden on those who would protect — they must demonstrate harm before development can be stopped; development proceeds unless stopped
- This default is not neutral. It reflects a specific set of values: that human economic activity is presumptively justified; that what is not valued economically has no standing to constrain economic activity; that the future is appropriately discounted relative to the present
- The axiom reverses this default on philosophical grounds: if living systems have worth independently of human valuation, then their destruction is presumptively a wrong that requires justification; if what is destroyed cannot be recreated, the bar for justification is very high; if what is destroyed was not created by those who destroy it, they bear the burden of demonstrating why the destruction is theirs to authorize
- The precautionary principle in environmental law — act to avoid serious or irreversible harm even in conditions of uncertainty — is the closest legal expression of this reversal, but weaker: it is a principle of caution, not a claim about where worth resides. The axiom provides the philosophical grounding that makes the precautionary principle more than a pragmatic hedge against unknown risk

**Practical implications for the Society:**
- Land use decisions: the default position is non-development; the burden falls on members who propose extractive use to demonstrate justification — that the use is worth the permanent cost, that what will be lost can be restored, that future members have been considered
- Agricultural practice: the goal is not to maximize yield from the land but to sustain the living community of the soil; interventions are evaluated against what they do to that community, not only what they produce for human consumption
- The commons: the Society's land is not an asset to be optimized; it is an inherited living system in which the Society is a participant; management decisions are made with the logic of the system in view, not only the preferences of current members

## Against natural capital: why economic framing fails

**What needs to be established:**
- That translating nature's worth into economic terms — however well-intentioned as a conservation strategy — concedes the foundational premise the axiom rejects
- That economic framing produces specific distortions that make it inadequate as a basis for the relationship the axiom describes

**The argument:**
- The ecosystem services framework assigns monetary values to the functions that natural systems perform for humans: carbon sequestration, water filtration, pollination, flood control, soil formation. The intent is to make nature's value legible to economic decision-makers who would otherwise ignore it
- The problem is not the numbers — the numbers may be accurate. The problem is the premise: once nature's value is expressed in dollars, it can be compared to other dollar values; once it can be compared, it can be outweighed; once it can be outweighed, its protection is contingent on winning the comparison. This is precisely the contingency the axiom denies
- The further problem: ecosystem services capture only the value that humans can identify and quantify. The living systems that have no identified human use — the species with no known function in the services humans value, the microbial communities whose role is unknown, the evolutionary relationships too complex to map — have no value in the ecosystem services framework. They will be destroyed, because they have no economic standing
- What is lost when a living system is translated into its economic value: its particularity, its evolutionary history, its relationships, its own logic — everything that makes it what it is rather than a quantity of ecosystem service production

**Key thinkers:**
- Sandel, *What Money Can't Buy: The Moral Limits of Markets* (2012) — market values crowd out non-market values; when you put a price on something, you change the terms on which it is considered; not everything that can be priced should be priced
- Monbiot, *Feral: Searching for Enchantment on the Frontiers of Rewilding* (2013) — the critique of conservation that manages nature for human benefit; the argument for rewilding as the approach that takes living systems' own logic seriously rather than managing it for human-defined outcomes

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom V** (no generation creates what it receives) — the companion axiom; Axiom V establishes that we don't own the earth in the proprietary sense; Axiom VI establishes that what we don't own has worth we are obligated to respect; together they define the Society's relationship to the natural world
- **Axiom VIII** (beings capable of suffering have genuine moral standing) — Axiom VIII extends moral consideration to sentient beings based on capacity for suffering; Axiom VI makes a different and broader claim about living systems that goes beyond sentience; the two claims are related but distinct; an ecosystem has the kind of worth Axiom VI describes even if none of its members is sentient in the relevant sense
- **Axiom III** (moral blindness) — the mechanisms of moral disengagement described in Axiom III operate on our relationship to living systems as powerfully as anywhere; the industrial food system, the global supply chain, the financial instruments that fund deforestation — all are designed to ensure that those making decisions never encounter what those decisions cost the living world

---

## The positions we reject

- Anthropocentrism in environmental ethics — the view that nature has value only as it serves human interests; this makes protection contingent on human utility and leaves no basis for protecting what cannot be shown to benefit humans
- The ecosystem services / natural capital framing — well-intentioned but foundationally concessive; it accepts that nature's worth is contingent on human valuation and commensurable with economic values; the axiom denies both premises
- The management model — the premise that humans stand above the systems they would manage, with adequate knowledge and appropriate authority to optimize them; we are embedded in these systems, our knowledge is deeply inadequate, and our track record of management is one of repeated unintended consequence
- The assumption of replaceability — that destroyed natural systems can be recreated through restoration, geoengineering, or synthetic biology; some things can be partially restored over human timescales; most cannot; the assumption of replaceability is used to authorize destruction that would not be authorized if irreversibility were taken seriously
- Development as default — the assignment of burden to those who would protect rather than those who would destroy; this is not a neutral starting point but a specific philosophical choice that the axiom's account of worth directly challenges
- Shallow ecology — the conservation of nature for human benefit rather than for what living systems are in themselves; the axiom requires the deeper position
