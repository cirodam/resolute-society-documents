# Axiom X: Power Tends to Corrupt and Always Tends to Concentrate

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The United States in 2026 presents one of the clearest available demonstrations of what power does when structural constraints fail to hold it. Wealth concentration has reached levels not seen since the Gilded Age. Three individuals possess more wealth than the bottom half of the American population. That concentrated economic power has converted — as it reliably does — into concentrated political power: in campaign finance, in regulatory capture, in media consolidation, in the revolving door between industry and the agencies designed to regulate it, in the appointment processes for institutions meant to be independent of political pressure
- This is not the story of unusually corrupt individuals — though some are corrupt. It is the story of what power does structurally, over time, when the mechanisms designed to constrain it weaken or are deliberately dismantled. The individuals involved are largely behaving as the incentive structures of their positions reward them for behaving. That is precisely the axiom's point: incentive structures that power creates are more powerful than individual virtue
- The regulatory state was designed over a century of reform efforts to counteract the tendency of concentrated private power to capture public institutions. The process of its capture has taken several decades and is largely complete in critical sectors: finance, energy, agriculture, pharmaceuticals. The capture was not primarily achieved through illegality — it was achieved through the systematic exploitation of legal mechanisms: the revolving door, lobbying, campaign contributions, the placement of industry representatives in regulatory agencies, the defunding of regulatory capacity. Legal. Illegitimate
- The tech platform monopolies represent the newest iteration of the same pattern: extraordinary concentrations of economic power converting with remarkable speed into political power, into control over the information environment, into the capacity to shape public discourse in ways that serve the interests of those doing the shaping. The pattern is the same; the medium is new
- Axiom IX identifies what legitimate authority looks like. This axiom explains why legitimate authority, once established, is always under pressure — pressure that does not require malice, that operates through incentive structures rather than intentions, and that will not be resolved by electing better people or appointing more virtuous leaders

---

## What the axiom claims

- **Power tends to corrupt** — not that power makes everyone corrupt, but that the conditions of holding power systematically generate incentives, habits, and perceptual distortions that tend in the direction of self-service rather than service to those the power is supposed to serve; this is documented across cultures, systems, and centuries; it is not a judgment about human nature but an observation about what power does to persons placed within it
- **Power always tends to concentrate** — not just corrupt individuals but power itself, as a structural tendency; concentrated power generates the capacity and incentive to become more concentrated; the mechanisms are structural and operate regardless of the intentions of those who hold power
- **Incentive structures are more powerful than individual virtue** — this is the crucial claim; good people placed in structural positions that reward certain behaviors will tend to exhibit those behaviors; the long record of institutions founded by people of genuine integrity that nonetheless drifted toward self-service is the evidence; virtue is real and necessary but not sufficient
- **No founding intention however pure protects an institution from these tendencies** — founding documents, mission statements, inspiring leadership, and genuine institutional culture are all goods but none of them constitutes a structural defense; the mechanisms of power concentration operate on good institutions as well as bad ones
- **Only structure does** — the specific design of institutions — the distribution of authority, the mechanisms of accountability, the transparency of decision-making, the barriers to entrenchment — is what protects against what power naturally does; this is the insight the Founders had at their best moments and failed to apply consistently
- This is not cynicism — cynicism is the counsel of despair, the conclusion that because power corrupts, nothing can be done; the axiom's conclusion is the opposite: because the tendency is structural and documented, the response is structural and designable; we can build institutions that resist the tendency; doing so requires knowing clearly what we are resisting

---

## The historical evidence: what power consistently does

**What needs to be established:**
- That the corruption and concentration of power is not the aberration of particularly bad regimes but the consistent pattern across systems, cultures, and centuries
- That the evidence is strong enough to treat as axiomatic — as the design assumption from which institutional architecture must proceed

**Key thinkers:**
- Acton, letter to Mandell Creighton (1887) — "Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely. Great men are almost always bad men." The most famous statement of the axiom, written by a Catholic historian reflecting on the papacy. What Acton observed was not primarily individual moral failure but the tendency of positions of power to produce systematic moral distortion in those who occupy them — the tendency to believe that what is good for the powerful is good in general, that the interests of those one governs are less real than one's own interests, that the normal moral constraints on action are suspended for those with sufficient authority
- The consistent pattern across political history: every empire, republic, and democracy that has succeeded in concentrating power has used it to maintain and increase that concentration; the Roman Republic's drift to autocracy; the successive failures of constitutional systems to hold concentrated power at bay; the conversion of revolutionary movements into the regimes they replaced. This is not pessimism — it is pattern recognition
- The American Founders' understanding: Madison, Hamilton, and Jay in *The Federalist Papers* proceeded from the assumption that power will be abused; their constitutional design — separation of powers, checks and balances, federalism, bicameralism, judicial review — was premised on the structural management of self-interest rather than the hope of virtuous leaders. Federalist No. 51: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary. If angels were to govern men, neither external nor internal controls on government would be necessary. In framing a government which is to be administered by men over men, the great difficulty lies in this: you must first enable the government to control the governed; and in the next place oblige it to control itself." The Founders' answer was structure; they were correct about the necessity and inadequate in the design

## The structural analysis: why power concentrates

**What needs to be established:**
- That the concentration of power is not the product of conspiracy but of structural incentives that operate reliably regardless of intention
- That the mechanisms are identifiable and therefore designable-against

**Key thinkers:**
- Michels, *Political Parties: A Sociological Study of the Oligarchical Tendencies of Modern Democracy* (1911) — the "iron law of oligarchy"; Michels studied socialist and labor parties — organizations founded explicitly to represent workers against elite power — and found that they consistently developed oligarchical leadership regardless of their founding principles; leadership requires coordination, coordination produces specialization, specialization produces expertise that ordinary members lack, expertise produces deference, deference produces entrenchment. "Who says organization, says oligarchy." The conclusion is not that organization is impossible but that every organization faces structural pressure toward oligarchy that must be actively resisted. Michels was pessimistic about whether resistance could succeed; the axiom is not — but it takes the structural pressure seriously as a design constraint
- Olson, *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965) — why concentrated interests consistently defeat diffuse publics in political contests; a small group that stands to gain a great deal from a specific policy has strong incentives to organize and lobby for it; a large group that would each lose a small amount from the same policy has weak incentives to organize against it; the result is systematic political bias toward concentrated interests regardless of the democratic preferences of the majority. This is not corruption in the ordinary sense — it is the operation of rational self-interest under asymmetric incentive structures; the outcome is a politics systematically tilted toward the organized few against the unorganized many
- Burnham, *The Managerial Revolution* (1941) — the insight that formal ownership is not the determinant of effective power; in both corporate capitalism and state socialism, actual power migrates to the class of technical managers who run complex organizations; formal owners and formal citizens lose effective control to those who hold operational expertise. The principle generalizes: formal authority and effective power regularly diverge; understanding who actually holds power requires looking past formal structures to the operational realities
- Acemoglu and Robinson, *Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty* (2012) — the distinction between extractive and inclusive institutions; extractive institutions are designed to concentrate wealth and power in the hands of a small elite; inclusive institutions distribute power widely and create conditions for broad-based prosperity; the key finding: extractive institutions are self-reinforcing — once a group gains political power through extractive institutions, they use it to maintain and strengthen those institutions; the dynamic is structural, not merely a matter of who holds office

## Why virtue is insufficient: the structural argument

**What needs to be established:**
- That the tendency of power to corrupt and concentrate is not addressed by ensuring that good people hold power
- That structural design, not character, is the appropriate response

**Key thinkers:**
- Madison, *Federalist* No. 51 (1788) — the argument for structural design over reliance on virtue; the design of government must assume that those who hold power will pursue their self-interest; the structural response is to create incentive structures that align self-interest with the public good ("ambition must be made to counteract ambition") and to distribute power among multiple actors with conflicting interests so that no single actor can dominate
- Tocqueville, *Democracy in America* (1835, 1840) — the insight about soft despotism; Tocqueville observed that the greatest threat to democratic society was not violent tyranny but a gentle, paternalistic despotism in which citizens gradually surrendered self-governance in exchange for comfort and security; "it does not break men's will, but softens, bends, and guides it; it seldom enjoins, but often inhibits, action; it does not destroy anything, but prevents much from being born." The insidious quality of this tendency is precisely that it does not require bad intentions — it is the natural outcome of a democracy where citizens are comfortable, where participation is difficult, and where professional managers gradually absorb what citizens once did themselves (a point that connects to Axiom XII)
- Arendt, *The Origins of Totalitarianism* (1951) — the analysis of how totalitarian movements arise not from the intentions of extraordinary evil but from the hollowing out of civil institutions, the atomization of citizens, and the creation of structural vacuums that concentrated power rushes to fill; the implication: what protects against the worst concentration of power is not good leadership but robust civic institutions, distributed authority, and citizens who exercise genuine participation in their own governance

**The personal virtue trap:**
- When institutions are in crisis, the natural response is to seek better leaders; this response is structurally inadequate because it assumes that the problem is the character of current occupants rather than the structure of the positions
- Good leaders placed in badly designed institutions will tend toward the behaviors the institution rewards; bad leaders placed in well-designed institutions will be constrained by the structure
- The historical record is consistent: institutions that relied on the virtue of their leaders — that lacked structural constraints on the exercise of power, that had no independent accountability mechanisms, that had no genuine distribution of authority — repeatedly failed when the leaders changed
- This does not mean character is irrelevant; persons of genuine integrity in positions of power will tend to exercise it better than persons without it; but they are not a substitute for structure and cannot be relied upon as a structural defense

## The contemporary form: institutional capture

**What needs to be established:**
- That the contemporary American form of power concentration operates primarily through legal mechanisms, not corruption in the conventional sense
- That "capture" describes the conversion of institutions designed to check power into instruments of the power they were designed to check

**Key thinkers:**
- Teachout, *Corruption in America: From Benjamin Franklin's Snuff Box to Citizens United* (2014) — the history of corruption as a concept in American political thought; the Founders' understanding of corruption was not limited to bribery (quid pro quo) but extended to any systemic tendency of those in public office to prioritize private interests over the public good; they designed constitutional structures against this broader understanding; the narrowing of "corruption" to explicit quid pro quo has left the broader phenomenon structurally unaddressed and legally protected
- Lessig, *Republic, Lost: How Money Corrupts Congress — and a Plan to Stop It* (2011) — the concept of dependence corruption; members of Congress are dependent on a small class of major funders for their ability to seek office; this dependence shapes their behavior not through explicit bargains but through the systematic attenuation of attention, priority, and concern for those who cannot fund them; "the economy of influence" operates below the threshold of legal corruption while producing systematic distortion of public purpose
- Stigler, "The Theory of Economic Regulation" (1971) — the economic analysis of regulatory capture; regulated industries have strong incentives and ample resources to influence the agencies that regulate them; those agencies, over time, tend to serve the industries they regulate rather than the public interest; this is not primarily the result of individual corruption but of the structural advantages that regulated industries have in the regulatory process

## The structural response

**What needs to be established:**
- That the structural tendency of power to corrupt and concentrate implies specific structural design requirements
- That "eternal vigilance" is not a slogan but a description of an ongoing institutional practice

**The design principles:**
- Distribution of authority: decisions of significant consequence should not rest with single individuals or small groups; distributed authority creates multiple veto points and requires broader consent
- Separation of functions: those who make rules should not be the same as those who implement them or those who adjudicate disputes about them; the concentration of legislative, executive, and judicial authority in a single actor is the structural definition of tyranny
- Term limits and rotation: positions of power should not be held indefinitely; long tenure produces entrenchment; rotation distributes power over time and prevents the accumulation of authority in any individual
- Transparency: the exercise of power is most readily checked when it is visible; secrecy is the natural ally of power concentration; transparency requirements are structural constraints on what power can do without accountability
- Independent oversight: the mechanisms of accountability cannot be controlled by those subject to accountability; oversight that reports to the overseen is not oversight
- Conflict of interest rules: those who exercise power should not simultaneously hold interests that benefit from its exercise; the structural alignment of personal interest with the direction of power is how institutional capture begins

**Eternal vigilance as structural practice:**
- "Eternal vigilance" means not a perpetual state of suspicion but a set of regular institutional practices: periodic review of whether structures are functioning as designed; auditing of the actual distribution of power versus its formal distribution; active mechanisms for raising challenges to concentrated authority; resistance to the normalization of power accumulation that is not formally prohibited
- The vigilance is structural because the tendency being resisted is structural; it does not require evil actors to operate; it operates on good actors through the incentives their positions create; the structural response must be equally impersonal

## What the Society is built on

- The axiom applies to the Society with the same force it applies to states and corporations; the Society is not exempt from the tendency of power to corrupt and concentrate by virtue of its founding intentions, however sincere
- Every position of authority within the Society — every leadership role, every divisional governance structure, every mechanism that gives some members more influence than others — is subject to the structural pressure the axiom describes
- The structural response within the Society:
  - No single person or faction should accumulate disproportionate authority over multiple domains
  - Accountability mechanisms must be structurally independent of those they hold accountable
  - Decisions of significant consequence to members must require genuine consent, not merely formal approval
  - Terms of authority must be defined, limited, and actually enforced
  - The capacity to challenge and change governance arrangements must be accessible to ordinary members, not only to those who already hold authority
  - Transparency about who makes what decisions, and on what basis, must be structural — not dependent on the goodwill of those who would be exposed by it
- The self-referential warning: the Society's founders and early leaders will have accumulated authority through the trust placed in them in the founding period; that authority, if not structurally constrained, will tend toward the same outcomes the axiom describes in every other institution; good intentions are not the defense; structure is

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom IX** (legitimacy is distinct from legality) — the paired axiom; IX identifies what legitimate authority looks like; X explains why legitimate authority is always under pressure; read together they describe the standard and the permanent threat to the standard; the structural response to Axiom X is what makes the commitments of Axiom IX durable rather than merely aspirational
- **Axiom III** (moral blindness) — the same mechanisms that allow moral blindness toward suffering operate in power's self-serving drift; those who hold power tend to develop perceptual frameworks in which their self-interest appears to coincide with the common good; the structural insulation from accountability that power creates is the structural analogue to the distance from consequence that Axiom III identifies; both require structural responses rather than moral appeals
- **Axiom XII** (institutions that absorb human functions destroy capacities) — Tocqueville's soft despotism is the connection; as power concentrates in institutions and citizens lose the practice of self-governance, the capacity for self-governance atrophies; concentrated power and diminished civic capacity are mutually reinforcing; the structural response to both is the cultivation of distributed authority and genuine self-governance at scale

---

## The positions we reject

- The great man theory of governance — the view that institutional outcomes are primarily determined by the character of those who lead institutions; this is empirically false at the institutional level; structural incentives consistently override individual character; the appropriate response to institutional failure is structural redesign, not the search for better leaders
- Founding document fetishism — the view that a well-designed founding document protects an institution from the tendency of power to corrupt and concentrate indefinitely; founding documents create initial structures; those structures are subject to the same pressures as everything else; maintenance of genuine structural constraints requires ongoing vigilance and periodic renewal, not deference to founding intent
- Cynicism — the view that because power tends to corrupt, nothing structural can be done; the axiom is a structural diagnosis, not a verdict; the same analysis that identifies the tendency identifies the structural responses; the Founders' design, however imperfect, demonstrates that structural constraints on power can function for extended periods; the task is to design better constraints, not to give up on the project
- The view that the tendency only affects bad actors — the claim that power corrupts bad people but leaves good people unaffected; this is false in the specific way the axiom names; the mechanism is the incentive structures that power creates, which operate on good people through legitimate-seeming pressures (the interests of the institution, the importance of maintaining authority to do important work, the perception that challengers are motivated by self-interest while one's own motives are pure) as much as through explicit temptations
- The privatization of accountability — the view that market competition or private institutional design can substitute for structural accountability in the exercise of power; private institutions are subject to the same structural tendency; competition may constrain some forms of power concentration but does not address the mechanisms of capture, entrenchment, and corruption that the axiom names
