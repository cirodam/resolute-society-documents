# Axiom II: The Things That Genuinely Matter Are Multiple and Irreducible

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The political history of value monism: one master value, pursued with conviction, used to override all others
- The axiom names this pattern explicitly: "that decision, pursued with conviction, has repeatedly licensed the destruction of obvious moral claims in the name of ideological consistency"
- Historical cases: Robespierre (virtue as master value), Soviet communism (class interest as master value), fascism (national will as master value)
- Contemporary case: neoliberalism — reducing all value to market efficiency; what cannot be priced has no standing
- The effective altruism movement as a live example: utilitarian maximization as a framework that licenses extreme conclusions in the name of consistency
- Why ideological consistency is a warning sign, not a virtue

---

## What the axiom claims

- Genuine goods are plural and sometimes conflict in ways that have no final resolution
- No master value exists from which all genuine goods can be derived
- Moral conflicts cannot be "calculated away" — judgment is irreducible
- This is not relativism: not all resolutions are equal
- The tension between genuine goods is a permanent feature of moral reality, not a problem awaiting solution
- The capacity to navigate genuine moral tension well is a serious human achievement

**The parallel to Axiom I:** What is true of our knowledge of the physical world is equally true of our knowledge of the good — moral reality resists our desire for simplicity just as physical reality does

---

## The philosophical case: value pluralism

**What needs to be established:**
- That genuine goods are irreducibly plural — not expressions of a single underlying value
- That conflicts between them are real, not apparent
- That this has been the consistent finding of serious moral philosophy across traditions

**Key thinkers:**
- Berlin, "The Pursuit of the Ideal" (1988) — the central statement; the claim that the search for a perfect society is not just impossible but incoherent; incompatible values are equally ultimate
- Berlin, "Two Concepts of Liberty" (1958) — positive and negative liberty as genuinely distinct goods that cannot be reduced to one another
- Berlin, *The Crooked Timber of Humanity* — "Out of the crooked timber of humanity, no straight thing was ever made" (Kant); the implications of pluralism for political thought
- Walzer, *Spheres of Justice* (1983) — different domains of life (family, market, politics, medicine) have different principles of justice; no master principle governs all; tyranny is the invasion of one sphere's logic into another
- Gray, *Two Faces of Liberalism* (2000) — extends Berlin; modus vivendi as the political response to irresolvable value conflict

---

## The philosophical case: judgment cannot be replaced by calculation

**What needs to be established:**
- That practical wisdom is a genuine capacity, not a placeholder for a formula we haven't found yet
- That the particular situation always matters in ways that general rules cannot fully anticipate

**Key thinkers:**
- Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* — phronesis (practical wisdom) as the capacity for good judgment in particular circumstances; the mean is not a formula, it is context-dependent; moral knowledge is different in kind from theoretical knowledge
- Williams, "A Critique of Utilitarianism" (1973) — moral residue: after a tragic choice, something genuine is lost even when the right choice was made; integrity as a value that utilitarian calculation cannot accommodate; the agent's own projects and commitments matter
- Nussbaum, *The Fragility of Goodness* (1986) — tragic conflict in Greek tragedy as philosophical argument; the vulnerability of genuine goods to each other; luck in human flourishing
- Nussbaum, capabilities approach — multiple irreducible human capabilities; a threshold account that doesn't require ranking them against each other

---

## The not-relativism distinction

**What needs to be established:**
- That value pluralism does not collapse into relativism
- That "no master value" is not the same as "no values" or "all values equal"

**Key claims:**
- Berlin explicitly: pluralism means values are objective but plural; relativism means values are subjective; these are opposite positions
- Some resolutions are better than others even without a master principle — judgment, context, and practical wisdom determine which
- The horizon of human values is not unlimited; there are things that fall outside it entirely (gratuitous cruelty, for instance) even without a master value to ground the exclusion
- Pluralism is harder than relativism, not easier — it requires the capacity to hold genuine tension without resolving it prematurely

---

## The positions we reject

- Utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill) — all value reducible to utility or preference satisfaction; conflicts are calculable in principle
- Kantian deontology in its strong form — all duties derivable from a single categorical imperative; moral complexity as a failure of reasoning
- Natural law theory — a single rational moral order from which all values can be derived
- Effective altruism as currently practiced — utilitarian maximization as a framework that treats all values as commensurable and all conflicts as calculable
- Relativism — all value claims equally valid; no external standard; the opposite error from monism
- Nihilism — nothing genuinely matters

**Question to clarify:** Is the target any single-value theory, or specifically theories that claim their master value licenses overriding obvious moral claims?
