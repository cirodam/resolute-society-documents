# Axiom VII: Community Is Constitutive of the Person

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The dominant political philosophy of the modern West — liberal individualism in its various forms — treats the person as prior to community: a self-sufficient subject who brings their nature with them into association and chooses community as a means to individual ends. This premise is wrong about what persons are, and acting on it at civilizational scale has produced predictable consequences
- The epidemic of loneliness is not a personal failing distributed across unfortunate individuals. It is a structural outcome. The US Surgeon General declared it a public health crisis in 2023. The epidemiological evidence is unambiguous: loneliness increases mortality risk at rates comparable to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. This is the measurable result of systematically dismantling the conditions in which genuine community forms
- The specific mechanisms of destruction are not accidents. Market logic converts communal relationships into transactions — what neighbors once exchanged freely becomes a purchased service, removing the reciprocity that made it community rather than commerce. Third places — spaces where relationships formed without the requirement to spend money — have been systematically eliminated or converted. Suburban design engineered the physical conditions of atomization into the landscape: no sidewalks, no proximity, no accidental encounter, no shared space that belongs to everyone. The attention economy manufactured the feeling of connection while delivering its structural opposite: algorithmic engagement that captures attention without generating the presence, mutual knowledge, or sustained commitment that actual relationship requires. Work that once organized stable communities around shared purpose was deliberately destabilized in the name of flexibility
- Each of these changes was made by someone, for reasons, serving specific interests. None of them was the inevitable outcome of progress. Together they constitute a systematic dismantling of the conditions in which persons become fully themselves — which is exactly what the axiom names as the wrong
- The polycrisis makes this urgent in a specific way: the challenges the present moment demands of communities — self-governance, mutual aid, collective decision-making under stress, the sustained work of building alternatives to failing systems — are precisely the capacities that require genuine community to develop and exercise. Atomized individuals cannot do this work. Networks of consumers cannot do this work. Only genuine community can, and genuine community has been methodically destroyed

---

## What the axiom claims

- We do not arrive in the world as finished individuals who then choose association — the liberal premise is empirically and philosophically false; persons are not pre-formed selves who subsequently enter community
- We become who we are through relationship, language, place, and belonging — these are not additions to a self that pre-exists them but the conditions through which a self becomes possible at all
- **Community is constitutive of the person** — not instrumental (a means persons use to achieve individual ends) but constitutive (the condition in which persons are formed); the self that would choose community has already been formed by community
- This is not a preference for togetherness — it is a claim about what persons are and what they require to remain fully themselves; a person deprived of genuine community is not merely inconvenienced; something essential to their personhood is damaged
- The deliberate destruction of community is therefore an attack on the conditions of personhood itself — not a social problem but a metaphysical one
- We are not exempt from these forces — the Society exists within the same environment and is subject to the same pressures; building genuine community requires active and ongoing resistance to mechanisms that will operate on the Society itself

## The philosophical case: the self is constituted through relationship

**What needs to be established:**
- That the person is not prior to their social relationships but formed through them
- That this is not a claim about what is convenient or beneficial but about what persons fundamentally are

**Key thinkers:**
- Aristotle, *Politics* (Book I) and *Nicomachean Ethics* — the human being is by nature a *zōon politikon* — a political animal, a creature of the polis; the person outside the city is either a beast or a god; language, practical reason, and the capacity for moral virtue are social achievements, not natural endowments that persons bring to community; the good life is not achievable in isolation — it requires the political community as its condition
- Hegel, *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807) — the master-slave dialectic; self-consciousness requires recognition from another self-consciousness; the self is not self-grounding but emerges through struggle for mutual recognition; the isolated self is not a free self but a contradiction. *Philosophy of Right* (1821) — ethical life (*Sittlichkeit*) is the concrete community in which abstract freedom becomes actual; family, civil society, and the state are not constraints on a pre-existing self but the conditions in which genuine selfhood is realized
- MacIntyre, *After Virtue* (1981) — the narrative self; we can only understand our actions in terms of narratives embedded in particular communities and traditions; "I can only answer the question 'What am I to do?' if I can answer the prior question 'Of what story or stories do I find myself a part?'"; the liberal self — unencumbered, prior to its ends, choosing from behind a veil of ignorance — is not a philosophical ideal but an incoherent abstraction
- Sandel, *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* (1982) — the critique of Rawls's "unencumbered self"; we cannot understand ourselves as prior to and independent of our ends, attachments, and communities; these are not chosen by a pre-existing self but are partly constitutive of who that self is; "to be a self is not to have a self"
- Taylor, *Sources of the Self: The Making of the Modern Identity* (1989) — identity is always oriented by a moral framework; that framework is always inherited from a community; one cannot have a self without a sense of what matters — and what matters is always worked out in conversation with others, within traditions, across particular histories

**The language argument:**
- Wittgenstein, *Philosophical Investigations* (1953) — the private language argument; there cannot be a language that only one person understands, that refers to essentially private experiences; language is necessarily public, rule-governed, practiced in community; if meaning is use, then the self that thinks in language is already a social self — the very medium of thought is communal
- Gadamer, *Truth and Method* (1960) — we are always already within a tradition; we do not choose our language or the inherited understandings it carries; understanding is always hermeneutical — a fusion of horizons between the reader and the tradition; the self that would choose its community has already been constituted by a prior community it did not choose

## The developmental evidence

**What needs to be established:**
- That the constitutive claim is not merely philosophical but empirically supported by what is known about human development
- That persons deprived of genuine relationship in critical periods do not merely miss an opportunity — they are genuinely damaged in their capacity for personhood

**Key thinkers:**
- Bowlby, attachment theory — the secure base; the internal working model; the finding that the quality of early attachment relationships shapes the architecture of the nervous system, the capacity for self-regulation, and the template for all subsequent relationships; what is formed in early relationship is not a set of habits but a structure of the self
- Winnicott — "there is no such thing as a baby; there is only a baby and someone"; the "holding environment"; the self emerges from a relational matrix and cannot be understood outside it; the capacity for genuine solitude itself — the ability to be alone — is a developmental achievement that depends on having been genuinely held
- Vygotsky, *Mind in Society* (1978) — the zone of proximal development; cognitive development is fundamentally social; the child can do more in genuine collaboration than alone; what the child can do with support today, they can do alone tomorrow; intelligence is not a pre-given individual property but a socially constituted achievement
- Mead, *Mind, Self and Society* (1934) — the self emerges through social interaction; you cannot have a self without having internalized the perspectives of others — what Mead calls the "generalized other"; the "I" (the spontaneous self) is only possible in relation to the "me" (the self as social object, seen through others' eyes); there is no self without this internalized social structure

**The evidence from deprivation:**
- The Romanian orphan studies and attachment research: children deprived of consistent relational care in early years show lasting deficits in cognitive function, emotional regulation, and social capacity that persist into adulthood even after placement in loving homes; what was not formed in the critical period cannot be fully formed later
- The epidemiology of loneliness: the biological evidence that chronic loneliness alters gene expression, immune function, sleep architecture, and cardiovascular health in ways that cumulate into dramatically increased mortality risk; this is not the effect of being sad about being alone but of being deprived of the relational condition the organism requires

## The destruction of community: mechanisms and consequences

**What needs to be established:**
- That the decline of community in contemporary society is not natural, inevitable, or accidental
- That specific mechanisms — designed and implemented by identifiable actors for identifiable purposes — have systematically dismantled the conditions in which community forms

**The mechanisms:**
- **Market logic and commodification:** Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944) — the disembedding of the economy from social relations; the treatment of labor, land, and social relationships as commodities; what was once exchanged in a web of obligation and reciprocity becomes a transaction between strangers. When care, knowledge, time, and presence become services purchased and sold, the reciprocity that made them community disappears; you are left with an economic relationship, not a human one. Sandel, *What Money Can't Buy* (2012): when market values invade non-market spheres, they don't just change the terms — they change the nature of what is exchanged
- **Third place elimination:** Oldenburg, *The Great Good Place* (1989) — the concept of third places; spaces that are neither home nor work; where relationships form without the requirement to consume; where people of different backgrounds encounter each other without agenda; the pub, the barbershop, the park, the library, the community hall. These have been systematically eliminated or converted to commercial spaces over the past half-century. Without them, the accidental encounters from which community grows cannot happen
- **Built environment:** Jacobs, *The Death and Life of Great American Cities* (1961) — the conditions for spontaneous street life and informal community: sidewalks, mixed uses, short blocks, buildings of varying age and cost, density sufficient for eyes on the street; their deliberate replacement by automobile-oriented design that separates uses, isolates individuals in private cars, and eliminates the pedestrian spaces where informal encounter occurs. Suburban design is not merely inconvenient — it is the physical infrastructure of atomization, engineered into the landscape
- **Work destabilization:** the deliberate replacement of stable employment relationships with gig work, contract labor, and "flexibility" that serves the interests of employers by eliminating the stable work relationships around which community once formed; people who change employers every two years and work from home do not build the collegial relationships that were once a significant source of community life
- **The attention economy:** Turkle, *Alone Together* (2011) — the substitution of networked presence for genuine presence; algorithmic engagement that captures attention without generating the mutual knowledge, sustained commitment, or genuine presence that relationship requires; the manufacturing of the feeling of connection while delivering its structural opposite

**The scale of the outcome:**
- Putnam, *Bowling Alone* (2000) — the systematic empirical documentation of social capital decline in America over the second half of the twentieth century; membership in civic organizations, frequency of informal socializing, trust in neighbors and institutions — all declining; the specific mechanisms Putnam identifies (television, sprawl, generational change, time pressure) are the same mechanisms the axiom names
- Murthy, *Together* (2020) — the US Surgeon General's account of loneliness as a public health crisis; the epidemiological evidence; the comparison to smoking; the finding that the United States is experiencing this at scale as a structural rather than individual phenomenon

## What the Society is built on

**The telos states it directly:** "Full orientation is not an individual achievement. It is only possible in genuine community — through real relationships, real stakes, real work done alongside people you are actually accountable to. The community is not the means by which persons develop. The community is the condition without which development is not possible."

**The eight capacities the telos describes cannot be developed in isolation:**
- Practical competence requires transmission — knowledge passed from those who hold it to those who are learning, in the context of actual shared work
- Practical wisdom requires practice in real situations with real stakes and real accountability — it cannot be developed in simulation or through reading
- Honest speech requires interlocutors one is genuinely accountable to — the courage required is meaningless without the presence of people who actually know you
- Accountability requires being seen over time by people who remember — not an audience but witnesses
- Care in the deep sense — "genuine attentiveness to the wellbeing of specific others over time" — presupposes specific others over time; it is not a general disposition but a relational practice
- Civic courage requires an actual civic structure in which showing up matters and absence is felt
- Moral perception is sharpened by encounter with the full reality of others — their suffering, their needs, their perspective — which requires genuine proximity and relationship
- Ecological restraint is not primarily an intellectual commitment but a form of embeddedness — the natural expression of understanding that you belong to something larger than yourself, which is the understanding that develops through belonging to something larger than yourself

**The self-referential warning:**
- The axiom does not describe a problem that only afflicts the surrounding society — it names forces that will operate on the Society itself
- Every time the Society converts a relationship into a transaction, it erodes the condition of its own existence; a community that charges for mutual aid has ceased to be a community in the constitutive sense
- Every time the Society substitutes online engagement for physical presence, it accommodates the very substitution it was built to resist
- Every time the Society allows belonging to become contingent on consumption — on paying dues, purchasing services, meeting productivity metrics — it has reproduced the logic it set out to replace
- These pressures will not be absent because the Society is founded in opposition to them; they will operate continuously because they are structural; the axiom names them so the Society can recognize them when they appear in its own operations

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom IV** (human dignity is equal, sacred, and inviolable) — persons have unconditional worth; Axiom VII specifies the conditions under which that worth can be actually realized; dignity without the community that makes personhood possible is abstract; the Society's obligation to build genuine community is grounded in both axioms together
- **Axiom XII** (institutions that absorb human functions destroy capacities) — the destruction of community described in Axiom VII is in large part the story of institutions absorbing communal functions; care became professional services; knowledge transmission became schooling; conflict resolution became litigation; mutual aid became insurance; the two axioms describe the same mechanism from different angles
- **Axiom XIV** (markets are made, not natural) — the conversion of communal relationships into transactions that Axiom VII identifies as a mechanism of community destruction is the result of specific political and economic choices described in Axiom XIV; the market logic that colonizes community did not appear naturally; it was constructed and can be constructed differently

## The positions we reject

- Liberal atomism — the view that persons are pre-social, that community is chosen by already-formed individuals for individual purposes, that the basic unit of political and moral analysis is the isolated individual; this is wrong about what persons are and has produced, at scale, the epidemic of loneliness the axiom describes
- The social capital framing — which treats community as a resource persons accumulate (Putnam's own framework tends this way) rather than the condition in which persons are formed; the distinction matters: resources can be substituted, traded, accumulated individually; constitutive conditions cannot
- The view that digital connection substitutes for physical presence — the attention economy's fundamental promise; the evidence is consistently against it; networked connection produces the experience of connection without the relational structure that makes it constitutive
- Market liberalism's assimilation of community into voluntary contract — the view that community is just a name for a set of agreements among individuals who retain exit rights at all times; genuine community requires commitment that is not fully revocable, accountability that is not fully optional, belonging that is not contingent on continued satisfaction
- The therapeutic model of community — in which the purpose of togetherness is individual healing and the community is a resource for personal development; this gets the direction backwards; the community is not for the person; the person is formed through the community
- Nostalgic communitarianism — the view that community was better in some prior moment and we should return to it; the axiom is not a claim about any historical community but about what persons require; the task is not recovery but construction
