# Axiom XIII: The Solutions of One Generation Become the Costs of the Next

---

## Why this axiom, why now

- The contemporary polycrisis is in significant part the bill arriving for complexity that was never paid down. The 2008 financial crisis: instruments designed to manage risk had become the mechanism of systemic risk; complexity designed to solve the problem of credit allocation had made the global financial system catastrophically fragile to disruption. The COVID-19 pandemic: globally optimized supply chains, made efficient through decades of complexity accumulation, proved unable to absorb even moderate disruption; the masks, the ventilators, the drugs were not available because the systems designed to make them efficiently available had traded robustness for optimization. The climate crisis: the fossil fuel infrastructure and industrial agricultural systems built to solve the problems of energy and food production are now the primary source of the problem their successors must solve
- These are not examples of systems that failed. They are examples of systems that succeeded — that solved real problems, that generated real prosperity, that justified the complexity they added at each step — until the accumulated complexity became more costly to maintain than it was worth, and a stress arrived that the complexity could not absorb
- The United States at 250 years presents the pattern in its most developed form. The tax code: seventy thousand pages of solutions to previous problems with the problems each solution generated. The legal system: centuries of precedent layering interpretation on interpretation until the system is comprehensible only to specialists and navigable only through expensive intermediaries. The healthcare system: insurance, billing codes, prior authorizations, appeals processes, and administrative procedures that now consume approximately a third of healthcare spending before a single patient is treated. The regulatory state: each regulation written in response to a failure the previous regulatory framework produced. These are not signs of dysfunction — they are the natural outcome of rational problem-solving accumulated without a mechanism for simplification
- The question the axiom poses is not whether complexity is bad but whether a society has the capacity to simplify voluntarily — because if it does not, complexity will be simplified involuntarily, which is what collapse means

---

## What the axiom claims

- **Solutions persist as infrastructure** — every layer of complexity added to solve a problem does not dissolve when the problem is solved; it becomes infrastructure, demanding maintenance, generating dependencies, and producing new problems that require new complexity to address; the complexity that saved a society in one generation burdens the next and breaks the one after
- **Returns on complexity diminish** — the investment in complexity pays off initially; the problems addressed by early layers of organization are the most serious and the solutions are most efficient; but as complexity accumulates, each additional layer produces less benefit than the last while the costs of maintenance compound; the moment arrives — invisibly, without announcement — when the cost of maintaining existing complexity exceeds the benefit it produces; from that moment forward the society is not solving problems but servicing its past solutions
- **The threshold is invisible** — this is the mechanism's most insidious feature; there is no clear signal when the balance tips; the complexity continues to look like the society's greatest achievement while functioning as the source of its greatest vulnerability; the constituencies that defend each layer of complexity are real and rational; the costs are diffuse and accumulating; the moment of tipping is recognizable only in retrospect
- **Brittleness results** — a society that cannot simplify becomes progressively less able to absorb stress; problems that an earlier, simpler version would have weathered become crises; crises that would have been manageable become catastrophes; efficiency and fragility travel together
- **Collapse is involuntary simplification** — collapse is not what happens to a society that fails; it is what happens to a society that succeeded and could not stop; it is the simplification the society could not achieve voluntarily; it is the bill arriving for complexity that was never paid down

## The historical case: Tainter and the collapse of complex societies

**What needs to be established:**
- That the pattern described in the axiom is not a theoretical possibility but the documented mechanism of collapse across human history
- That it is structural rather than accidental — not the result of bad leadership or external misfortune but of the logic of problem-solving itself

**Key thinker:**
- Tainter, *The Collapse of Complex Societies* (1988) — the foundational academic treatment; Tainter examined the collapse of the Western Roman Empire, the Western Chou Dynasty, the Mauryan Empire, the Gupta Empire, the Western Han, the Mesopotamian empires, the Minoan civilization, the Mycenaean civilization, the Indus Valley civilization, the Western Roman Republic, the Mesoamerican highland empires, the Lowland Classic Maya, the Chacoan society of the American Southwest, and others; in each case, he found the same mechanism: the society had invested in complexity to solve problems; the investment had worked; the complexity had become costly to maintain; the marginal return on additional complexity had declined; the society had passed the point where maintaining complexity was producing net benefit; subjects experienced the state as extractive; collapse followed — typically not as catastrophic destruction but as a reversion to lower levels of complexity, often experienced by ordinary people as relief from the burden of maintaining the system
- The specific mechanism: complexity is a problem-solving strategy; it is expensive; societies invest in it because the problems it solves are real and the benefits are genuine; but every additional unit of complexity produces less benefit than the last (diminishing returns) while adding costs that do not diminish; the curve of benefit and the curve of cost inevitably intersect; after that intersection, additional complexity is net negative; but the society cannot stop adding complexity because each new problem seems to require it
- The inability to simplify: every layer of complexity generates constituencies — bureaucrats whose positions depend on it, industries built around it, populations dependent on the services it provides, legal and institutional frameworks that assume it; voluntary simplification requires defeating all these constituencies simultaneously; it is politically and practically nearly impossible; so complexity accumulates until stress breaks what voluntary action could not simplify

**The Roman case:**
- The canonical example; the Western Roman Empire's "collapse" in the fifth century was not primarily a military defeat — it was the culmination of a century and a half of declining marginal returns on the complexity required to maintain the Empire; the cost of the legions, the bureaucracy, the roads, the administration outpaced the Empire's ability to extract resources to pay for them; taxation became crushing; subjects in many provinces welcomed the Germanic kings as an improvement over Roman administration; the "collapse" simplified the political structure and, for many ordinary people, reduced the burden of maintaining it

## Ibn Khaldun and the civilizational cycle

**What needs to be established:**
- That the pattern is not a modern discovery but the oldest sustained observation about how civilizations work
- That it operates on a generational timescale that the axiom names directly

**Key thinker:**
- Ibn Khaldun, *Muqaddimah* (1377) — the oldest and most sophisticated theory of civilizational cycles; Ibn Khaldun identified *asabiyyah* (social solidarity, group cohesion, the capacity for collective action) as the engine of civilizational rise; tribal and nomadic peoples have strong *asabiyyah* — the capacity for mutual support and collective action that comes from necessity and proximity; they conquer and build civilizations; but the civilization they build gradually consumes the *asabiyyah* that produced it; luxury, specialization, institutional complexity, and the growth of intermediary classes erode the direct bonds of mutual obligation; the third or fourth generation inherits the civilization without the social fabric that built it; it becomes unable to defend or renew itself; another group with stronger *asabiyyah* — simpler, more cohesive, less burdened by complexity — rises and the cycle repeats
- The three-generation observation: Ibn Khaldun noted that dynasties typically lasted approximately three to four generations — the builders, those who inherit what the builders made, and those who inherit the structure without the understanding of what it cost; this is not a historical coincidence but the expression of a structural mechanism; it is also the timescale the axiom on capacity loss names: within two generations, what was built is no longer understood by those maintaining it

## Contemporary forms: where we are now

**The financial system:**
- The financial innovations of the late twentieth century — derivatives, securitization, collateralized debt obligations, credit default swaps — were solutions to real problems: how to price and distribute risk, how to make capital available to those who needed it, how to hedge exposure. Each instrument was rational. The accumulated complexity of the system made it impossible for anyone — regulators, banks, investors — to understand the aggregate risk exposure; the complexity designed to manage risk became the mechanism of its amplification; the 2008 crisis was the stress that revealed the fragility the complexity had produced

**Healthcare:**
- Every administrative procedure in the American healthcare system was added to solve a real problem: the prior authorization exists because of previous fraud; the billing code exists to provide specificity for reimbursement; the appeal process exists to protect patients from arbitrary denial; the credentialing requirement exists to ensure quality. Each is rational. Together they constitute an administrative apparatus that absorbs a third of healthcare expenditure, that no individual can navigate without significant expertise or professional help, and that has become the primary barrier between patients and the care they need

**Infrastructure and technical debt:**
- Software systems accumulate technical debt — workarounds, patches, dependencies built on obsolete systems — that make them simultaneously indispensable and unfixable; the systems that run critical financial, governmental, and logistical infrastructure in the United States are built on layers of solutions to previous problems; some run on programming languages that are no longer taught; the people who understood the original systems have retired; the complexity is maintained by people who understand their piece of it and no one who understands the whole

**The regulatory state:**
- The US Code of Federal Regulations runs to approximately 180,000 pages; each rule was written in response to a specific problem that previous rules had failed to address; the accumulation is not irrational — each rule is a response to a genuine failure; but the aggregate complexity is navigable only by specialists, produces compliance costs that fall disproportionately on smaller actors who cannot afford the specialists, and generates the regulatory capture described in Axiom X because only those with sufficient resources can participate meaningfully in the regulatory process

## Brittleness: the fragility of the optimized

**What needs to be established:**
- That the efficiency produced by complexity accumulation and optimization is inseparable from fragility
- That robustness and efficiency trade against each other and that the contemporary choice has been systematically in the direction of efficiency

**Key thinker:**
- Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (2012) and *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable* (2007) — the systematic treatment of fragility; Taleb distinguishes fragile (harmed by volatility and disorder), robust (unaffected by moderate volatility), and antifragile (strengthened by volatility and disorder); complex, optimized systems are fragile: they perform efficiently under expected conditions and fail catastrophically under unexpected ones; simple, distributed, redundant systems are more robust — less efficient under expected conditions but capable of absorbing disruption; the optimization that made modern supply chains, financial systems, and healthcare systems efficient is precisely the optimization that removed the redundancy and slack that would have made them robust
- The "black swan" problem: optimized complex systems are calibrated to handle known risks and known variability; they are catastrophically exposed to events outside the range of what was modeled; the 2008 crisis, the COVID pandemic, the supply chain disruptions — all were predictable in kind if not in timing; the systems were optimized in ways that made them unable to absorb them

**The redundancy principle:**
- Robustness requires redundancy — backup capacity, alternative pathways, slack in the system that looks wasteful under normal conditions and proves essential under stress; complex, optimized systems eliminate redundancy as waste; the Just-In-Time supply chain eliminates warehousing and buffer stock as inefficiency; the single-source supplier arrangement eliminates the cost of maintaining alternatives; the optimized financial system eliminates the capital buffers that would absorb losses; in each case, the redundancy that looks like waste is actually the robustness budget; when it is spent, the system has no reserves

## The distinction from Axiom XII

- Axiom XII describes what happens to *people* when institutions absorb human functions: capacities atrophy, dependency grows, the knowledge goes extinct
- Axiom XIII describes what happens to *systems* when solutions accumulate: complexity grows, returns diminish, brittleness develops, the system becomes ungovernable and then fails
- They are related: the institutional absorption of human functions (Axiom XII) is one of the mechanisms by which complexity accumulates (Axiom XIII); and the complexity that accumulates in systems reduces the capacity of people to operate outside those systems (Axiom XII); the two processes reinforce each other
- But they are analytically distinct: Axiom XII is a claim about human capacities; Axiom XIII is a claim about system dynamics; both must be understood to understand the present situation fully

## What the Society is built on

- The Working Axioms state the design principle directly: "Strength, for a Resolute Society, is the capacity to function with less." This is the specific counter to the complexity accumulation mechanism — not the accumulation of resources, capacity, and institutional structure, but the development of members who can function effectively with minimal institutional support
- The Society will face pressure to add complexity in response to every problem it encounters: when conflict arises, to add dispute resolution mechanisms; when coordination fails, to add administrative structures; when resource allocation is contested, to add governance layers; each addition will be rational; each will be justified; each will make the next addition more likely and the cost of simplification higher
- The structural response: the Society should build with an explicit simplification bias — asking not only whether a new layer of structure solves the present problem but whether it can be removed once the problem is solved; designing for modularity rather than interdependency; maintaining the capacity to function at lower levels of organizational complexity; treating redundancy as robustness rather than waste
- The self-referential warning: the Society's greatest periods of growth and problem-solving will be the periods of greatest risk; the complexity added when solving real problems is the complexity that will burden the next generation of members; the Society must build mechanisms for periodic simplification — for reviewing existing structures and removing those whose costs exceed their benefits — as a standing institutional practice, not an occasional intervention

## Connections to other axioms

- **Axiom V** (no generation creates what it receives) — the axiom of intergenerational obligation runs in both directions; Axiom V establishes that we are obligated to pass on what we received; Axiom XIII names the mechanism by which that obligation is violated: the solutions we create become costs that compound for those who inherit them; read together, they describe both the positive obligation (pass it on intact) and the specific failure mode (burden those who come after with the complexity of your solutions)
- **Axiom X** (power concentrates) — complexity accumulation and power concentration reinforce each other; every layer of complexity creates constituencies that defend it; those constituencies are sources of concentrated interest that, per Axiom X, exercise disproportionate influence on whether simplification occurs; the inability to simplify complex systems is in significant part the story of Axiom X operating on the constituencies that complexity creates
- **Axiom XII** (institutions absorb capacities) — as described above; the two axioms describe related but distinct mechanisms of the same underlying dynamic: systems accumulate complexity, people lose capacity, the combination produces brittleness and dependency that cannot easily be reversed

---

## The positions we reject

- Progressivism about complexity — the view that more sophisticated, more layered, more institutionally complex arrangements are always improvements on simpler ones; the axiom does not reject sophistication, but it rejects the equation of sophistication with strength; the sophistication that cannot absorb stress is fragility wearing the appearance of achievement
- The view that collapse is always catastrophic — Tainter's analysis shows that collapse is frequently experienced as relief rather than disaster; the simplification that collapse forces may restore robustness and reduce the burden of maintaining systems that had become extractive; the axiom is not an argument for avoiding collapse at any cost but for achieving voluntary simplification before involuntary simplification is forced
- The view that past solutions can simply be removed — the constituencies, dependencies, and institutional structures built around existing complexity are real; simplification is not merely a matter of recognizing that something is no longer useful and removing it; it requires political will, the management of transition costs, and the willingness to accept short-term disruption for long-term robustness; acknowledging this does not make simplification unnecessary — it describes the work required
- Technological solutionism — the view that each new layer of technological complexity resolves the problems generated by the previous layer without adding new ones; this is empirically false as a general claim; new technologies solve old problems and generate new complexity, new dependencies, and new fragility; the net complexity tends to increase with each cycle
- The counsel of mere conservation — the view that the axiom implies preserving existing arrangements against change; the axiom is not conservative in this sense; it says that solutions become costs, not that solutions should never be attempted; it says the costs must be paid, which means both adding complexity when genuinely necessary and simplifying when complexity is no longer earning its keep

## Primary texts

**The foundational treatment:**
- Tainter, *The Collapse of Complex Societies* (1988) — the essential work; Chapter 4 on the marginal return theory of collapse

**The civilizational cycle:**
- Ibn Khaldun, *Muqaddimah* (1377) — Books I and II on *asabiyyah* and the rise and fall of dynasties; available in the Rosenthal translation

**Fragility and antifragility:**
- Taleb, *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable* (2007)
- Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (2012) — especially Parts I and II

**Systems behavior:**
- Gall, *The Systems Bible* (originally *Systemantics*, 1977) — the laws of systems and their failures; accessible and important
- Meadows, *Thinking in Systems: A Primer* (2008) — the foundational concepts of systems dynamics; leverage points; the behavior of complex systems

**Historical cases:**
- Gibbon, *The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* (1776-1789) — the canonical account; Volumes I and II for the period most relevant to the complexity argument
- Homer-Dixon, *The Upside of Down: Catastrophe, Creativity, and the Renewal of Civilization* (2006) — applies complexity and energy return on investment analysis to contemporary civilization; a direct application of Tainter's framework to the present

**Robustness and design:**
- Jacobs, *Dark Age Ahead* (2004) — the contemporary mechanisms of cultural forgetting; the accumulation of institutional complexity that forgets what it was meant to serve
- Scott, *Seeing Like a State* (1998) — on the fragility of systems that eliminate local knowledge and variation in favor of legibility and standardization
