## Section One: Nature of the Organization

### What the Society Is

The Resolute Society is a cooperative mutual benefit organization founded on the principle of *ekonomia* — the ancient understanding, articulated by Aristotle and documented across pre-market cultures, that economic activity exists to serve life and meet genuine need, not to generate surplus or accumulate wealth.

The Society is organized as a cooperative under applicable state law, with governance structured on the principle of one member, one vote. It serves its members through collective purchasing, skills exchange, mutual aid, food production, practical infrastructure, and community resilience building. It does not serve investors. It has no shareholders. It generates no profit and is architecturally designed to prevent profit generation.

The Society's economic principle — *ekonomia* — is not a value held alongside other organizational values. It is the architectural principle from which every design choice derives. An institution with no profit mechanism cannot be evaluated as if it had one.

### The Ekonomia Commitment

The distinction between *ekonomia* and *chrematistics* was drawn by Aristotle in the *Politics* and the *Nicomachean Ethics*. Ekonomia is household management — production and exchange oriented toward meeting genuine needs, measured by sufficiency and the flourishing of the community. Chrematistics is the art of money-making — accumulation for its own sake, measured by the size of the surplus.

The entire modern commercial economy is chrematistic. The Internal Revenue Code was developed to capture value generated by chrematistic activity — wages, profits, capital gains, rents, dividends — in an economy where those categories described most economic activity worth taxing.

The Society is explicitly and architecturally ekonomic. The following design commitments make this structural rather than merely claimed:

- **Cost pricing.** All goods and services within the Society's circuit are priced at the cost of inputs plus labor valued at community-assessed contribution rates. No margin is captured. No surplus is generated by pricing above cost.
- **Demurrage.** The kin currency carries a holding cost that discourages accumulation beyond sufficiency. A currency designed to lose value when hoarded is a currency designed against chrematistic accumulation.
- **Member-backed supply.** The kin supply is fixed at 60,000 per member — tracking the circuit's productive capacity rather than monetary policy decisions. No central authority inflates the supply for institutional benefit.
- **No profit distribution.** The Society has no mechanism for distributing profit to investors because it has no investors and generates no profit. Surplus patronage allocations return to members based on participation, not capital.
- **Covenant obligation.** Every member signs a covenant committing to *ekonomia* as an organizing principle of their participation. The commitment is not aspirational. It is enforceable through the Society's Division of Conciliation.

An institution with no profit motive, no profit mechanism, and no profit distribution cannot be characterized as engaging in commercial activity under the IRS's own tests for commercial character. The profit motive is absent by design, documented in the founding architecture, and enforceable through the governance structure.

## Section Two: Nature of the Kin Currency

### What Kin Is

Kin is the Society's internal medium of exchange. It is a closed-loop, non-convertible mutual credit instrument denominated in member-backed productive capacity. It functions as internal accounting for covenant exchange between members of the Society's circuit.

Kin is not a dollar substitute. It is not a convertible virtual currency. It is not a security. It is not a commodity. It is not a pre-issued fiat instrument. It is a record of contribution and a claim on community productive capacity that exists only within the covenant circuit and has no value or existence outside it.

### The Mutual Credit Architecture

Kin operates on mutual credit principles. When two members transact — John contributes labor and receives a credit of 45 kin; Bill receives that labor and incurs an obligation of 45 kin — the credit and obligation materialize simultaneously from the transaction itself. No pre-existing kin changed hands. The aggregate kin in the system at any moment reflects the distribution of contribution and obligation among members, with the total supply fixed at 60,000 per member.

The 60,000 per member supply rule is a liquidity mechanism — ensuring the circuit has sufficient medium to facilitate exchange without friction — and a philosophical statement that membership itself represents productive capacity worth denominating. The supply tracks the community's actual productive capacity automatically: new members bring new capacity and new kin simultaneously; departing members remove both.

A member's kin balance reflects their participation standing in the circuit — the degree to which their contributions and draws are in balance. It is not a bank account. It is not a dollar deposit. It is a participation record denominated in a unit the Society defines and controls entirely.

### Non-Convertibility as Load-Bearing Design

Kin cannot be converted to dollars through any Society mechanism. There is no redemption window. There is no cash-out function. There is no secondary market. A member who holds 400 kin holds a claim on the community's productive capacity — food, skills, repair, tools — accessible only through covenant membership and only within the circuit.

The IRS's own guidance distinguishes convertible virtual currency — which it treats as property subject to capital gains — from non-convertible closed-loop instruments redeemable only within specific ecosystems. Kin sits squarely in the non-convertible closed-loop category. The IRS has implicitly recognized this category as distinct from taxable virtual currency without directly ruling on it.

Dollar convertibility is the mechanism through which the IRS identifies taxable gain in virtual instrument contexts. Remove dollar convertibility and the gain mechanism disappears. Kin's non-convertibility is not a limitation — it is the design feature that most clearly establishes its non-commercial character.

### The Dollar Ore Conversion Mechanism

Members may contribute dollar currency — referred to within the Society as *dollar ore*, reflecting its character as a raw material to be deployed rather than a measure of value — to the Society's collective purchasing function in exchange for kin. The conversion rate is set daily by reference to a commodity basket of staple goods at a defined local reference store, ensuring the rate reflects real food purchasing power rather than arbitrary denomination.

Dollar ore contributed through this mechanism is irrevocably converted to collective productive capacity — food, supplies, shared resources — and cannot be withdrawn, returned, or redeemed for dollars through any Society mechanism. The one-way valve between the dollar economy and the kin circuit is absolute by design and by covenant commitment.

The member who contributes dollar ore receives kin reflecting the community's assessment of their contribution to collective purchasing power. They do not receive a dollar deposit. They do not receive a convertible instrument. They receive a claim on community productive capacity that exists entirely within the circuit.

### Dual Pricing as Institutional Direction

Every listing on the Society's exchange platform displays both a dollar price and a kin price. The dollar price is listed first. This sequencing is not incidental. It is an institutional instruction: use dollars if you reasonably can. Kin is listed second because it is the second choice — the option for when the first choice is genuinely unavailable.

An organization attempting to shield transactions from taxation would do the opposite. It would suppress dollar prices, obscure the relationship between kin and dollar value, make dollar options difficult or invisible. The Society does the inverse at every step. It leads with the dollar price, publishes the conversion rate transparently, and records which currency settled each transaction. The platform is designed to direct members toward dollar exchange wherever dollars are accessible and to document precisely where they are not.

A representative listing reads: *Yard mowing — $45 / 38 kin.* The dollar price is listed first as the preferred option. The kin price is listed second as the alternative for members whose dollar budgets cannot accommodate it. The platform is not presenting two equivalent options. It is presenting a hierarchy: dollars first, kin when dollars aren't possible.

This architecture transforms every kin settlement into documented evidence of dollar unavailability. When a transaction settles in kin despite a dollar price being listed first and a dollar option being explicitly available, the platform records that fact. The member saw the dollar price. The dollar option was offered. The transaction settled in kin. The gap between the listed dollar price and the kin settlement is the signature of dollar constraint, captured transaction by transaction in an auditable record.

The Society does not ask the IRS to accept on faith that kin transactions reflect dollar unavailability. It offers the complete transaction record — every listing, every dollar price offered, every settlement currency chosen — and invites the IRS to identify a single transaction in which a member with uncommitted dollars chose kin over dollars for a service they could have purchased in dollars without consequence. That transaction does not exist in the record. The platform's design makes it nearly impossible for it to exist, because the platform actively directs dollar-capable members toward dollar settlement.

The dual pricing architecture is an institutional declaration: dollars are the preferred medium. Kin exists for the members dollars cannot reach. The record proves, transaction by transaction, which situation each settlement reflects.

### Analogical Support

**Casino Chips**

Casino chips are closed-loop instruments redeemable only within the issuing casino's ecosystem. They facilitate exchange within a private circuit. They have no external market value independent of the casino's redemption commitment. The IRS does not treat casino chips received during play as taxable income because they are not gain — they are a medium of exchange within a closed system.

Kin shares this structural character. It is a closed-loop instrument redeemable only within the Society's covenant ecosystem, with no external market value. The distinction that favors kin is that casino chips exist to facilitate explicitly chrematistic activity — gambling, where profit motive is the explicit purpose. Kin exists to facilitate explicitly ekonomic activity with no profit motive on either side. If the IRS does not tax closed-loop instruments facilitating chrematistic activity, it has a steeper hill to climb taxing closed-loop instruments facilitating non-commercial mutual aid.

**Video Game Currencies**

Non-convertible virtual currencies earned through gameplay — V-Bucks, Shark Cards, Crowns, and analogous instruments — are not treated as taxable income when received through in-game activity. The IRS has focused its virtual currency enforcement on convertible instruments with established external market values. Non-convertible closed-loop instruments earned through activity within private ecosystems have not been characterized as taxable income.

Kin earned through contribution within the Society's covenant ecosystem occupies structurally analogous ground — with the additional protections of non-commercial mutual aid character, *ekonomia* as organizing principle, covenant membership context, and the no-displacement argument that commercial game currency ecosystems do not possess. The analogy supports the non-taxable character of kin while the distinctions further strengthen it.

**The WIR Bank**

The WIR Bank, operating in Switzerland since 1934, issues a complementary currency accepted among member businesses as a medium of exchange. Its counter-cyclical behavior — WIR transactions increase when conventional credit tightens — demonstrates empirically that complementary currencies fill gaps the conventional economy leaves rather than displacing transactions that would otherwise occur. No successful tax authority challenge to the WIR's basic operating model has occurred in its nine decades of operation.

## Section Three: The Displacement Argument

### The IRS Substance Over Form Concern

The IRS's substance over form doctrine asks whether a transaction is economically equivalent to a taxable dollar transaction wearing different clothes. Applied to the kin circuit, the question is: are these exchanges simply dollar transactions denominated in a private currency to avoid taxation?

The answer is no. Categorically. The proof begins with a first principle of economic logic that requires no legal doctrine to establish.

### The First Principle: Displacement Requires a Dollar Transaction to Displace

The IRS barter rules exist to prevent tax avoidance through substitution. The paradigm case: a lawyer who normally bills $500/hour takes a painting instead of cash. The dollar transaction was real and available. The barter was a choice made to avoid it. The substitution is what makes barter income taxable — the taxpayer received the economic equivalent of dollar income through a different medium while avoiding dollar taxation.

This logic has a foundational premise: the dollar transaction must have been possible. Displacement requires a prior dollar transaction to displace. If no dollar transaction was ever possible, there is nothing to displace and the substitution logic has no purchase.

Every kin transaction in the Society's circuit is a transaction between parties whose dollar budgets were fully committed to obligations the kin circuit cannot satisfy. The dollar transaction was not rerouted through kin. It never existed. Kin did not displace it. Kin created it.

*Dollars are more powerful than kin. Kin is available where dollars are not.*

Dollars are universally accepted, externally redeemable, and capable of meeting any obligation the external economy imposes. But that universality comes at a cost — dollars are controlled externally, extracted from communities by design, and for the Society's membership structurally unavailable in any discretionary quantity.

Kin is available within the circuit to any member who has contributed. It cannot pay an electric bill. It cannot satisfy an external debt. It cannot be spent outside the covenant community. It is less powerful than dollars in every dimension that matters to the external economy. It is available in the domain where dollars are not.

These currencies are not competitors. They serve different domains. Dollars handle obligations to the external economy. Kin handles exchange within the community economy. A member manages two budgets simultaneously — a dollar budget fully committed to obligations only dollars can meet, and a kin budget available for everything the circuit provides. The kin budget does not reduce the dollar budget. It operates in the domain the dollar budget structurally cannot reach.

No rational actor chooses a less powerful instrument when a more powerful one is available for the same purpose. Every kin transaction is therefore self-evidencing proof that the dollar alternative was either unavailable or nonexistent for that transaction at that moment. Displacement is not merely unproven. It is the one outcome the first principle of rational economic behavior eliminates.

### Jennifer and Bill: The Argument Made Human

Jennifer has $200 in her bank account. Her electric bill is $180. Her phone bill is due. Rent is next week. She pays Bill in kin to mow her yard.

The IRS displacement argument requires that Jennifer had a dollar alternative available and chose kin instead. But Jennifer's $200 was never available for yard mowing. Every dollar she holds is pre-allocated to obligations the kin circuit cannot satisfy — utilities, rent, phone, food. These are not preferences competing for discretionary spending. They are survival obligations enforced by the external economy in dollars only. Jennifer's dollar budget had zero discretionary capacity before the yard conversation started.

The relevant question is not whether Jennifer had dollars. It is whether she had *uncommitted* dollars available for this specific transaction. She did not. The $200 is not spending money. It is survival infrastructure already spoken for.

This distinction is the core of the displacement argument. The barter substitution doctrine assumes a taxpayer with discretionary dollars who elects non-dollar exchange. The assumption of discretion is load-bearing. Without it, there is no election, and without an election, there is no substitution. Jennifer did not choose kin over dollars for yard work. She allocated her dollars to survival obligations — as any reasonable person must — and used kin for an exchange her dollar budget structurally could not accommodate.

Dollar triage under genuine scarcity is not a choice in the sense the barter rules assume. Under genuine scarcity, every dollar is load-bearing. The question is not which pleasures to fund but which emergencies to defer. Jennifer's yard is not competing with her electric bill for discretionary spending. Her electric bill wins before the competition begins. The IRS barter substitution doctrine presupposes a taxpayer making choices among options. Jennifer is not choosing. She is triaging under constraint.

Bill's situation is where the argument becomes most precise — and where a surface-level challenge must be answered directly.

The IRS might concede that Jennifer lacked discretionary dollars but argue that Bill had alternatives. Bill mows yards for dollars in the regular economy. He has dollar-paying customers. He could have allocated his labor toward them instead of Jennifer. His acceptance of kin, the IRS might argue, was a choice — a decision to serve a kin-paying customer when dollar-paying customers were available elsewhere. That choice, the argument goes, was an election.

This argument fails because it identifies the wrong transaction. The relevant question is not whether Bill's labor has a dollar market in the abstract. It is whether Jennifer's yard was going to get mowed for dollars. It was not. Ever. By anyone. Bill's dollar-paying customers are not Jennifer. They are different people with different dollar budgets in different circumstances. The existence of dollar-paying customers elsewhere does not change what Jennifer's specific need required.

This is the point where the covenant structure becomes legally significant rather than merely descriptive. Bill and Jennifer did not find each other through a market. The market would have allocated Bill's labor toward dollar-paying customers and left Jennifer's yard unmowed. They found each other through membership in an institution that explicitly exists to serve needs the dollar market leaves behind. Bill knows Jennifer. He knows her situation. The covenant they share is a mutual commitment to serve each other's genuine needs regardless of dollar capacity.

Bill's acceptance of kin is therefore not a market pricing decision. It is a covenant obligation honored between two members who both understood that the dollar transaction was never coming for this specific need in this specific relationship. The IRS argument — Bill had dollar-paying alternatives, therefore his kin acceptance was an election — proves too much. By that logic, any skilled person who serves their community at below-market rates, volunteers time, or accepts non-dollar compensation when dollar-paying alternatives exist is making a taxable election. The tax code does not work that way. People are permitted to serve their communities, honor their commitments, and allocate their labor toward needs rather than markets without that choice being characterized as tax avoidance.

The stronger form of the argument stands: Bill accepted kin because he understood that kin was the only way Jennifer's yard would get mowed at all. His acceptance is not evidence that dollar-paying customers were unavailable to him generally. It is evidence that this transaction — Jennifer's need, in this community, under this covenant — had no dollar pathway. Without kin, the transaction does not occur in a different medium. It does not occur at all.

Both parties' choices, read together, constitute mutual proof of this. Jennifer's use of kin reveals that her dollar budget had no uncommitted capacity for this transaction. Bill's acceptance of kin reveals that he understood no dollar pathway existed for this specific need. The transaction is consensual evidence, from both sides simultaneously, that what the IRS calls a displaced dollar transaction was never possible — not in the abstract, but for these two people, in this community, at this moment.

Jennifer and Bill represent the modal case in the Society's membership. The kin circuit does not compete with the dollar economy for transactions the dollar economy can reach. It serves the transactions the dollar economy structurally cannot — not because dollar-capable parties don't exist somewhere, but because they don't exist for these needs, in these communities, between these people.

### The Structural Asymmetry

The exchange relationship between dollars and kin is permanently and structurally asymmetric. Dollars can always become kin — through the Society's conversion mechanism, any member with dollar ore can convert it to kin at any time. Kin can never become dollars — by design, by covenant, and by the one-way valve that is load-bearing to the entire legal architecture.

This asymmetry produces a definitive revealed preference test. A rational dollar-holding actor who wanted to participate in the kin circuit would accept dollars for their labor and convert to kin through the conversion mechanism when they wished to spend within the circuit. They have no rational motivation to accept kin directly in lieu of dollars for a transaction that could have been conducted in dollars — because dollars are more valuable and the conversion option is always available.

Therefore: any member who accepts kin directly for labor or goods reveals by that choice that dollars were either insufficient at that moment or that the transaction is circuit-specific in a way that has no dollar equivalent. The choice of kin is self-evidencing proof of dollar constraint or circuit-specific value creation. There is no third category. A rational dollar-holding actor does not voluntarily accept a less powerful instrument when a more powerful one is available for the same purpose.

To argue displacement, the IRS must demonstrate that a dollar transaction would have occurred absent the kin circuit. The structural asymmetry demonstrates the opposite — that rational dollar-holding actors use dollars, and that kin use reveals dollar constraint. Tax law does not impute irrational behavior to taxpayers. The revealed preference is the economic substance. The economic substance is dollar scarcity or circuit-specific value. Neither is displacement.

### The Complete Taxonomy of Rational Kin Motivations

The first principle and structural asymmetry together produce a closed logical system. Every rational motivation for transacting in kin reflects either dollar constraint or circuit-specific value. No rational motivation exists that constitutes displacement of a dollar transaction that would otherwise have occurred.

The taxonomy is as follows:

- **Fully committed dollar budget — payer side.** The payer has no uncommitted dollars available for this transaction. Every dollar they hold is pre-allocated to survival obligations the kin circuit cannot satisfy. Jennifer and her electric bill. The modal case. The dollar transaction was not displaced by kin — it was foreclosed by the payer's dollar obligations before the kin transaction was contemplated. No dollar was displaced because no discretionary dollar existed to be displaced.
- **Demurrage pressure — payer side.** The payer holds kin approaching the demurrage threshold and has rational incentive to spend it before value decays. This motivation is entirely internal to the circuit — it has no dollar equivalent because dollars do not carry demurrage. The transaction occurs because of kin-specific dynamics the dollar economy cannot produce. This is not displacement of a dollar transaction. It is a transaction the dollar economy could not have generated under any circumstances. The demurrage motivation is not an exception to the no-displacement argument. It is confirmation that the kin circuit has genuine internal logic distinct from dollar logic — producing transactions that are circuit-native rather than dollar substitutes.
- **Circuit access — payee side.** The payee accepts kin because the circuit provides goods and services their dollar budget cannot easily reach — cost-priced food, skills exchange, repair network access, community relationships that dollars cannot purchase. They accept a less power
ful instrument because it unlocks specific value the more powerful instrument cannot access in the same way. The payee is not choosing kin over dollars for this transaction. They are choosing access to the circuit's domain, which is not a dollar domain.
- **Dollar scarcity — payee side.** The payee has low kin balance and needs circuit access. Accepting kin directly for labor is more efficient than accepting dollars and converting. The rational calculation is still dollar-constraint driven, just expressed on the receiving end. The payee is managing their kin budget as a distinct resource from their dollar budget.
- **Covenant participation.** The payee accepts kin as an expression of membership commitment — building participation standing, supporting the circuit's liquidity, honoring the covenant obligation to contribute. Even here the rational calculation is forward-looking and circuit-specific. The payee is not choosing kin over dollars for this transaction. They are investing in future circuit access and community standing that dollars cannot buy in the same way. The motivation is circuit-native, not dollar-avoiding.

The taxonomy is complete. Every rational motivation for a kin transaction falls into one of these categories. Every category reflects either dollar constraint or circuit-specific value with no dollar equivalent. The IRS displacement argument requires identifying a rational motivation for accepting kin in lieu of dollars that none of these categories covers. No such motivation exists. The system is closed.

The first principle closes it. Dollars are more valuable than kin. Rational actors do not voluntarily accept less valuable instruments when more valuable ones are available for the same purpose. Every kin transaction is therefore self-evidencing proof of dollar constraint or circuit-specific value. Displacement is not merely unproven. It is the one motivation the first principle of rational economic behavior eliminates.

### The Empirical Proof

If the Society works — if kin circulation produces flourishing where dollar scarcity produced stagnation — that outcome is itself the argument.

A community that could not function suddenly functioning is not evidence of dollar transaction displacement. It is evidence that no dollar transactions were available to displace. The flourishing is new. It did not exist before. Value was created where none existed — not redirected from dollar channels that were never open to these communities.

The Society intends to document this empirically from the founding of the first chapter — tracking what transactions occurred before the circuit, what productive capacity sat idle, what needs went unmet, and what changed after. The delta between before and after is the evidence that kin enabled rather than displaced. That documentation will be available to any reviewing authority.

### Reembedding: When There Is No Market to Displace

The displacement analysis so far has focused on dollar constraint — members whose dollar budgets are fully committed and who transact in kin because no discretionary dollars exist. That analysis handles the modal case. But it does not fully account for a class of circuit transactions in which the dollar market was never absent due to individual constraint. It was absent because the dollar market structurally excludes these communities regardless of individual circumstances.

Karl Polanyi's concept of reembedding is the correct frame here. Polanyi's central argument was that the self-regulating market disembeds economic activity from the social relationships that historically gave it meaning and constraint — separating labor from community, land from stewardship, exchange from obligation. The Society's circuit is explicitly a reembedding project: taking productive activity that the market disembedded and placing it back inside covenant relationship, mutual obligation, and community accountability.

This distinction — market transaction versus reembedded covenant exchange — is not merely philosophical. It has direct legal significance for the displacement analysis. The barter rules presuppose a market transaction: two parties who could have exchanged dollars chose a non-dollar medium instead. Reembedded covenant exchange is not that. It is a different category of human activity that the market framework cannot describe.

Healthcare makes this concrete. The average individual health insurance premium in the United States runs $700-800 per month. A family plan runs $1,800-2,000 per month. A primary care visit without insurance costs $150-300 out of pocket. A specialist visit costs $250-400. For a household earning $35,000 a year in a rural community — which describes a significant portion of the Society's prospective membership — health insurance was never a line item that got cut. It was never a realistic option. Millions of working people fall in the coverage gap: earning too much for Medicaid, too little to make marketplace plans affordable even with subsidies. They don't access the healthcare market. They go without.

When a physician member of the Society provides health consultations in the circuit for kin, the IRS displacement framework asks: what would this have cost in dollars? The abstract answer is $150-300 per visit. But that answer presupposes a market transaction that was available and bypassed. These members were never accessing that dollar market. They were going without care. Or they were deferring until problems became emergencies and then accumulating medical debt they cannot discharge.

The physician is not substituting kin for her dollar billing rate. She is providing care in a domain the dollar economy structurally foreclosed — to members she would never encounter as paying patients, under a covenant relationship that has no commercial equivalent. The relevant comparison is not her dollar rate. It is the absence of care that existed before she joined.

This is reembedding in Polanyi's precise sense. The physician's labor was disembedded by the market — allocated by price signals toward patients who could pay, away from patients who could not. The circuit re-embeds it inside covenant relationship, where the allocation principle is need rather than dollar capacity. That reallocation is not a market transaction in a non-dollar medium. It is a fundamentally different kind of human activity.

The IRS barter rules were not written for this. They were written for the lawyer who takes a painting instead of cash — a market participant making a medium-of-exchange substitution. They have no framework for taxing the reembedding of economic activity in covenant community because that category did not exist in the framers' conceptual world. Applying market transaction rules to reembedded covenant exchange is not enforcement of the tax code. It is a category error in its application.

The reembedding argument does not stand alone. It reinforces and deepens the displacement argument: these are not dollar transactions rerouted. They are transactions the dollar economy never made available, being made available for the first time through covenant relationship. The FMV imputation asks what they would have cost in a market. The prior question — whether a market existed for these specific people, for these specific needs, in these specific communities — must be answered first. For the class of transactions the circuit reembeds, the answer is no. There was no market. There is nothing to displace.

### The Fair Market Value Problem

The IRS barter rules impute fair market value to goods and services exchanged — what would this cost in dollars? That imputation assumes a dollar market exists for the thing being exchanged.

The Society's dual pricing architecture lists a dollar equivalent alongside every kin price. The IRS may use those figures to establish fair market value for imputation purposes. The Society does not contest this. It invites it.

But fair market value for imputation purposes is a different question from whether a dollar market existed for this specific transaction between these specific parties. The platform's listing of *Yard mowing — $45 / 38 kin* establishes a conversion rate — what the service costs if dollars are available. It does not establish that $45 in uncommitted dollars was available to Jennifer at the moment of the transaction. It was not. The conversion rate and the available market are not the same thing.

For the class of transactions the circuit reembeds — healthcare, legal counsel, skilled trades, specialized knowledge — the FMV problem is more fundamental. The abstract dollar price exists. The market for these services among these members does not. A physician's consultation listed at $200 / 160 kin establishes what the service costs in a market that was never accessible to the recipient. The FMV imputation answers a counterfactual that was never true: what would this have cost if this member could have paid for it? They could not. They did not. They went without until the circuit made it possible.

Even accepting the IRS's imputed FMV, the displacement question remains: was a dollar transaction possible and avoided, or was no dollar transaction ever possible for this member for this need? The transaction record, combined with member financial data and the documented absence of prior access, demonstrates the latter.

The deeper problem is what follows from imputation. If Bill's yard-mowing labor is imputed at $45 of barter income, the IRS expects Bill to pay roughly $15 in dollar taxes on that transaction. Bill transacted in kin precisely because no dollar offer was available. The IRS is demanding dollar taxes from someone who performed a service in a non-dollar medium because no dollar medium existed for either party. This is not a collection problem to solve later. It is a category error in the application of the barter rules.

The rules presuppose that conversion to dollars is possible — that the taxpayer can sell the painting, sell the stock, satisfy a dollar tax liability from the value received. Kin cannot be converted. The mechanism the barter rules rely on to make non-dollar income taxable in dollars does not function here. Where no discretionary dollar market existed, no dollar income was avoided. Where no conversion pathway exists, no dollar tax can be paid. The category error is the IRS's, not the Society's.

### The Moral Argument

This section states plainly what the preceding sections establish structurally.

The communities the Society serves have been systematically deprived of the dollar liquidity that the tax code presupposes. The dollar economy did not fail to reach these communities by accident. Deindustrialization, extraction, the concentration of productive surplus in institutions and individuals located elsewhere, the deliberate organization of local economies to export value rather than circulate it — these are documented historical processes that produced dollar scarcity as an outcome.

The state's taxing authority rests on a social contract: the state provides conditions for productive activity; individuals and communities engage in that activity; the state captures a portion of the surplus to fund collective needs. In dollar-scarce communities the state failed its side of that contract. It did not provide conditions for productive activity. It provided conditions for extraction and then withdrew.

The kin circuit operates in the space the contract never covered — not in defiance of it but in the vacuum it left. Taxing kin transactions in dollar-scarce communities is asserting a claim on value the state created no conditions to produce. It is extraction continuing by a different mechanism after the original extraction left nothing dollar-denominated worth taking.

Jennifer's electric bill is due Friday. Bill needs food his dollar wages cannot cover. The Society gives them a way to help each other that the dollar economy foreclosed. If the dollar economy wished to capture these transactions through taxation it had the opportunity to make dollars available to these communities in sufficient quantities. It did not. The flourishing that results from the kin circuit is new value — created by covenant members in community with each other, with no profit motive, in service of mutual sufficiency. It belongs to the community that produced it.

## Section Four: Cooperative Law Framework

### Cooperative Structure

The Society is organized as a cooperative mutual benefit entity under applicable state law. Member transactions within the Society's circuit are patronage activity — members using their own cooperative's services — rather than commercial exchange between parties with potentially adverse interests.

The IRS has never successfully argued that cooperative members' patronage of their own cooperative constitutes taxable displacement of external market transactions. Cooperative patronage is the entire basis of cooperative law. Members using their cooperative's services in the cooperative's internal accounting unit is normal cooperative activity, recognized and accommodated by Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code.

### Subchapter T Treatment

Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code governs the tax treatment of cooperative patronage allocations. When a cooperative member participates in cooperative activity — contributing labor, purchasing from the store, using the tool library — the cooperative may issue written notices of allocation reflecting that participation.

The Society's kin credits are properly characterized as written notices of allocation denominated in the Society's internal unit. Non-qualified written notices of allocation — which the Society issues — are not taxable to the member at issuance. They are potentially taxable at redemption, but redemption of a cooperative allocation for goods and services priced at cost, with no profit component, in a non-commercial mutual aid circuit with no dollar convertibility, produces minimal to no taxable value for the reasons established in the preceding sections.

The cooperative's internal denomination of its accounting in kin rather than dollars is a recognized cooperative accounting practice. A cooperative is not required to denominate its internal accounts in dollars. The choice to denominate in kin — a unit that reflects the community's actual productive capacity rather than an external monetary authority's decisions — is consistent with cooperative law and expressive of the Society's economic sovereignty commitments.

### The Treasury Balance

The Society's treasury holds the unearned portion of each member's potential 60,000 kin allocation — the productive capacity committed by covenant membership that has not yet been activated through contribution and exchange. This balance is the Society's internal accounting of member-backed productive capacity denominated in its own unit.

The treasury balance is not a dollar asset. It is not income to anyone. It is not a pool of pre-issued currency being distributed. It is a balance sheet entry in a non-dollar unit reflecting the productive potential of the membership — analogous to a cooperative's internal valuation of its own productive capacity, which does not constitute taxable income to the cooperative or its members.

## Section Five: The Ekonomia Doctrine in Tax Context

### Historical Development of the Income Tax

The federal income tax was established by the Sixteenth Amendment in 1913 and the Revenue Act of the same year. The modern framework developed substantially through the 1930s and 1950s. The economy those framers contemplated was wage labor, commercial exchange, and capital income. The foundational concept of income itself derives from the Haig-Simons definition — consumption plus change in net worth — which is a chrematistic framework measuring accumulation rather than sufficiency.

The framers of the income tax were addressing the concentration of enormous wealth in industrial and financial capital — the surplus generated by large-scale chrematistic activity. They were not addressing mutual aid exchange between covenant community members, subsistence production, informal labor sharing, or complementary currency circuits. These things were not in the conceptual frame because the political economy being taxed did not include them.

The result is not a deliberate exclusion of non-market mutual aid from favorable tax treatment. It is an absence — a gap where the law simply did not go because the framers' frame did not extend there. The Society operates in that gap.

### What Income Actually Requires

The leading case on the definition of income, *Eisner v. Macomber* (1920), defined income as the gain derived from capital, labor, or both — with *gain* as the operative word. Something received must represent an accretion to wealth, a flow of value from a source, to constitute income.

In a genuine equal exchange oriented toward sufficiency — labor for food of equivalent value, repair for produce of equivalent value — there is no gain. Both parties end where they started in terms of net worth, with different things. The IRS barter rules paper over this by imputing market value to both sides and treating the exchange as two simultaneous taxable transactions. But that imputation is itself a chrematistic assumption — that everything has a market value and that market value is the correct measure of economic reality.

In a circuit explicitly designed to operate outside market valuation, with cost pricing, demurrage, non-convertibility, and covenant commitment to *ekonomia*, the market value imputation has no basis. The exchange is not two taxable transactions. It is one reciprocal act of mutual sufficiency between covenant members.

### The Household Production Gap

The tax code's most revealing structural feature for the Society's argument is its treatment of household production. Cooking your own meals, growing your own food, repairing your own equipment — none of this is taxable income despite producing real economic value. The imputed value of household production in the United States runs into the trillions annually. It is entirely untaxed.

If you hire someone to cook, clean, or repair — the payment is income to the recipient. The economic activity is identical. The only difference is whether it passes through a market transaction.

The tax code taxes market transactions and ignores non-market equivalents. This is not a philosophical choice about non-market economics. It is a practical limitation — non-market production is not observable and therefore not taxable. The consequence is that the code structurally presupposes market exchange as the domain of taxation.

The Society's kin circuit is non-market exchange by design — not observable through dollar channels, not motivated by profit, not oriented toward accumulation. It sits in the same structural shadow as household production — outside the market, outside the code's presupposed domain — with the additional protection of a recognized cooperative structure providing established treatment for the transactions that do require accounting.

## Section Six: The Four Distinguishing Criteria

The Society proposes the following four criteria as the operational definition distinguishing mutual aid exchange from commercial market activity. When all four are present, the exchange is non-commercial in character regardless of whether it involves a medium of exchange or an accounting system.

- **Membership basis.** The exchange occurs between members of a defined community bound by covenant obligations to each other. Not between strangers. Not open to anyone willing to pay. Between people in ongoing relationship who have accepted mutual obligation as the condition of membership.
- **Sufficiency orientation.** The exchange is oriented toward meeting genuine need rather than generating surplus. Cost pricing rather than market pricing is the operational expression of this. The measure of success is whether needs are met, not whether a margin was captured.
- **Non-fungibility.** The medium of exchange cannot be converted to dollars or used outside the community circuit. It is a relationship accounting tool with no value outside the relationships it records. Non-fungibility removes the dollar reference point on which the fair market value imputation depends.
- **Relationship primacy.** The relationship between exchanging parties precedes, survives, and is deepened by the transaction rather than being incidental to it or liquidated by it. The exchange is evidence of covenant membership, not a commercial event that happens to occur between people who know each other.

The Society's kin circuit satisfies all four criteria simultaneously and by architectural design. When all four conditions are met, the exchange is non-commercial in character regardless of the accounting mechanism used to record it. This distinction is grounded in the anthropological literature on reciprocity, the philosophical distinction between *ekonomia* and *chrematistics*, and the tax code's own recognition that commercial intent is a necessary condition for income characterization.
