# Mortuary Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Mortuary Division exists to ensure that the Resolute Society can care for its dead with dignity and without creating public health hazards — today and under whatever conditions tomorrow brings. It does this by maintaining the knowledge, capacity, and protocols to manage death at community scale, independent of the funeral industry or municipal infrastructure.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around death care — an instrument of collective action, not a provider of services. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by what the community can do.

Care of the dead was a community and family function for most of human history. The professionalization of death care by the funeral industry is a recent development — within the memory of living grandparents, families washed, dressed, and buried their own. That knowledge is recoverable. Recovering it is not a matter of replacing grief with logistics — it is a matter of ensuring the community is not helpless when institutional systems are unavailable.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no member's remains are handled in ways that create public health hazards or contaminate water and food sources
- That death is documented even when institutional systems — hospitals, coroners, vital records offices — are unavailable or overwhelmed
- That the community has the capacity to care for its dead at any scale the situation demands, without depending on commercial or government services
- That death care knowledge is actively held broadly enough across the community that it cannot be lost

## Core Functions

- **Death Documentation** — maintaining records of deaths, causes, and chain of custody when institutional systems are unavailable or overwhelmed
- **Body Care** — washing, shrouding, and preserving remains in the interval between death and interment
- **Interment** — burial and other disposition, with proper siting, depth, and distance from water and food sources
- **Infectious Remains Protocols** — handling remains from communicable disease deaths according to elevated safety procedures
- **Advance Planning and Grief Support** — documenting member wishes before they are needed and supporting bereaved households through loss

## Death Documentation

The division maintains records of every death within the community — who died, when, where, the apparent cause, and who was present. This function matters even when — especially when — institutional systems are unavailable. A community that cannot account for its dead cannot track disease patterns, cannot resolve questions of property and dependents, and cannot provide bereaved families with the records they will eventually need when institutions resume functioning.

**Death certificates** in normal circumstances require the signature of a licensed physician, physician assistant, nurse practitioner, or coroner. When those systems are unavailable, the community cannot issue legally valid certificates — but it can and must maintain its own internal death registry. Community records created during a period of institutional failure become the evidentiary basis for legal proceedings once institutions recover. They should be treated accordingly: thorough, specific, and preserved in multiple copies.

**Every death record should capture at minimum:** full name and any identifying information, date and time of death or discovery, location, apparent cause or circumstances, names of individuals present or who discovered the body, and the name of whoever is responsible for the remains. If a Healthcare Division member or anyone with medical knowledge is available to assess the body, their observations should be documented as well.

**Cause of death determination has direct operational consequences.** A person who died from a non-communicable cause — injury, heart failure, chronic illness — can be handled under standard protocols. A person who died from a suspected communicable disease requires elevated protocols (see Infectious Remains Protocols). The division must never assume a benign cause of death when the circumstances are unclear, particularly if the death occurred during an active disease outbreak in the community.

**A community death registry** should be maintained as a formal division record — not individual household records that can be lost, but a centralized log the division owns and maintains. It should be kept in written form, not solely digital, and copies should be held in multiple locations. In an extended crisis involving multiple deaths, this registry becomes a critical community document.

**Notification** is the division's first responsibility after a death is reported. The division should establish a clear protocol: who is notified, in what order, and what triggers each next step. Family or household members are notified first if not already aware. The division then takes responsibility for coordinating what happens next — documentation, body care, and interment — so that bereaved family members are not left to manage logistics alone in the immediate aftermath of loss.

## Body Care

Body care is the preparation of remains in the interval between death and interment — washing, positioning, shrouding, and preserving the body as long as the situation requires. This was a family and community function for most of human history. It requires no specialized equipment, no chemicals, and no professional training. What it requires is knowledge, composure, and appropriate protective equipment.

**Time is the primary constraint.** Decomposition begins within hours of death, accelerated by heat. In warm conditions, visible decomposition can begin within 24 hours. Cooling is the most effective tool for extending the window between death and burial. A body kept below 40°F decomposes at a fraction of the rate of one left at room temperature. Where mechanical refrigeration is unavailable, ice packed around — not directly on — the body significantly slows decomposition. Dry ice (solid CO2) is more effective than water ice: it maintains lower temperatures, sublimates rather than melting into pooled water, and does not require drainage management. The body should be placed in the coolest available location — a shaded, ventilated room, a root cellar, or any space that can be kept cool.

**Positioning should happen before rigor mortis sets in.** Rigor mortis begins within two to four hours of death, peaks at around twelve hours, and resolves over the following 24-48 hours. Before rigor sets, the body is pliable and can be arranged naturally — on its back, arms at the sides or crossed over the chest, eyes and mouth gently closed. After rigor has fully set, positioning becomes difficult or impossible without forcing. The division's protocols should ensure that body positioning happens promptly after death, not hours later.

**Washing** is traditional in nearly every human culture and has both hygienic and spiritual significance. A body is washed with clean water and mild soap, dried, and dressed or shrouded. Gloves should be worn. Standard fluid-resistant gloves are sufficient for washing remains from non-infectious deaths — elevated PPE is required for suspected communicable disease deaths (see Infectious Remains Protocols). Natural orifice leakage after death is normal and should be anticipated; absorbent materials and careful positioning manage this. The division should train members in basic washing procedures so that families who wish to participate in caring for their own dead can do so with confidence.

**Embalming is not required.** This is widely misunderstood. No US state legally mandates embalming in most circumstances. Embalming — the injection of formaldehyde-based preservative into the vascular system — is a mid-19th century development that grew from the Civil War need to transport soldiers' bodies home over long distances. It is a commercial practice, not a public health requirement. Natural burial without embalming is legal in every US state. The division should not acquire or use embalming chemicals. Cooling, prompt interment, and proper shrouding are sufficient.

**Shrouding** is the simplest and most universal preparation for burial. A shroud is a length of plain cloth — linen, cotton, or any natural fiber — wrapped around the body and tied or pinned at the head, waist, and feet. It requires no sewing, no special materials, and no equipment. The division should maintain a supply of natural-fiber cloth for this purpose. Families who wish to use a casket may do so; a plain wooden box requires basic carpentry skills and is traditional in many communities.

**Religious and cultural requirements** vary significantly and must be known before they are needed. Muslim tradition requires burial within 24 hours of death if possible, preceded by specific washing and shrouding practices. Jewish tradition also calls for prompt burial and prohibits embalming. Other traditions have specific requirements around who may touch the body, how it should be positioned, and what it should be dressed in. The division's advance planning program (see Advance Planning and Grief Support) should document each member's wishes, including any religious or cultural requirements, while they are living.

## Interment

Interment is the burial and final disposition of remains. Done correctly, it is a public health protection measure as much as it is a ritual act. Done incorrectly — wrong location, insufficient depth, proximity to water — it becomes a contamination source that can persist for years.

**Burial siting is a public health decision first.** A grave must be located a minimum of 150 feet from any well or groundwater source and at least 50 feet from any surface water — streams, ponds, or drainage channels. The bottom of the grave must be at least two feet above the seasonal high water table; in flood-prone areas or areas with high water tables, this constraint may eliminate many otherwise convenient sites. The grave should be sited on elevated, well-drained ground, away from any area that collects standing water. Decomposition leachate — the liquid byproduct of decomposition — moves through soil and reaches groundwater; siting violations are not theoretical risks.

**Depth must prevent disturbance.** The traditional six feet of depth is not arbitrary — it reflects the need to keep remains below the reach of scavenging animals and below the zone of surface soil disturbance. The practical minimum is three to four feet of soil cover above the remains; six feet total grave depth is the appropriate standard where soil conditions allow. Sandy or loose soil may require deeper burial or reinforced grave walls to prevent collapse.

**Lime does not accelerate decomposition.** This is a common misconception. Calcium hydroxide (hydrated lime) added to a grave raises soil pH, which actually slows microbial decomposition. Its practical value is in odor control and short-term fly deterrence during the burial process, not in reducing the timeline to full decomposition. Adding lime is not a substitute for proper depth and siting.

**Natural burial is legal and appropriate.** Every US state permits natural burial — interment of an unembalmed body in a biodegradable shroud or plain wooden container, without a vault or liner. Most states require a burial permit, separate from the death certificate, which is typically issued by the local registrar of vital records or county health department. The division should understand the permit requirements in its jurisdiction and maintain relationships with the relevant authorities. In a sustained crisis where those authorities are unavailable, the division should document its own burial records as thoroughly as possible to support retroactive permitting when normal functions resume.

**Home burial on private property** is legal in most US states, though some states prohibit it or require specific conditions. The division should establish the applicable rules in its jurisdiction. Where permitted, home burial on member-owned private property is a viable interment option that does not require a designated cemetery.

**A community burial ground** should be identified and developed as a division priority. This is a parcel of land — owned, leased, or established through agreement with a willing landowner — designated for community use as a burial site. The site should meet all siting requirements, be accessible, be on land not needed for agriculture or construction, and be on ground above the seasonal high water table. The division should establish the site, document its location and legal status, and maintain it as a standing community resource rather than identifying a site reactively when it is urgently needed.

**Mass casualty situations** require specific guidance. WHO guidance is clear: individual burial is always preferable to mass graves. Mass graves complicate later identification, prevent family grieving, and are not necessary for public health even in high-mortality events. The primary epidemic risk after mass casualty events comes from the living — crowded conditions, contaminated water, poor sanitation — not from the dead. The division should resist pressure to resort to mass burial except under extreme circumstances, and should document the location, identity, and circumstances of every individual buried even when doing so is logistically difficult.

**Cremation** requires sustained temperatures of 1,400 to 1,800°F for two to four hours — conditions that cannot be reliably produced without purpose-built equipment. Open-air cremation (funeral pyres) is legal in a small number of US states and has been the traditional practice across many cultures. The division should understand what is legally available in its jurisdiction. Where cremation is not a practical option, it should not be treated as one.

## Infectious Remains Protocols

Most bodies do not pose elevated infection risk to those handling them. The widespread belief that the dead cause epidemics is not well-supported — WHO guidance is explicit that dead bodies do not cause disease outbreaks after natural disasters, and that most pathogens die with or shortly after their host. The epidemic risk in high-mortality events comes from the living: crowded conditions, contaminated water, broken sanitation. Fear of the dead has historically driven harmful practices — hasty mass burials, burning of bodies without cause — that serve no public health purpose and prevent families from grieving properly.

However, a specific set of diseases do pose real and serious risks from remains. These exceptions are narrow but critical, and the division must treat them as categorically different from standard body care.

**The diseases that require elevated protocols are:**

- **Cholera** — *Vibrio cholerae* survives in gut contents and bodily fluids for hours to days after death. The primary vector is diarrhea and vomit. Contact with these fluids — including during body washing — can transmit infection directly.
- **Typhoid** — *Salmonella typhi* behaves similarly. Fluids from a typhoid death are infectious.
- **Viral hemorrhagic fevers** — Ebola, Marburg, and related viruses are among the most dangerous cases. Viral load peaks at the moment of death, and the body is more infectious immediately after death than the person was while living. West African Ebola outbreaks spread significantly through traditional body-washing practices. A confirmed or suspected hemorrhagic fever death must be treated as an extreme hazard.
- **Plague** — *Yersinia pestis* in pneumonic form can be transmitted from remains. Bubonic plague poses lower risk from body handling than from flea vectors, but the pneumonic form is airborne.

**Standard body care — washing, positioning, shrouding — must not be performed on remains where any of these diseases are suspected.** The body should not be washed. It should be shrouded or bagged with minimal handling and buried promptly. Family members who wish to participate in body care must be protected or redirected — this is one of the most difficult conversations the division will have, and it must be handled with both clarity and compassion.

**PPE for infectious remains handling:** N95 or better respirator, fluid-resistant gown or apron, double nitrile gloves, eye protection, and rubber boots. The number of people handling the body should be minimized — two or three is sufficient for most situations. No one with open wounds or compromised skin should handle infectious remains.

**Body bags** are the gold standard containment for infectious remains. Where body bags are unavailable, heavy-duty plastic sheeting — 6 mil or thicker — can be used to wrap and seal the body. The exterior of the wrapping should be treated as contaminated. Handlers should not touch their faces during handling and should remove PPE carefully to avoid self-contamination, removing the outer glove layer before touching PPE closures.

**All fluid contact surfaces** — the area where the body was found or cared for — should be treated as contaminated in suspected infectious disease deaths. A 0.5% chlorine solution (one part household bleach to nine parts water, or calcium hypochlorite mixed to equivalent strength) is effective against most pathogens including cholera and hemorrhagic fever viruses. Surfaces should be soaked, not just wiped.

**Burial follows standard protocols with additional urgency.** Prompt burial at proper depth and siting reduces risk. Lime can be added around — not directly on — the shrouded remains for additional odor and fly control. The burial site should be marked clearly and its location recorded, both for community records and to prevent future inadvertent disturbance.

**Close contacts of the deceased must be notified immediately.** Anyone who had contact with the person while ill, or who participated in body care without adequate PPE, should be informed of the suspected cause of death and advised to monitor for symptoms. This notification is a division responsibility and should be coordinated with the Healthcare Division at once. A death from a communicable disease is a community-level event, not a household one — the division must treat it accordingly.

**When a death pattern suggests an outbreak** — multiple deaths from similar causes, deaths clustering in time or place, deaths with common symptoms — the division should immediately escalate to the Healthcare Division and treat all subsequent deaths as potentially infectious until proven otherwise. Outbreak recognition may fall to the Mortuary Division before it falls to anyone else, because the division sees deaths across the community.

## Advance Planning and Grief Support

The division's work begins before any member dies. Knowing what a person wants — how they wish to be cared for, where they wish to be buried, who should be notified, what religious or cultural practices matter to them — transforms the chaotic immediate aftermath of a death into a set of tasks the division can execute with purpose. Advance planning is what makes dignified care possible under pressure.

**Every member should have a death care record on file with the division.** This is a simple document that captures: burial or disposition preferences; any religious or cultural requirements for body care and interment; who should be notified immediately upon death; any specific wishes for memorial service or community acknowledgment; and information about dependents or household members who will need support. This record should be completed while the member is living and well, reviewed periodically, and held by the division in a protected location separate from the member's household. A record that exists only in the home of the deceased is not available when it is needed.

**The division should not wait for members to come to it.** Conversations about death are avoided in most communities until they cannot be. The division should normalize this planning — framing it as practical preparation, not morbidity — and work through membership systematically until every member has a completed record. Knowledgeable and trusted community members who are comfortable with these conversations should lead the program.

**The immediate aftermath of a death places acute practical demands on a bereaved household.** Food, childcare, basic logistics — the things a person managed while living that now fall on those remaining. The division's grief support function coordinates with Food Security, Housing Security, and other divisions to ensure bereaved households are not left to manage alone. This is not a peripheral function. A community that abandons its bereaved to practical hardship on top of grief has failed a basic obligation.

**Memorial services** are the community's formal acknowledgment of loss. They matter for cohesion and resilience — a community that cannot grieve together cannot sustain itself through extended hardship. The division should support and help organize memorial gatherings appropriate to the deceased's wishes and the community's culture. These need not be elaborate; they must be real. The form should follow what the community and family actually need, not a formula.

**Children should not be excluded from death care or memorial practice.** Children have historically participated in the full arc of death — caring for the dying, washing the dead, attending burial, grieving openly in community. Removing children from these processes is recent and has not served them well. The division should support families in including children in age-appropriate ways, and should treat children's grief as real and requiring direct acknowledgment.

**Grief is not a problem to resolve.** The division's support role is not to accelerate mourning or return households to function as quickly as possible. It is to ensure that bereaved members have what they need — practical support, community presence, time — to grieve fully. Extended or complicated grief, particularly in communities experiencing multiple losses over a sustained period, may require more sustained support than the division can provide alone. The division should maintain awareness of members experiencing prolonged difficulty and coordinate with whatever community mental health resources exist.

**A community death record** — maintained by the division, separate from individual household records — documents every death the community has experienced, its circumstances, and how it was managed. This record serves multiple purposes: legal documentation for eventual institutional use, epidemiological awareness for the Healthcare Division, and community memory. A community that has no record of who it has lost has lost something beyond the individuals themselves.

## Division Relationships

**Healthcare**
The Healthcare Division is the Mortuary Division's closest partner. Healthcare members are the most likely to determine or confirm cause of death — a determination with direct consequences for how the Mortuary Division handles remains. The two divisions must coordinate on any death where communicable disease is suspected, and the Mortuary Division should treat the Healthcare Division as its primary escalation point when a death pattern suggests an outbreak. The flow of information runs both directions: Mortuary informs Healthcare of deaths and circumstances; Healthcare informs Mortuary of known or suspected diseases among the dying.

**Water Security**
Burial siting is a water protection decision. The Mortuary Division must coordinate with Water Security on the location of any burial ground or individual grave relative to water sources, wells, and watersheds. The Water Security Division's mapping function should inform burial site selection, and any proposed burial ground should be reviewed against the water source map before it is established.

**Agriculture**
Burial sites must not overlap with land in agricultural use or planned for agricultural use. The division should coordinate with Agriculture on land use to ensure that burial grounds and individual graves are sited away from food production areas and that any future land use changes account for burial site locations.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on advance planning, community education, and developing the knowledge and infrastructure needed before a death occurs. As conditions deteriorate and institutional systems become unavailable or overwhelmed, active body care, documentation, and burial operations become central. The division does not change what it does — it scales what it does.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Document deaths and maintain the community death registry
- Coordinate body care and interment for members who have died
- Manage infectious remains with elevated protocols when communicable disease is present
- Support bereaved households with practical assistance and community presence

In a mass casualty scenario — epidemic, disaster, sustained period of elevated mortality — all of these functions must operate simultaneously and at scale. The supplies, knowledge, burial ground, and community relationships to do so must be in place before that scenario occurs, not assembled during it.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Build advance planning records**
The division's first priority is completing a death care record for every member while they are living. This costs nothing and requires no infrastructure. It is the foundation of everything else — without it, the division cannot know what members want, who to notify, or what religious and cultural requirements apply.

**2. Establish a community burial ground**
A site must be identified, assessed against all siting requirements, and legally established before it is urgently needed. This includes understanding the permitting requirements in the jurisdiction, securing the land, and documenting the site's location and status. A community that identifies its burial ground during an active crisis is already behind.

**3. Stockpile supplies**
The division should pre-position: body bags or heavy-duty plastic sheeting, nitrile gloves and fluid-resistant PPE, N95 respirators, natural-fiber shroud cloth, lime (calcium hydroxide), and the materials for simple wooden containers. These are not expensive and take up little space. Waiting until they are needed guarantees they will not be available.

**4. Build community knowledge**
Body care, shrouding, burial procedures, and grief support should be known by enough community members that the division is never dependent on a single person. Training should be organized around knowledgeable community members teaching others — ideally including members who have participated in traditional home death care, hospice work, or natural burial. The knowledge is not technically complex; what it requires is composure and practice.

**5. Operate community programs**
Advance planning becomes an ongoing program as membership grows and records require updating. Memorial services, grief support, and maintenance of the community death registry become standing operational responsibilities.

## Division Documents

The Mortuary Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt.

**Member Death Care Record** — a fill-in document completed by each member capturing burial preferences, religious and cultural requirements, notification list, dependent information, and memorial wishes. Held by the division and reviewed periodically.

**Community Death Registry** — a running log maintained by the division documenting every death within the community: identity, date and circumstances, cause of death where known, and disposition of remains. Kept in written form with copies held in multiple locations.

**Body Care and Burial Guide** — an instructional guide covering the full arc of community death care: washing and shrouding, cooling and preservation, burial siting requirements, grave construction, natural burial permitting, and interment procedures.

**Infectious Remains Protocol** — a stand-alone operational document covering the specific diseases that require elevated handling protocols, PPE requirements, containment procedures, surface decontamination, contact notification, and escalation to Healthcare.
