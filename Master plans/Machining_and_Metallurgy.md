# Machining and Metallurgy Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Machining and Metallurgy Division exists to work metal — from raw material through finished part — and in doing so to sustain the material capability of every other division in the Society. It sources metal, processes it, shapes it, and machines it to tolerance. Its outputs are the parts, tools, machines, and repairs that everything else depends on.

This Division is foundational in a way that is distinct from every other division including the obligated ones. Food security requires tools. Water security requires pumps and fittings. Housing requires fasteners and structural hardware. Communications requires antenna mounts and equipment housings. The Courier Division's vehicles require fabricated components. When those tools break, wear out, or fail — and they will — the Machining and Metallurgy Division is what stands between the Society and the permanent loss of that capability. A Society that cannot make and repair its own metal parts becomes progressively dependent on supply chains it does not control, until one day it cannot function at all. This Division exists to prevent that outcome.

The Division is also the first of what will become a broader manufacturing cluster. The pattern it establishes — raw material sourcing, processing, fabrication, precision work, knowledge transmission — is the pattern manufacturing divisions follow. Future divisions working in other material domains will build alongside it, not from scratch.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for four things:

- That the Society can source, process, and work metal from raw material through finished part, independent of external supply chains
- That every other division's tools, equipment, and metal components can be fabricated, maintained, and repaired within the Society
- That machining capability — the apex of precision metalwork — is developed and held as a standing Society capacity, not an occasional convenience
- That this capability is held in enough members that it cannot be lost to the departure or death of any individual

## Core Functions

- **Material Sourcing and Processing** — acquiring metal from ore, scrap, and stock; processing raw material into workable form through smelting, refining, and stock preparation
- **Smithing and Forging** — shaping metal through heat and force; producing forgings, castings, and wrought forms as the foundation of fabricated parts
- **Welding and Joining** — permanently joining metal components through fusion welding, brazing, and mechanical fastening
- **Machining** — precision material removal to produce parts to specification; operating and maintaining lathes, mills, drills, and grinding equipment
- **Tool Production and Repair** — fabricating new tools and restoring worn or damaged ones for use across all divisions
- **Knowledge Transmission** — ensuring every process the Division depends on is actively taught and practiced broadly enough that it cannot be lost

## Material Sourcing and Processing

Metal does not begin as stock. It begins as ore in the ground or as scrap in the environment. The Division holds responsibility for the full chain from raw material to workable stock, so that its fabrication capability does not depend on the continued availability of commercially produced metal.

**Scrap metal** is the most immediately available raw material in any post-disruption environment. Vehicles, appliances, structural steel, copper wiring, aluminum fixtures — the material legacy of industrial civilization is distributed across every landscape and represents an enormous reserve of workable metal. The Division should develop a systematic understanding of what scrap is available in the Society's geographic area, what alloys it represents, and how to sort and process it. Scrap is not uniform — mixing incompatible alloys produces inferior material. The Division must be able to identify what it has before it melts it.

**Iron ore** is present in Georgia in accessible deposits, primarily as limonite and goethite in the piedmont region. Smelting iron from ore requires a bloomery or blast furnace, charcoal or coke as fuel and reducing agent, and sufficient temperature to reduce iron oxide to metallic iron. The bloomery — the oldest iron smelting technology, requiring no industrial infrastructure — produces wrought iron directly from ore in a single operation. Wrought iron is tough, malleable, and weldable, making it well suited to smithing. The Division should hold bloomery operation as a standing capability even when commercially produced steel is available, because it is the capability that survives the failure of every supply chain above it.

**Steel** — iron with controlled carbon content — can be produced from wrought iron through carburization, or from scrap through careful alloy management in a furnace. The Division should understand the carbon content requirements for different applications: low-carbon steel for structural and general fabrication, medium-carbon for tools requiring hardness, high-carbon for cutting edges and springs. The ability to produce steel of appropriate carbon content from available raw material is a more advanced but essential capability.

**Non-ferrous metals** — copper, aluminum, brass, bronze, lead, tin — have distinct properties and applications that iron and steel cannot substitute for. Copper for electrical conductors and heat exchangers, aluminum for lightweight structures, bronze for bearings and bushings, lead for radiation shielding and certain seals. The Division should maintain awareness of non-ferrous material sources and processing requirements alongside its primary iron and steel work.

**Charcoal production** is inseparable from the Division's smelting and forging functions. Charcoal — produced by pyrolysis of wood in a low-oxygen environment — is the fuel that predates coke and coal for metallurgical use and can be produced anywhere wood is available. The Division should produce its own charcoal rather than depending on commercial supply, and should coordinate with the Agriculture Division on managed wood sources for charcoal production.

## Smithing and Forging

Smithing is the foundation of the Division's fabrication capability. Before a part can be machined to precision, it must exist as a rough form. Forging produces that form — and often produces finished parts directly, particularly for items where the grain structure of forged metal is superior to machined stock.

**The forge** is the Division's primary heat source for smithing operations. A coal or charcoal-fired forge with a bellows or blower produces the temperatures required to bring steel to working heat. The forge should be designed and built to operate on charcoal when coal is unavailable. A forge that can only run on commercial coal is a forge with a known expiration date.

**Hand forging** — shaping hot metal with hammer and anvil — is the foundational skill from which all other smithing derives. A skilled smith can produce a remarkable range of forms with hammer, anvil, and a small set of hardy tools: drawn stock, upset sections, punched holes, bent angles, welded joints, and tapered points. The Division should hold hand forging as a genuine competency in multiple members — not as a demonstration craft but as a production capability.

**Power hammers and trip hammers** — mechanically driven hammers that multiply the force available for forging — extend the Division's capacity to work heavier stock and produce larger forgings with less physical labor. A spring hammer or Dupont-style trip hammer can be built from available materials and driven by an electric motor, an internal combustion engine, or a water wheel where water power is available. The Division should develop mechanically assisted forging capability alongside hand forging.

**Casting** — pouring molten metal into a mold — produces forms that forging and machining cannot efficiently create: complex geometries, hollow sections, multiple identical parts from a single pattern. Sand casting, the most accessible casting method, requires a pattern, a flask, casting sand, and a crucible furnace. The Division should maintain sand casting capability for producing parts that casting serves better than forging or machining.

**Heat treatment** — controlled heating and cooling of steel to achieve specific hardness, toughness, and spring properties — is as important as shaping. A correctly shaped blade that is not properly hardened will not hold an edge. A spring that is not properly tempered will fail. The Division must hold heat treatment knowledge as an integral part of its metalworking capability, not as an afterthought to fabrication.

## Welding and Joining

Welding joins metal permanently through fusion — melting the base metal at the joint, with or without filler material, to create a continuous metallic bond. It is among the most versatile fabrication capabilities available and is essential for structural repair, equipment maintenance, and complex part fabrication.

**Arc welding** — using an electric arc to generate the heat required for fusion — is the most widely applicable welding process for steel. Shielded metal arc welding (stick welding) is the most resilient arc process: it requires only a welder, electrodes, and a power source, and can be performed in field conditions where other processes cannot. The Division should hold stick welding as its primary arc welding capability. Electrodes are a consumable that will eventually become unavailable through commercial supply; the Division should develop the knowledge to produce bare electrodes that can be coated locally, extending welding capability beyond commercial electrode supply.

**Gas welding and brazing** — using an oxygen-acetylene or oxygen-propane flame — requires compressed gas cylinders that present their own supply chain vulnerability. However, gas welding equipment is highly portable, requires no electricity, and is well suited to thin material and non-ferrous metals. The Division should maintain gas welding capability alongside arc welding and should develop the knowledge to produce acetylene through calcium carbide reaction as a supply chain independent gas source.

**Forge welding** — joining iron and steel through heat and hammer pressure, without electricity or gas — is the oldest joining process and the one that requires no consumables beyond fuel and flux. A forge weld made properly is as strong as the parent metal. Forge welding requires higher skill than arc welding and is more limited in application, but it is the welding capability that remains when all other options are exhausted. The Division should hold forge welding as a genuine skill, not a historical curiosity.

**Mechanical fastening** — bolts, rivets, pins, keys — is not welding but belongs in the joining domain. Riveting in particular is a joining method that requires no heat, no electricity, and no consumables beyond the rivets themselves, which the Division can produce. For structural assemblies that must be disassembled for maintenance, and for joining dissimilar metals that cannot be welded, mechanical fastening is often the correct choice. The Division should hold riveting and mechanical fastening alongside its fusion joining capabilities.

## Machining

Machining is the precision removal of material to produce parts to specification — to the tolerances that make components interchangeable, mechanisms functional, and assemblies reliable. It is the apex of the Division's capability and the function that most directly enables the Society to produce and repair complex equipment rather than merely maintaining what it already has.

**The lathe** is the most fundamental machine tool. A lathe rotates a workpiece against a cutting tool, producing cylindrical forms — shafts, pins, bushings, threaded fasteners, valve seats, and the countless round parts that mechanisms depend on. A functional metal lathe allows the Division to produce replacement parts for any equipment with cylindrical components, which is most equipment. The Division should own at least one metal lathe and should develop the skill to use it to tolerance across multiple members.

**The milling machine** removes material from flat and contoured surfaces, producing slots, pockets, flat faces, and complex profiles that the lathe cannot. Together with the lathe, a milling machine covers the majority of machining operations required in general manufacturing and repair. The Division should develop milling capability alongside turning.

**The drill press** produces accurate holes at controlled depth and angle — a capability that hand drilling cannot reliably match. Precision hole location is required for fastener patterns, bearing seats, and fluid passages. A drill press is simpler and more accessible than a lathe or mill and should be among the Division's first machine tool acquisitions.

**Grinding** — precision material removal using abrasive wheels — finishes surfaces to tolerances tighter than cutting tools alone can achieve, and is the primary method for sharpening cutting tools. The Division should maintain surface grinding and tool grinding capability. Grinding wheels are a consumable; the Division should stockpile wheels and develop knowledge of dressing and truing worn wheels to extend their useful life.

**Manual operation is primary.** Computer numerical control (CNC) machining offers speed and repeatability beyond manual operation but depends on electronics, software, and power quality that may not be reliably available. The Division's machining capability should be grounded in manual operation — a machinist who can produce a part to tolerance by hand, reading a micrometer and working a manual lathe, retains full capability when CNC systems are unavailable. CNC capability may be added as a supplement where conditions support it, but it is never the foundation.

**Measurement and metrology** are inseparable from machining. A part produced without accurate measurement is not a machined part — it is a shaped piece of metal. The Division must hold calibrated measurement instruments — micrometers, calipers, gauges, squares, levels — and the knowledge to use them correctly. Measurement instruments require calibration against known standards; the Division should maintain reference standards and calibration procedures so that its measurements remain reliable over time.

## Tool Production and Repair

The Division's most broadly valuable function is the production and maintenance of tools used across every division of the Society. Every division uses tools. Every tool wears, dulls, breaks, or eventually fails. The Division's ability to restore worn tools and produce new ones from available material is what keeps the Society's material capability intact as manufactured tools become unavailable.

**Tool production** encompasses the full range of hand tools and implement components the Society's divisions use: blades, chisels, punches, hammers, hoes, axes, drawknives, planes, saws, awls, needles, hooks, clamps, and the countless specialized tools each division's work requires. The Division should maintain an awareness of what tools each division depends on and should be capable of producing each of them from available metal stock.

**Tool repair and restoration** is more immediately valuable than new production under most conditions. A worn axe head reground to a correct geometry and hardened properly outperforms a new axe head produced hastily from inferior stock. The Division should develop strong diagnostic capability — the ability to assess a worn or damaged tool and determine the correct restoration approach — alongside its fabrication skills.

**Sharpening** is the most frequent tool service the Division provides and the one with the broadest impact on the Society's daily functioning. Dull tools are slower, less effective, and more dangerous than sharp ones. The Division should establish a regular tool sharpening service available to all divisions and should train members across the Society in basic edge maintenance so that tools do not wait for the Division when simple touch-up is what they need.

## The Steam Engine as Capstone Capability

The Division's highest expression of integrated capability is the production of a functional steam engine from materials the Society sources and processes itself. This is not an aspirational gesture toward industrial nostalgia. It is the practical threshold at which the Society achieves genuine mechanical power independence — the ability to generate motive force from wood and water, without dependence on liquid fuel, electrical infrastructure, or any supply chain it does not control.

It is also genuinely difficult. A steam engine is not a project for a Division that has recently learned to forge or has just acquired its first lathe. It requires mastery across the full range of the Division's capabilities simultaneously — and at its center is a challenge that has no margin for error: the design and construction of a safe pressure vessel. A boiler is a container holding steam under significant pressure. The energy stored in a pressurized boiler is enormous. A failure — a weld that cracks, a seam that opens, steel of insufficient quality or thickness, a safety valve that doesn't function — is not a breakdown. It is an explosion. The Division does not attempt steam engine construction until it has genuinely mastered pressure vessel work, and it does not treat that threshold as a formality to be passed through quickly. It earns it.

A functional steam engine changes the Society's material situation in ways no other single achievement can match. Mechanical power can be directed to a power hammer, multiplying the Division's own forging capacity. It can drive a lathe or mill, enabling machining operations too large or too sustained for human power. It can turn a generator, producing electricity from combustion without fuel supply dependency. It can drive a pump, lifting water at volumes no hand pump can match. It can power a thresher, a sawmill, a grain mill. Every division that uses mechanical equipment benefits when the Society can produce the engine that drives it.

The steam engine is also the proof of capability. You cannot build one without having genuinely mastered everything the Division is responsible for. The boiler requires steel of correct carbon content, rolled or forged to consistent thickness, welded to pressure vessel standards, and hydrostatically tested before use. The cylinder requires precise boring to tolerance — a true machining operation that cannot be faked or approximated. The piston must fit the cylinder to a clearance measured in thousandths of an inch. The valves must seal against steam pressure. The crankshaft and connecting rod must be machined, fitted, and balanced. Every failure mode in the Division's knowledge chain shows up in a steam engine that leaks, knocks, loses power, or fails catastrophically. A Division that can build a functional engine has demonstrated genuine competency across its full domain.

**The appropriate starting point** is a simple single-cylinder steam engine of modest power — sufficient to drive a small lathe or power hammer, not to propel a locomotive. Complexity should be deferred until the fundamental design is proven and the Division's members understand the engine's behavior under load. The Cornish beam engine and the simple oscillating cylinder engine are historically proven designs of appropriate complexity for a Division developing this capability for the first time. Both can be built with the machine tools the Division already holds and from materials it can produce.

**Boiler construction and pressure vessel safety** are the most critical knowledge domain in steam engine work and the one where inadequate knowledge has the most serious consequences. A boiler failure under pressure is catastrophic. The Division must develop genuine boiler-making competency — understanding of steel selection, welding procedure qualification for pressure service, and hydrostatic testing protocol — before operating any steam equipment. This knowledge should be treated with the same seriousness as the most dangerous processes the Division handles, because it is.

**Steam as a power distribution system** extends the engine's value beyond direct mechanical drive. Steam can be piped from a central boiler to multiple points of use, driving different equipment at different locations without requiring an engine at each point. A Society with a central steam plant and a distribution network has flexible mechanical power available across its hub or workshop area from a single fuel source. This is a longer-horizon capability but one the Division should hold in view as it develops its steam competency.

The Division achieves its steam engine capability not by acquiring a manufactured engine but by building one. The act of building is the point — a Division that understands its engine because it made every part of it can repair it, modify it, and eventually build a better one. A Division that acquired an engine it did not build understands it only until something goes wrong.

## Knowledge Transmission

The Division holds some of the most technically demanding knowledge in the Society. Metallurgy, machining, and heat treatment require genuine expertise that develops over years of practice — there are no shortcuts, and the consequences of inadequate knowledge show up in parts that fail, tools that don't hold an edge, and welds that crack under load. The Division must transmit this knowledge deliberately and continuously.

The apprenticeship model is not optional here — it is the only model that works. A person cannot learn to machine a part from a manual. They learn by standing next to someone who can do it, running the machine under supervision, making mistakes in conditions where mistakes are correctable, and developing the feel and judgment that written instruction cannot convey. The Division should structure its knowledge transmission around active production apprenticeships, not classroom instruction.

The Division should maintain multiple members competent in each core process — smithing, welding, machining, heat treatment — so that the loss of any one member does not eliminate a capability. Single-member competency in any process is a standing transmission priority.

## Division Relationships

**All Divisions**
Every division depends on this one for tool production, equipment repair, and fabricated components. The Division should maintain a current understanding of each division's critical tool and equipment dependencies — what would most damage their operations if it broke and could not be repaired — and should treat those items as priority production and maintenance concerns.

**Agriculture**
The heaviest tool users in the Society. Tillage implements, planting tools, harvest equipment, irrigation fittings, and processing machinery all require fabrication and ongoing maintenance. The Agriculture Division's operational continuity is closely tied to the Machining and Metallurgy Division's responsiveness.

**Energy**
Energy production equipment — generators, wind turbines, water wheels, solar mounting hardware — requires precision fabrication and ongoing maintenance that the Division provides. The Energy Division and the Machining and Metallurgy Division should maintain a particularly close working relationship given the Society's dependence on functioning energy infrastructure.

**Education**
The knowledge the Division holds is among the most demanding the Society transmits. The Education Division should treat metallurgy, smithing, and machining as priority domains for apprenticeship development and should work with the Division to ensure that knowledge transmission is actively supported rather than left entirely to the Division's own initiative.

**Book Arts**
The Book Arts Division's press equipment, type, and binding tools require metal fabrication and maintenance. The Division should be familiar with the Book Arts Division's equipment and capable of repairing or replacing its components.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division runs its shop, produces and repairs tools for all divisions, develops its machining capability, processes available scrap and stock, and transmits its knowledge through active apprenticeships. The shop is always running. A Division whose forge is cold and whose lathe is idle is a Division that is losing capability.

As conditions deteriorate and commercially produced metal, tooling, and equipment become unavailable, the Division's sourcing and processing functions become increasingly critical. The transition from commercial stock to processed scrap and smelted ore should not be a crisis response — the Division should be developing and practicing these capabilities continuously so that the transition is a shift in proportion rather than an emergency startup.

At the floor, the Society can still work metal. A fire hot enough, a hammer, an anvil, and a member who knows how to use them can produce a functional tool from available scrap. That is the oldest manufacturing capability in human history and it requires nothing that cannot be found or made. The Division builds from that floor upward — not away from it.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Establish the shop**
Identify the physical space. Acquire or build the forge, anvil, and basic smithing tools. This is the foundation everything else builds on and the capability that can be established with the least infrastructure.

**2. Develop smithing competency**
Identify members with existing metalworking knowledge. Begin active apprenticeships in hand forging. Produce tools for other divisions from the first day — practice and production are the same activity.

**3. Acquire machine tools**
Prioritize in order: drill press, lathe, milling machine. Each extends the Division's precision capability significantly. Identify members to develop machining competency alongside each acquisition.

**4. Establish welding capability**
Acquire stick welding equipment. Develop welding competency across multiple members. Begin forging welding practice alongside arc welding.

**5. Establish material sourcing**
Map available scrap sources in the Society's area. Begin charcoal production. Develop bloomery smelting capability. These are longer-horizon capabilities but should begin development early.

**6. Establish the tool service**
Make tool sharpening and repair available to all divisions on a regular schedule. This is the Division's most immediately valuable service and builds the relationships that make the Division central to Society operations.

## Division Documents

**Tool and Equipment Registry** — a current record of tools and equipment held by each division that the Machining and Metallurgy Division is responsible for maintaining or can produce replacements for. The primary instrument for anticipating production and repair demand.

**Material Inventory** — current stock of metal in all forms: processed scrap by alloy type, raw ore, wrought iron, steel stock by carbon content and section, non-ferrous materials. Updated continuously.

**Process Manual** — documented procedures for each core process: bloomery smelting, charcoal production, heat treatment sequences by material and application, welding procedures by joint type, machining setups for common part types. Held in physical form and maintained in coordination with the Education Division.

**Shop Log** — the daily record of Division production and repair activity: what was made, for whom, from what material, by whom. The primary record of Division output and the basis for understanding demand across divisions.
