# Forestry Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Forestry Division exists to manage the community's forested land as a long-term asset — providing timber for construction, firewood for heat and cooking, and non-timber products from the forest, while ensuring that the forest is in better condition when passed to the next generation than when the division received it. It does this by maintaining a clear picture of the community's forest resource, harvesting sustainably against a calculated yield, milling logs into usable lumber, and distributing firewood and forest products to members and divisions that need them.

The forest is the community's most durable and locally-producible material base. A well-managed woodlot produces timber, fuel, food, and medicine indefinitely without purchased inputs. It stores water, moderates temperature, provides wildlife habitat, and protects soil. A community that manages its forest well holds a resource that cannot be replicated quickly — it takes generations to grow and can be destroyed in years by overcutting or neglect. The division manages against that reality.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That the community's forested land is inventoried, managed, and harvested within a calculated sustainable yield — not drawn down faster than it grows
- That the community can produce timber, lumber, and firewood from its own land sufficient to meet construction and heating needs without depending on commercial supply chains
- That the non-timber products of the forest — food, medicine, fiber, and materials — are inventoried, harvested appropriately, and available to the community
- That forest knowledge — silviculture, felling, milling, species identification, and ecology — is held broadly enough across the community that it cannot be lost

## Core Functions

- **Forest Inventory and Assessment** — maintaining a current picture of the community's forest: species composition, standing timber volume, health, and sustainable annual yield
- **Sustainable Harvest** — felling, limbing, and bucking timber within the calculated yield, using safe technique and appropriate tools
- **Milling and Wood Processing** — converting logs to usable lumber, slabs, and dimensional stock for Construction and other divisions
- **Firewood Production** — harvesting, processing, seasoning, and distributing firewood as the community's primary solid fuel
- **Non-Timber Forest Products** — inventorying and harvesting nuts, berries, mushrooms, medicinal plants, sap, bark, and other forest outputs
- **Forest Stewardship and Planting** — managing invasive species, protecting regeneration, enrichment planting, and establishing forest where it does not yet exist
- **Arboriculture and Hazard Trees** — assessing and managing trees near community structures, maintaining fire clearance, and supporting orchard management
- **Tool Management** — maintaining the division's chainsaws, crosscut saws, splitting equipment, peaveys, and portable mill
- **Skills and Teaching** — distributing forest knowledge and safe felling technique across the membership

## Forest Inventory and Assessment

The division cannot manage what it has not measured. The forest inventory is the foundation of every other function — it establishes what species are present, what volume of standing timber exists, what the annual growth rate is, and what a sustainable harvest looks like. Without it, harvest decisions are guesswork and overcutting is likely.

**The standing timber inventory.** The standard field method is the plot-based cruise: a series of fixed-radius sample plots placed at regular intervals across the forest. Within each plot, the diameter at breast height (DBH) of every tree above a minimum size is recorded, along with species. DBH measurements are converted to standing volume using published species-specific tables. Plot results are averaged and scaled to the total forested area to produce an estimate of standing volume by species across the property. This is not a complex procedure — it requires a diameter tape, a compass, and reference tables — and it produces the reliable baseline the division needs.

**Sustainable annual yield.** Average forest growth in the eastern US ranges from 50 to 150 board feet per acre per year for mixed hardwood, depending on site quality, species composition, and stand age. Productive managed stands can exceed this. The division calculates sustainable yield by applying an appropriate growth rate to the inventoried acreage: a 20-acre woodlot with a conservative yield of 80 board feet per acre per year can sustainably produce 1,600 board feet annually — enough for a modest construction program. Harvesting above the sustainable yield draws down the capital; harvesting below it builds it. The division should know which it is doing.

**Species composition.** The inventory records what is present, but the management function decides what should be present. A forest dominated by fast-growing but low-value species may benefit from selective harvest to favor slower-growing, higher-value species. A forest with good species composition may need only light management to remain productive. The division should understand the local species mix — what grows on this site, what produces the timber and non-timber products the community most needs, and what management actions would shift the composition toward community benefit over a decades-long horizon.

**Forest health.** The inventory should note signs of disease, insect damage, and structural problems: dead and dying trees, unusual crown dieback, visible fungal fruiting bodies at the base of trees, excessive leaning and widow-makers (dead branches hung up in the canopy above working areas). A diseased or damaged stand often presents harvest opportunities — trees that will die anyway are better harvested than left to decay — but health problems can also indicate systemic issues that management needs to address rather than just harvest around.

**Updating the inventory.** The inventory should be refreshed at minimum every five years, and harvest records kept annually so that cumulative take can be compared against cumulative sustainable yield. A division that keeps no harvest records cannot tell whether it is managing sustainably. Records are the mechanism that holds the division accountable to the principle.

## Sustainable Harvest

Felling trees is hazardous work. More experienced loggers are injured and killed in the woods each year than in almost any other occupation. The division approaches harvest with disciplined technique, never working alone, and never rushing. A harvest operation that is too slow is recoverable. An injury in the woods, far from the road, is not.

**Safe felling technique** is the division's most important operational competency. Every division member who operates a chainsaw or participates in felling should understand and be able to execute: the three-cut felling method (face cut, back cut, hinge wood), reading the lean of a tree and planning the felling direction accordingly, establishing and maintaining a clear retreat path, the two-tree-length exclusion zone around any felling operation, and the conditions that require aborting a cut — unexpected lean, another person in the zone, a chainsaw that kicks back. These are not optional refinements; they are the minimum baseline for working safely in the woods.

**The crosscut saw** is the division's hand-tool alternative to the chainsaw. A sharp, well-set crosscut saw operated by two experienced sawyers can fell and buck timber at meaningful production rates. The crosscut saw requires no fuel, produces no exhaust, is repairable from stock materials, and is significantly quieter than a chainsaw. The division should maintain a full-length two-person crosscut saw, know how to file and set it, and have enough members who can use it effectively that the division is not paralyzed when fuel or chainsaw parts are unavailable.

**Log selection and marking.** Trees selected for harvest should be marked before cutting begins, reviewed by the division lead, and confirmed against the harvest plan. The selection criteria should reflect long-term stand management: favoring the removal of suppressed and damaged trees, trees competing with high-value specimens, and trees whose felling opens growing space for regeneration. Even-aged monoculture harvest — clearcut or near-clearcut — should not be the division's default approach; uneven-aged management that takes a portion of mature trees while protecting regeneration produces a more resilient forest over time.

**Log handling.** A felled tree is a large, heavy object on uneven ground. Moving it to the mill or firewood processing area requires either mechanical assistance — a tractor, a come-along, a logging arch — or draft animals, or human labor with peaveys and cant hooks on favorable terrain. The division should have a realistic plan for log extraction that does not depend on equipment that may be unavailable. Cutting logs to manageable length at the felling site reduces the handling challenge and makes human-powered extraction more feasible.

## Milling and Wood Processing

Felling a tree does not produce lumber. Converting a log to usable dimensional stock requires milling — either with a portable sawmill or by hand — followed by seasoning before the wood can be used in construction. The division manages this full process, from log to dried lumber delivered to Construction.

**Portable bandsaw mills** are the practical community-scale option for log-to-lumber conversion. A bandsaw mill clamps the log on a track and passes the blade through it in controlled cuts, producing boards and timbers of whatever dimension the operator specifies. A competent operator on a well-maintained machine can produce several hundred to over a thousand board feet per day. The machine is capital-intensive but durable with maintenance, and the capacity it creates is significant: a community with a portable bandsaw mill can produce all the dimensional lumber Construction needs from its own logs.

**Chainsaw mills** — a fixture attached to a chainsaw that guides a ripping cut along the length of a log — are lower capital and more portable than bandsaw mills. They are slower, produce a rougher cut, consume more material as sawdust, and require significant operator endurance. The division should hold chainsaw milling as a backup capacity rather than a primary production method.

**Hand milling.** A two-person pit saw — a long rip saw worked vertically between two sawyers, one above the log on a platform and one below — is the supply-chain-independent alternative to any powered mill. It requires no fuel, no machinery, and produces serviceable lumber. It is extremely labor-intensive: a skilled pair of pit sawyers might produce 100 to 200 board feet per day. The division should understand pit-saw technique and be capable of demonstrating it, so the skill is not lost.

**Timber framing stock.** The Construction Division's timber framing program requires large-section timbers — often 6x6, 8x8, or larger — that cannot be produced by typical commercial lumber operations. The division should mill timber framing stock as a standing supply function for Construction, cutting the species and dimensions the Construction Division specifies. Locally milled timber, air-dried, is often stronger than kiln-dried commercial lumber of the same species.

**Wood seasoning.** Green lumber — freshly milled wood — contains 30–100% moisture by weight and cannot be used in most construction applications. As it dries, it shrinks across the grain and may warp, twist, or check. The general rule for air drying is one year per inch of thickness for hardwoods; softwoods dry faster. Lumber stacked in stickers (small spacers between layers), in a location with good air circulation and protection from rain, dries reliably if slowly. The division must plan for seasoning time — a Liberty House that Construction needs in six months requires lumber that was milled six months ago. The division should maintain a standing inventory of drying lumber in common dimensions, restocked continuously rather than milled on demand.

## Firewood Production

Firewood is the division's highest-volume output and one of the community's most essential resources. Heating with wood requires planning across multiple years — firewood cut this year is typically not well-seasoned until next year — and the division should manage firewood supply as a multi-year standing inventory rather than an annual production exercise.

**Species selection.** Not all wood is equal as firewood. Dense hardwoods — oak, hickory, locust, maple, beech — produce significantly more heat per unit volume than lighter hardwoods or softwoods. Softwoods (pine, spruce, fir) ignite more easily and are useful for kindling and as a starter fuel but burn faster and leave more creosote in flues. The division should know which species are available locally, what their BTU output is, and favor high-density hardwoods for primary heating fuel. The local Cooperative Extension office publishes species-specific firewood data for most regions.

**Seasoning is mandatory.** Green wood is half water. Burning green wood wastes a significant portion of its energy evaporating moisture, produces more smoke, deposits more creosote in chimneys, and is harder to ignite. A cord of well-seasoned hardwood is substantially more valuable as fuel than a cord of green wood from the same species. The division should split and stack firewood in a location with good airflow, under cover from rain but open to wind, and hold it for a minimum of one year before distribution — two years for dense species like oak. The division should maintain at minimum a two-year rolling inventory: the current year's production plus the prior year's production seasoning.

**Splitting and processing.** Most firewood species must be split before stacking to accelerate drying. A splitting maul and wedges are the basic tools; a hydraulic splitter is faster for large volumes. The division should hold both — the splitter as primary equipment and the maul and wedges as the supply-chain-independent fallback. Splitting parties are productive collective labor events and reasonable candidates for community work days.

**Firewood allocation.** The division coordinates firewood distribution with Energy, which tracks heating needs across the community. Members with efficient wood heating appliances and well-insulated homes consume significantly less firewood than members with inefficient stoves in poorly insulated spaces. The Energy Division's weatherization and stove efficiency work reduces the volume the Forestry Division needs to supply, and the two should maintain a shared understanding of the community's actual heating demand.

## Non-Timber Forest Products

The forest produces food, medicine, fiber, and materials beyond timber. These products are underutilized in most communities and represent a significant resource that requires no planting, little management, and only knowledge and attention to harvest.

**Mast and nuts.** Oaks, hickories, black walnuts, butternuts, and chestnuts produce calorie-dense food in quantities that can be significant at community scale. Black walnuts and hickories require processing — cracking and picking out the meat — that is labor-intensive but producible at home or in small groups. Acorns require leaching to remove tannins, after which they can be ground into flour. The American chestnut is being restored through breeding programs; Chinese and other non-native chestnuts are productive now. The division should inventory what mast-producing trees are present on community land and in the surrounding landscape and develop a harvesting calendar.

**Wild mushrooms.** Edible fungi — hen of the woods, chicken of the woods, chanterelle, morel, lion's mane, oyster — are available in most forest environments and are nutritionally significant. The division should maintain species identification competency for edible species and corresponding competency for toxic lookalikes. Confident edible mushroom identification requires direct instruction from an experienced person, not independent study from books or apps; the stakes of misidentification are too high to learn any other way.

**Medicinal plants.** Most forests contain plants with documented medicinal properties. The division's function here is inventory and access, not medicine — identifying what is present and ensuring that the Healthcare Division can access what it needs. The division and Healthcare Division should develop a shared inventory of medicinally significant species present on community land, and the Forestry Division should manage those areas to protect rather than disturb the plants.

**Maple syrup.** Where sugar or red maples are present, sap collection in late winter (when nights are below freezing and days are above) produces a sweetener that can be concentrated into syrup or sugar. The process requires tapping trees, collecting sap in volume, and boiling it down — roughly 40 gallons of sap to one gallon of syrup. The energy to boil is significant; an efficient evaporator and access to firewood makes production practical. Tapping does not harm trees when done correctly — a properly tapped maple can be tapped annually for the life of the tree.

**Bark and fiber.** The inner bark of basswood (linden) can be processed into fiber for cordage and weaving. The bark of oaks contains tannins used to cure leather. Birch bark is waterproof and has been used for containers, roofing, and fire-starting. The division should be aware of these uses and capable of harvesting bark in ways that do not kill the trees.

## Forest Stewardship and Planting

Sustainable harvest is a floor, not a ceiling. The division manages the forest as a long-term asset, not merely extracts from it. Active stewardship — protecting regeneration, managing invasive species, enrichment planting, and establishing forest where it does not yet exist — is the difference between a forest that remains stable and one that improves over generations.

**Protecting regeneration.** After harvest, the forest regenerates from seeds, sprouts, and existing understory plants. Deer browse is the most consistent threat to forest regeneration in the eastern US — a deer population that exceeds the carrying capacity of the land will suppress seedling growth below the threshold needed for canopy replacement. The division should assess browse pressure in harvested areas and, where necessary, coordinate exclusion fencing or browse management with Agriculture.

**Invasive species management.** Invasive plants — autumn olive, multiflora rose, Japanese knotweed, garlic mustard, mile-a-minute, buckthorn, and others depending on region — compete with and suppress native forest regeneration. The division should be able to identify the invasive species present on community land and manage them in harvested and edge areas, where light penetration creates conditions that favor invasive establishment. This is not a one-time treatment but a recurring management function.

**Enrichment planting.** Gaps created by harvest are opportunities. Rather than leaving gaps to regenerate from whatever seeds blow in, the division can plant high-value species — fruit and nut trees, timber species with long-term value, native species that improve forest composition. Enrichment planting transforms the forest over decades; a division that begins this work early leaves a measurably better forest for the people who come after.

**Where no forest exists.** Communities on agricultural land or urban and suburban land may have little to no existing forest. The division's stewardship function in this case is establishment — planting trees with a multi-decade horizon. Fast-growing nitrogen-fixing species (black locust, alder) can establish canopy quickly, improve soil, and provide early firewood while slower-growing high-value species develop behind them. Even a small woodlot of two to five acres, if planted now, produces firewood within a decade and significant timber within two. The division should develop a planting plan on any available land and pursue it continuously.

**Riparian buffers.** Forest along streams and waterways plays a disproportionate role in water quality, stream bank stability, and flood management. The Water Security Division's interest in watershed protection overlaps directly with the Forestry Division's stewardship function. The two divisions should jointly prioritize maintaining forested buffers along any waterways that flow through or near community land, and restoring them where they have been cleared.

## Arboriculture and Hazard Trees

Not all trees the division manages are in the forest. Community trees — trees in and around dwellings, along paths, near common buildings — require assessment and maintenance distinct from forest management. The division holds the tree knowledge and the felling and climbing competency; it applies that competency in the built environment as well as the woods.

**Hazard tree assessment.** A dead tree near a structure, a tree with significant crown dieback, a tree leaning toward a building — these are hazards the division should identify and address before they become emergencies. The annual inspection cycle should include a pass through community structures and common areas looking for tree hazards. A tree that falls on a Liberty House in a storm is a Construction emergency, a potential Healthcare emergency, and a Forestry failure mode.

**Coordinated removal.** Removing a tree near a structure is technically more demanding than forest felling — there is rarely a clear felling direction and the consequences of a mistake fall on infrastructure and potentially people. The division should approach near-structure removals with increased caution: rigging, sectional removal, and coordination with Construction on what structures need protection.

**Fire clearance.** The Fire Division's defensible space requirements — clearance of dead vegetation and ladder fuels around structures — include tree management. The Forestry Division should coordinate with Fire on what clearance standards require and should perform the tree work that meets those standards. Trees that are removed for fire clearance may produce usable timber or firewood; the division should recover that material rather than leaving it as additional fuel load.

**Orchard support.** Fruit and nut trees in or near the community are primarily under Agriculture's purview, but pruning, disease identification, and the tree work that goes beyond agricultural management belongs to Forestry. The two divisions should coordinate on orchard tree health and structural pruning.

## Tool Management

The division's tools are divided between harvesting and milling equipment. Both require diligent maintenance; a chainsaw with a dull chain and a portable mill with a dull blade are slow and dangerous.

**Harvesting tools:** chainsaws in working condition with a supply of chain oil and spare chains, a crosscut saw filed and set for the wood being cut, axes (felling, limbing, and splitting), splitting mauls and wedges, cant hooks and peaveys for log handling, and a mechanical advantage system (come-along or block and tackle) for log extraction on difficult terrain.

**Milling equipment:** the portable bandsaw mill with a supply of spare blades, a chainsaw mill as backup, files for chainsaw chain maintenance, stickers for lumber stacking, and moisture meter for tracking seasoning progress.

**Chainsaw maintenance.** A chainsaw that is not properly maintained is both slow and unsafe. The division should hold working knowledge of chain filing and tensioning, air filter cleaning, bar maintenance, and fuel system servicing. Chain filing is a skill that takes practice to develop; a poorly filed chain cuts slowly and increases kickback risk. Every chainsaw operator should be able to file their own chain.

**Crosscut saw care.** A crosscut saw must be filed with specialized files to the correct tooth geometry and set (bent alternately in opposite directions) to produce a kerf wide enough that the blade does not bind. This is a precise skill that most communities have lost. The division should develop at least one member with full crosscut saw filing competency before that competency is needed under pressure.

## Skills and Teaching

Forest work is dangerous, technically specific, and deeply skilled. It is also increasingly rare in communities that have had multiple generations with no relationship to working forests. The division's teaching function is the mechanism by which forest knowledge survives beyond the members who currently hold it.

**Safe felling is non-negotiable.** No member should participate in felling operations without instruction in safe technique. The division should offer felling instruction — ideally hands-on in the field with direct supervision — before allowing new members to participate in harvest operations. The instruction covers not only technique but the habits that prevent injury: working with a partner, establishing retreat paths before cuts begin, keeping the zone clear, never rushing.

**Crosscut saw and hand tool competency.** The division should ensure that enough members know how to use and maintain a crosscut saw, a felling axe, a splitting maul, and basic log-handling tools that these skills persist through membership turnover. These are the supply-chain-independent alternatives to chainsaw harvesting, and they should not be novelties.

**Species identification.** Forest management, mushroom harvesting, medicinal plant access, and mast production all require knowing what is present. The division should offer plant identification walks — in spring for wildflowers and early mushrooms, in summer for understory species, in fall for mast and late mushrooms — and develop species identification competency across a broad enough slice of the membership that the knowledge is not concentrated in one or two people.

**Forest ecology basics.** Members who understand how forests grow, regenerate, and change are better equipped to make decisions about management — and to hold the division accountable to its stewardship principles. The division should teach the basics of succession, sustainable yield, and the ecological roles of key species, not as academic material but as practical context for the division's work.

**Mill operation.** Operating a bandsaw mill safely and effectively is a skill that takes time to develop. The division should maintain at least three members with meaningful mill experience so that the milling function is robust to departure or injury.

## Division Relationships

**Construction**
The division's primary customer for timber and lumber. Construction needs dimensional lumber for framing, large-section timbers for timber framing, and materials for repair work. The two divisions should maintain a standing communication on what lumber dimensions and quantities Construction anticipates needing and what the division can realistically produce, so that milling and seasoning lead time can be planned against actual demand. Lumber that Construction needs in eight months needs to be milled now.

**Masonry**
The Masonry Division's lime production requires significant fuel — a lime burn is fuel-intensive. The division should supply firewood for kiln operations as a standing function. Charcoal production, if the community develops it, is a Forestry function: controlled-atmosphere burning of wood produces charcoal suitable for lime kilns, forges, and the Machining and Metallurgy Division's metalwork.

**Energy**
The most important relationship for firewood. The Energy Division tracks heating needs across the community and coordinates with the Forestry Division on firewood allocation. The two divisions should jointly understand the community's actual annual firewood demand so that the Forestry Division can confirm its production is meeting it. The Energy Division's work on stove efficiency and weatherization directly reduces the Forestry Division's production burden; the two should coordinate explicitly rather than treating their functions as independent.

**Agriculture**
The two divisions share the largest overlap in land use. Agroforestry — the integration of trees with agricultural production — is most productive when designed jointly. Windbreaks and shelterbelts that protect crops and livestock are tree systems that Forestry plants and maintains. Woodland grazing, where livestock are managed in forested areas, requires coordination on browse pressure and forest regeneration. Wood chips and leaf mold from forest operations are significant compost inputs for Agriculture. The two divisions should develop a shared land use picture rather than managing their respective domains in isolation.

**Food Security**
The forest produces food — nuts, berries, mushrooms, sap products — that Food Security should be aware of and incorporate into community food planning. The Forestry Division inventories and harvests; Food Security integrates forest foods into the community's food picture. Firewood supply directly affects Food Security's ability to cook; a shortage of seasoned firewood is a Food Security problem as well as an Energy Division problem.

**Water Security**
Forest cover is the most effective watershed protection a community can maintain. Trees intercept precipitation, increase infiltration, stabilize stream banks, and filter runoff. The Forestry Division's stewardship of riparian buffers and hillside forest cover directly affects Water Security's groundwater recharge and water quality. The two divisions should jointly prioritize forested buffer maintenance as a non-negotiable stewardship commitment.

**Fire Division**
Defensible space and fire clearance around structures is a standing coordination point. The Forestry Division performs the tree work; the Fire Division specifies the standards. Where the community is in or adjacent to fire-prone landscapes, this relationship is critical — a community that has not maintained fire clearance is not one that can be defended under a wildfire scenario.

**Healthcare**
The Forestry Division inventories and provides access to medicinal plants and fungi on community land. Healthcare specifies what it needs; the division manages those areas to protect rather than disturb them. This is a modest but irreplaceable interface — wild-harvested medicinal plants are not substitutable from any supply chain if the forest does not hold them.

**Machining and Metallurgy**
Charcoal from hardwood is the historical fuel for iron and steel work. If the community develops metalworking capacity, the Forestry Division is the charcoal supply. Charcoal production — retort kilns or simple mound methods — should be developed as a secondary capability if Machining and Metallurgy identifies it as needed.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions, the division focuses on the inventory and sustainable harvest program, the standing firewood inventory, continuous milling against Construction's needs, and enrichment planting. Non-timber product harvesting proceeds as seasons allow. The division is building forest capital as much as it is spending it.

As conditions deteriorate and commercial lumber becomes unavailable or expensive, the division's milling function shifts from supplementary to essential. Construction's ability to build depends on what the division can mill. Chainsaw fuel supply becomes a planning constraint — the division should have fuel stockpiled and should develop crosscut saw capacity to reduce dependence on it. Where fuel is unavailable, hand milling techniques become the only option; the division should have begun developing them before that point.

Firewood is the most resilient energy pathway the community has. A supply chain that runs from the community's own forest to the split and seasoned pile does not pass through any commercial system. The division's ability to maintain that supply chain under any conditions is among its most important functions.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete the forest inventory**
Before any harvest, the division needs a baseline picture of what forest resource the community holds. Species composition, standing volume, and a calculated sustainable yield establish the parameters within which all harvest decisions are made. The inventory should be completed and documented before the first timber harvest.

**2. Establish the firewood program**
Begin harvesting and splitting firewood immediately, prioritizing dead and downed wood first. Start the multi-year seasoning inventory — wood cut and stacked now will be ready next year. The community's firewood supply should reach a two-year standing inventory within the first two years of the division's operation.

**3. Assess and address hazard trees**
A pass through all community structures and common areas to identify hazard trees is an early safety function. Trees that are dead, dying, or structurally compromised near structures should be removed before they become emergencies.

**4. Establish milling capacity**
Acquire or confirm access to a portable bandsaw mill and develop operator competency. Log the first timber milling operation and deliver dimensioned lumber to Construction. The milling capacity should be operational before Construction has an urgent need it cannot meet.

**5. File crosscut saw competency**
At least two division members should hold full crosscut saw filing and use competency before chainsaw fuel supply becomes a concern. This is the kind of skill that is easy to defer and costly to lack at the moment it is needed.

**6. Begin enrichment planting**
Plant high-value species in harvest gaps and on any land where forest establishment is possible. The planting horizon is long — trees planted this year produce firewood in a decade and timber in two — but it must begin early to benefit the community in any reasonable timeframe.

## Division Documents

**Forest Inventory Register** — the standing timber inventory by species and diameter class, updated on a five-year cycle, with annual harvest records appended so that cumulative take can be compared against cumulative sustainable yield. The primary accountability document for the sustainable harvest commitment.

**Harvest Plan** — an annual document specifying which areas of the forest are to be harvested, what trees are selected, what the target volume is against the sustainable yield calculation, and what the felling method and extraction plan are.

**Lumber Inventory** — a current record of lumber in the milling and seasoning pipeline: species, dimensions, milling date, estimated dry date, and quantity. Maintained in coordination with the Construction Division so that both divisions have a realistic picture of available stock and lead time.

**Firewood Inventory** — a current record of firewood by year of cutting, species, estimated volume, and location. Allows the division to confirm that a two-year rolling inventory is maintained and to allocate appropriately between members and divisions.

**Non-Timber Products Map** — a documented inventory of significant non-timber forest resources: mast-producing trees by species and location, known mushroom habitat, medicinal plant areas, maple stands. Updated as new resources are found and as conditions change.

**Species Identification Guide** — a locally-specific illustrated reference for the tree, plant, and fungal species present on community land: edible and medicinal uses, harvest methods and seasons, toxic lookalikes where relevant. Built from direct field observation rather than copied from general references.
