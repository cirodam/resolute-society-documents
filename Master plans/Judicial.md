# Judicial Division — Master Plan

## A Note on the Name

The word judicial carries Roman roots — from *iudicium*, the act of careful weighing and discernment. It names what this Division actually does: sit with difficult situations honestly and render considered judgments about what they require. It does not carry the meaning of the American legal system — adversarial process, punitive sentencing, winners and losers, permanent records that follow people through their lives. The Judicial Division has nothing to do with American law and does not import its logic, its procedures, or its assumptions. Members who have had experiences with the American legal system should understand that what happens in this Division is categorically different in orientation, method, and purpose.

---

## Mission

The Judicial Division exists to repair what has been broken. When harm is done within this community — between members, or between a member and the Society — the Division does not ask what punishment is deserved. It asks what repair is possible, what the person harmed needs, and what genuine accountability from the person who caused harm looks like. When members enter into agreements with each other and those agreements become disputed, the Division provides the process through which disputes are resolved honestly and relationships are preserved where possible.

The Division holds the Society's commitment to restorative justice not as an aspiration but as an operational reality. It is the institution through which that commitment is honored in the hardest cases — the ones where someone has been genuinely hurt, where trust has been broken, where the easiest path would be punishment rather than repair.

The Division is constitutionally permanent. It cannot be dissolved by the General Assembly. Its permanence is the condition of its independence, and its independence is the condition of its function. A judicial body that can be dissolved by the body it may be called to adjudicate against is not a judicial body — it is a formality.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for three things:

- That harm done within the community — between members, or between a member and the Society — is addressed through a process centered on the needs of those harmed and the possibility of genuine repair
- That contractual disputes between members are resolved through a fair process whose outcome both parties can accept as honest
- That the Division operates with genuine independence, particularly in cases where the Society itself is a party

## Jurisdiction

The Division holds jurisdiction over three categories of matter:

**Harm between members** — any situation in which the actions of one member have caused injury to another, whether physical, material, relational, or otherwise. This includes harm that was intentional and harm that was not. The nature of the harm shapes what repair requires; it does not determine whether the Division has jurisdiction.

**Harm between a member and the Society** — any situation in which a member believes the Society, through its bodies or officers, has caused them harm; or in which the Society, through its bodies or officers, brings a matter concerning a member's conduct toward the Society. Both directions of this relationship fall within the Division's jurisdiction.

**Contractual disputes between members** — any dispute arising from an agreement between members that the parties cannot resolve directly. The Division does not require that agreements be formally registered to fall within its jurisdiction, though formal registration is encouraged and creates a clearer record for any proceeding.

## Orientation

Everything the Division does flows from a single foundational commitment: persons are not identical to their worst moments, and the purpose of addressing harm is repair, not punishment.

This commitment has consequences that shape every aspect of how the Division operates.

**The needs of the person harmed come first.** Before any question about the person who caused harm is addressed, the Division attends to what the person harmed needs — their safety, their material situation, their healing, and their voice in the process. A proceeding that reaches a satisfying resolution for the person who caused harm while leaving the person harmed feeling unseen has failed at its primary obligation.

**Genuine accountability is more demanding than punishment.** Punishment ends when the sentence is served. Genuine accountability requires honest acknowledgment of what was done, understanding of what it cost the person harmed, demonstrated commitment to change, and repair where repair is possible. This is harder than punishment, not easier. The Division does not offer an easy path to those who caused harm. It offers a meaningful one.

**Repair takes recognizable forms.** What repair looks like depends on what was broken and what the person harmed needs. The Division works toward outcomes that may include: direct acknowledgment of what was done and what it cost; material restitution where material harm occurred; structured accountability processes where ongoing demonstration of change is required; community service that benefits those affected or the broader community; supervised reintegration where trust must be rebuilt gradually; and in cases where the safety of members requires it, separation from the community — understood not as punishment but as the protection the community owes its members when genuine safety cannot otherwise be assured. Separation is chosen only when safety requires it and never as a substitute for the harder work of repair.

**The dignity of every person in a proceeding is inviolable.** This includes the person who caused harm. The Division does not conduct proceedings in ways that humiliate, expose unnecessarily, or treat any participant as less than a full person. Accountability does not require degradation. The Division resists any pressure — from the community, from aggrieved parties, from the weight of public opinion — to use its proceedings as a vehicle for punishment dressed as process.

## Structure

**Mediators** are the Division's primary practitioners. A Mediator is assigned to every matter that comes before the Division and holds primary responsibility for its resolution from intake through outcome. The Mediator is not a neutral facilitator who holds no view — they are an active participant who brings genuine judgment to the situation, asks the questions that need to be asked, names what they observe, and works toward resolution with genuine care for everyone involved. In straightforward matters, the Mediator resolves the case. In matters of sufficient gravity, the Mediator convenes a Jury and remains involved throughout.

The Mediator role requires people who combine practical wisdom with genuine moral seriousness — people who can sit with difficult situations without flinching, who can hold the needs of the person harmed and the humanity of the person who caused harm simultaneously, and who can tell the difference between resolution that is genuinely restorative and resolution that merely closes a case. These capacities develop through experience and cannot be manufactured through training alone. The Division should identify members with these qualities and support their development deliberately.

**Juries** are convened for matters of sufficient gravity — serious harm, complex contractual disputes, or any matter where the Mediator determines that the weight of the decision requires broader judgment. Juries are selected by sortition from the full membership. They are not asked to render a simple verdict. They are given a structured set of questions specific to the type of matter before them and asked to exercise their full practical judgment in answering each one. The questions are developed and maintained by the Division and vary by matter type — the questions appropriate to a serious physical harm are different from those appropriate to a contractual dispute.

**The Division Officer** manages day-to-day operations and is selected by and accountable to the Committee that governs the Division. The Officer does not hold decision authority over active proceedings — that authority belongs to Mediators and Juries. The Officer's role is operational: ensuring the Division functions, that Mediators are supported, that records are maintained, and that the Division's processes remain faithful to its principles.

**The governing Committee** is drawn by sortition from the full membership and holds oversight authority over the Division Officer and the Division's operations. The Committee's sortition basis is the structural guarantee of the Division's independence — it cannot be stacked by any faction, including the General Assembly.

## The Society as Party

When a member brings a matter against the Society — alleging that the Society, through its bodies or officers, has caused them harm — the Division faces its most significant structural challenge. The Society is both the party to the dispute and the institutional context within which the Division operates. The risk of capture — of the Division serving the Society's institutional interests rather than the member's genuine claim — is real and must be named honestly.

The Division's structural independence is the primary protection against this risk. The governing Committee is selected by sortition and is not appointed by or accountable to the General Assembly. The Division is constitutionally permanent and cannot be dissolved in response to an unfavorable proceeding. Mediators and Jury members are drawn from the full membership, not from Division staff appointed by the Society's leadership.

These structural protections do not eliminate the risk entirely. They reduce it. The Division must also hold a cultural commitment to independence that goes beyond structure — a commitment by its Mediators, its Juries, and its governing Committee to hold the member's claim honestly regardless of the institutional discomfort that may produce. A finding against the Society is not a failure of the Division. It is the Division doing its job.

When the Society brings a matter against a member — alleging conduct that harms the Society or violates membership obligations — the same independence applies in the other direction. The Society does not get favorable treatment because it is the Society. The member's dignity, their right to be heard fully, and the possibility of repair rather than expulsion are held with the same seriousness they would be in any other matter.

## Contractual Disputes

The Society's internal economy — the kin currency, the exchange of labor and goods between members, the mutual obligations that community life generates — produces agreements between members. Most of these agreements will be informal and will be honored without dispute. Some will not.

The Division holds jurisdiction over disputes arising from any agreement between members, formal or informal. Members are encouraged to register significant agreements with the Division before any dispute arises — a registered agreement creates a clear record that simplifies any subsequent proceeding significantly. The Division should make registration straightforward and should encourage it without requiring it.

**The orientation toward contractual disputes** is different from the orientation toward harm. Harm proceedings center on the needs of the person harmed and the possibility of repair. Contractual disputes center on what the parties actually agreed to, what each party has done in relation to that agreement, and what resolution honors the agreement honestly. The Division's role is not therapeutic in contractual matters — it is interpretive and adjudicative. It determines what the agreement meant, whether it was honored, and what is owed.

This does not mean the Division is indifferent to relationship in contractual matters. Members who dispute an agreement are members who must continue to live and work together. Resolution that is technically correct but leaves both parties feeling wronged has not served the community. The Division attends to relationship even in contractual proceedings, without allowing that attention to distort its honest reading of what was agreed.

## Division Relationships

**The Watch**
The Watch responds to immediate safety threats and transfers accountability to the Judicial Division once safety is secured. The two divisions must maintain a close working relationship so that this handoff is smooth — the Division receives what the Watch transfers without requiring the Watch to do the Division's work, and the Watch does not attempt to do the Division's work in the name of efficiency. The boundary between them is clear: the Watch acts to ensure safety; the Division acts to address what happened and what it requires.

**The General Assembly**
The Division is independent of the General Assembly and cannot be dissolved by it. The General Assembly does not direct the Division's proceedings and does not review its outcomes. The Division reports to the General Assembly on its operations — volume of matters, types, outcomes — in aggregate form that does not compromise the confidentiality of individual proceedings. This reporting serves the General Assembly's governance function without creating a channel through which the Assembly can influence individual cases.

**All Members**
The Division's function depends on members bringing matters to it honestly and engaging its processes in good faith. A community in which members resolve harm through retaliation rather than the Division, or allow disputes to fester rather than bringing them forward, is a community in which the Division cannot do its work. The Division should be accessible, known to every member, and understood as a genuine resource rather than a last resort.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division operates primarily through Mediators handling individual matters as they arise. The volume of proceedings in a healthy community should be modest — not because harm never occurs, but because a community with strong relationships, clear governance, and genuine mutual accountability generates fewer unresolved conflicts than one without these things. A Division that is rarely needed is not a Division that has failed. It may be a sign that the community is working.

The Division should not wait for matters to be brought to it before it considers how it will handle them. Mediators should be developed continuously. Jury question sets should be maintained and reviewed. The Division's processes should be known to the membership before they are needed, so that a member facing harm or a member who has caused it knows what the Division offers and how to access it.

At the floor, the Society can always address harm. Two members, a witness, and a commitment to honesty are the irreducible minimum of a restorative process. The Division's structure and processes build on that minimum — they do not replace it.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Identify and develop Mediators**
The Division's function depends entirely on the quality of its Mediators. Identifying members with the practical wisdom, relational capacity, and moral seriousness the role requires is the first and most important task. These members should be supported in developing their capacity through practice, reflection, and mentorship from anyone in the community with relevant experience.

**2. Establish the governing Committee**
Conduct the sortition draw for the governing Committee. Establish the Committee's operating rhythm and its relationship to the Division Officer role.

**3. Develop Jury question sets**
For each major matter type — serious harm between members, harm between member and Society, contractual dispute — develop the structured question set Juries will work from. These should be specific enough to guide judgment without constraining it.

**4. Make the Division known**
Every member should know the Division exists, what it handles, how to bring a matter to it, and what to expect from its process. This should be part of new member orientation and should be communicated clearly across the membership before the Division is called on for the first time.

**5. Establish the agreement registration system**
Create a simple, accessible way for members to register agreements with the Division. Make registration the obvious thing to do for any significant agreement rather than an afterthought.

## Division Documents

**Matter Record** — the confidential record of each proceeding: the nature of the matter, the parties, the Mediator assigned, the process followed, and the outcome reached. Held by the Division and not accessible to the General Assembly or other bodies except as required by the Division's own governance.

**Jury Question Sets** — the structured question sets used in Jury proceedings, organized by matter type. Maintained by the Division and reviewed periodically to ensure they continue to serve the Division's restorative orientation.

**Agreement Registry** — the record of member agreements registered with the Division, held in a form accessible to the Division for reference in any subsequent dispute proceeding.

**Annual Operations Report** — the aggregate report provided to the General Assembly: number and type of matters handled, outcomes by category, and any observations about patterns the Division has observed that are relevant to the Society's broader governance. Contains no identifying information about individual proceedings.
