# Fire Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Fire Division exists to prevent fires, maintain the community's fire response capacity, and take on active fire response when professional fire services are unavailable or unable to respond. It slots directly into the structure of existing professional fire departments — supporting and deferring to them when they are functional, and filling the gap when they are not.

In stable conditions, the division's work is almost entirely preventive: inspecting structures, educating members, maintaining equipment, and running drills. The response capacity it builds is the same capacity the community would need if the fire department could not come. That capacity should be treated as insurance — maintained continuously, deployed rarely.

The technical and physical standards the division implements are defined in the Fire Safety Regulation. The master plan does not repeat those standards — it organizes the division around them.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That the community's built environment meets the fire safety standards defined in the Fire Safety Regulation, and that members understand why those standards exist
- That the community's fire response infrastructure — water supply, pumping equipment, access routes, and response team — is maintained and ready
- That the division can function as the community's primary fire response capacity if professional services are unavailable
- That members know what to do when fire occurs — evacuation, assembly, and the actions that save lives in the minutes before any response arrives

## Core Functions

- **Prevention and Inspection** — regular inspection of structures for compliance with the Fire Safety Regulation, with particular attention to ignition sources, egress, spacing, and vegetation management
- **Community Fire Education** — ensuring every member understands the fire behaviors and safety practices that determine survival outcomes
- **Infrastructure Maintenance** — maintaining water supply, pumping equipment, access routes, and fire response tools in ready condition
- **Training and Drills** — training the response team in the operations defined in Regulation 1.8 and running regular drills to verify readiness
- **Active Response** — directing the community's fire response when professional services are unavailable, using the triage framework and priorities defined in Regulation 1.8
- **Coordination with Professional Services** — maintaining a working relationship with local fire departments and knowing how to call for, receive, and support professional response

## Prevention and Inspection

Prevention is the division's highest-leverage function. A fire that does not start requires no response. A fire that does not spread requires less response. Every hour the division spends on prevention is worth more than the equivalent time spent on response readiness.

**The inspection program** covers every structure in the community on a regular cycle — at minimum annually, with higher-risk structures inspected more frequently. The inspection is not adversarial. It is the division's primary mechanism for identifying and correcting hazards before they become fires. Inspectors apply the Fire Safety Regulation's standards, explain the physics behind each finding, and work with members to identify practical corrective actions.

**Inspection priorities by risk:** Community buildings — kitchens, assembly spaces, medical facilities — are inspected most frequently because their fire risk is highest and their loss most consequential. Tier 3 dense construction is inspected more frequently than dispersed Tier 1 structures. Structures with active ignition sources — cooking fires, wood stoves, fuel storage — receive attention at each inspection for ignition source management standards.

**The ignition source focus:** The majority of structure fires begin with a manageable ignition source — a wood stove with inadequate clearance, a cooking area with grease accumulation, an overloaded electrical connection, a chimney with creosote buildup. The division's inspection program treats ignition source management as the first and most important line of prevention because it addresses fires before they start.

**Vegetation management oversight:** Before each dry season, the division reviews vegetation management in the defensible space zones around all community structures — Zones 1, 2, and 3 as defined in Regulation 1.9. Zone 1 clearance — the five feet immediately surrounding each structure — receives the most attention, because the evidence on structure survival in wildfire is unambiguous: the condition of the immediate zone is the single most predictive factor for whether a structure survives.

**Tracking and follow-up:** Inspection findings are logged. Corrective actions are given a completion timeline based on the severity of the hazard. The division follows up on open findings and does not allow them to age indefinitely. A finding that is logged but never resolved is a finding that did not happen.

## Community Fire Education

The division's education function ensures that every member has the knowledge to make correct decisions during a fire — not after the fire department has been called and is on the way, but in the seconds and minutes before any response arrives. Those minutes determine most fire outcomes.

**What every member must know:**

- The community fire warning signal and what to do when they hear it
- Where the evacuation assembly point is and how to reach it
- The two-exit principle: every sleeping space has two independent ways out, and which those are for every space they sleep in
- That closed bedroom doors buy 10-20 minutes of survival time — a fact so counterintuitive and so well-evidenced that it warrants explicit and repeated community education
- That smoke rises and the survivable air in a fire is low to the floor — crawling below the smoke to an exit is not an instinctive behavior and must be taught
- That a fire discovered in its early stages can sometimes be suppressed with a bucket of water or a fire extinguisher — and that a fire that has reached flashover cannot be, and the correct response is to get out and stay out
- That water must never be applied to a grease fire

**Training sessions** are offered regularly and tied to specific contexts: new member orientation, seasonal fire risk briefings before dry seasons, post-drill discussions. The division does not treat fire education as a one-time orientation item — it is a standing community education program.

**The fire safety officer's teaching role:** The fire safety officer is the division's primary educator, but the division should not be the sole source of fire knowledge in the community. Members who have professional or extensive experiential knowledge of fire — former firefighters, wildland fire experience, construction experience with fire-rated assemblies — are division assets. The division identifies these members and involves them in the education program.

## Infrastructure Maintenance

The community's fire response infrastructure is defined in Regulation 1.8. The division is responsible for maintaining it in ready condition at all times.

**Water supply:** Community cisterns and natural water sources used for fire response are inspected regularly for capacity, accessibility, and condition. Cistern covers, access hatches, and fill connections are maintained. Natural source access routes are kept clear and passable year-round. The fire water supply is maintained separately from the potable water supply and treated as a dedicated reserve.

**Pumping equipment:** Fire pumps are started and run under load monthly. This is non-negotiable — a pump that has not been run in six months will not start reliably during an emergency. Fuel for the fire pump is held in a dedicated reserve not drawn on for routine community purposes. Pump maintenance — filters, fuel lines, primer, impeller — is performed on schedule and documented.

**Hose and hand tools:** Fire hoses, nozzles, and fittings are inspected for condition and compatibility. Hand tools — axes, shovels, rakes — are maintained sharp and accessible. Equipment storage is clearly marked and accessible to response team members without searching.

**Access routes:** Vehicle access routes to all structures — where the community has vehicle-based response capacity — are maintained at minimum 12 feet width and 13 feet 6 inches vertical clearance. Foot access paths are maintained at minimum 6 feet width and are kept free of debris that would impede personnel movement with equipment.

**Equipment inventory:** The division maintains a current inventory of all fire response equipment, its location, its condition, and its last maintenance date. Equipment that is degraded, missing, or in need of repair is flagged and addressed. An equipment list that shows complete readiness but has not been physically verified recently is not a reliable inventory.

## Training and Drills

The response team defined in Regulation 1.8 — a minimum of four members trained in basic fire response operations — is the division's active response capacity when professional services are unavailable. Training and drills maintain that capacity.

**Response team training covers:**
- Pump operation: starting, priming, throttle management, and shutdown under field conditions
- Hose handling: deployment, connection, nozzle operation, and the physical demands of managing a charged line
- Exposure protection: applying water to adjacent structures to prevent ignition when a structure is already lost
- The triage framework: which structures are Priority 1, Priority 2, and Priority 3 as established in the community's fire response plan, and what actions correspond to each
- Evacuation coordination: ensuring members are moving away from the fire while the response team moves toward it

**Drills run quarterly at minimum.** Not every drill is a full activation — tabletop exercises, equipment checks, and partial scenario drills count toward the quarterly cadence. At least once per year the division runs a full scenario drill: warning signal activation, response team mobilization, pump startup, and simulated suppression operations. The full drill reveals gaps that tabletop exercises do not.

**Drill debrief is mandatory.** Every drill produces a written debrief: what worked, what failed, what needs correction, and by when. The debrief record is how the division learns from its drills rather than simply completing them.

**Coordination with professional services in drills:** Where the local fire department is willing to participate in community drills, that participation is sought. Professional firefighters observing or participating in community drills provide feedback that the division cannot generate internally, and the relationship built through joint drills improves coordination if professional response is ever needed.

## Active Response

When fire occurs and professional services cannot respond in time or at all, the division directs the community's fire response. The response plan defined in Regulation 1.8 — triage priorities, response roles, warning and communication — is the operational foundation. The division's role is to execute that plan, not to improvise.

**The fire safety officer serves as incident commander.** All response personnel take direction from the incident commander. A response in which multiple people are giving independent directions to the same team is a response that will fail. Command is established immediately when the response team activates.

**Life safety before property protection.** The first priority in any fire event is ensuring all members are evacuating and accounted for. The response team does not begin suppression or exposure protection operations while members are still inside burning or threatened structures. The Community Care Division is the division's partner in ensuring members with mobility limitations are evacuating — pre-established protocols between the two divisions govern this coordination.

**Triage is applied immediately.** The incident commander applies the triage framework from Regulation 1.8 to determine which structures are defensible with available resources and which are already lost. Resources are committed to defensible exposures, not to the source fire once it is beyond suppression capacity. The hardest discipline in community fire response is accepting that a structure is lost and directing resources to what can still be saved.

**Professional service coordination:** When professional fire services can respond, the division's role changes immediately. The incident commander establishes contact with the arriving fire officer, provides a situation report — what is burning, what exposures are threatened, water supply location, access route — and subordinates the community response to professional command. Community response personnel do not freelance once professional services are on scene.

**Post-event documentation:** After any fire event — suppressed or lost — the division documents what happened: ignition source, time of detection, response timeline, resources deployed, outcome, and any failures in prevention or response that the event revealed. This documentation is used to improve both the prevention program and the response plan.

## Division Relationships

**Healthcare Division**
Members with mobility limitations require coordinated evacuation support. The division and the Community Care Division establish pre-event protocols with the Healthcare Division for ensuring medical needs are addressed during evacuation — particularly members dependent on equipment, temperature-sensitive medications, or specific transport conditions.

**Community Care Division**
The division's primary partner in evacuation. Members served by the Community Care Division are identified in the division's evacuation planning, and the Community Care Division's transport protocols govern how those members move when the fire warning sounds. The two divisions maintain a shared current list of members requiring evacuation assistance and review it whenever it changes.

**Transit Division**
Evacuation at community scale requires transit capacity. The division coordinates with the Transit Division so that evacuation transport is pre-organized — who goes where, which vehicles serve which areas, what the assembly points are — before any activation is needed.

**Water Security**
Fire water supply and potable water supply are separate systems but draw on shared infrastructure — water sources, storage capacity, and pumping equipment. The division coordinates with Water Security to ensure that fire water reserves are not inadvertently consumed by potable uses and that any water system stress that affects fire supply is communicated immediately.

**Housing Security**
Fire prevention compliance and housing standards intersect. Structures that do not meet fire safety standards are flagged to Housing Security where the issue relates to construction type, spacing, or structural modification. The two divisions coordinate on new construction review to ensure fire safety standards are incorporated before structures are built, not retrofitted afterward.

**Communications**
The fire warning signal, evacuation instructions, and all-clear notifications flow through Communications. The division coordinates with Communications on the warning signal itself — ensuring it is distinct from other community signals and known to all members — and on how information reaches members during an event.

## Operational Posture

The division operates in two distinct modes.

**When professional fire services are functional:** The division's work is entirely preventive and preparatory. Prevention inspections run on schedule. Equipment is maintained. Drills run quarterly. The response team stays trained. The division maintains its relationship with the local fire department — knowing who to call, what information to provide, and how to support professional response when it arrives. The community's fire response infrastructure is a supplement to professional capacity, not a replacement.

**When professional fire services are unavailable or non-responsive:** The division activates its full response function. The fire safety officer assumes incident command. The triage plan is executed. The division operates as the community's primary fire response capacity for as long as professional services are unavailable. This mode may last hours or months depending on the conditions producing the dysfunction. The division's prevention and infrastructure work in the first mode determines how effective it will be in the second.

The transition between modes should be anticipated and planned. The division establishes in advance what constitutes reliable professional response unavailability — response times beyond a defined threshold, confirmed service suspension, communication failure with the fire department — and what actions the transition triggers. Members should not be uncertain about whether the division is activated during an actual fire event.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Designate the fire safety officer and backup**
The division's first act is naming an incident commander with training adequate to lead a fire response. The backup fire safety officer is designated simultaneously. The division does not begin its prevention or response functions until command is established.

**2. Conduct the community fire assessment**
Walk every structure, map water supply points, assess access routes, identify Priority 1 structures, and produce the initial version of the community fire response plan. This assessment is the foundation of the prevention program and the response plan simultaneously.

**3. Establish and verify the fire response infrastructure**
Test the pump. Verify cistern capacity and access. Clear access routes. Inventory equipment. The infrastructure described in Regulation 1.8 must be verified functional before the division can claim any response capacity.

**4. Train the response team**
Four members minimum are trained in pump operation, hose handling, exposure protection, and the triage framework. Training precedes the first drill.

**5. Conduct the first inspection cycle**
Every structure in the community is inspected against the Fire Safety Regulation. Findings are documented. Corrective actions are assigned. The inspection cycle then runs on its annual schedule.

**6. Run the first full drill**
Before the division considers itself operationally ready, it runs a full scenario drill — warning signal, mobilization, pump startup, simulated operations. The debrief from this drill closes gaps before the division is needed.

## Division Documents

**Community Fire Response Plan** — the operational plan for active fire response: Priority 1 structures and their locations, triage decision framework, response team assignments, incident command succession, water supply points, access routes, and evacuation assembly points. Referenced in Regulation 1.8. Updated whenever community conditions change.

**Inspection Log** — a record of every structure inspection: date, inspector, findings, corrective actions assigned, timeline, and follow-up status. The running record of the prevention program.

**Equipment Inventory and Maintenance Log** — a current record of all fire response equipment, its location, its condition, and its maintenance history. Includes the monthly pump test log.

**Training and Drill Record** — a record of all training sessions and drills: date, participants, scenario, outcomes, and debrief findings with corrective actions.

**Evacuation Registry** — in coordination with the Community Care and Transit Divisions: a current list of members requiring evacuation assistance, their locations, their specific needs, and their assigned transport. Updated whenever member circumstances change.

**Coordination Contact Record** — contact information and established protocols for the local fire department and any mutual aid agreements with sister Societies or neighboring communities. Updated annually and verified before each dry season.
