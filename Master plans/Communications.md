# Communications Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Communications Division exists to ensure that every member of the Resolute Society can reach and be reached by the community — under normal conditions and under whatever conditions tomorrow brings. It does this by maintaining a layered communications infrastructure that degrades gracefully rather than failing completely, by educating members about the nature of the tools they use, and by building the human relationships and physical capacity that make communication possible even when no infrastructure remains.

The Division serves two relationships: member to member within the Society, and Society to Society within the federation. These are its primary obligations. External public communications is a secondary function and is not the organizing purpose of the Division's work.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for four things:

- That every member can reliably reach the community and be reached by it, regardless of infrastructure conditions
- That the Society maintains communications capacity with sister Societies within the federation
- That members understand the nature of every communications platform they use, including what those platforms extract from them
- That the Society's communications infrastructure is owned and controlled by the Society to the greatest degree its capacity allows, and that dependency on corporate infrastructure is actively and honestly managed

## Core Functions

- **Infrastructure** — building, maintaining, and operating the Society's layered communications stack across all four tiers
- **Protocols** — establishing clear conventions for how communications flow within the Society and between Societies, so that members know where to look and what to expect
- **Education** — ensuring every member understands the tools they use, their failure modes, their costs, and the alternatives
- **Emergency Readiness** — maintaining the lower communication tiers so they are functional before they are needed, not assembled during the crisis that requires them
- **Federation Liaison** — maintaining scheduled contact with sister Societies and ensuring the infrastructure for inter-Society communication exists at every tier

## The Layered Infrastructure Model

The Society's communications infrastructure is organized in four tiers. Each tier is slower and lower in bandwidth than the one above it. Each is more resilient. The Society maintains all four simultaneously so that the loss of any tier — or any combination of tiers — does not mean the loss of communication. Resilience is not a backup plan. It is the design.

**Tier 1 — Digital, Internet-Dependent**
The fastest and most convenient tier. Email, encrypted messaging applications, shared documents, video calls. First to fail under grid stress, platform disruption, or internet outage. The Society uses this tier for routine coordination but does not depend on it for anything critical. No governance process, no emergency protocol, and no essential function of the Society may exist only at this tier.

**Tier 2 — Digital, Local Network**
Communications that function without internet connectivity but require local power and local networking infrastructure. A self-hosted server on a local mesh network allows messaging, shared documents, and coordination within the Society's physical area without any dependence on external infrastructure. The Society's hubs are the natural nodes of this mesh — each hub hosts a local network node, and the nodes connect to form the Society's local network. This tier requires the Society to own and operate its own hardware at each hub. It fails when local power fails, but can be sustained by the Society's own energy infrastructure in coordination with the Energy Division.

**Tier 3 — Radio**
Communications that function without internet, without local networking, and with minimal power. Amateur radio — HAM radio — is the backbone of this tier. Radio equipment is stationed at each hub, and the Society maintains licensed operators distributed across its hub network so that the loss of any one operator or any one hub does not eliminate the tier. Radio can sustain voice communication and, with appropriate equipment, digital text transmission across significant distances. It is the primary inter-Society communications channel when higher tiers have failed. It requires discipline — scheduled contact windows, clear protocols, and trained operators — but it functions when nothing else does.

**Tier 4 — Physical**
The floor. Human beings carrying messages on foot, by bicycle, or by vehicle between hubs. Physical notice boards posted at each hub. Scheduled in-person assemblies at hubs whose time and place are known in advance and do not require any communication to attend. This tier has no infrastructure to fail. It requires only that members know their hub, that notice boards are maintained, and that the Society has designated messengers who know their routes between hubs. Because every member is within walking or biking distance of a hub, the hub network guarantees that no member falls off the communications stack entirely when higher tiers fail. Physical messengers traveling hub-to-hub between Societies are the inter-Society equivalent. In a sustained grid-down scenario this tier may become the primary means of communication for extended periods. The Society designs for this rather than assuming it will not happen.

## Hubs and the Physical Communications Network

The Society's hub network is the physical foundation of its communications infrastructure. A hub is a designated physical meeting space serving a defined geographic area, sized so that every member within that area is within walking or biking distance. Every member belongs to a hub. The hub network together covers the Society's full membership.

For communications purposes, hubs serve four functions simultaneously: they are the nodes of the Tier 2 local mesh network, the stations for Tier 3 radio equipment, the locations of Tier 4 physical notice boards, and the assembly points members go to when higher communication tiers have failed. The hub is where the stack bottoms out — and because every member can reach their hub without any infrastructure, the stack never bottoms out below the hub.

This means the Society's communications resilience is only as strong as its hub coverage. A member who cannot reach a hub within walking or biking distance falls off the lower tiers of the stack. Hub coverage gaps are communications vulnerabilities. The Division should maintain a current map of hub coverage and flag any members who fall outside a hub's catchment area to the General Assembly. Expanding hub coverage is a joint responsibility of the Communications Division and whatever body governs physical infrastructure — closing a coverage gap restores full-stack communications resilience to the members affected.

Each hub should maintain at minimum: a physical notice board, radio equipment and a relationship with at least one licensed operator serving that hub, and a connection to the Society's local mesh network. Hub communications infrastructure is the responsibility of the Communications Division to specify and the hub itself to maintain, with Division support.

## Internal Communications

The Society's internal communications serve three distinct functions: routine coordination, governance, and emergency. Each has different requirements and should not be collapsed into a single channel.

**Routine coordination** is the day-to-day communication of divisions, working groups, and members going about the Society's common life. It is the highest volume, lowest stakes function. It naturally lives at Tier 1 and Tier 2. The Society should operate a self-hosted communication platform — a Matrix server or equivalent — for this function, accessible to all members, owned by the Society, and subject to the privacy commitments of Article XXI. This platform is not a social media product. It has no engagement optimization, no algorithmic curation, no notification mechanics designed to create compulsion. It is a tool members use when they choose to use it.

**Governance communications** carry higher stakes and require greater reliability. Assembly notices, referendum announcements, petition filings, and the distribution of motions, rationales, and dissents must reach every member through a channel that does not depend on members actively monitoring any particular platform. The Society should maintain a primary governance communications channel — ideally a simple mailing list hosted on Society infrastructure — and a physical backup: a notice posted at every hub. Every governance communication goes through both. A member who receives nothing digitally can still attend an assembly if they check their hub's notice board. This redundancy is not a convenience — it is a constitutional requirement. Governance that can only be accessed through a corporate platform is governance that can be interrupted by that platform.

**Emergency communications** require a protocol that members have practiced before the emergency, not assembled during it. The Society should establish a clear emergency communications sequence: when Tier 1 fails, members move to Tier 2; when Tier 2 fails, members monitor the radio frequency at the designated contact window through their hub's equipment; when radio is unavailable, members go to their hub. Every member should know this sequence by memory, and every member knows their hub. The Division should drill the full sequence periodically — including the physical assembly at hubs — so that the protocol is practiced before it is needed.

**On corporate platforms**: the Society does not prohibit members from communicating through corporate platforms. It does not require them to. The Society's own institutional communications — governance notices, division coordination, official records — do not flow through platforms the Society does not control. Members who use corporate messaging applications for personal coordination are making their own choices about their own communications. The Division's responsibility is to ensure they make those choices with clear understanding of what those platforms are, not to police how members communicate privately.

## Member Education on Communications Platforms

The Division holds a specific educational responsibility that sits alongside but is distinct from the Division of Education's broader curriculum. Every member should understand the following before relying on any communications platform.

**Corporate platforms are not neutral infrastructure.** They are products designed to generate revenue, primarily through advertising and data sales. The mechanics of that revenue generation — engagement optimization, data collection, behavioral profiling — operate whether or not the user is aware of them. A member who uses a corporate platform for Society communications is feeding information about the Society's membership, relationships, and activities into systems designed to extract value from that information. This is not a reason to refuse all use of corporate platforms. It is a reason to use them with clear eyes about what is happening.

**Encryption is not privacy.** End-to-end encrypted messaging protects the content of messages from interception. It does not protect metadata — who is communicating with whom, how often, at what times, from what locations. Metadata analysis is often more revealing than content. A member who sends encrypted messages through a corporate platform may be protecting the words while exposing the relationship. Members should understand this distinction.

**Convenience is a design choice, not a neutral feature.** The most convenient platform is usually the most extractive. Friction in communications tools is not always a flaw. Sometimes it is the cost of using infrastructure that does not treat the user as a product. The Society asks members to accept some friction in the service of genuine ownership over their communications.

**Platform dependency is a vulnerability.** Any Society function that can only be performed through a platform the Society does not control can be interrupted by that platform. Deplatforming, terms-of-service changes, and corporate decisions have interrupted community organizing with no notice and no recourse. The Society does not build critical functions on infrastructure it does not own.

## Society-to-Society Communications

The federation covenant requires that Societies maintain active communications with their sisters — not only in crisis but as a standing operational practice. The infrastructure for mutual aid exists before it is needed, or it does not exist when it is needed.

**At Tier 1**, inter-Society communication flows through encrypted channels between designated division liaisons. Each obligated Division maintains a direct communications relationship with its counterpart Division in every sister Society. The Communications Division maintains a current directory of liaison contacts across the federation and ensures it is updated as Society membership and leadership changes.

**At Tier 2**, inter-Society digital communication requires that both Societies maintain compatible local network infrastructure. The Division should establish and document compatibility standards with sister Societies so that Tier 2 communication is possible without negotiation during an emergency.

**At Tier 3**, radio is the primary inter-Society channel. The Society should establish scheduled contact windows with each sister Society — fixed times on fixed frequencies at which a licensed operator is monitoring. These windows should occur at minimum weekly under normal conditions and can be increased in frequency as conditions require. Contact windows should be known to all licensed operators in the Society, not just the Division officer, so that the absence of any individual does not break the link. Digital modes over radio — which allow text transmission over amateur radio frequencies — extend what can be communicated at this tier significantly.

**At Tier 4**, inter-Society physical communication travels hub-to-hub. Each Society designates a gateway hub — the hub whose messenger routes extend to sister Societies — and a liaison individual or small team responsible for that travel on a known schedule. Messenger routes connect gateway hubs between Societies, not arbitrary points. Routes, schedules, and recognition protocols should be established before they are needed, and the gateway hub designation should be known across the Society's membership so that messages intended for a sister Society know where to go.

## Infrastructure Ownership and the Migration Path

The Society's goal is to own and operate the infrastructure its communications depend on. This is a direction, not a day-one requirement. An early Society will necessarily begin with tools it does not control. The Division's responsibility is to manage that dependency honestly rather than pretend it does not exist, and to move consistently toward independence as the Society's capacity grows.

**The migration path has a clear priority order.** Governance communications migrate off corporate infrastructure first — this is the highest stakes function and the one most vulnerable to platform disruption. Routine coordination migrates second. Emergency communications should never depend on corporate infrastructure at any stage of the Society's development.

**Self-hosted infrastructure requires maintenance.** A Society server is not a set-and-forget installation. It requires someone with the technical knowledge to maintain it, update it, back it up, and restore it when it fails. The Division should identify and develop this capacity within its membership. A single point of technical knowledge is a single point of failure. At least two members should be capable of maintaining the Society's self-hosted infrastructure at any time.

**Tier 3 and Tier 4 infrastructure should be established early.** Radio capability and physical messenger protocols are not expensive or complex to establish. They are only difficult to establish in the middle of a crisis. The Division should have licensed operators and functioning radio equipment before they are needed, not after.

## Division Relationships

**Energy**
Communications infrastructure at Tier 2 and below requires power. The self-hosted server, the radio equipment, and the charging infrastructure for Tier 1 devices all depend on power that the Energy Division maintains. In a grid-down scenario the Communications Division's ability to maintain Tier 1 and Tier 2 function depends entirely on the Energy Division's capacity to sustain local power. These two divisions must coordinate closely on power requirements and backup power planning.

**Education**
The member education function described above is a Communications Division responsibility but should be integrated into the broader formation curriculum the Division of Education maintains. Communications literacy — understanding platforms, metadata, encryption, and infrastructure dependency — belongs in the foundational curriculum alongside practical skills.

**All Divisions**
The Communications Division maintains the infrastructure through which all other divisions coordinate. It does not direct that coordination — it enables it. Division officers should participate in establishing the communications protocols their divisions use and should ensure their members know how to reach them through the full communications stack, not only through the most convenient tier.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division focuses on infrastructure maintenance, member education, and the cultivation of radio operators and physical messenger capacity. These are not emergency functions — they are standing obligations that must be maintained continuously to be available when conditions change.

As conditions deteriorate, the Division shifts attention down the tier stack — ensuring Tier 2 and Tier 3 infrastructure is functioning and that members know to use it. The transition between tiers should not require a crisis declaration. Members should move naturally to lower tiers as higher ones become unreliable.

At the bottom of the stack the Society can always communicate. Every member knows their hub. Every hub has a notice board. Messengers know their routes between hubs. The assembly time is known without needing to be communicated. This is not a failure state. It is the floor the Society has designed for — and because the hub network covers every member, the floor holds.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Establish hub notice boards and physical protocols**
Every hub gets a notice board and a designated messenger. Establish hub-to-hub messenger routes and schedules. Identify the gateway hub for inter-Society contact. This requires no technology and no budget, and it establishes the floor of the stack from day one.

**2. Establish radio capability**
Identify and license operators across the hub network — at least one per hub where possible. Acquire and station radio equipment at each hub. Establish contact windows with any existing sister Societies. This is the most time-intensive tier to establish and should begin immediately.

**3. Establish governance communications off corporate infrastructure**
A simple self-hosted mailing list paired with physical notices posted at every hub. Governance notices flow through both from the Society's founding.

**4. Establish member education protocol**
Every new member receives a clear orientation to the Society's communications stack — their hub, what tools the Society uses, what corporate platforms extract, and the emergency sequence. The emergency sequence should be drilled with the full membership within the first year.

**5. Build self-hosted routine coordination infrastructure**
A Matrix server or equivalent, with nodes at each hub connecting through the local mesh. Maintained by at least two technically capable members. Routine division coordination moves here.

## Division Documents

**Communications Stack Reference** — a single-page document every member receives at orientation, describing the four tiers, the Society's specific infrastructure at each tier, the emergency sequence, and their hub's location and assembly schedule. Simple enough to memorize. Updated whenever the infrastructure changes.

**Hub Communications Infrastructure Guide** — specifications for the communications infrastructure each hub is responsible for maintaining: notice board standards, radio equipment requirements, mesh network node setup, and the messenger role. Used by hub stewards and reviewed by the Communications Division annually.

**Federation Contact Directory** — a current list of liaison contacts at every sister Society, organized by division. Maintained by the Communications Division and distributed to all division officers.

**Radio Operations Protocol** — contact windows, frequencies, digital mode standards, and operating procedures for licensed operators. Maintained by the Division and reviewed annually.

**Platform Education Guide** — a plain-language explanation of how corporate communications platforms work, what they extract, and what the alternatives are. Part of new member orientation and available to all members.
