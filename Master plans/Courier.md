# Courier Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Courier Division exists to move things. Messages, goods, materials, supplies — anything physical that needs to travel between members, between hubs, or between Societies moves through the Division. It operates as the Society's physical logistics network: a postal service whose post offices are the hubs, whose routes connect them on known schedules, and whose capacity scales from routine member exchange to emergency supply movement when conditions require it.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for three things:

- That anything a member needs to send reaches its destination reliably, on a known schedule, through a network every member can access
- That hubs function as effective dispatch and receiving points — that things arrive in good order and members can retrieve what is waiting for them
- That emergency logistics capacity exists and can be activated when any division requires urgent physical movement of critical supplies

## Core Functions

- **Route Operations** — running scheduled routes between hubs on known timetables, moving whatever has been received for dispatch
- **Hub Logistics** — receiving, logging, holding, and dispatching items at each hub; maintaining the order and security of hub dispatch areas
- **Emergency Logistics** — mobilizing the Division's full capacity for urgent movement of critical supplies when normal operational tempo is insufficient
- **Inter-Society Routes** — maintaining physical courier connections to sister Societies on scheduled runs

## The Hub Network as Postal Infrastructure

Every hub in the Society's network functions as a local post office. Members bring items to their hub for dispatch. Items arriving for local members are held at the hub until collected. The Division runs routes between hubs, moving whatever has been received since the last run. Members come to their hub — the hub is the interface between them and the network.

**Receiving at the hub** means the hub maintains a designated dispatch area where members deposit items for sending, with a log recording what was received, its destination, its declared contents, and its weight. Items are packaged and labeled by the sender before deposit. The hub's designated logistics steward maintains the dispatch area, logs incoming items, and ensures items are secure until the next route run.

**Dispatch from the hub** means that when a route courier arrives, they collect everything logged for outbound dispatch, verify the log, and depart on schedule. Arriving items are logged into the hub's receiving record, held in a designated area, and made available for pickup. Members are notified of arriving items through whatever communications tier is currently functioning — at minimum through the hub's physical notice board.

**Hub storage is temporary by design.** The Division's accountability for an item ends when it is logged into the receiving hub's record. Items held beyond a defined pickup period are returned to the sender on the next route run.

**Every hub's dispatch schedule is public information.** Members need to know when the next route run leaves their hub and when items sent today will arrive. The Division maintains and publishes current route schedules at every hub.

## Route Operations

Routes are the spine of the Division. A route is a scheduled run between two or more hubs, operating on a fixed timetable, moving whatever has been received for dispatch along that route since the previous run.

**Route design** should minimize total distance while maintaining acceptable transit times between any two points in the hub network. Direct routes handle local movement between nearby hubs. Connections through intermediate hubs handle longer distances. The Division should map its full route network and calculate maximum transit times between all hub pairs so that senders can set realistic expectations.

**Route frequency** reflects actual demand. A high-traffic route between closely spaced hubs may run daily. A low-traffic route between distant hubs may run twice weekly. The Division assesses demand regularly and adjusts frequency accordingly. A route that runs once a week is a materially different service than one that runs daily — frequency determines practical usefulness.

**Couriers** run defined routes on defined schedules, collect outbound items from each hub on the route, deliver inbound items, and verify hub logs. Couriers should know their routes well enough to run them without digital navigation — route knowledge is practical knowledge held in people, not only in devices.

**Transport modes** are matched to route characteristics. Motor vehicles handle longer distances and heavier loads under current conditions. Human-powered transport — primarily cycling — is the resilient fallback as fuel and vehicle maintenance become more difficult. The Division develops its human-powered capacity continuously rather than waiting for motorized options to fail. Walking is the floor — routes should be walkable even when they are not normally walked, so that no route fails entirely when other options are unavailable.

**Weight and volume limits** per route run are established based on transport mode and the courier's realistic capacity. Items exceeding limits are held for a combined run or arranged as a dedicated freight movement.

**On-time reliability** is the Division's primary service quality metric. A courier service that runs on schedule is useful. One whose schedule is unpredictable is not a service — it is a hope. The Division treats schedule adherence as a standing operational priority and investigates causes of delay rather than accepting them.

## Emergency Logistics

Under emergency conditions — when a division requires urgent movement of critical supplies that cannot wait for the next scheduled run — the Division activates emergency logistics capacity.

**Emergency logistics differs from routine operations** in that it is unscheduled, prioritized over routine runs if resources require a choice, and reserved for genuine urgency. The Division does not activate emergency logistics for requests that routine scheduling can accommodate — the emergency tier loses its meaning if used routinely.

**Activation** occurs when a division officer formally requests emergency logistics support, describing what needs to move, where, and why the normal schedule is insufficient. The Division officer assesses the request and determines whether emergency activation is warranted.

**During Society-wide crises** the Division may be directed by the General Assembly to prioritize specific cargo. Water, medical supplies, and food take precedence over all other cargo when member subsistence is at risk. The Division maintains a clear internal protocol for this prioritization so that it does not require deliberation during the emergency itself.

**The Division's emergency capacity is its current route capacity redirected.** Emergency activation means redirecting existing courier capacity, extending courier hours, or calling on members with vehicles or cycling capacity who are not routine Division staff. The Division maintains a roster of members willing to support emergency logistics and keeps it current.

## Inter-Society Routes

The Division extends its hub network outward to sister Societies through inter-society routes connecting the Society's gateway hub to the corresponding gateway hub of each sister Society on a known schedule.

**Inter-society routes are longer and less frequent** than intra-society routes — weekly as a baseline, increasing as inter-society exchange grows. Items destined for a sister Society are routed to the gateway hub through the intra-society network, then dispatched on the inter-society route. Items arriving from a sister Society enter the intra-society network at the gateway hub for delivery onward.

**Inter-society couriers** should have established relationships with their counterparts at sister Society gateway hubs. These relationships are operationally important — a courier who is known and trusted at the destination hub encounters fewer complications than one who is a stranger. The Division treats inter-society courier relationships as assets to be maintained.

**Emergency inter-society logistics** — movement of critical supplies under mutual aid obligations — follows the same activation logic as intra-society emergency logistics but requires coordination with the receiving Society's Courier Division. Established communication between courier operations is maintained so this coordination requires no startup time during an emergency.

## Division Relationships

**Communications**
Physical message delivery is a courier function. The Communications Division establishes protocols for what physical messages look like and how they flow through the hub network. The Courier Division moves them. The two divisions coordinate so that the transition from digital to physical communication is seamless — a member who needs to send a physical message knows exactly how to do it through the courier network.

**All Divisions**
Every division that moves physical materials depends on the Courier Division's route network. Division officers communicate regular logistics needs so that route planning reflects actual demand. Unexpected large shipments that exceed route capacity require advance notice.

**General Assembly**
Emergency logistics activations that cross Society-level priorities are directed by the General Assembly or by agreement among relevant division officers under emergency governance provisions.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division runs its scheduled routes, maintains its vehicles and equipment, and develops its human-powered courier capacity alongside motorized operations. The emphasis is on schedule reliability.

As conditions deteriorate and motorized transport becomes more difficult, the Division shifts progressively toward human-powered operations. This shift should not be an emergency response — cycling and walking capacity are developed continuously so the transition is a change in proportion rather than a scramble.

At the floor, the Society can always move things. A person on foot carrying a message or a package between hubs is the oldest logistics operation in human history. It is slow and low-volume. It is completely resilient. The Division builds toward this floor as bedrock, not as a failure state.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Map the route network**
Establish routes between all hubs, calculate transit times, set initial route frequencies based on hub population and estimated demand. Publish the schedule at every hub.

**2. Establish hub logistics at each hub**
Designate the logistics steward at each hub. Set up the dispatch and receiving area. Establish the log format and begin accepting items for dispatch.

**3. Identify and develop courier capacity**
Identify members willing to run routes. Assess vehicle availability. Begin developing cycling capacity alongside motorized operations from the start — not as a fallback but as a parallel capability.

**4. Establish inter-society routes**
Coordinate with sister Society Courier Divisions to establish gateway hub connections, route schedules, and inter-society logistics protocols.

## Division Documents

**Route Schedule** — the complete current schedule of all intra-society and inter-society routes: departure hub, departure time, stops, arrival hub, arrival time, frequency. Posted at every hub and updated whenever schedules change.

**Hub Dispatch and Receiving Log** — the standardized log maintained at each hub recording all items received for dispatch and all items received for pickup: date, sender, destination, declared contents, weight, courier name, and dispatch or receiving time.

**Emergency Logistics Protocol** — the internal procedure for emergency activation: how requests are made, how they are assessed, how courier capacity is redirected, and how inter-society emergency coordination works.

**Courier Route Guide** — for each route, a detailed guide covering the full route path, hub locations and contacts, known route considerations, and the contingency path if the primary route is impassable. Held by each courier and updated as conditions change.
