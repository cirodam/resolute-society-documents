# Wild Harvest Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Wild Harvest Division exists to develop and maintain the community's capacity to take food and materials from wild sources — through hunting, trapping, fishing, and aquaculture — and to manage those harvests sustainably so the resource base improves rather than declines over time. It does this by holding the skills, equipment, and ecological knowledge needed to harvest effectively across seasons, processing wild game and fish into usable food for distribution through the Food Security Division, and stewarding the habitat and wildlife populations that the community depends on.

Wild harvest is among the oldest human food systems. In most of the continental United States, deer, fish, small game, and waterfowl represent a significant and renewable protein source that requires no planted fields, no purchased feed, and no supply chain beyond the community's own skill and equipment. A community that can hunt, trap, and fish effectively has food security that operates entirely outside any commercial system. That capacity must be built, practiced, and held — it does not exist simply because the land does.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That the community maintains active hunting, trapping, and fishing capacity across seasons, producing wild protein as a meaningful contribution to the community's food supply
- That wild harvest is conducted within legal requirements and at levels the local wildlife population can sustain — protecting the resource base for the long term
- That wild game and fish are handled safely from kill to delivery, without waste and without health risk
- That harvest skills, firearms safety, and ecological knowledge are held broadly enough across the membership that the division's capacity does not depend on any single person

## Core Functions

- **Hunting** — pursuing and taking deer, turkey, small game, waterfowl, and other game animals within season and legal limits
- **Trapping** — setting and maintaining trap lines for food animals and furbearers
- **Fishing** — rod fishing, trotlines, nets, and other methods appropriate to local waters
- **Aquaculture** — managing community fish ponds as a managed protein production system
- **Wild Game Processing** — field dressing, butchering, and preparing wild game and fish for delivery to Food Security
- **Habitat Stewardship** — managing the land and water conditions that support healthy wildlife populations
- **Equipment Management** — maintaining firearms, ammunition, traps, fishing gear, and processing equipment
- **Safety and Legal Compliance** — ensuring all harvest activity meets legal requirements and is conducted safely
- **Skills and Teaching** — distributing harvest and processing knowledge across the membership

## Hunting

Deer are the division's most important game animal across most of the continental United States. A mature whitetail deer yields 50 to 100 pounds of boneless meat — the caloric equivalent of months of protein for one person. Whitetails are abundant, widely distributed, and highly adaptable. The division should treat deer hunting as a primary food production function, not a recreational activity, and should organize its hunting program to take the maximum sustainable harvest the local deer population supports.

**Harvest planning.** The division should develop a realistic picture of the deer population on and near community land. State wildlife agencies publish county-level harvest data and population estimates; the division should use this data alongside its own scouting to understand what the local herd can support. Overhunting depletes the population; systematic underhunting wastes a renewable food resource. The division should set a harvest target before each season and work toward it.

**Firearms selection.** For deer and large game, centerfire rifles and shotguns in appropriate calibers are the standard. Archery equipment — compound or traditional bow — extends the hunting season significantly, as archery seasons open earlier than firearms seasons in most states and create less pressure on the deer herd. The division should develop archery competency alongside firearms competency, both to extend the season and to reduce ammunition dependence. Muzzleloaders offer a further extension of season and, importantly, can be loaded with hand-cast projectiles and black powder produced from local materials — making them the most supply-chain-independent hunting technology available.

**Small game** — rabbits, squirrels, grouse, pheasant — provides a consistent harvest across the season with lower per-animal yield but higher availability and less pressure on any single species. A shotgun competent member can take meaningful small game harvests on almost any outing. The division should not overlook small game in favor of deer alone; a season of consistent small game harvest contributes meaningfully to the community's protein supply.

**Turkey** is the second most important big game animal in the eastern and southern United States. A mature gobbler yields 10 to 15 pounds of meat. Turkey hunting requires patience, calling skill, and camouflage — a different technique than deer hunting. The division should develop turkey hunting competency as a distinct skill.

**Feral hogs** are established across much of the South and increasingly the Midwest. They are typically not subject to seasonal restriction and can be taken year-round in most states where they are present. A large hog yields 80 to 150 pounds of meat. Feral hogs are destructive to agricultural land but represent a significant and largely unregulated food source where present.

**Waterfowl** — ducks and geese — are important seasonal protein in many regions. Waterfowl hunting requires federal and state licensing beyond standard hunting licenses, decoys, calls, and access to water. The division should develop waterfowl capacity where local geography supports it.

## Trapping

Trapping produces food and furbearing animals with significantly less time per animal than hunting, once trap lines are established and checked. A well-run trap line can produce consistent harvest with a few hours of work per day. It is among the most efficient wild protein acquisition methods available.

**Food animals.** Rabbits are the most important trap-line food animal — abundant, fast-reproducing, and catchable with simple snares or cage traps. Squirrels, muskrats, and beavers are significant secondary food sources where present. Beaver in particular produces substantial meat from a large animal and should not be overlooked.

**Furbearers.** Raccoon, fox, coyote, mink, otter, and beaver produce pelts that the Leatherwork Division can tan and use. The division should coordinate with Leatherwork on which fur species are most useful and prioritize trap lines accordingly.

**Trap types.** The division should maintain competency in three trap systems:
- **Body-grip (Conibear) traps** — kill traps appropriate for beaver, muskrat, and mink in water sets; highly effective and relatively quick
- **Foothold traps** — versatile traps for land and water sets across a range of species; require proper sizing by species and careful set placement
- **Snares** — wire or cable loops appropriate for rabbits and small game; simple, lightweight, and producible without purchased parts

**Trap set placement** is a learned skill that takes time to develop. The best trap placement reads animal sign — tracks, trails, feeding areas, latrines — and positions the trap at natural pinch points in the animal's travel. A poorly placed trap in good habitat produces nothing; a well-placed trap in moderate habitat produces consistently.

**Check intervals.** All traps must be checked at required intervals — typically every 24 to 48 hours under state regulations, and as often as possible for humane reasons. A trapped animal that is not checked promptly suffers unnecessarily, and unchecked traps are a significant source of legal violations. The division should establish a check schedule before setting any trap line, with named responsibility for each line.

**Legal requirements** for trapping vary significantly by state — license requirements, species restrictions, legal trap types, required check intervals, and prohibited methods all differ. The division must hold working knowledge of its state's trapping regulations and comply with them.

## Fishing

Fish are among the most productive wild protein sources available — a single successful trotline can yield more meat in a night than a full day of deer hunting. The division should approach fishing as a production operation, not a pastime, using methods scaled to food output rather than sport.

**Trotlines** are the division's most productive freshwater fishing method. A trotline is a horizontal main line anchored at both ends, with dropper lines and baited hooks every two to three feet. A 50-hook trotline set overnight on a productive catfish waterway can yield 20 to 50 pounds of fish. Trotlines require minimal active attention — set in the evening, checked in the morning — and scale easily. The division should master trotline fishing wherever catfish are present.

**Rod fishing** is lower yield per hour than trotlines but more flexible and appropriate for a wider range of species and water types. The division should maintain rod fishing competency as a complementary method, particularly for trout, bass, and panfish in waters where trotlines are impractical or restricted.

**Cast nets and seine nets** are productive where legal. A cast net thrown over a school of baitfish or panfish in shallow water produces volume quickly. Seine nets require two people and produce significant harvest in appropriate water. Both methods are restricted or prohibited in some states and waterbodies; the division must know local regulations before deploying nets.

**Fish traps and weirs** are traditional high-efficiency methods — a funnel-shaped trap or rock weir that concentrates fish and holds them alive — that are heavily regulated or prohibited in most states. The division should understand their design and historical use as knowledge, and verify legal status before any deployment.

**Species knowledge.** The division should hold working identification knowledge for all gamefish in local waters — not only to target productive species but to identify and properly handle non-target catch, including protected species that must be released unharmed.

**Ice fishing** is relevant where waters freeze solid. It requires different equipment (auger, tip-ups or jigging rods, shelter) and extends the fishing calendar through winter months when other food acquisition is limited.

## Aquaculture

A managed fish pond is the closest equivalent to livestock farming that the Wild Harvest Division operates — a controlled environment that produces consistent, predictable yield with appropriate management. A single one-acre pond stocked correctly can produce 500 to 1,000 pounds of fish per year under active management, indefinitely.

**Species selection.** The most productive and manageable combination for the eastern and southern United States is the bass-bluegill polyculture: largemouth bass as predator and bluegill as forage and harvest species. Bluegill reproduce prolifically; bass control their numbers and provide a harvestable predator population. Channel catfish can be added as a bottom-feeding complement. For the community's primary food production goal, channel catfish monoculture — stocked at 1,500 to 2,000 per acre with feed — produces the highest meat yield per acre when supplemental feed is available.

**Water quality** is the primary management variable. Dissolved oxygen is the critical parameter — fish die quickly when oxygen is depleted, typically in hot weather or following algae blooms. The division should monitor water quality through summer months and understand the conditions that stress fish populations.

**Stocking and harvest balance.** The pond must be harvested at a rate that matches its production — overstocking depletes food resources and stunts fish; underharvesting leads to overpopulation and similarly stunted fish. State fisheries offices provide stocking recommendations; the division should consult these and track its harvest against stocking levels.

**Pond construction** for a new pond is a significant earthworks project coordinated with the Agriculture Division and the Masonry Division for dam construction.

## Wild Game Processing

A harvested animal is not yet food. Field dressing, skinning, butchering, and cooling the carcass correctly are what convert the kill into safe, usable meat. These skills are as important as hunting skill, and the division must hold them across enough members that a successful hunt is never followed by a handling failure.

**Field dressing** should occur immediately after the kill — within the hour where possible. The body cavity is opened and the organs removed to allow the carcass to cool. Speed matters: bacteria multiply rapidly in warm meat. The division should ensure that every member who hunts can field dress the animals they pursue.

**Cooling the carcass** is the most critical food safety step. Meat must be brought below 40°F within a few hours of kill. In cold weather this happens naturally; in warm weather the carcass must be packed with ice, hung in a cool space, or delivered to a cooler immediately. A deer killed in October in cool temperatures can be hung and aged for a week to improve flavor; the same deer killed in August requires ice within the hour. The division must have a concrete cooling plan for warm-weather harvests before the season opens.

**Aging.** Venison aged at 34 to 38°F for 7 to 14 days is significantly more tender and better flavored than venison butchered immediately. Aging requires reliable cold storage — a refrigerated space or, in appropriate weather, a cool shaded area. The division should coordinate with the Food Security Division on cold storage access for aging.

**Butchering.** The division should hold full butchering competency for deer, hogs, and the small game and waterfowl it pursues. This includes breaking the carcass into primals, deboning, and producing cuts in forms appropriate for Food Security's processing and distribution. Organ meats — liver, heart, kidneys — are highly nutritious and should be recovered, not discarded. Bones should be delivered to Food Security for broth production.

**Fish processing.** Fish must be processed quickly — within hours of harvest in warm conditions. The division should hold filleting competency for all species it targets and should have cooler space at any active fishing operation sufficient to hold the day's catch safely until delivery to Food Security.

**Hide and fur delivery.** All hides from deer and large game, and pelts from furbearing trap animals, should be delivered to the Leatherwork Division promptly with basic field preparation — salted or cooled to prevent deterioration.

## Habitat Stewardship

Wildlife populations are not fixed. They respond to food availability, cover, water access, and hunting pressure. The division manages habitat to support healthy, productive populations — not merely extracting from whatever exists.

**Edge habitat** — the transition zone between forest and open land — is among the most productive wildlife habitat. Deer, turkey, rabbit, and many other species concentrate at edges. The division should work with the Forestry Division and Agriculture Division to maintain and create productive edge habitat on community land: forest openings, brushy field margins, and brushpiles that provide cover.

**Water.** Wildlife requires reliable water access. Where natural water is limited, small ponds, seeps, or water sources maintained for wildlife support significantly higher game populations. The division's aquaculture ponds serve a secondary wildlife watering function.

**Food plots.** Small planted plots — clover, brassicas, winter wheat, or corn — provide concentrated nutrition for deer and turkey through periods of natural food scarcity. A quarter-acre food plot at a forest edge can significantly concentrate deer movement in predictable locations. The division coordinates with Agriculture for seed and planting support.

**Harvest pressure management.** The division should rotate hunting pressure across the community's land, avoiding repeated high-pressure hunting of the same locations. Consistent overhunting of specific areas displaces wildlife and degrades habitat; distributed, moderate pressure leaves the population healthy and the animals less wary.

**Population monitoring.** The division should maintain a running assessment of game populations through trail cameras, sign observation, and harvest data. A population that is declining requires reduced harvest; a population that is growing supports increased harvest. State wildlife agencies are a resource for regional population data.

## Equipment Management

The division's equipment is divided between firearms and ammunition, trapping gear, fishing equipment, and processing tools. Each category requires distinct maintenance and management.

**Firearms.** The division maintains a community inventory of hunting firearms — rifles, shotguns, and bows — along with the cleaning equipment and spare parts needed to maintain them. Firearms must be stored securely when not in use: locked storage that prevents unauthorized access, particularly by children. The division coordinates with the Watch Division on safe firearms storage standards.

**Ammunition.** The division maintains a standing ammunition stockpile and should develop reloading competency as a supply-chain-independent alternative to commercial ammunition. Reloading requires a press, dies, primers, powder, and projectiles — with the exception of primers and powder, all components can be produced or salvaged locally. The division should maintain reloading equipment and a supply of primers and powder as a resilience measure.

**Traps.** The division maintains an inventory of body-grip traps, foothold traps, and snare materials in a range of sizes. Traps require periodic cleaning — rust and human scent reduce effectiveness — and replacement of worn components. The division should maintain enough trap inventory to run meaningful trap lines without being depleted by normal attrition.

**Fishing equipment.** Rods, reels, hooks, line, and trotline materials require ongoing maintenance and restocking. The division should hold a significant supply of hooks and line — the consumable components — and should develop the knowledge to produce improvised hooks and cordage from local materials when commercial supply is unavailable. Hand-line fishing requires no reel at all; the division should ensure members can fish effectively with minimal equipment.

**Processing equipment.** Sharp knives are the most important processing tool — a dull knife is dangerous and produces poor results. The division should hold skinning knives, fillet knives, a meat saw or reciprocating saw for larger carcasses, a cutting board, and a game hoist for hanging deer and large game during processing. Knife sharpening competency should be universal among division members.

## Safety and Legal Compliance

Wild harvest is a domain where safety failures and legal violations both carry serious consequences. The division holds a higher obligation to safety culture than most divisions because the tools involved — firearms, traps, knives — are hazardous, and because legal violations can result in the loss of licenses that the entire community depends on.

**Firearms safety.** The four rules of firearms safety are non-negotiable: treat every firearm as loaded; never point at anything you are not willing to destroy; keep your finger off the trigger until ready to fire; know your target and what is beyond it. No member participates in division hunting operations without demonstrated competency in safe firearms handling. Hunter education certification — required for first-time hunters in every state — should be completed by every hunting member and is the baseline, not the ceiling.

**Trap safety.** Set traps are hazards to children, pets, and non-target animals. The division should post trap line locations with the Watch Division and any affected households, and should clearly mark trap locations. No member should set traps near community infrastructure, paths, or areas with significant foot traffic without explicit coordination.

**Zoonotic disease awareness.** Wild animals carry diseases that can infect humans. Chronic wasting disease (CWD) in deer — present and spreading in many US states — is a prion disease with no known cure; the CDC recommends against consuming meat from CWD-positive animals. Tularemia is transmissible from rabbits through skin contact; gloves should be worn when handling rabbits. Trichinosis is a risk in bear and feral hog meat and requires thorough cooking. The division should work with the Healthcare Division to establish handling and consumption guidelines for the disease risks present in its region.

**Legal compliance.** Hunting, trapping, and fishing are among the most thoroughly regulated food production activities. Licenses, seasons, bag limits, legal methods, and size restrictions are all defined in state regulation and change annually. The division should designate a member responsible for tracking regulatory requirements and communicating them to all members before each season. Violations carry fines, license revocation, and in serious cases criminal penalties. A community that loses its hunting and fishing licenses has lost a food source — regulatory compliance is not a bureaucratic inconvenience but a protection of the community's long-term capacity.

## Skills and Teaching

Wild harvest skills are less common than they were a generation ago and are not evenly distributed. The division's teaching function ensures that competency does not concentrate in a few experienced members while the rest of the membership is dependent on them.

**Hunter education.** Every member who hunts should complete state hunter education certification. The course covers firearms safety, hunting ethics, wildlife identification, and legal requirements. It is the floor of competency, not the ceiling.

**Mentored hunting and fishing.** Experienced hunters bringing less experienced members on actual hunts — not instruction days, but real hunts — is the most effective way to transmit practical skill. Reading deer sign, making shot selection decisions, field dressing an animal in the field: these are learned through experience alongside a more skilled person, not in a classroom.

**Processing as a community skill.** Butchering and fish processing should not be limited to division members. The division should offer regular processing sessions — particularly after successful deer seasons — that are open to the broader membership. Processing a deer is a practical skill that benefits anyone in the community who hunts or receives wild game.

**Trap building.** The division should teach members to build simple snares and fish traps from locally available materials — wire, cord, wood. Commercial traps are more reliable, but the knowledge to produce functional traps without them is the supply-chain-independent baseline.

## Division Relationships

**Food Security**
The primary downstream relationship. Wild game and fish are delivered to Food Security for distribution, preservation, and community kitchen use. The two divisions must maintain clear coordination on delivery timing, quantities, and form — Food Security needs to know what is coming and when so it can plan processing and distribution. The division should deliver butchered cuts, not whole carcasses where possible, so Food Security's processing load is manageable.

**Leatherwork**
All hides from harvested deer, hogs, and large game, and pelts from trapped furbearers, go to the Leatherwork Division. The division should deliver hides salted or chilled promptly after butchering. The Leatherwork Division should communicate which fur species are most useful so trap line priorities can be adjusted accordingly.

**Forestry**
Forested land is the primary hunting habitat. The Forestry Division's harvest and management decisions affect game populations — timber harvest creates edge habitat and browse; dense closed canopy suppresses it. The two divisions should coordinate on habitat outcomes in the forest management program. The Forestry Division's knowledge of local species and vegetation is directly relevant to the Wild Harvest Division's scouting and habitat work.

**Agriculture**
Agricultural land edges are productive wildlife habitat, and the two divisions share an interest in their management. Food plots require seed and planting support from Agriculture. Feral hog management is a shared concern — hogs that damage crops are both an Agricultural problem and a harvest opportunity. The two divisions should coordinate on land use at their shared margin.

**Healthcare**
Zoonotic disease guidelines — handling protocols for CWD-endemic areas, tularemia precautions, safe internal temperatures for game cooking — should be developed in coordination with Healthcare and distributed to all members who handle wild game. The division should also coordinate with Healthcare on any unusual animal behavior or mortality that could indicate disease in the local wildlife population.

**Watch**
Secure firearms storage standards and the location of active trap lines are both Watch Division concerns. The division should coordinate with Watch on storage protocols and should communicate trap line locations to avoid hazards for members in the field.

**Machining and Metallurgy**
The division's knives, traps, and fishing hardware require maintenance and occasional fabrication. Machining and Metallurgy can produce hooks, trap components, and replacement knife blades from available metal. The division should communicate its hardware needs and work with Machining and Metallurgy on component production as commercial supply becomes less reliable.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions, the division maintains active hunting, trapping, and fishing programs across all legal seasons, manages the community fish pond as a standing production operation, and develops skill across the membership through mentored hunting and processing events. Wild harvest supplements the community's food supply without being depended upon as the primary source.

As conditions deteriorate and commercial food supply becomes less reliable, the division's output becomes load-bearing rather than supplementary. Harvest volume should increase to meet the community's protein needs; the pond should be managed at maximum sustainable production; and trap lines should be expanded. The division's equipment maintenance, ammunition supply, and skills distribution become critical — equipment failures and skill gaps that were tolerable when wild harvest was supplementary are not tolerable when it is essential.

The division should never be in a position where its first deer season after a disruption is also its first serious deer season. The equipment, skills, and habitat relationships must exist before they are urgently needed.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete licensing and legal compliance baseline**
Before any harvest, the division must ensure that all participating members hold the appropriate licenses and have completed required training. Inventory what licenses are needed for the species and methods the division intends to use and ensure they are obtained before seasons open.

**2. Assess local wildlife and water resources**
Scout the community's land and surrounding area for game sign, productive water bodies, and habitat quality. Identify the primary species the division will target, the locations most likely to produce harvest, and the seasonal calendar for each species.

**3. Establish equipment inventory**
Conduct a full inventory of firearms, traps, and fishing gear available within the membership. Identify gaps against the division's planned program and address them. Establish secure firearms storage. Confirm that all firearms in the inventory are functional and maintained.

**4. Establish the fish pond**
If community land supports a pond, begin the permitting, earthwork, and stocking process early — a new pond takes a season or more to establish productive populations. If a pond already exists, assess its current population and condition.

**5. Run the first full season**
Complete a full annual harvest cycle — hunting season, trap line season, and a full fishing season — tracking harvest volume, methods, and outcomes. The first full season produces the data the division needs to plan the second one.

**6. Build processing competency broadly**
Offer community butchering and fish processing sessions open beyond the division. Every member who might one day need to field dress a deer or fillet a fish benefits from learning it before the need is acute.

## Division Documents

**Harvest Log** — a running record of all wild harvest: date, species, location, method, member, and yield in pounds delivered to Food Security. Accumulated across seasons to track total production, identify productive locations and methods, and confirm harvest levels against sustainable yield estimates.

**Equipment Inventory** — a current record of all division firearms, traps, fishing equipment, and processing tools: condition, location, and maintenance status. Includes ammunition stockpile quantities by caliber.

**Seasonal Calendar** — the division's annual planning document: season dates for all target species, license renewal dates, trap line setup and takedown schedule, pond management tasks, and food plot planting calendar. Published each year before the first season opens.

**Wildlife and Habitat Assessment** — a current picture of the community's game populations, productive water bodies, and habitat condition. Updated annually from scouting, trail camera data, and harvest results. Includes the division's sustainable harvest estimate for primary species.

**Legal Compliance Reference** — the current year's summary of applicable state hunting, trapping, and fishing regulations: seasons, bag limits, legal methods, license requirements, and any special restrictions on community land or local water bodies. Updated each year when state regulations are published.
