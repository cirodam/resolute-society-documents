# Masonry Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Masonry Division exists to build and maintain the community's stone, brick, concrete, and earthen work — ensuring that foundations are sound, cisterns hold water, chimneys draw safely, and the community holds the knowledge and material capacity to do masonry work without dependence on commercial supply chains. It works in close coordination with the Construction Division, whose carpentry and wood-frame work sits on the foundations the Masonry Division provides, and with Fire, Water Security, Food Security, and Agriculture, each of whose work intersects with masonry structures.

Masonry is among the most durable and locally-sourceable building capacity a community can hold. Stone, clay, and lime are available in or near most landscapes. Masonry structures built well outlast the people who built them. A community that knows how to work with these materials has a resilience asset that does not depend on supply chains — but that asset only exists if the knowledge is held, the materials are sourced, and the skills are practiced before they are needed.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That the community's masonry structures — foundations, chimneys, cisterns, retaining walls, and earthen buildings — are maintained in safe and functional condition, and that problems are identified and addressed before they become failures
- That the community can build new masonry work — foundations for new construction, cisterns for water storage, chimneys for heating and cooking, ovens, retaining walls, and earthen structures — when members need them
- That the community holds working knowledge of lime production, earthen masonry, and stone work sufficient to perform essential masonry functions without commercial cement or manufactured materials
- That masonry knowledge is distributed broadly enough across the membership that the division is not the only repository of masonry skill

## Core Functions

- **Masonry Assessment** — maintaining a current picture of the condition of all community masonry: foundations, chimneys, cisterns, retaining walls, and earthen structures
- **Repair** — repointing mortar joints, crack repair, chimney repair, patching, and other masonry maintenance
- **New Masonry Work** — foundations for Construction Division projects, cisterns, chimneys and fireplaces, retaining walls, ovens and kilns
- **Lime Production** — producing lime from locally available limestone as the community's supply-chain-independent mortar and plaster binder
- **Earthen Building** — developing and maintaining competency in cob, adobe, and rammed earth construction using locally sourced clay, sand, and fiber
- **Material Knowledge** — identifying and characterizing local stone, clay, and aggregate sources; understanding what the local material base can provide
- **Tool Management** — maintaining the masonry tool set: trowels, jointing tools, levels, chisels, hammers, forms, mixing equipment
- **Skills and Teaching** — distributing masonry knowledge across the membership

## Masonry Assessment

Masonry structures fail differently than wood-frame structures. The failure modes are well-defined and readable when you know what to look for: mortar joint erosion, crack patterns that indicate settlement or thermal movement, spalling from freeze-thaw cycling, moisture infiltration, and chimney crown deterioration. The division maintains a current picture of all community masonry against these failure modes.

**The assessment cycle.** All masonry structures — foundations, chimneys, cisterns, retaining walls, and earthen buildings — are assessed on an annual cycle. Chimneys and fireplaces are assessed before heating season each year, since chimney failure during use creates immediate fire risk. The assessment looks at:

- **Foundations**: crack patterns (distinguishing uniform settlement from differential settlement and from structural overload), moisture staining indicating drainage problems, efflorescence indicating water infiltration through the masonry
- **Chimneys**: crown condition, cap presence and condition, mortar joint erosion, spalling, any visible separation from the structure it penetrates, interior flue condition where accessible
- **Cisterns and water storage**: crack integrity, water tightness, condition of any inlet and outlet penetrations, interior surface condition
- **Retaining walls**: plumb and alignment, crack patterns, drainage (a retaining wall without drainage behind it is carrying hydrostatic load it was not designed for), base erosion
- **Earthen structures**: erosion at the base and top of walls, crack patterns indicating settling or shrinkage, condition of any protective plaster or finish, and moisture damage at the wall-roof interface

**Crack reading.** Not all cracks indicate structural problems, but all cracks should be read. Hairline cracks in mortar joints are normal thermal movement. Stair-step cracking following mortar joints indicates settlement — the pattern and direction tell the division where the settlement is occurring. Horizontal cracking in a retaining wall indicates lateral earth pressure exceeding design capacity. Vertical cracking in a chimney above the roofline indicates differential thermal movement. The division should hold working knowledge of crack reading and apply it systematically rather than noting cracks without interpreting them.

**Member-reported concerns.** Members who notice masonry problems — new cracks, a chimney that smokes back into the house, a cistern that seems to be losing water, a retaining wall that has visibly moved — should report them promptly. Many masonry problems are most economically addressed when caught early; deferred masonry repair is consistently more expensive than timely repair.

## Repair

Masonry repair is technically distinct from replacement. The division's repair function preserves existing masonry rather than demolishing and rebuilding wherever preservation is sound.

**Repointing** — replacing deteriorated mortar in existing masonry joints — is the most common masonry repair. Mortar joints are the designed sacrifice element of masonry: softer than the masonry units, they erode first, and their erosion is what should be repaired rather than the units themselves. A repointing mortar must be matched to the existing masonry: lime-based mortar for stone and historic brick, Portland-based mortar for modern concrete block. A mortar that is harder than the masonry it fills will transfer stress to the units, causing spalling; this is the most common error in repointing work. The division must hold working knowledge of mortar selection and mixing.

**Chimney repair** requires specific attention because chimney failure has direct fire safety consequences. Deteriorated chimney crowns, missing caps, eroded mortar joints, and cracked flue liners all create pathways for combustion gases and heat to reach combustible structure. The Fire Division should review chimney repair work before the chimney returns to service; the two divisions should maintain a standing coordination relationship for this.

**Crack repair.** Repairing a crack without understanding its cause produces a crack that returns. The division addresses the cause — drainage problem, settlement source, inadequate support — before patching the crack. Where a crack indicates ongoing structural movement, the division monitors it over time before repairing, to confirm movement has stopped.

**Earthen repair.** Earthen walls erode at their bases from splash-back and at their crowns from rain. Protective earthen plaster is the first line of defense; maintaining plaster is easier than repairing the wall beneath it. The division should establish regular plaster maintenance as a standing function for any earthen structures the community holds, rather than allowing plaster to fail and then addressing the wall erosion that follows.

## New Masonry Work

The division builds new masonry when the community needs it — primarily foundations for Construction Division projects, cisterns, chimneys, retaining walls, and ovens. Each type has distinct technical requirements.

**Foundations.** Masonry and concrete foundations are the division's primary coordination point with the Construction Division. The division lays the foundation; Construction takes over at the sill plate or above-grade threshold. This handoff must be clearly understood by both divisions and confirmed at the start of every joint project. The foundation must be:

- Set below the local frost depth, to prevent frost heave
- On soil with adequate bearing capacity, confirmed by the site assessment the Construction Division performs
- Properly drained, since masonry foundations that sit in water deteriorate — drainage at the footing is the division's responsibility to specify and install
- Dimensionally accurate and level, since the wood-frame structure that Construction builds above it is only as good as the plane it sits on

The division should hold working knowledge of both poured concrete foundations and mortared stone foundations. Stone foundations are supply-chain-independent and, where good stone is available, often the more durable choice. Poured concrete is faster and dimensionally precise but requires Portland cement, which is commercially produced. The material choice should be made in light of what is available locally and the community's resilience priorities.

**Chimneys and fireplaces.** Chimneys must be sized to the appliance they serve, built with an appropriate flue liner (clay tile or equivalent), and finished with a crown and cap that shed water and exclude wildlife. The division should understand the relationship between firebox size, flue area, and draw — an undersized flue relative to the firebox produces a smoky fireplace; the geometry is well-established and not guesswork. All chimney work is reviewed by the Fire Division before being put into service.

**Cisterns and water storage.** A masonry cistern must be waterproof, which requires either an inherently waterproof lime or Portland mortar mix or an applied waterproof render on the interior surface. The Water Security Division should be involved in cistern design — inlet and outlet placement, overflow, and cover requirements are Water Security's domain; the division provides the structure.

**Retaining walls.** Retaining walls carry lateral earth load, which is frequently underestimated. A dry-laid stone retaining wall relies on mass, batter (the backward lean into the slope), and the quality of its stone selection and placement. A mortared retaining wall must have drainage behind it — without drainage, water pressure builds and eventually topples the wall. The division should not build retaining walls without understanding the drainage requirement and confirming it is addressed.

**Ovens and kilns.** Brick or stone ovens for bread and cooking, and kilns for pottery or lime burning, are specialized masonry structures with distinct thermal requirements. The refractory materials that go in the firebox and flue — firebrick, high-alumina mortar — are different from structural brick and mortar. The division should develop competency in oven construction both for the Food Security Division's practical needs and because the kiln-building skill is directly transferable to lime burning.

## Lime Production

Lime is the division's most important supply-chain-independent material. Portland cement — the standard binder for modern mortar and concrete — is manufactured industrially and is not producible at community scale. Lime is. Limestone is heated to approximately 900°C in a kiln, driving off carbon dioxide to produce quicklime (calcium oxide). Quicklime is then slaked with water to produce hydrated lime or lime putty, which is the binder for lime mortar and lime plaster.

The process requires:
- **Limestone** — the raw material; the division must identify local limestone sources and assess their suitability
- **A kiln** — a structure capable of sustaining approximately 900°C for the hours required to calcine the stone; can be built from stone and refractory clay
- **Fuel** — wood in sufficient quantity; lime burning is fuel-intensive and the fuel requirement should be calculated before the first burn, not discovered during it
- **Slaking** — quicklime is mixed with water in a controlled process; the reaction is strongly exothermic and dangerous if done carelessly

Lime mortar is slower to cure than Portland mortar and has lower compressive strength, but it is more flexible, self-healing (lime mortars can recrystallize across fine cracks), breathable (which matters for stone and historic masonry), and reproducible from local materials. A community that can produce lime can produce mortar, plaster, limewash, and agricultural lime indefinitely from local stone.

**Lime putty.** Lime slaked with an excess of water and allowed to mature produces lime putty — a smoother, more workable binder than bagged hydrated lime. Putty matured for months is noticeably superior to freshly slaked lime. The division should maintain a standing stock of maturing putty so it is available when needed rather than produced on demand.

The division should complete at least one lime burn before lime becomes a necessity. The process has enough technical nuance — kiln design, temperature management, slaking procedure — that the first burn is a learning experience. The division should not attempt lime production for the first time under pressure.

## Earthen Building

Earthen building — cob, adobe, and rammed earth — uses locally available clay-rich soil as the primary structural material. These methods are supply-chain-independent, thermally massive, and produce excellent buildings in appropriate climates. The division develops and maintains earthen building competency as a standing capability.

**Material assessment and preparation.** Good earthen building requires clay-rich soil mixed with sand in the right proportions, with fiber (straw or similar) added for tensile reinforcement. Raw soil from most sites is not ready to use without assessment and adjustment. The division must hold working knowledge of:

- **The squeeze test and ribbon test** — simple field tests for clay content in a soil sample
- **The jar test** — settling soil in water to read the proportions of clay, silt, sand, and gravel
- **Elutriation** — using water and controlled settling time to separate soil fractions; clay particles stay suspended longest and can be decanted off as clay slip, while sand and gravel settle quickly and remain. By adjusting flow rate or settling time, specific grain-size fractions can be isolated for different uses. This technique requires only containers, tubing, and a water source — it turns locally available soil into the separated clay and graded sand that earthen construction requires. The Water Security Division uses the same technique to grade sand for slow sand filtration; the two divisions share this knowledge base.

The proportions for good cob and adobe are similar: roughly 15–25% clay content in the final mix, with enough sand to prevent excessive shrinkage cracking and enough fiber to provide tensile reinforcement. Getting this proportion right takes local testing — proportions from reference texts are starting points, not answers. The division should not assume that any reference proportion applies to local soil without testing.

**Cob.** Cob is monolithic earthen construction — the mix is built up in courses and worked while wet, producing walls without joints. It is highly sculptable, requires no forms, and is appropriate in any climate where the walls can be protected from rain erosion. Cob walls are strong in compression and weak in tension — they carry vertical load well but are vulnerable to lateral forces and to tension that develops during seismic events. The same structural logic that governs other masonry applies: cob must be adequate in cross-section for the loads it carries and must be protected from water at the base and top.

**Adobe.** Adobe uses the same mix as cob but formed into bricks that are sun-dried before use. It allows faster wall construction than monolithic cob and permits quality control at the brick stage. Adobe requires extended dry weather during both production and construction, making it most appropriate for arid and semi-arid climates. The division should assess whether local climate allows adequate adobe production before committing to it as a primary method.

**Rammed earth.** Rammed earth uses slightly damp soil compacted in forms to produce dense, stable walls. It requires formwork, a ramming tool, and more physical labor per unit volume than cob. It produces walls of excellent durability and thermal mass. Rammed earth is appropriate where form-building capacity and labor are available.

**The earthen building principle.** Develop competency through actual building, not study alone. A division member who has built a cob structure understands cob in its material behavior and real limitations. One who has only read about it does not. The division should complete at least one earthen building project — even a small storage shed or oven surround — before earthen methods become the primary option under pressure.

## Material Knowledge

The division maintains working knowledge of what masonry materials exist in or near the community's landscape and what those materials can be used for.

**Local stone.** Different stone types have different structural and workable properties. Granite and other igneous stones are extremely hard, durable, and difficult to dress. Sandstone is softer and easier to work but more porous and frost-sensitive. Limestone is the most versatile — workable, structurally sound, and the source material for lime production. Slate splits predictably into flat sheets useful for roofing and paving. The division should survey what stone is locally available, in what quantities and at what extraction difficulty, and what each type is suitable for.

**Clay.** Good clay is necessary for earthen building, brick production, refractory mortar for kilns and ovens, and cob. Clay quality varies significantly by source. The division should identify local clay sources and characterize their clay content, shrinkage behavior, and fired strength where that matters.

**Sand and aggregate.** Mortar, concrete, and earthen mixes all require graded aggregate. The particle size distribution matters — a mortar sand and a concrete aggregate have different requirements. The division should identify local sand and aggregate sources and understand what each is suitable for.

**Brick.** Where local clay is suitable and fuel is available, the division may produce fired brick. Brick production requires significantly more fuel and temperature control than earthen building — the kiln must reach and sustain 900–1100°C. Brick production is a secondary capability; the division should not plan on it as a primary material source unless it has successfully completed at least one burn.

## Tool Management

Masonry tools are distinct from carpentry tools and should be managed as a separate inventory, coordinated with the Construction Division's tool library.

The essential masonry tool set includes: trowels (pointing, margin, and brick), cold chisels and bolsters for splitting and dressing stone, club hammers, jointing tools, a brick hammer, a line and pins for course alignment, a spirit level, a plumb bob, mixing hoes and a mortar trough or mixing equipment, and forms for concrete and adobe work.

Masonry tools are robust but require maintenance. Trowels cleaned and stored properly last decades; trowels left with hardened mortar are permanently degraded. The division establishes cleaning and storage expectations for all masonry tools and enforces them at the point of return.

## Skills and Teaching

Masonry skill is rarer in most communities than carpentry skill, and the supply chain dependence of modern construction — Portland cement, factory brick, commercial stone — means most members have no experience with the material-independent methods the division prioritizes.

**Household baseline.** The masonry knowledge every household should hold: how to recognize eroding chimney mortar joints and understand why this matters, how to apply a basic lime plaster patch, how to read simple crack patterns in masonry. This is not construction skill — it is recognition and early-reporting skill, and it is valuable enough to teach broadly.

**Division skill distribution.** Lime production, earthen building, stone masonry, chimney construction, and concrete foundation work should each be held by enough division members that no single departure creates a gap. These are not identical skills; the division should map its skill coverage explicitly and identify gaps before they become problems.

**The apprenticeship model.** Masonry skill is best transmitted through supervised work on real projects. Division projects are teaching opportunities; the division pairs members who are developing skill with those who hold it, rather than sorting members by skill level and assigning each to separate work.

## Division Relationships

**Construction**
The primary working relationship. The division provides masonry and concrete foundations for all Construction Division projects. The two divisions must maintain clear coordination on the foundation-to-structure handoff, on site drainage, and on any structural masonry that is part of a building Construction is otherwise framing. They should share assessment records for structures that have both masonry and wood-frame elements, and coordinate tool inventory where practical.

**Fire**
All chimney and fireplace work is coordinated with the Fire Division. The Fire Division reviews chimney work before it is put into service. The division should understand the fire safety implications of chimney design — adequate flue sizing, proper clearances, cap and crown requirements — well enough to build correctly without relying on Fire Division review to catch errors after the fact.

**Water Security**
Cisterns and water storage tanks are joint projects — the division provides the structure, Water Security specifies inlet, outlet, overflow, and treatment requirements. The two divisions share knowledge of elutriation and sand and aggregate grading, which applies to both masonry material preparation and slow sand filtration construction.

**Food Security**
Bread ovens, root cellars, and food storage structures with masonry or earthen construction are built and maintained by the division in coordination with Food Security's specifications. A bread oven that does not hold heat correctly is a masonry problem; a bread oven that is the wrong size for the community's baking volume is a Food Security planning problem. The two divisions should design these structures collaboratively.

**Agriculture**
Retaining walls, terracing, drainage channels, and hardscaping that supports the agricultural land base are division work. Agriculture's swale and berm earthworks and the division's retaining wall and drainage work often address the same landscape problems and should be designed in alignment rather than in parallel.

**Energy**
Thermal mass — the ability of masonry and earthen walls to absorb, store, and release heat — is directly relevant to the Energy Division's passive heating and cooling strategy. Rocket mass heaters (a high-efficiency combustion system that stores heat in a masonry thermal mass bench) sit at the intersection of both divisions' work. The division should understand thermal mass design well enough to build structures that perform as the Energy Division intends.

**Forestry**
Lime burning is fuel-intensive — the Forestry Division supplies the firewood for kiln operations as a standing function. If the community develops charcoal production, that is also a Forestry function, and charcoal is the preferred fuel for sustained high-temperature kiln work. The two divisions should coordinate on fuel supply for any planned lime burn well in advance, since a burn requires more wood than a daily heating fire by a significant margin.

**Education**
Lime production, earthen building, and stone masonry are knowledge the community cannot afford to hold in only one or two people. The Education Division's Transmission principle — that knowledge held in one person is one generation from being lost — applies directly here. The two divisions should coordinate on identifying and developing masonry apprentices, particularly for the technically demanding skills: lime burning, foundation work, and chimney construction.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions, the division focuses on the annual assessment cycle, mortar joint and chimney maintenance, skill development, material sourcing, and maintaining active lime production capacity. New masonry work proceeds as other divisions identify needs.

As conditions deteriorate and commercial cement becomes unavailable or expensive, lime mortar replaces Portland mortar as the primary binder. This transition should not be a shock — the division maintains active lime production and lime mortar competency in stable conditions specifically so that the shift is seamless. Similarly, earthen building methods and locally quarried stone are available as primary construction materials when commercial alternatives are not, but only if the skills and material sources have been developed and confirmed in advance.

The division should never encounter a condition where its essential functions — providing mortar, foundations, cisterns, and chimneys — are constrained by skills it has not developed. The time to develop lime production is not when Portland cement is unavailable. The time to develop earthen building is not when lumber is scarce. These capacities must exist before they are needed.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Material survey**
Before the division can plan its work, it needs a clear picture of what masonry materials exist locally: stone type and availability, clay quality and location, limestone for lime production, and sand and aggregate sources. This survey drives all subsequent material planning and is the foundation of the division's supply-chain-independent strategy.

**2. Chimney and foundation assessment**
These are the highest-consequence masonry elements in the community. Chimney failure creates immediate fire risk; foundation failure is the most expensive structural problem a community faces. The division should assess every chimney and visible foundation before beginning any new construction work.

**3. Establish lime production**
Complete at least one lime burn from local limestone before lime becomes a necessity. Document the process, the fuel requirements, the kiln design, and the quality of the resulting lime. Maintain a stock of maturing lime putty as a standing practice from that point forward.

**4. Complete one earthen building project**
Build a small cob or adobe structure — a storage shed, a small community space, an oven surround — that takes the division through the full process from soil testing to finished wall. This is the minimum competency confirmation for earthen methods; the project scale matters less than its completion.

**5. Launch household baseline instruction**
Teach community members to recognize chimney mortar erosion, read basic crack patterns, and apply simple plaster repair. This creates a distributed early-warning system for masonry problems and reduces the repair load from small problems that were caught late.

## Division Documents

**Masonry Condition Register** — a current record of all community masonry structures: last assessment date, findings by category (immediate action required / action within timeline / monitor), corrective actions assigned and status. Maintained in coordination with the Construction Division's Structural Condition Register; structures with both masonry and wood-frame elements have entries in both.

**Material Source Record** — documentation of local stone, clay, limestone, and aggregate sources: location, character, quantity estimate, quality assessment, and appropriate uses. The foundation of the division's supply-chain-independent material planning.

**Lime Production Record** — for each lime burn: limestone source, kiln design used, fuel quantity, temperature management, yield, and quality assessment of the resulting lime. Accumulated across burns to refine the process and establish reliable procedures.

**Masonry Project Record** — for every completed masonry project: location, scope, materials and mix designs used, as-built conditions. Maintained permanently as part of the community's built environment record, coordinated with the Construction Division's Construction Project Record.

**Earthen Building Guide** — an instructional document covering soil testing, mix design, and construction technique for cob, adobe, and rammed earth. Based on actual local soil conditions rather than generic reference material — the mix proportions and soil sources should reflect what has been tested and confirmed locally.
