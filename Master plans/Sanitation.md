# Sanitation Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Sanitation Division exists to ensure that every member of the Resolute Society has access to safe, dignified sanitation — today and under whatever conditions tomorrow brings. It does this by maintaining the knowledge and infrastructure to manage human waste safely at both household and community scale, independent of municipal infrastructure.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around sanitation — an instrument of collective action, not a provider of services. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by what the community can do.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for three things:

- That no member is without a functioning sanitation solution due to infrastructure failure
- That human waste is managed in ways that do not contaminate water sources or create disease vectors
- That sanitation knowledge — household planning, waste treatment, community-scale solutions — is actively developed and held broadly enough across the community that it cannot be lost

## Core Functions

- **Household Sanitation** — ensuring every household has functioning sanitation or a concrete plan for when municipal systems fail
- **Community Sanitation** — maintaining latrine and sanitation facilities for situations where household sanitation is unavailable
- **Waste Treatment** — managing, composting, and safely disposing of human waste at household and community scale
- **Household Sanitation Planning** — building household-level sanitation knowledge and planning capacity across the community

## Household Sanitation

The division ensures that every member household has a functioning sanitation solution. Most households have never considered what they would do if their toilet stopped working — and most people do not realize how quickly that scenario can arise when municipal systems fail.

**Municipal sewage infrastructure is not uniformly resilient.** Many sewage systems rely on electric pumping stations to move waste uphill toward treatment facilities. When power fails, these stations stop, and sewage backs up — sometimes into homes. Households that are gravity-fed to the treatment plant may continue to function normally; households served by pump-dependent lines may not. Every member should know which situation their home is in. Septic systems, being entirely gravity-fed, continue to function without power and represent a more resilient baseline.

**Toilets can be flushed without running water.** Pouring water directly into the toilet bowl — not the tank — activates a flush using gravity alone. A standard toilet requires 1.6 to 3.5 gallons per flush, older models up to 7 gallons. This extends household toilet function as long as water is available. However, if the sewage system itself is down or backing up, continued toilet use risks sewage entering the home — at that point, alternative sanitation is necessary.

**Every household needs an alternative sanitation plan** for when the toilet cannot be used. The most practical and accessible option is a bucket toilet: a 5-gallon bucket fitted with a toilet seat lid, lined with a bag, with sawdust, wood ash, or cat litter added after each use to absorb moisture, control odor, and reduce pathogens. This is a low-cost, immediately deployable solution that any household can maintain. The division should ensure every member household has the materials to implement this.

**Siting of any outdoor sanitation** — a latrine, a bucket waste disposal area, or a composting operation — must follow minimum distance rules that most people do not know. Human waste must be located a minimum of 100 feet from any water source (wells, streams, ponds, rainwater collection) and downhill and downwind from both water sources and dwellings. Areas that flood or have high water tables must be avoided — waste will leach directly into groundwater. Siting violations are the primary mechanism by which sanitation contaminates water supplies.

**Flies are a disease vector, not just a nuisance.** The fecal-oral disease transmission chain — feces to fingers to flies to food to fluids — makes clear why covering waste and controlling fly access to sanitation facilities matters. Sprinkling lime (calcium hydroxide) or wood ash in a latrine or bucket toilet after each use raises pH, kills pathogens, controls odor, and deters flies. This is a traditional practice with real public health value.

**Handwashing** is the single most important behavior in breaking fecal-oral disease transmission. It requires soap, water, and at least 20 seconds of scrubbing. Wood ash can substitute for soap — it raises the pH of water sufficiently to kill pathogens. A tippy tap — a simple hands-free handwashing station made from a water jug suspended on a stick — keeps the water container clean during use and can be built from scrap materials. The division should teach tippy tap construction as part of its household sanitation program.

A two-jug field sink extends this further for general washing. An elevated jug with a spigot or tilt valve serves as the water source; a second jug below captures the greywater for appropriate disposal. This setup requires no plumbing, uses widely available food-grade plastic jugs, conserves water since flow is controlled manually, and keeps greywater contained rather than running on the ground. It is practical for handwashing, dish washing, and food preparation — anywhere a functional sink is needed without infrastructure.

**Soap production** is a community sanitation skill that becomes essential when commercial supply is disrupted. Soap is not a complex product — it is the result of saponification, a chemical reaction between fat and lye (alkali). Every community that has ever existed has produced its own soap from locally available materials, and that knowledge is recoverable.

Lye can be produced directly from wood ash. Hardwood ash — oak, hickory, maple — produces stronger lye than softwood ash; the ash must be clean and free of coal, debris, or ash from painted or treated wood. Water is percolated slowly through a vessel packed with ash and collected at the bottom as lye water (a solution of primarily potassium carbonate and potassium hydroxide). The traditional test for adequate lye concentration is the egg float: a fresh egg floated in lye water should sit at the surface with roughly the area of a quarter above the waterline. If the egg sinks, the lye is too weak; if it floats higher, it may be too strong. Commercial sodium hydroxide (lye) is more reliable and stores well, making it a practical item to stockpile in quantity while available — it keeps indefinitely when sealed dry.

Fats for soapmaking include lard, tallow (rendered beef fat), and most vegetable oils. Raw animal fat must be rendered before use: cut into small pieces, melt slowly over low heat, strain out solids, and allow to cool and solidify. The fat-to-lye ratio determines whether the finished soap is balanced — too much lye produces caustic soap that damages skin; too little leaves unreacted fat and produces soft, rancid-smelling bars. Saponification values vary by fat type and should be calculated using a lye calculator when precision is possible. In the field, traditional ratios (approximately 3 pounds of fat to 10 ounces of sodium hydroxide for bar soap) provide a workable starting point.

Cold process soap — mixing lye solution with melted fat at around 100°F, stirring to trace (the point where the mixture thickens to the consistency of light custard), and pouring into molds — requires a cure time of four to six weeks before use. During curing, residual lye neutralizes and water evaporates; fresh soap is caustic and should not be used on skin. Hot process soap, cooked over heat until saponification completes, can be used within one to two weeks. Both methods produce functional soap from identical ingredients.

**Lye safety** must be understood before any production attempt. Sodium hydroxide and strong lye water are highly caustic and will cause serious chemical burns on contact with skin or eyes. Gloves and eye protection are mandatory. Lye must always be added to water — never water to lye — as the reverse can cause a violent, spattering exothermic reaction. Aluminum containers react with lye and must never be used; stainless steel, glass, or food-grade plastic are appropriate. The heat generated when lye dissolves in water is significant — the solution must cool before combining with fat.

The division should treat soap production as a community knowledge and production priority, not a household curiosity. A community that can produce its own soap from ash and rendered fat has closed a critical supply chain dependency. The division documents should include a soap production guide, and soap production should be part of the division's knowledge transmission program.

## Community Sanitation

The division maintains sanitation facilities for situations where household sanitation is unavailable — members who have lost access to their home facilities, community gatherings, and any scenario where a group of people needs shared sanitation infrastructure. Community sanitation requires more planning and management than household sanitation, and must be established before it is urgently needed.

**The pit latrine** is the foundation of community sanitation without infrastructure. A basic pit latrine is a hole 3-5 feet deep, 2-3 feet wide, and 3-4 feet long, covered by a slab with a small hole for use and a structure around it for privacy. The slab must cover the pit completely — fly access to the pit is the primary disease vector and must be prevented. A cover over the hole when not in use further reduces fly access.

**The Ventilated Improved Pit (VIP) latrine** is a straightforward upgrade over the basic pit that dramatically reduces odor and fly populations. A vent pipe — painted black to absorb heat — extends from the pit to above the roof of the structure. The heat differential creates a draft that draws odors and flies upward and out. Flies entering the dark latrine are attracted to the light at the top of the vent pipe and die there rather than returning to food preparation areas. VIP latrines should be the community standard where construction allows.

**Pit capacity** must be planned against community size. A pit fills at approximately 40-50 liters per person per year. For 50 people, that is roughly 2,000-2,500 liters annually. When a pit approaches capacity it must be closed — covered and sealed — and a new one opened. The division should maintain awareness of pit fill rates and plan new construction before existing facilities reach capacity. A twin-pit system — two pits alternating in use — allows one pit to rest and decompose while the other is active. After one to two years, the rested pit's contents are safe to handle and can be used as soil amendment.

**Siting rules apply identically to community latrines** as to household sanitation: minimum 100 feet from any water source, downhill and downwind from both water and dwellings, and away from flood-prone or high water table areas. Community latrines serve more people and concentrate waste — siting errors have proportionally larger consequences.

**A handwashing station must be adjacent to every latrine.** This is not optional. The latrine and the handwashing station are a single unit — one without the other is incomplete. A tippy tap (see Household Sanitation) is the appropriate low-infrastructure solution.

**Scale planning:** a minimum of one latrine per 20 people is the baseline standard for community sanitation in crisis conditions. For a community of 50, that is at minimum two to three latrines. Privacy and separation by gender should be maintained wherever possible — this is both a dignity and a safety issue.

**Menstrual hygiene** is frequently omitted from community sanitation planning and must not be. Community facilities must include private spaces with water access and appropriate disposal options. The division should ensure that reusable menstrual products — cups, cloth pads — are part of its supply planning, as disposable products may be unavailable in extended disruption.

## Waste Treatment

Human waste is not simply a disposal problem — properly managed, it is a resource. The division's waste treatment function closes the loop between sanitation and soil fertility, converting a public health hazard into a usable agricultural input. This connection between sanitation and agriculture is one of the oldest and most important in human history.

**The critical distinction is between blackwater and greywater.** Blackwater is toilet waste — urine and feces — and carries the pathogen load that makes sanitation a public health concern. Greywater is wastewater from sinks, showers, and laundry, which carries far lower pathogen levels and can be managed differently. Separating these two streams makes both easier to handle and significantly reduces the volume of waste requiring careful treatment.

**Greywater** can be reused directly for subsurface irrigation through simple mulch basins or subsurface drip systems, reducing demand on potable water. It should not be stored — greywater left standing begins to develop pathogen loads within 24 hours. It should not be applied to root crops or leafy greens where it will contact edible parts. Beyond those constraints, greywater reuse is low-risk and high-value.

**Composting human waste** — humanure — is the primary treatment method for blackwater at community scale without infrastructure. Human waste is high in nitrogen and requires carbon material — sawdust, wood chips, straw, or dry leaves — added in sufficient quantity to balance the pile and control odor. The composting pile must reach thermophilic temperatures of at least 131°F (55°C) and sustain that temperature for a minimum of three days to kill most pathogens. Ascaris eggs — roundworm — are the most temperature-resistant common pathogen and require the full thermal kill cycle to be neutralized.

**A two-bin composting system** keeps one bin active and one resting. The active bin receives fresh material; the resting bin finishes decomposition over one to two years. After that period, the finished compost is safe to handle and apply to crops. The division should not rush this timeline — patience is the primary safety mechanism in composting human waste.

**Dumpsters as collection and transport vessels** offer a practical alternative to pit-based waste management that significantly simplifies the agricultural loop. A standard large rolling dumpster, fitted with a toilet seat hole cut in the top and a simple privacy structure around it, functions as a community latrine with one critical advantage over a pit: the waste is mobile. When a dumpster reaches capacity, it can be transported directly to the composting operation, emptied, treated with lime, and returned to service. This eliminates the labor of exhuming a pit and turns waste collection into a discrete, manageable batch process. Dumpsters are widely available in most communities, often at low or no cost, making them a practical item to stockpile. A vent pipe can be added using the same VIP principle as a pit latrine to control odor and flies during use.

A critical planning constraint is weight, not volume. Human waste is mostly water and is heavy relative to its apparent volume — a community of 50 people produces 60-80 kg of waste per day before any carbon material is added. The limiting factor on how full a dumpster can get before it must be moved is the rolling capacity of the dumpster and whatever equipment is transporting it, not how full it appears. Smaller dumpsters emptied on a regular schedule are likely more practical than larger ones left to fill, as they remain movable with less specialized equipment.

**Lime treatment** is the simplest pathogen reduction method for waste that cannot be composted immediately. Adding calcium hydroxide (hydrated lime) to a pit or collection vessel raises pH to levels that kill most pathogens within hours. It does not eliminate all pathogens and is not a substitute for full composting, but it substantially reduces risk and controls odor and flies in the interim.

**The agricultural connection** should be explicit. Finished humanure compost is a legitimate soil amendment — high in nitrogen, phosphorus, and organic matter. The division should coordinate with the Agricultural Division on the use of treated waste as a community soil resource. This closes a nutrient cycle that industrial agriculture has broken, and it is one of the more consequential things a community can do for long-term soil fertility.

## Household Sanitation Planning

The division's household sanitation planning program exists to ensure that every member household has a working plan for sanitation before it is needed. As with water and food, the goal is knowledge and capacity held broadly across the community, organized by the division but owned by the households themselves. Instruction should be organized around knowledgeable community members teaching others.

**Every household needs a sanitation plan.** That plan should answer three questions: what is this household's current sanitation infrastructure and what are its failure points; what alternative sanitation will the household use if that infrastructure fails; and does the household have the materials to implement that alternative immediately. The division should assist members in developing these plans and maintain awareness of which households are prepared and which are not.

**Every household should pre-position alternative sanitation materials** before they are needed. At minimum: a 5-gallon bucket with a toilet seat lid, a supply of bags, and sawdust, wood ash, or cat litter for covering waste. These materials are inexpensive and take up little space. A household that has to locate these items during a crisis has waited too long.

**Members should understand their current infrastructure.** Many people — particularly renters — do not know whether their home is served by municipal sewage or a septic system, or whether their municipal sewage connection is gravity-fed or pump-dependent. The division's planning program should help every member answer these questions about their own home. A household on a septic system has a fundamentally different failure profile than one on a pump-dependent municipal line.

**Greywater management** should be part of every household plan. When municipal water and sewage are disrupted together, the volume of wastewater generated by a household drops significantly — but sinks, washing, and food preparation still generate greywater that must go somewhere. Simple household greywater management — directing it to a mulch basin or subsurface area away from water sources — prevents it from becoming a nuisance or health hazard.

**The 5-gallon bucket** is one of the most versatile pieces of community resilience equipment available — it appears across water storage, food storage, fermentation, rainwater collection, sanitation, and more. Its versatility is exactly what makes cross-contamination a genuine risk. The community should adopt and enforce a simple color coding standard: sanitation buckets are designated by a specific color and never repurposed for food or water use under any circumstances. This standard should be established early, communicated clearly, and treated as non-negotiable across all divisions that use buckets.

**The household sanitation plan should be written down**, not kept in someone's head. The division should provide a simple template that captures the household's infrastructure situation, alternative plan, and material inventory. Written plans survive personnel changes and can be reviewed and updated as circumstances change.

## Division Relationships

**Water Security**
Sanitation and water security are inseparable. Improper waste management is the primary mechanism by which water sources become contaminated. The two divisions must coordinate closely on the siting of all sanitation infrastructure relative to water sources, and the Sanitation Division should treat water source protection as a standing constraint on every decision it makes. The Water Security Division's testing program is also the Sanitation Division's early warning system — patterns of contamination in water sources may indicate sanitation failures that need to be identified and corrected.

**Agriculture**
The Sanitation Division is a producer of agricultural inputs. Finished humanure compost and treated waste are soil amendments that the Agricultural Division can use. The two divisions should coordinate on composting operations, timing, and the safe transfer of treated material. This relationship turns a waste management problem into a resource recovery program.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on household planning, education, and building the community's sanitation knowledge base. As conditions deteriorate and municipal infrastructure becomes unreliable, alternative sanitation and waste treatment become active operational priorities. The division does not change what it does — it scales what it does.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Support households in implementing alternative sanitation when municipal systems fail
- Operate and maintain community latrine facilities
- Manage waste collection, treatment, and transfer to composting operations
- Protect water sources from sanitation-related contamination

The division must be capable of sustaining all of these functions simultaneously and indefinitely. The materials, knowledge, and infrastructure to do so should be in place before they are urgently needed.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Map current sanitation infrastructure**
The division's first task is to understand the community's existing sanitation situation — which households are on septic systems, which are on gravity-fed municipal sewage, and which are on pump-dependent lines. This determines each household's failure profile and informs where the division's planning effort is most urgent.

**2. Build household preparedness**
Every household should have alternative sanitation materials pre-positioned and a written sanitation plan before a disruption occurs. The division should work through the membership systematically, prioritizing households most vulnerable to infrastructure failure.

**3. Establish waste treatment capacity**
The division should establish its composting operation and acquire the dumpsters and materials needed for collection and transport before community-scale sanitation is required. Composting infrastructure takes time to develop — it cannot be stood up in the middle of a crisis.

**4. Build community sanitation infrastructure**
Latrine sites should be identified, materials pre-positioned, and construction plans ready before they are needed. A community that has to design and build latrines during an active sanitation emergency is already behind.

**5. Operate community programs**
Household sanitation planning becomes an ongoing education and knowledge transmission program. The division maintains community sanitation facilities, manages the waste treatment cycle, and coordinates with Agriculture on finished compost.

## Division Documents

The Sanitation Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt.

**Household Sanitation Planning Guide** — an instructional guide covering household infrastructure assessment, alternative sanitation options, greywater management, handwashing, and bucket use standards. Designed to help any household develop and execute its own sanitation plan.

**Household Sanitation Plan Template** — a fill-in document each member household completes identifying its current infrastructure, alternative plan, pre-positioned materials, and siting considerations for outdoor sanitation.

**Community Latrine Construction Guide** — construction plans and operational protocols for pit latrines and VIP latrines at community scale, including siting rules, capacity planning, and maintenance.

**Waste Treatment and Composting Guide** — operational procedures for humanure composting, greywater management, lime treatment, dumpster collection and transport, and safe transfer of finished compost to agricultural use.
