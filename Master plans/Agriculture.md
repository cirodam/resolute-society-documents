# Agricultural Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Agricultural Division exists to build and sustain the community's capacity to produce food. It does this by supporting existing farmers, developing household growing capacity, maintaining the community's seed supply, coordinating agricultural labor, and building the soil fertility that all food production depends on. The division does not own or operate farms — it organizes the community around agriculture.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around food production — an instrument of collective action, not a farm operator. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by what the community can grow.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That the community maintains a current understanding of its agricultural capacity and potential
- That farmers and household growers have the support, seeds, and labor they need to produce food
- That soil fertility is actively built and maintained as a long-term community asset
- That agricultural knowledge — growing, seed saving, soil management — is actively recovered and held broadly enough across the community that it cannot be lost

## Core Functions

- **Assessment** — maintaining a current understanding of the community's agricultural landscape: existing farms, soil conditions, growing capacity, and potential
- **Seed Library** — maintaining and distributing seeds, preserving genetic diversity across locally adapted varieties
- **Household Agricultural Support** — building food growing capacity at the household level across the community
- **Farm Support and Coordination** — supporting existing farmers with resources, planning, and community coordination
- **Labor Coordination** — mobilizing community labor for planting, harvesting, gleaning, and other agricultural work
- **Soil and Compost** — building and maintaining long-term soil fertility as a community asset
- **Irrigation Support** — helping farms and households plan, build, and maintain irrigation infrastructure
- **Animal Husbandry and Veterinary Support** — supporting livestock keeping and animal health across the community

## Assessment

The division maintains a current understanding of the community's agricultural landscape — who is farming, what they are growing, what the soil can support, and where untapped production capacity exists. Without this picture, support and coordination efforts are uninformed. Assessment is not a one-time survey but an ongoing function that updates as conditions, membership, and land use change.

**Farm inventory** is the starting point. The division should develop a clear picture of every farm and market garden operating within or connected to the community — what land they work, what they produce, at what scale, and what their constraints are. Labor, inputs, storage, and market access are the most common limiting factors for small farms. Understanding what each farm needs is prerequisite to supporting it. The USDA Census of Agriculture publishes production data at the county level; the USDA Farm Service Agency maintains records of registered farms; and county agricultural extension offices maintain relationships with most active farmers in their area.

**Soil assessment** is essential and non-obvious. Soil quality cannot be determined by looking at it. County extension offices offer soil testing for $15-20 per sample, returning pH, nutrient levels, and organic matter content — the three variables that most directly determine what a piece of land can grow. Soil pH is the most critical single factor: most vegetables grow best between 6.0 and 7.0; outside that range, nutrients become unavailable to plants even when present in the soil. The USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service publishes detailed soil mapping data for the entire country through the Web Soil Survey, which allows soil types, drainage characteristics, and production potential to be assessed for any parcel before setting foot on it.

**Growing conditions** vary significantly at the local level. The USDA Plant Hardiness Zone map and local frost date records establish the growing season length — the window between last spring frost and first fall frost. Microclimates matter: south-facing slopes are warmer and longer-seasoned; low-lying areas are frost pockets; urban areas retain heat. The division should understand the range of microclimates across the community's agricultural land and match crops to conditions accordingly.

**Land potential** extends well beyond active farms. The division should assess how much land within the community is potentially arable but currently unused — lawns, vacant lots, abandoned fields, institutional grounds. A single acre under productive cultivation can supply a significant portion of one to four people's caloric needs depending on crops and methods. Understanding the gap between current production and potential production gives the division a realistic picture of what the community could grow.

**Equipment and infrastructure** are as important as land. The division should maintain an inventory of agricultural equipment in the community — tractors, tillers, hand tools, irrigation systems — and understand who owns what and on what terms it might be shared or borrowed. Community equipment access can dramatically lower the barrier to entry for household and small-scale growers.

## Seed Library

The division maintains a seed library — a community collection of open-pollinated seeds that members can access, grow, save, and return. The seed library is not a static supply depot but a living, regenerating resource that grows more adapted to local conditions with each generation it is grown. It is one of the most important long-term assets the division maintains.

**The critical distinction is between open-pollinated and hybrid seeds.** Open-pollinated varieties produce seeds that grow true to the parent plant and can be saved and replanted indefinitely. Hybrid seeds — labeled F1 — are crosses between two parent lines that produce vigorous first-generation plants but unpredictable offspring. Hybrid seeds cannot be reliably saved. A seed library built on hybrid seeds is dependent on commercial suppliers every season. The library must be built exclusively on open-pollinated varieties, preferably heirlooms that have been maintained for generations and carry accumulated local adaptation.

**Seed saving varies significantly by crop.** Self-pollinating crops — tomatoes, beans, peas, lettuce, peppers — are the easiest to save with little risk of cross-pollination between varieties. Cross-pollinating crops require more management. Corn must be isolated by at least a quarter mile from other corn varieties, or timed so varieties do not tassel simultaneously — and requires a minimum population of 200 or more plants to maintain genetic diversity, a non-obvious requirement that many seed savers miss. Squash varieties cross readily within the same species but not between species — Cucurbita pepo, maxima, and moschata are separate species and do not cross, which allows multiple species to be grown without isolation. Brassicas — cabbage, kale, broccoli, collards — cross freely with each other and with wild mustard and require careful isolation.

**Seeds must be fully mature before harvest** — which is not the same as when they are eaten. Tomato seed tomatoes should be overripe and soft. Bean and pea pods should be left to dry fully on the plant. Squash saved for seed should remain on the vine well past the eating stage. Harvesting seeds too early is one of the most common saving mistakes.

**Tomato seeds require a fermentation step.** The gel coating surrounding each seed contains germination inhibitors that must be removed. Seeds fermented in water for two to three days — the mixture will develop mold on the surface — are then rinsed clean. This mimics the natural fermentation that occurs inside a rotting fruit.

**Seed storage requires cool, dark, and dry conditions.** Moisture is the primary enemy of stored seeds. Silica gel desiccant packs in sealed containers significantly extend viability. Most vegetable seeds remain viable for three to five years under good storage; tomatoes and squash can last seven or more. Short-lived seeds — onions, parsnips, parsley — should be refreshed every one to two years. Freezing dry seeds in airtight containers can extend viability to decades.

**The library operates as a regenerating commons.** Members borrow seeds, grow crops, save seeds from their harvest, and return a larger quantity than they borrowed. This requires that members know how to save seeds — making seed saving instruction an integral part of the library program. The division should track what varieties it holds, in what quantities, and when they were last grown, to ensure nothing goes unrefreshed too long.

**Distributed storage is a non-negotiable principle of the seed library.** Seeds concentrated in a single location are one fire, flood, or theft away from total loss. The library should maintain a primary collection and at minimum one complete duplicate stored in a separate location. Beyond that, active distribution to member households is itself a form of disaster recovery — seeds being grown and saved across many households exist in dozens of locations simultaneously and cannot be lost in a single event. The library's resilience is proportional to how widely its genetics are distributed across the community. A seed library that is actively used and saved by many members is far more disaster-resistant than one that is carefully maintained in a single room.

## Household Agricultural Support

Growing food at the household level is a household responsibility. A member household that has space and capacity to grow vegetables is expected to do so — it is part of what a household in the Resolute Society does, not an optional extra. The division's role here is to teach, enable, and support that obligation — providing seeds, knowledge, and guidance — not to grow food on behalf of households that could grow it themselves. The division organizes and supports; members grow.

**A household garden's primary contribution is nutrition, not calories.** This is an important expectation to set correctly. A typical home garden produces significant amounts of vegetables — leafy greens, tomatoes, peppers, herbs — but will not replace staple calories for most households. The exceptions are calorie-dense crops like sweet potatoes, potatoes, and dried beans, which can produce meaningfully at small scale. Members should understand their garden as a source of nutrients, variety, and resilience rather than caloric sufficiency, and plan their food security accordingly.

**Starting small is the right approach.** Most new gardeners fail because they start too large. A single 4x8 raised bed or a few containers is a better starting point than a large plot — it is manageable, produces real food, and builds the knowledge and confidence to scale up. The division's household support program should guide members through this progression rather than pushing ambitious first-year plans that fail.

**Raised beds are the most accessible entry point** for most households. They sidestep soil quality and contamination issues common in urban and suburban settings, drain well, warm faster in spring, and are easier to manage than in-ground beds. Standard dimensions are four feet wide — reachable from both sides without stepping in — by eight to twelve feet long, six to twelve inches deep. The growing medium should be a blend of compost and topsoil rather than native soil alone.

**Container growing** makes household food production viable even without land — on balconies, patios, and rooftops. Container size matters: herbs and lettuce need one to three gallons; tomatoes and peppers need at minimum five gallons, preferably more; root vegetables need twelve or more inches of depth. Containers dry out faster than ground soil, often requiring daily watering in summer.

**Perennial food plants are among the highest-value investments a household can make** and are frequently overlooked in favor of annual vegetables. Fruit trees, nut trees, berry bushes, asparagus, and perennial herbs produce food for years to decades without replanting. A household with established fruit trees and berry bushes has a fundamentally different food security profile than one without. The long establishment time — fruit trees typically take three to seven years to produce meaningfully — makes planting perennials an urgent priority. The sooner they go in, the sooner they produce. The division's household support program should treat perennial planting as a first-year priority for every member household with land.

**Mulching and composting** are the two most impactful practices a household grower can adopt. A two to four inch layer of straw, wood chips, or leaves over garden beds dramatically reduces water needs, suppresses weeds, and builds soil organic matter as it breaks down. Household composting — kitchen scraps and yard waste — produces free fertilizer and improves soil with every season. Worm composting (vermicomposting) works in small spaces including indoors and produces high-quality compost from kitchen scraps with minimal effort.

## Farm Support and Coordination

The division supports existing farmers — it does not run farms. The distinction matters: the division's role is to remove the constraints that limit what farmers can already do, and to coordinate across farms so the community's agricultural capacity is greater than the sum of its parts.

**Labor is the most consistent constraint on small farms.** A farm with land, seeds, and knowledge but insufficient labor will underperform in every season. The division's labor coordination function (see below) is the primary tool for addressing this, but farm support begins with understanding each farm's labor needs across the seasonal calendar — when planting requires many hands, when harvests are time-sensitive, when routine maintenance can be done with fewer people.

**Market access is the second major constraint.** Small farms often produce more than they can sell through existing channels. The division can help by organizing collective purchasing among members — essentially a community-supported agriculture model in which members collectively subscribe to farm output before the season, providing farmers with capital and a guaranteed market simultaneously. This is one of the most practical and high-impact forms of farm support the division can provide.

**Crop planning coordination** is a non-obvious division function. Individual farmers make planting decisions independently, which can result in community-wide surplus of some crops and shortage of others. The division, with visibility across all farms, can facilitate conversation about what is being grown and what gaps exist — not by directing farmers, but by sharing information that allows better collective decisions. This coordination becomes more important as conditions deteriorate and community food sufficiency depends on deliberate production planning.

**Input support** connects farms with resources the division has access to. Compost from the Sanitation Division is a direct agricultural input the division should facilitate transferring to farms. Bulk seed purchasing through the seed library reduces individual farm seed costs. Shared equipment — particularly for soil preparation and harvest — reduces capital requirements for small operations.

**Pest and disease information sharing** across farms benefits all growers. What one farm encounters in a given season, neighboring farms are likely to encounter. The division should maintain a simple information network — even informal — through which farmers share what they're seeing. Early warning of pest pressure or disease outbreak allows faster collective response.

**Extension services** are a largely underutilized resource. County agricultural extension offices provide free technical assistance, soil testing, and crop recommendations. The USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service offers free conservation planning support to farmers. The division should ensure that every farm connected to the community is aware of and accessing these services, and should help facilitate those connections where needed.

**Connecting new growers with experienced ones** is among the most valuable things the division can do. Farming knowledge is transmitted most effectively through mentorship and practice — the same knowledge recovery principle that applies to household food preparation applies here. The division should actively facilitate relationships between experienced farmers and new or aspiring ones within the community.

## Labor Coordination

Agricultural labor is uniquely time-sensitive. Harvest windows for many crops are measured in days — a field of tomatoes or a grain harvest delayed by a week of missing labor can mean significant or total crop loss. Planting windows are similarly constrained by weather and frost. The division's labor coordination function exists to ensure that farms connected to the community can mobilize the hands they need, when they need them.

**The historical model is mutual aid.** Barn raisings, harvest bees, and threshing rings were the mechanisms by which agricultural communities organized collective labor before mechanization. Members worked each other's land on the understanding that the obligation was reciprocal. The Resolute Society's labor coordination function is a revival of this model — agricultural labor contributions should be tracked through the society's ledger-keeping function as part of the mutual obligation that binds members together.

**Not all agricultural tasks require the same skills.** Harvesting most crops is unskilled labor — anyone can pick beans or pull sweet potatoes. Pruning fruit trees, operating equipment, saving seeds from specific crops, and managing soil amendments require more knowledge. The division should maintain awareness of what skills its labor pool holds, so that skilled tasks can be matched to members capable of doing them correctly.

**Gleaning requires rapid mobilization.** When a farm has surplus produce available after harvest, or when a commercial farm opens its fields to gleaning, the window to act is short. The division's labor coordination function is what makes gleaning operationally viable — a standing roster of members who can be called on quickly is the difference between gleaning happening and the food going to waste.

**A seasonal agricultural calendar** makes labor needs predictable and plannable. Spring centers on bed preparation and planting; summer on weeding, irrigation, and early harvests; fall on major harvests and preservation; winter on planning, seed saving, and equipment maintenance. The division should publish and maintain a community agricultural calendar that gives members visibility into when labor will be needed and of what kind, so participation can be planned rather than improvised.

**Organized work parties** — scheduled community labor events at farms — are the primary operational mechanism. They are social as well as productive, and the social dimension is not incidental. The relationships built between members working together on a farm are part of what makes the community coherent. Work parties should be treated as community events, not just labor deployments.

## Soil and Compost

Soil is the foundation of all agriculture, and building it is the division's longest-horizon responsibility. It takes approximately 500 years for nature to form one inch of topsoil. Industrial agriculture has depleted much of what took centuries to build. The division treats soil fertility as a long-term community asset — something that must be actively built and protected, not consumed.

**Healthy soil is a living ecosystem.** A teaspoon of healthy soil contains billions of microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, protozoa, nematodes — that drive nutrient cycling, water retention, and plant health. Soil organic matter is the most important measurable indicator of soil health. A one percent increase in organic matter allows soil to hold an additional 20,000 gallons of water per acre. It is built by adding compost, cover crops, and mulch, and destroyed by tillage, compaction, and bare soil.

**Tillage is the primary enemy of soil health.** Every pass of a tiller or plow destroys the fungal networks and soil structure that took years to build. Reduced tillage and no-till systems build soil faster, retain moisture better, and require less labor over time. Sheet mulching — layering cardboard and compost directly over existing vegetation — converts lawn to productive garden without any digging. The division should promote no-till and reduced tillage approaches across all community growing.

**Composting is the division's primary soil-building tool.** The composting process requires a balance of carbon-rich materials — straw, wood chips, dry leaves, cardboard — and nitrogen-rich materials — kitchen scraps, grass clippings, fresh plant material, manure. The ideal ratio is roughly 25-30 parts carbon to one part nitrogen by weight. A pile smelling of ammonia has too much nitrogen; a pile that fails to heat has too much carbon. A hot compost pile must be at least three feet in each dimension to generate sufficient internal heat, and should be turned regularly to introduce oxygen and speed decomposition. Finished compost smells earthy and looks like dark, crumbly soil.

**Community-scale composting** aggregates materials from multiple households and sources to produce volume sufficient for hot composting. Tree services regularly give away wood chips at no cost — they pay to dispose of them otherwise — making chips an abundant and free source of carbon material. Coffee shops produce large volumes of nitrogen-rich grounds. Municipal yard waste is available in most communities. The division should establish relationships with these sources as part of its compost operation.

**Humanure compost from the Sanitation Division** is a legitimate and valuable agricultural input — high in nitrogen and phosphorus — and should be explicitly integrated into the agricultural composting program. The division should coordinate with Sanitation on finished compost transfer and application.

**Cover crops** are plants grown specifically to improve soil between production seasons. Leguminous cover crops — crimson clover, hairy vetch, cowpeas, winter peas — fix nitrogen directly from the atmosphere through root bacteria. One acre of crimson clover can fix 100-200 pounds of nitrogen per year, replacing the need for purchased fertilizer. Cereal rye is the most cold-hardy winter cover crop and grows in nearly any soil condition. Cover crops prevent erosion, suppress weeds, and feed soil biology year-round. The division should treat cover cropping as a standard practice on all community-connected growing land between seasons.

**Soil pH management** is non-obvious but critical. Most vegetables grow best between pH 6.0 and 7.0. Lime — calcium carbonate or dolomitic lime — raises pH in acidic soils and takes months to take effect; it must be applied well ahead of the growing season. The division should ensure that all community-connected growing operations conduct regular soil testing and manage pH accordingly.

## Irrigation Support

The division helps farms and households plan and build irrigation infrastructure appropriate to their land, water sources, and crops. Water is the most common limiting factor in summer production in most of the United States, and the difference between irrigated and unirrigated growing is often the difference between a harvest and a crop failure.

**Drip irrigation is dramatically more efficient than overhead watering.** Overhead sprinklers and flood irrigation deliver water at 50-70% efficiency — much of it evaporates or runs off before reaching roots. Drip irrigation delivers water directly to the root zone at 90% or greater efficiency, reducing water use by half or more while improving plant health by keeping foliage dry. The division should promote drip irrigation as the standard for all community-connected growing operations and assist members in designing and installing systems.

**Gravity-fed irrigation eliminates the need for pumps.** A water source elevated above the growing area — a storage tank, a pond on higher ground, a rooftop cistern — can feed an entire irrigation system with no electricity and no mechanical parts. Where topography allows, the division should help farms and households design gravity-fed systems as the baseline. Even modest elevation differences — a tank raised ten feet — provide sufficient pressure for drip systems.

**Mulching is the most impactful irrigation reduction measure available.** A two to four inch layer of straw, wood chips, or leaves over garden beds reduces water loss from soil evaporation by 50% or more, keeps soil cooler in summer heat, and builds organic matter as it breaks down. The division should treat mulching as a prerequisite to any irrigation planning — reducing the irrigation requirement is always preferable to meeting a higher one.

**Swales and earthworks** capture and slow water movement across a landscape, allowing it to infiltrate rather than run off. A swale — a level trench dug along the contour of a slope — intercepts surface runoff and spreads it across the landscape, recharging groundwater and keeping soil moist far longer than unmodified land. These are permanent landscape features that reduce irrigation needs for years or decades after installation. The division should develop the knowledge to design and install basic earthworks and offer this as a farm and household support service.

**Irrigation scheduling** matters as much as irrigation method. Watering in the morning allows foliage to dry before the heat of the day, reducing disease pressure. Watering at midday loses significant water to evaporation. Watering in the evening leaves foliage wet overnight, promoting fungal disease. The division's instruction program should cover basic irrigation scheduling alongside infrastructure support.

## Animal Husbandry and Veterinary Support

The division supports community members who keep livestock — it does not operate its own animal operations. Small-scale livestock keeping is one of the most practical additions to a household or farm's food security profile, providing protein, fat, eggs, dairy, and draft power with relatively modest inputs. The division's role is to build this capacity across the community and ensure that members keeping animals have the knowledge and support to do so effectively.

**Small livestock are the appropriate focus for community-scale animal husbandry.** Chickens provide eggs and meat, are manageable in urban and suburban settings, and require modest space and feed. Dual-purpose breeds — which produce both reasonable egg and meat yields — are more resilient than breeds optimized for one or the other. Rabbits are among the most feed-efficient livestock available, converting feed to meat at roughly a 4:1 ratio compared to cattle at 8:1 or more, are quiet, require minimal space, and reproduce rapidly. Goats provide milk and meat, are hardy and adaptable, and can browse vegetation that other livestock cannot — though they require secure fencing. Pigs are excellent converters of food waste and crop scraps into meat but require more space and management than the others.

**Feed self-sufficiency is the critical long-term concern.** Animals that depend entirely on commercial feed are vulnerable to the same supply chain failures as the rest of the food system. The division should help livestock keepers understand what their animals can forage, what crop wastes and kitchen scraps can substitute for purchased feed, and how to grow fodder crops. An animal that can largely feed itself from the land and the community's food waste is fundamentally more resilient than one that cannot.

**Basic animal health knowledge must be distributed across the community.** The division should not assume that a veterinarian will be available when needed. Members keeping livestock should be able to recognize common signs of illness, understand basic biosecurity — preventing disease from spreading between flocks and herds — and know how to manage common conditions without professional veterinary care. The division should build relationships with local veterinarians and facilitate the transfer of practical animal health knowledge to livestock keepers in the community.

**Zoonotic diseases** — illnesses that can spread from animals to humans — require explicit awareness. Many common livestock diseases have human health implications. The division should ensure that members keeping animals understand basic hygiene practices that prevent zoonotic transmission: handwashing after animal contact, proper handling of animal waste, and recognizing signs of illness in animals that may indicate human health risk.

**Butchering and slaughter** are distinct skill sets that most people no longer have. The division should treat these as part of the knowledge recovery program — knowledgeable community members teaching others — and ensure that the community maintains this capacity rather than depending on commercial processing facilities that may not be available under stress.

## Division Relationships

**Food Security**
The Agricultural Division is the primary internal food source for the Food Security Division. The two divisions must maintain ongoing coordination on harvest timing, crop volumes, and what is available for storage and processing. The Agricultural Division produces; Food Security stores, processes, and distributes. This handoff is the most consequential inter-division relationship in the society's food resilience system.

**Sanitation**
The Sanitation Division produces humanure compost that is a direct agricultural input. The Agricultural Division should coordinate with Sanitation on compost readiness, volumes, and application scheduling. This relationship closes a nutrient cycle that benefits both divisions and the community's long-term soil fertility.

**Water Security**
Agriculture is a significant water consumer. The Agricultural Division should maintain awareness of the community's water landscape — particularly irrigation sources and seasonal availability — and coordinate with Water Security on agricultural water use, especially during drought conditions.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on building knowledge, supporting farms, developing household growing capacity, and improving soil fertility over time. As conditions deteriorate, food production becomes more urgent and the division's coordination role — labor, crop planning, input supply — becomes more active. The division does not change what it does; it scales what it does.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Maintain the seed library and ensure seeds are distributed and actively grown
- Coordinate agricultural labor for planting, harvesting, and gleaning
- Support farms with inputs, information, and community resources
- Build and maintain soil fertility across community-connected growing land
- Expand household growing capacity across the membership

The division must be capable of sustaining all of these functions simultaneously and indefinitely. Agricultural capacity — soil, seeds, knowledge, relationships — takes years to build and cannot be created on demand during a crisis.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete the agricultural assessment**
The division's first task is to develop a clear picture of the community's agricultural landscape — who is farming, what they are growing, what the soil can support, what land is potentially arable, and what equipment exists in the community. This picture informs every other priority.

**2. Establish the seed library**
The seed library should be established early, built around open-pollinated varieties suited to the local growing conditions, and distributed to member households as quickly as possible. Genetic diversity held in many hands is more resilient than genetic diversity held in one location.

**3. Connect with existing farmers**
Before any other farm support activity, the division must build relationships with farmers in and around the community. Understanding their constraints, needs, and capacity is prerequisite to supporting them effectively.

**4. Launch household growing support**
The division should begin assisting member households with growing plans, raised bed establishment, and perennial planting immediately. Perennial plants in particular should go in as early as possible — every year of delay is a year of lost production.

**5. Establish composting operations**
Community-scale composting should be established to begin building the soil amendment supply that long-term agricultural productivity depends on. Relationships with wood chip, yard waste, and food scrap sources should be developed alongside composting infrastructure.

**6. Operate community programs**
Organized work parties, a seasonal agricultural calendar, and active labor coordination become ongoing operational programs once the foundational work is in place.

## Division Documents

The Agricultural Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt.

**Household Growing Guide** — an instructional guide covering raised bed construction, container growing, crop selection, perennial planting, composting, and mulching. Designed to help any household establish and grow its own food production capacity.

**Seed Saving Guide** — an instructional guide covering the distinction between open-pollinated and hybrid seeds, crop-specific seed saving techniques, isolation requirements, seed maturity, fermentation methods, and storage. Includes guidance on maintaining genetic diversity.

**Soil Health and Composting Guide** — an instructional guide covering soil organic matter, no-till practices, composting ratios and methods, cover crops, pH management, and the integration of humanure compost into agricultural production.

**Community Agricultural Assessment Framework** — a methodology for surveying and documenting a community's agricultural landscape: farm inventory, soil assessment, land potential, equipment inventory, and growing conditions. Designed to be conducted locally and updated annually.
