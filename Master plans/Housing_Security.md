# Housing Security Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Housing Security Division exists to ensure that no member of the Resolute Society sleeps without a roof over their head — and that the housing members have is safe, stable, and habitable. It does this by identifying housing insecurity before it becomes crisis, maintaining the capacity to shelter members immediately when crisis arrives, and building pathways to stable long-term housing for members who need them.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around housing security — an instrument of collective action, not a housing provider. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by whether every member has a place to sleep tonight.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no member sleeps without shelter due to housing loss or crisis
- That the community maintains the capacity to provide emergency and transitional housing without depending on external institutions
- That members at risk of housing loss are identified and supported before crisis occurs
- That the community possesses a standardized, buildable housing solution it can deploy for members in need

## Core Functions

- **Assessment** — identifying members who are housing insecure or at risk, and maintaining a current understanding of the community's housing landscape
- **Emergency Shelter** — providing immediate shelter for members in acute housing crisis
- **Transitional Housing** — providing short-term stable housing while permanent solutions are developed
- **Long-term Housing Support** — helping members secure and maintain stable long-term housing
- **Relocation Support** — helping members relocate when their current housing situation cannot be stabilized
- **Housing Conditions** — ensuring that members' existing housing is safe and habitable
- **Liberty House Program** — designing, building, and deploying a standardized simple dwelling the community can construct for members in need

## Assessment

The division maintains a current understanding of who in the community is housing insecure and who is at risk of becoming so. Housing insecurity is a spectrum — the division's responsibility does not begin at the point of homelessness. It begins with identifying vulnerability before crisis arrives.

**Housing insecurity takes many forms.** Members who are homeless are the most visible end of the spectrum, but housing insecurity includes members who are behind on rent or mortgage and facing eviction or foreclosure; members in overcrowded situations that are unstable; members in substandard housing that is unsafe or uninhabitable; members spending more than 30-50% of their income on housing and vulnerable to any disruption; and members whose housing is tied to a relationship or situation that may end. The division should maintain awareness of where each member sits on this spectrum, not only who is currently without shelter.

**Early identification enables early intervention.** A member two months behind on rent is far easier to support than one who has already been evicted. The division should develop the trust and relationships within the community that allow members to disclose housing vulnerability before it becomes crisis — which requires that the division handle this information with strict confidentiality. Housing insecurity carries significant stigma; members will not seek help if they fear judgment or exposure.

**Vulnerability factors** are the warning signs the division should watch for. Income disruption, recent job loss, relationship breakdown where housing was shared, medical crisis or debt, domestic violence, and recent release from incarceration all significantly elevate housing risk. The division should be attentive to these circumstances among members and reach out proactively rather than waiting for a member to present in crisis.

**The community's housing landscape** should be mapped and maintained. This includes understanding what housing resources already exist — local emergency shelters, transitional housing programs, government assistance programs — as well as identifying housing assets within the membership itself. Members with spare rooms, guest houses, ADUs, or vacant properties represent potential housing capacity the division can mobilize for members in need. Knowing these assets exist before they are needed is the difference between a rapid response and a scramble.

## Emergency Shelter

Emergency shelter is the division's most time-sensitive function — it is the response to someone who needs a roof over their head tonight. The standard is safety and dignity, not comfort. The goal is to stabilize a member immediately so that longer-term planning can begin.

**The first 72 hours after housing loss are the most critical.** People in acute housing crisis are often in shock, making poor decisions under extreme stress. A rapid, organized division response in this window sets the trajectory for what comes next. This requires that the division have a plan before the call comes — not improvised at midnight when someone has nowhere to go.

**The host family model is the most immediate and dignified emergency shelter option.** Member households who agree in advance to host someone in housing crisis for a defined short period — up to 30 days — provide a private, human-scale alternative to institutional shelter. This requires trust, clear expectations, and active support from the division throughout the placement. The division should maintain a standing list of member households willing to host, updated regularly, so that a match can be made quickly.

**Community space provides scalable shelter** when a host family is unavailable or when multiple members are displaced simultaneously — as in a disaster scenario. Any large, climate-controlled community space can serve this function with the addition of cots, bedding, and hygiene access. The division should identify viable community spaces in advance and understand what would be needed to activate them.

**Emergency situations vary and require different responses.** Eviction may come with very little notice. Domestic violence escape requires immediate safety and strict confidentiality about the member's location — the division must never disclose where a domestic violence survivor is sheltering. Fire or disaster displacement may involve multiple households at once. Someone leaving incarceration with nowhere to go needs support from the moment of release. The division should understand the specific dynamics of each scenario and have a response framework for each.

**Immediate needs beyond shelter must be anticipated.** A member in housing crisis typically needs more than a bed. Food, hygiene access, phone access, medication, transportation, and safe storage for belongings are all immediate needs. Critically, pets are a major barrier — many people in crisis will refuse shelter that cannot accommodate their animals. The division's emergency shelter response should address these needs as part of a complete response, not as afterthoughts.

**Emergency shelter has a defined duration.** Without a clear timeline, emergency arrangements become permanent by default — which is unfair to hosts and delays real solutions for the member in crisis. Thirty days is a reasonable standard for emergency shelter before transition to more stable housing is expected. The division should communicate this clearly at the start of any placement and begin transitional planning immediately.

## Transitional Housing

Transitional housing bridges the gap between emergency shelter and stable long-term housing. It is temporary but stable — enough permanence for a member to catch their breath, address what caused the crisis, and build a path to stable housing. The standard duration is 90 days to six months, though some situations warrant longer. Like emergency shelter, it must have a clear exit plan from the beginning.

**The purpose of this period is productive stabilization.** A member in transitional housing should be actively working on the conditions that led to housing loss — whether that is income instability, debt, a relationship situation, health, or something else. The division should not simply warehouse people in transitional housing and wait; it should be actively supporting the member's progress toward a permanent solution. This means regular check-ins, connection to relevant resources, and honest assessment of whether the current plan is working.

**Privacy and stability are the minimum standards.** Transitional housing should provide a private space — a room, at minimum — rather than dormitory or communal sleeping arrangements. Shared common areas are acceptable; shared sleeping space is not. The dignity of the arrangement directly affects a member's ability to stabilize and move forward.

**The Liberty House is the division's primary transitional housing tool.** A Liberty House placed on a member's property — functioning as an ADU — provides private, dignified transitional housing without requiring the community to acquire dedicated property. Multiple Liberty Houses across the membership create a distributed transitional housing network that can absorb several households simultaneously. The division should actively develop this capacity as a standing resource rather than scrambling to find housing when need arises.

**The Housing First principle should guide the division's approach.** Evidence consistently shows that stable housing must precede — not follow — addressing other challenges. Programs that require sobriety, treatment compliance, or behavioral conditions before providing housing are less effective than those that house people first and address other needs from a position of stability. The division should not attach conditions to transitional housing that prevent members from accessing it.

**Barriers to conventional housing** are real and must be understood. Criminal records, eviction history, poor credit, and lack of rental history prevent many people from accessing the private rental market even when they have income and are ready to be good tenants. These barriers are one of the strongest arguments for the community controlling its own housing — through the Liberty House program and member-hosted arrangements — rather than depending on commercial landlords who run standard screening processes.

**A financial arrangement during transitional housing** should be discussed at placement. Some programs ask members to contribute a portion of their income to a savings account during transitional housing, building toward a deposit on permanent housing. This approach both prepares the member financially and maintains a sense of agency and responsibility. The division should think through what arrangement serves each member's situation.

## Long-term Housing Support

Long-term housing support helps members move from transitional stability to permanent housing security. The goal is not indefinite division involvement — it is self-sufficiency. The division's role is to help members access the resources, knowledge, and conditions they need to maintain stable housing on their own.

**Government housing programs are underutilized and poorly understood.** The Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher Program provides federal rental assistance but has waitlists that often run years long — members should apply immediately upon becoming eligible, not when they are in crisis. LIHTC (Low-Income Housing Tax Credit) properties are privately owned subsidized housing that often have shorter waitlists than Section 8 and are less well known. USDA Section 502 Direct Loans offer very favorable homeownership terms for low-income households in rural areas. Emergency Solutions Grants fund homelessness prevention and rapid rehousing at the local level. The division should maintain a current map of what programs are available locally, their eligibility requirements, and how to apply — and proactively connect members to them.

**Members should be on every applicable waitlist now.** Housing assistance waitlists are not something to apply to when housing is lost — by that point, the wait may be years. The division's proactive assessment function should ensure that every member who may eventually qualify for housing assistance has applied, even if they do not currently need it.

**Homeownership is more stable than renting** and should be a long-term goal where achievable. First-time homebuyer programs, down payment assistance, and favorable loan programs (including USDA rural homeownership loans) are accessible to more people than many realize. The division should help members understand and navigate these pathways.

**Community land trusts** are a model worth developing over time. A community land trust holds land permanently in trust while homeowners own their homes — when a home is sold, appreciation is shared with the trust, keeping the home affordable for the next resident. This permanently removes housing from the speculative market and builds a community-controlled affordable housing stock that cannot be lost. It is a long-horizon investment, but one the Resolute Society should consider as it grows.

**Preventing future housing loss** is as important as resolving current housing loss. Once a member has stable housing, the division should support them in maintaining it — building emergency savings, understanding tenant rights, and addressing habitability issues before they escalate. A member who loses housing a second time within a few years represents a failure of long-term support, not just a new crisis.

**Legal aid** is a critical and underused resource. Tenant rights vary significantly by state and locality, and many members facing eviction or habitability problems have legal recourse they are unaware of. Local legal aid organizations provide free legal assistance to low-income tenants. The division should know what legal resources exist in the community and connect members to them early — before an eviction notice becomes a completed eviction.

## Relocation Support

Relocation is sometimes the most practical path to housing stability — not a failure. When local housing costs are persistently unaffordable, when a member needs distance from a dangerous situation, or when family or support networks elsewhere would better sustain a member than the current location, moving is the right answer. The division's role is to help make it possible.

**Geographic variation in housing costs is significant.** What is unaffordable in one city may be readily accessible in a rural area or a lower cost-of-living region. The division should understand the housing landscape beyond the immediate community and be prepared to help members identify destinations where their income and resources can support stable housing. Moving to a lower cost area is a legitimate and often highly effective housing stability strategy.

**The practical barriers to relocation must be addressed directly.** Moving costs money — truck rental, gas, deposit and first month's rent in a new location, temporary storage. Members in housing crisis rarely have these resources. The division should be prepared to provide financial support for moves when relocation is the right solution, and should understand what government programs (FEMA in disaster scenarios, certain HUD programs) may provide relocation assistance in specific circumstances.

**Domestic violence relocation requires particular care.** Speed, confidentiality, and safety are the priorities. The destination must not be disclosed. Address confidentiality programs — which exist in most states — provide a substitute mailing address through a state agency, preventing an abuser from locating a survivor through public records. The National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-SAFE) provides relocation resources and guidance. The division should know how to activate these resources quickly.

**Children have specific rights during housing instability.** The McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act guarantees children experiencing homelessness the right to immediate school enrollment, transportation to their school of origin, and access to services without delays from documentation requirements. The division should be aware of these rights and ensure that member families with children know and can exercise them during relocation.

**Connection to the destination community** should be part of every relocation. A member moved to a city where they know no one and have no community support has not been fully served. The division should help make warm connections to community resources — mutual aid networks, social services, and wherever possible, other Resolute Society chapters — in the destination. As the society grows across multiple locations, relocation between chapters becomes a concrete option that strengthens both the departing and receiving communities.

## Housing Conditions

A roof over someone's head is not enough if the roof is leaking, the heat doesn't work, or the walls are growing mold. The division's responsibility extends to the condition of housing members currently occupy — ensuring it is safe, habitable, and maintainable. A housed member in uninhabitable housing is still a housing security problem.

**Habitability has a legal definition.** Most states recognize an implied warranty of habitability — landlords are legally required to maintain rental properties in livable condition. Serious deficiencies including lack of heat, significant mold, structural failure, no running water, and pest infestation typically violate this standard and give tenants legal recourse including rent withholding, repair-and-deduct, and lease termination. These rights are widely unknown and rarely exercised. The division should ensure members in substandard rental housing know their rights and are connected to legal aid when needed. Local housing inspectors can also compel landlords to address serious habitability violations.

**Weatherization is the highest-impact, lowest-cost housing improvement available.** Air sealing — stopping drafts — saves more energy than adding insulation and dramatically improves comfort. Common air leaks occur around electrical outlets, plumbing penetrations, attic hatches, and windows and doors. Door sweeps, weatherstripping, outlet gaskets, and window film are inexpensive interventions any member can install. Two federal programs provide free weatherization assistance to low-income households: the Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) and LIHEAP (Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program). Most eligible members do not know these programs exist. The division should actively connect qualifying members to them.

**Heat in winter is a life safety issue.** Hypothermia can develop indoors at temperatures well above freezing, particularly for elderly members and young children. Carbon monoxide — produced by any combustion heating appliance including gas heaters, wood stoves, and generators — is odorless, colorless, and lethal. Every member home should have a working carbon monoxide detector. No combustion appliance should ever be operated indoors without proper ventilation. The division should treat CO detector coverage across member households as a basic safety standard.

**Extreme heat is increasingly a life safety issue.** Heat kills more Americans annually than any other weather event. Indoor temperatures above 90°F can cause heat stroke, particularly in elderly members. Passive cooling measures — cross-ventilation, window shading, reflective window film — reduce indoor temperatures significantly without air conditioning. The division should identify members without cooling capacity during heat events and connect them to cooling centers (libraries, community centers) or other support.

**Mold requires fixing the moisture source, not just the mold.** Cleaning mold without addressing the underlying moisture problem — a roof leak, plumbing leak, poor ventilation, or flooding — guarantees the mold returns. Significant mold infestations carry serious respiratory health risks and require professional remediation. The division should treat reported mold as a health issue requiring root cause resolution, not a cosmetic problem.

**Community repair capacity** is the division's most direct tool for addressing housing conditions. Members with carpentry, plumbing, electrical, and weatherization skills helping other members with basic repairs — organized as community work parties — build housing resilience across the membership. This is another application of the knowledge recovery principle: practical home maintenance and repair skills that were once common are increasingly rare, and the community should actively rebuild them.

## Liberty House Program

The Liberty House is the division's answer to a question communities have faced throughout history: how do you build dignified housing quickly, cheaply, and with the people you have? The name draws on the Liberty Ship — the standardized cargo vessel the United States produced at massive scale during World War II, designed not for perfection but for rapid construction by ordinary workers. A Liberty House is not the ideal home. It is a good home that a community can actually build.

**The design principles are deliberate.** The Liberty House ranges from 400 to 800 square feet depending on the variant — large enough to house a small family with genuine comfort, small enough to be built quickly and affordably by community members. It is rectangular in form, single story, with a simple shed or gable roof. Every design decision favors buildability over complexity. The structure is designed around standard lumber dimensions so that material waste is minimal and cuts are straightforward. Ceiling heights use standard lumber lengths. Wall panels can be framed flat on the ground and tilted up, reducing the need for scaffolding. A team of six to eight people with basic carpentry skills can frame a Liberty House in a day.

**The standard features are non-negotiable.** Every Liberty House must be well-insulated — minimum R-13 walls, R-30 roof — to be genuinely habitable across seasons. Metal roofing is specified over asphalt shingles for its 40-50 year lifespan versus 20-30 for shingles; the premium is worth it. Concrete pier foundations allow faster installation and, where needed, future relocation of the structure. Cross-ventilation through thoughtful window placement reduces cooling needs in warm climates. The structure must be weathertight and energy-efficient from day one, not as an afterthought.

**The Liberty House is versatile by design.** The same structure serves as emergency housing, transitional housing, a permanent small home for a member who chooses to live simply, or an ADU on a member's property that houses a member in need. This versatility is what makes it a division asset rather than a single-use tool. A Liberty House placed on a member's property today as an ADU can house a member in crisis tomorrow without requiring any new construction.

**The community builds it.** This is what separates the Liberty House from a purchased manufactured home. The building process is intentional: community members are trained in the standard design, organized into build teams, and deploy together to construct Liberty Houses as community events. The build is social as well as productive. Over time, the community accumulates both a stock of Liberty Houses and a body of members who know how to build them — which is itself a form of resilience.

**The division is responsible for maintaining the standard design.** This means developing and documenting the Liberty House design — in multiple variants for different household sizes — and keeping that design updated, freely available to all Resolute Society chapters, and buildable with materials available in any region. The design belongs to the community, not to any individual or firm.

**Permitting must be understood locally.** Building permits are required in most jurisdictions and the division must understand local requirements before construction. Some jurisdictions have simplified ADU permitting that reduces barriers. The division should understand what applies locally and design variants that work within those constraints where possible.

## Division Relationships

**Food Security**
Members in housing crisis are among the most food-insecure. The Housing Security Division is a standing referral source for the Food Security Division — when a member enters emergency or transitional housing, food access should be addressed immediately as part of the response. The two divisions should maintain close coordination on members in acute need.

**Water Security and Sanitation**
Members without stable housing may lack access to safe water and sanitation. The division should coordinate with Water Security and Sanitation to ensure that members in emergency or transitional housing have access to both, and that any Liberty House or transitional housing facility meets basic water and sanitation standards before occupation.

**Healthcare**
Housing insecurity and poor health are deeply connected — each drives the other. The division should maintain a close relationship with the Medical Division to ensure that members in housing crisis receive health support alongside housing support, and that members with significant health conditions are prioritized in housing placements.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on assessment, early intervention with at-risk members, building the Liberty House program, and developing the host family and community repair networks. As conditions deteriorate and housing insecurity increases across the membership and broader community, emergency shelter and transitional housing functions become more active and more central.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Identify and support members at risk of housing loss before crisis occurs
- Provide immediate emergency shelter for members in acute crisis
- Manage transitional housing placements and support members through them
- Deploy Liberty Houses for members in need
- Address habitability issues in members' existing housing
- Support members in accessing long-term housing resources and assistance

The division must be capable of sustaining all of these functions simultaneously. Housing crises do not space themselves out conveniently — the division may be managing multiple active placements while responding to a new emergency. Capacity must be built in advance.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete the housing assessment**
The division's first task is to develop a current picture of housing security across the membership — who is stably housed, who is at risk, and who is in active need. This includes mapping housing assets within the membership: members with spare rooms, ADUs, or vacant properties who could host or house other members.

**2. Build the host family network**
Before any housing crisis arrives, the division should recruit and prepare member households willing to provide emergency hosting. This network is the division's most immediately deployable resource and costs nothing to maintain.

**3. Develop the Liberty House design**
The division should commission or develop the standard Liberty House design — in at least two variants for different household sizes — document it completely, and begin training community members in its construction. The first Liberty House build should be treated as a community event and a proof of concept.

**4. Map external resources**
The division should develop a current map of housing assistance programs, legal aid resources, emergency shelters, and government programs available in the community. This map must be maintained — programs change, waitlists open and close.

**5. Establish community repair capacity**
The division should identify members with relevant skills — carpentry, plumbing, electrical, weatherization — and begin organizing community repair capacity for addressing housing conditions across the membership.

**6. Operate ongoing programs**
Assessment, early intervention, and active case management of members in housing need become ongoing operational programs once the foundation is in place.

## Division Documents

The Housing Security Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt.

**Liberty House Design and Construction Guide** — the complete standardized design for the Liberty House in all approved variants, with construction documentation sufficient for a trained community build team to construct one. Freely available to all Resolute Society chapters.

**Housing Resource Navigation Guide** — a framework for identifying and accessing housing assistance programs, legal aid, government programs, and community resources. Designed to be adapted locally by each chapter with current program information.

**Emergency Housing Response Protocol** — operational procedures for responding to a member in acute housing crisis, including the host family placement process, community space activation, domestic violence protocols, and the 30-day emergency shelter timeline.

**Member Housing Assessment Framework** — a methodology for conducting housing security assessment across the membership, identifying vulnerability factors, and maintaining a current picture of housing needs and assets within the community.
