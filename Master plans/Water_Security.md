# Water Security Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Water Security Division exists to ensure that every member of the Resolute Society has access to safe water — today and under whatever conditions tomorrow brings. It does this by maintaining a thorough understanding of the local water landscape, developing the capacity to source and treat water independent of any single system, and ensuring that knowledge and infrastructure are held broadly across the community.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around water security — an instrument of collective action, not a provider of services. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by what the community can do.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no member goes without safe water due to inability to access or treat it
- That the community maintains a current and accurate understanding of its local water landscape
- That the community maintains meaningful water storage and treatment capacity against disruption
- That water knowledge — sourcing, treatment, household planning — is actively developed and held broadly enough across the community that it cannot be lost

## Core Functions

- **Assessment and Mapping** — maintaining a current understanding of the local water landscape: water tables, surface water, watersheds, rainfall patterns, and source vulnerability
- **Sourcing** — identifying, developing, and maintaining access to water sources independent of any single supply system
- **Treatment** — maintaining the capacity to make water safe at both community and household scale
- **Distribution** — ensuring water reaches members who cannot access or treat it independently
- **Household Water Planning** — building household-level water storage, treatment, and sourcing capacity across the community

## Assessment and Mapping

The division maintains a current and accurate understanding of the local water landscape. This is not a one-time task — water availability shifts seasonally, drought conditions change year to year, and infrastructure vulnerabilities evolve. Assessment is an ongoing function that informs everything else the division does.

**Water tables** are the upper boundary of saturated groundwater underground. They are not fixed — they rise in wet seasons and fall in dry ones, and can drop significantly during extended drought. Water table depth and behavior can be understood through USGS groundwater monitoring data, which is publicly available, and through county well records, which are typically public record and show historical well depths and yields in a given area. The division should develop a working understanding of the local aquifer system — how deep it is, how it recharges, and how it has behaved historically under drought conditions.

**Surface water** — rivers, streams, ponds, and lakes — is generally the most visible and accessible water source but the most vulnerable to contamination and seasonal variation. Many smaller streams run dry during summer drought in ways that are not obvious without historical observation. The USGS operates stream gauges at many locations across the country, providing real-time and historical flow data. The division should identify and document all surface water sources within reasonable reach, note their seasonal behavior, and assess what is upstream — agricultural runoff, industrial sites, and urban runoff all affect water quality and treatment requirements.

**Watersheds** define what drains where. Understanding the watershed of any surface water source means understanding what contaminants it may carry. USGS topographic maps and the USGS StreamStats tool allow watershed boundaries to be identified and mapped. The division should map the watersheds of its primary surface water sources and maintain awareness of significant land uses within them.

**Rainfall** is both a direct water source through harvesting and a primary driver of groundwater recharge and surface water levels. NOAA publishes historical rainfall data by location, including monthly distributions and drought frequency. Monthly distribution matters more than annual totals for planning — a region that receives most of its rain in winter and faces dry summers requires different infrastructure than one with even year-round rainfall. A rough planning figure for rainwater harvesting: one inch of rain falling on 1,000 square feet of roof yields approximately 600 collectable gallons after losses.

**Springs** are points where groundwater reaches the surface naturally and can be among the most reliable water sources available. The USGS maintains records of known springs. The division should identify and document any springs within the community's area, assess their reliability, and understand which aquifer feeds them.

**Vulnerability assessment** should be a standing output of the mapping function. The division should maintain a clear picture of which sources fail first under which conditions — municipal treatment plants require electricity and chemical inputs; electric well pumps fail without power; surface water is always present but requires treatment; rainfall is intermittent and requires storage. Understanding the failure sequence allows the division to plan sourcing and treatment priorities before a crisis rather than during one.

## Sourcing

The division is responsible for identifying, developing, and maintaining access to water from multiple independent sources. Dependence on any single source — including municipal supply — is a vulnerability. The division should maintain a clear sourcing hierarchy: what is used under normal conditions, what is activated under disruption, and what is drawn on when primary and secondary sources fail.

**Municipal supply** is the default source under normal conditions for most communities. It requires electricity, chemical inputs, and functional treatment infrastructure — all of which can fail. It is a valid primary source but cannot be the only one.

**Wells** are among the most reliable independent water sources available. Drilled wells are preferable to dug wells — they access deeper aquifers, are less vulnerable to surface contamination, and do not dry out as easily. The critical non-obvious issue with wells is the pump. Most residential and community wells use electric submersible pumps, which fail without power. A hand pump — such as the Simple Pump or Bison Pump — can be installed alongside an existing electric pump in the same well casing, allowing continued water access during power outages. This conversion is relatively inexpensive and represents one of the highest-value infrastructure investments the division can facilitate. Well yield matters: a well that produces two gallons per minute cannot sustain heavy continuous use without recovering.

**Rainwater harvesting** collects precipitation from rooftops into storage tanks. The collection surface matters: metal roofs introduce the least contamination; asphalt shingles introduce some petroleum compounds; cedar shakes can harbor biological growth. A first-flush diverter — a device that routes the first flow of rain away from storage — is essential. The first water off any roof washes concentrated contaminants from dust, bird droppings, and debris; that water should not enter the storage tank. IBC totes (intermediate bulk containers — 275-330 gallon food-grade plastic tanks in metal cages) are widely available used at low cost and are practical for both household and community-scale rainwater storage.

**Surface water** — rivers, streams, ponds, lakes — is broadly available but always requires treatment before consumption. The division should identify viable collection points, assess seasonal reliability, and understand contamination risks from upstream. Intake should be positioned off the bottom to avoid sediment and protected from debris.

**Springs**, where they exist, can be highly reliable and produce water that is generally cleaner than surface water, though still requiring testing. A spring box — a simple concrete or stone collection chamber built at the spring outlet — captures and protects spring flow for practical use. The division should develop any viable springs within the community's area.

**Water storage** is inseparable from sourcing. No source delivers water continuously on demand — storage bridges the gap between collection and use. The minimum planning figure is one gallon per person per day for drinking and basic sanitation; two to three gallons for realistic daily needs. A community of 50 people requires at minimum 50 gallons per day and realistically 150-250 gallons. All storage containers must be food-grade — containers that previously held chemicals cannot be safely used for water storage. Treated water stored in sealed containers remains potable indefinitely; untreated stored water should be rotated every six to twelve months.

## Treatment

The division maintains the capacity to make water safe at both community and household scale. Water that appears clean can be dangerous — biological and chemical contaminants are invisible. The division must ensure that no member consumes untreated water from any source other than verified municipal supply, and must maintain treatment capacity that does not depend on that supply remaining functional.

**Different contaminants require different treatment methods.** No single method removes everything. The division should understand what each method does and does not address.

**Boiling** is the most reliable method for killing biological contaminants — bacteria, viruses, and protozoa. A rolling boil for one minute is sufficient at most elevations; three minutes above 6,500 feet. Boiling does not remove chemical contamination, heavy metals, or sediment. It is fuel-intensive and not practical as a primary community-scale method, but it is the fallback that requires no equipment.

**Filtration** removes sediment and biological contaminants depending on filter type. Filter pore size determines what is removed — this is the critical non-obvious detail. A filter rated at 0.2 microns removes bacteria and protozoa but not viruses, which are smaller. Hollow fiber filters such as the Sawyer Squeeze are effective and durable for biological contaminants at household scale. Ceramic filters remove bacteria and protozoa, can be cleaned and reused indefinitely, and are among the most resilient long-term filtration options. Activated carbon filters remove chemicals, chlorine, and improve taste but do not address biological contamination. Reverse osmosis removes nearly everything — bacteria, viruses, heavy metals, most chemicals — but requires pressure and produces significant waste water. No filter alone is sufficient for all scenarios.

**Chemical treatment** with chlorine is the most practical method for disinfecting water at community scale. Household unscented bleach containing 6-8.25% sodium hypochlorite can be used at 8 drops per gallon of clear water, 16 drops for cloudy water, with a 30-minute contact time before drinking. However, liquid bleach degrades rapidly — it loses significant effectiveness within six months of opening. Calcium hypochlorite (pool shock at 68-78% concentration) stores far longer and is far more concentrated: one pound can treat approximately 10,000 gallons. The division should stockpile calcium hypochlorite rather than liquid bleach for long-term treatment capacity. Chlorine is effective against bacteria and viruses but has reduced effectiveness against Cryptosporidium — a protozoan parasite that requires filtration or extended contact time.

**UV treatment** kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa without chemicals and leaves no taste. It requires electricity and clear water — turbidity blocks UV light and renders treatment ineffective. Water must be filtered before UV treatment. UV is a valuable component of a multi-stage system but cannot stand alone.

**Treatment is typically multi-step.** The sequence matters: first settle and pre-filter to remove sediment and turbidity; then filter for biological contaminants; then disinfect chemically or with UV to address viruses. Skipping steps reduces effectiveness. The division should establish and teach a standard treatment sequence for each source type in its area.

**Slow sand filtration** is a traditional community-scale technology that filters water biologically and physically using layers of sand and gravel, requiring no chemicals and no electricity. A biological layer called the schmutzdecke develops on the surface of the sand and is responsible for most of the filter's effectiveness — it must not be disturbed or scraped away. Scraping the top layer to clean a clogged filter destroys the schmutzdecke and requires a ripening period of several weeks before the filter is effective again; this is the non-obvious operational constraint that most people miss. Slow sand filtration also cannot handle highly turbid water — above roughly 10-20 NTU, suspended particles clog the filter rapidly. Turbid source water must be pre-settled before entering the filter. Slow sand filtration has been used for community water treatment for centuries and remains viable as a low-infrastructure community system, typically paired with chlorination for final disinfection.

**Fast (rapid) sand filtration** operates at 50 to 100 times the flow rate of slow sand filtration, making it practical for higher-volume community treatment. Unlike slow sand filtration, it is purely mechanical — no schmutzdecke develops. Because there is no biological layer, pre-treatment is required: alum (aluminum sulfate) or another coagulant is added to the source water first, causing fine suspended particles to clump together and settle out before filtration (a process called flocculation and sedimentation). The filter must also be backwashed regularly — water flow is reversed to lift and clean the sand bed, flushing accumulated solids out. Fast sand filtration removes turbidity and some biological contaminants but provides no viral kill and no chemical removal on its own; disinfection is always required after it. It is more complex to operate than slow sand but handles a wider range of source water quality.

**Sand grading by elutriation** is the technique for preparing your own filter sand when commercial filter-grade sand is unavailable. River sand, creek sand, and builder's sand all contain a mix of grain sizes — too fine and the filter clogs and flows too slowly; too coarse and filtration is inadequate. Elutriation separates sand by grain size using upward water flow: water is directed upward through a column of mixed sand at a controlled velocity. Lighter, finer particles are carried upward and out; heavier, coarser particles settle and remain. By adjusting the upward flow rate, specific grain size ranges can be selected. For a slow sand filter, the target effective grain size is roughly 0.15 to 0.35 millimeters — fine enough to filter well, coarse enough not to clog. This process requires no equipment beyond containers, tubing, and a water source with adjustable flow, and turns locally available sand into usable filter media.

**Testing** is the only way to know water is safe. The division should establish a regular testing protocol for any community water source. Basic coliform testing — which detects fecal contamination — is inexpensive and available through county extension offices and health departments. The division should also be aware of naturally occurring contaminants specific to its region: arsenic is elevated in certain western aquifers, iron and manganese affect many groundwater sources, and radon can be present in well water in granite-rich areas. These require specific treatment beyond standard biological disinfection.

## Distribution

The division ensures safe water reaches every member who needs it. The primary model is member-based — members collect water from a community distribution point and manage it at home. Delivery exists as a parallel capability for members who cannot transport water independently.

**Water is heavy.** A gallon weighs 8.34 pounds. A standard 5-gallon container weighs over 40 pounds full. This is the defining constraint of water distribution — it shapes container choice, transport method, and who can realistically carry it. The division should standardize around containers that are manageable for most members. Five-gallon food-grade buckets with lids are widely available and practical. Larger containers (55-gallon drums) require equipment to move and are appropriate for stationary storage, not member transport.

**The community water point** — a central location where treated water is available for members to fill their containers — is the most practical community-scale distribution model. It is low-infrastructure, easy to manage, and widely used in communities around the world that operate without pressurized distribution systems. The division should establish and maintain at least one water point, with the location chosen for accessibility to as many members as possible.

**Sanitation at the distribution point** is a non-obvious operational concern. Dirty or improperly stored containers brought to the water point can recontaminate treated water during filling. The division should establish clear protocols for container cleanliness and train members in proper water handling — including never touching the inside of a container or the dispensing equipment with bare hands.

**Gravity-fed distribution** eliminates the need for pumps. A storage tank elevated as little as ten feet above the distribution point provides meaningful water pressure — approximately 4.3 psi per ten feet of elevation. Where feasible, the division should design storage and distribution infrastructure to use gravity rather than powered pumps, preserving function in grid-down scenarios.

**Existing community assets** represent distribution capacity the division does not need to build. Fire apparatus — particularly tanker trucks — carry 1,000 to 3,000 or more gallons and can deliver directly to locations without infrastructure. Agricultural water tanks mounted on trucks or trailers serve the same function. The division should identify these assets within the community and establish relationships with their owners or operators before they are needed. A volunteer fire department with a tanker and a willingness to cooperate is a significant water distribution resource. These relationships are part of the division's distribution network, not emergency improvisations.

**Delivery** must exist for members who cannot transport water — disabled members, those without vehicles, those in acute health crisis. Water delivery is more labor-intensive than food delivery due to weight. The division should maintain a current delivery list and treat it as a standing operational responsibility. In extended disruption, the number of members requiring delivery may grow significantly.

**Quantity at distribution** should be pre-planned. The minimum is one gallon per person per day; two to three gallons covers basic cooking and hygiene needs. Distribution quantities should be calculated per household member and tracked against the division's available supply.

## Household Water Planning

The division's household water planning program exists to ensure that every member household has a working understanding of its water situation — where water comes from, how much it stores, how to treat it, and how long it can sustain itself without outside supply. As with food, the goal is knowledge and capacity held broadly across the community, organized by the division but owned by the households themselves. Instruction should be organized around knowledgeable community members teaching others.

**Every household needs a water plan.** That plan should answer four questions: what are this household's water sources in order of priority; how much water can the household store; how will the household treat each source type; and how long can the household sustain itself before requiring resupply. The division should assist members in developing and writing these plans, and should maintain awareness of which households have adequate capacity and which do not.

**Household water storage** should be built around food-grade containers. Five-gallon buckets are manageable and versatile. Fifty-five-gallon drums provide bulk storage but require a pump or hand siphon to access — they cannot simply be tipped and poured. IBC totes (275-330 gallons) are practical for households with the space. A WaterBOB — a large bladder that fits inside a bathtub and holds up to 100 gallons — can be filled quickly from a tap when disruption is anticipated, making it a valuable low-cost addition to any household's capacity.

**Overlooked household water sources** should be part of every member's awareness. A standard water heater holds 30 to 80 gallons of relatively clean water accessible via the drain valve at the base — most people do not think of this as a water reserve, but it is a significant one. The toilet tank (not the bowl) holds one to three gallons of clean water. Swimming pools, while not potable without treatment, represent a large volume of water useful for sanitation and can be treated for drinking. These sources extend household resilience significantly without any additional infrastructure.

**Household treatment capacity** should match the household's likely sources. A household near a stream needs filtration and disinfection capability. A household on municipal supply needs the ability to treat stored water that has been sitting. Every household should maintain a minimum supply of treatment materials — calcium hypochlorite or water purification tablets, and at minimum a ceramic or hollow fiber filter. The division's instruction program should ensure members understand which treatment method to apply to which source.

**Water hygiene** is as important as treatment. Waterborne illness spreads rapidly when water is handled improperly even after treatment — through dirty containers, unwashed hands, or cross-contamination during dispensing. The division should teach the safe water chain: treat, store in a clean sealed container, dispense without contaminating the opening, and wash hands before handling water. Handwashing is the single most effective disease prevention measure available and should be treated as a non-negotiable household practice regardless of water availability constraints.

**Rainwater harvesting at the household level** — connecting downspouts to storage tanks with first-flush diverters — is among the most practical infrastructure investments a household can make. The division should assist members in sizing and installing household rainwater systems appropriate to their roof area and local rainfall patterns.

## Division Relationships

**Sanitation**
Water security and sanitation are inseparable. Inadequate sanitation is the primary cause of water source contamination — human waste near wells, streams, or rainwater collection systems introduces pathogens that defeat even good treatment practices. The Water Security Division and the Sanitation Division must coordinate closely on the location of sanitation infrastructure relative to water sources, and the Water Security Division should include sanitation practices in its household water planning program.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on mapping, relationship building, and developing household and community capacity. As conditions deteriorate, treatment and distribution become more urgent and more central — the division does not change what it does, it scales what it does.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Monitor and assess water sources and quality
- Treat water at community scale for member use
- Distribute water to members who cannot access or treat it independently
- Support and expand household water capacity across the community

The division must be capable of sustaining all of these functions simultaneously and indefinitely. Infrastructure, treatment supplies, and community relationships should be built with that standard in mind, not with the assumption that disruption is temporary.

## Establishment Priorities

The division's first work is foundational. Before the community can source, treat, and distribute water reliably, it must understand the water landscape it operates in and develop the relationships and infrastructure that will sustain it under any condition.

**1. Map the water landscape**
The division's first task is to develop a clear picture of the community's water environment — water tables, surface water sources, watersheds, rainfall patterns, springs, and existing well infrastructure. This includes a vulnerability assessment: what fails first, what remains available, and where the gaps are for members already without reliable access.

**2. Build relationships**
The division should establish connections with water-adjacent community assets before they are needed — volunteer fire departments with tanker apparatus, agricultural operators with water tanks, county extension offices for testing, and well drillers and pump installers in the area. These relationships are infrastructure.

**3. Plan community water infrastructure**
With a grounded understanding of the water landscape, the division plans its physical infrastructure — hand pump conversions on existing wells, rainwater harvesting systems, community storage and treatment capacity — sized to the actual community and realistic about what can be built and maintained.

**4. Build capacity**
The division builds out planned infrastructure and develops treatment knowledge and household water capacity across membership. Hand pump installations, household rainwater systems, and treatment supply stockpiles are all concrete establishment goals.

**5. Operate community programs**
The community water point and regular distribution capability come online once the foundation is in place. Household water planning becomes an ongoing program rather than a startup effort.

## Division Documents

The Water Security Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt.

**Household Water Planning Guide** — an instructional guide covering household water storage, treatment options by source type, overlooked water sources, rainwater harvesting, and water hygiene. Designed to help any household develop and execute its own water plan.

**Household Water Plan Template** — a fill-in document each member household completes identifying its water sources in priority order, current storage capacity, treatment methods, and estimated days of self-sufficiency. The division maintains completed templates for all member households.

**Community Water Treatment Guide** — an operational guide covering the full treatment sequence for each source type, community-scale chlorination with calcium hypochlorite, slow sand filtration construction and operation, and testing protocols.

**Water Source Assessment Framework** — a general methodology for mapping and assessing local water sources: what data to collect, where to find it, how to evaluate source reliability and contamination risk, and how to document findings for ongoing use.
