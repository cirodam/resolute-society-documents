# Healthcare Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Healthcare Division exists to ensure that every member of the Resolute Society has access to basic healthcare — preventive and acute — today and under whatever conditions tomorrow brings. It does this by building community-level capacity to manage illness, injury, and chronic disease, and by training the lay health workers who carry that capacity into every household.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around health — an instrument of collective action, not a provider of services. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by what the community can do.

Every human community until very recently maintained its own health capacity — midwives, herbalists, lay healers, and knowledgeable elders who managed most of what ailed people without physicians or hospitals. The professionalization of medicine has concentrated that knowledge in institutions and credentials, leaving most communities helpless when those institutions are unavailable. That knowledge is recoverable. The community health worker is not an innovation — it is a recovery of something that was always there.

The division builds on a foundation of individual and household competence. Basic first aid — stopping bleeding, treating a burn, recognizing when something is serious — is a competency every member is expected to hold. Household medication management, first aid kit maintenance, and awareness of each member's health needs are household-level responsibilities. The division does not substitute for these. It builds on them, extends beyond them, and through the community health worker program supports members in maintaining them. A household that cannot manage its own first aid kit or does not know its members' chronic conditions has not delegated those tasks to the division — it has left a gap that no division can fully fill.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no member goes without basic healthcare due to inability to access or afford professional care
- That the community maintains the preventive health practices — hygiene, nutrition, disease surveillance — that reduce the burden of illness before it requires treatment
- That chronic disease management continues for members who depend on it, even when pharmaceutical supply chains and clinical infrastructure are disrupted
- That health knowledge — preventive care, acute care, chronic disease management, maternal care, mental health — is actively held broadly enough across the community that it cannot be lost

## Core Functions

- **Assessment and Triage** — evaluating illness and injury to determine severity, appropriate care, and when referral to higher-level care is necessary
- **Preventive Health** — maintaining the hygiene, nutrition, immunization, and disease surveillance practices that reduce illness before it requires treatment
- **Acute Care** — managing common illness and injury at the community level when professional care is unavailable or inaccessible
- **Chronic Disease Management** — supporting members with ongoing conditions — diabetes, hypertension, asthma, and others — through monitoring, medication management, and lifestyle support
- **Maternal and Reproductive Health** — prenatal care, community-supported birth, and postpartum support
- **Mental Health** — crisis response and sustained psychological support for members under hardship
- **Pharmaceutical Management** — stockpiling, storing, and managing medications and medical supplies against disruption of normal supply chains
- **Community Health Worker Program** — training lay members to carry health knowledge and basic care capacity into every household in the community

## Assessment and Triage

Assessment and triage is the division's gateway function — the systematic evaluation of illness and injury to determine what is happening, how serious it is, and what response it requires. Done well, it directs resources to where they are needed most and prevents the division from attempting care beyond its capacity. The most important skill in triage is recognizing when a situation exceeds community-level care and requires referral to higher-level medical services while those services remain available.

**The primary triage question is: is this person sick or not sick?** Experienced medical providers develop an intuitive sense of this — a gestalt formed by years of pattern recognition. Lay health workers need explicit criteria to make the same determination. The division's training program should build those criteria into its community health workers.

**Vital signs are the foundation of assessment.** Every community health worker should be trained to measure and interpret four basic vital signs:

- **Respiratory rate** — breaths per minute, counted for a full 30 seconds and doubled. Normal in adults is 12-20. Rates above 24 or below 10 are red flags. Respiratory rate is among the most sensitive early indicators of serious illness and is routinely overlooked because it requires only counting — no equipment. A patient breathing faster than normal is telling you something important.
- **Heart rate** — pulse at the wrist (radial) or neck (carotid), counted for 30 seconds and doubled. Normal adult range is 60-100. Rates above 120 at rest suggest significant physiological stress. A weak, thready pulse alongside a rapid rate suggests shock.
- **Blood pressure** — requires a cuff (sphygmomanometer). Normal is below 120/80. Systolic pressure below 90 is a warning sign of shock. The division should maintain functional blood pressure cuffs and train health workers in their use.
- **Temperature** — a fever above 103°F (39.4°C) in adults is significant; above 104°F (40°C) is a serious concern. Abnormally low temperature (below 96°F/35.6°C) indicates hypothermia or, in the context of infection, severe sepsis.

**Consciousness level** is assessed using the AVPU scale: Alert (fully conscious and oriented), Voice (responds to verbal stimulus), Pain (responds only to painful stimulus), Unresponsive (no response). Any level below Alert in the context of acute illness requires immediate escalation. Sudden changes in mental status — confusion, agitation, uncharacteristic behavior — are among the most reliable red flags for serious illness or injury.

**Capillary refill time** is a simple bedside test for circulatory adequacy. Press firmly on a fingernail or the skin of the sternum for five seconds, then release. Color should return within two seconds. Delayed return — the skin stays pale or white for longer — indicates poor peripheral perfusion and warrants urgent evaluation.

**Red flags that require immediate escalation** beyond community-level care:

- Signs of stroke: sudden facial drooping, arm weakness, or speech difficulty (FAST — Face, Arms, Speech, Time)
- Signs of heart attack: chest pain or pressure, especially with radiation to the jaw or left arm, sweating, and shortness of breath
- Respiratory distress: labored breathing, use of neck and shoulder muscles to breathe, inability to complete sentences, blue tint to lips or fingertips
- Signs of sepsis: fever or abnormally low temperature combined with rapid heart rate, rapid breathing, and altered mental status
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Loss of consciousness or seizure
- Severe allergic reaction: throat swelling, difficulty breathing, hives combined with low blood pressure

**The division must know its limits.** Community-level care can manage a wide range of illness and injury — but it cannot replace surgery, advanced airway management, intensive monitoring, or many pharmaceutical interventions. A community health worker who recognizes what exceeds their capacity and refers appropriately is more valuable than one who attempts care beyond it. The division should maintain current knowledge of what higher-level care is available and accessible in its area — hospitals, urgent care, emergency services — and treat referral as a standing option, not a last resort.

## Preventive Health

Preventive health is the division's highest-leverage function. Most of the disease burden a community faces under disrupted conditions is preventable — not through advanced medicine, but through the same practices that communities have used for centuries: clean water, safe waste disposal, food hygiene, and early identification of illness before it spreads. The division's preventive health program puts those practices into consistent community-wide practice.

**Handwashing** is the single most effective individual disease prevention measure available. It must be treated as non-negotiable community practice — not a suggestion. Twenty seconds with soap and water before food handling, after toilet use, after contact with sick individuals, and after handling waste prevents the fecal-oral transmission chain that drives diarrheal disease, hepatitis A, typhoid, and many other illnesses. The division's preventive health program reinforces handwashing across all settings, coordinates with the Sanitation Division on handwashing station infrastructure, and treats lapses as a genuine health risk to address rather than a personal choice to respect.

**Disease surveillance** is the division's early warning system. Community health workers in regular contact with member households are positioned to notice patterns — multiple households reporting similar symptoms, illness clustering in time or location, unusual severity in what appears to be a common illness. The division should establish a simple, consistent reporting mechanism that aggregates what health workers observe and allows the division to detect outbreaks early. An outbreak identified when three households are sick is manageable; the same outbreak identified when thirty households are sick is not.

**Vector control** addresses the insects and animals that transmit disease. Mosquitoes transmit West Nile virus, dengue, and — in some regions — malaria. Ticks transmit Lyme disease and Rocky Mountain spotted fever. Rodents carry hantavirus, leptospirosis, and historically, plague via fleas. Vector control is not glamorous, but it is consequential: standing water should be eliminated or treated (a tablespoon of vegetable oil on a rain barrel kills mosquito larvae by suffocating them); tick exposure should prompt full-body checks and prompt removal with fine-tipped tweezers; rodent entry points into food storage should be sealed. The division should understand which vectors are prevalent in its region and ensure members know how to reduce exposure.

**Immunization status** should be understood and maintained across the community. Tetanus is a particular priority — it is endemic in soil and enters through wounds, and protection wanes over time (boosters are recommended every ten years). Hepatitis A spreads through contaminated food and water and becomes more likely under disrupted sanitation conditions. The division should maintain awareness of which members are current on core immunizations and facilitate access to vaccines while routine healthcare infrastructure is functional.

**Nutritional deficiencies** are rare under normal conditions but can emerge rapidly under prolonged food disruption. Scurvy — vitamin C deficiency — develops within weeks of a diet lacking fresh fruits and vegetables; early symptoms are fatigue, joint pain, and easy bruising before the classic bleeding gums appear. Pellagra (niacin deficiency) and beriberi (thiamine deficiency) can develop from monotonous grain-heavy diets without dietary diversity. These are not historical curiosities — they are predictable consequences of food disruption that the division should recognize and prevent through dietary guidance and, where necessary, supplementation. The division should maintain a supply of basic vitamins as part of its pharmaceutical stockpile.

**Dental health** is consistently omitted from community health planning and should not be. Dental infections do not resolve on their own — untreated, they spread along tissue planes into the jaw, throat, and airway, and can become rapidly life-threatening. Basic preventive dental hygiene — brushing with fluoride toothpaste, flossing, and limiting sugar intake — prevents the vast majority of dental problems. The division should include dental hygiene supplies in its household preparedness recommendations and should ensure at least some community health workers have basic training in recognizing dental infections that require escalation.

**Isolation and contact protocols** limit the spread of infectious illness once it appears. A sick member should be isolated from the household's common spaces as much as practical — separate sleeping area, separate eating utensils, a dedicated caregiver rather than rotating household members in and out. The caregiver should wear a mask and wash hands after any contact. These are not extraordinary measures; they are what families did routinely before the germ theory of disease was formally understood, because the practical observation that sick people make others sick predates the scientific explanation by centuries.

**Overcrowding amplifies disease transmission** in ways that are predictable and important to plan for. Tuberculosis, influenza, meningitis, and respiratory illness generally spread most readily in crowded, poorly ventilated spaces. Any scenario that concentrates members — emergency shelter, community gatherings during an outbreak — creates transmission risk that the division must actively manage through ventilation, spacing, and symptom screening at entry.

## Acute Care

The division maintains the capacity to manage common illness and injury at the community level — not as a substitute for professional medical care, but as the layer of care that functions when professional care is unavailable, inaccessible, or unnecessary for the condition at hand. The majority of what makes people ill or injured does not require a physician or hospital. What it requires is correct knowledge applied promptly.

**Wound care** is among the most common acute care needs and among the most mismanaged. The most important intervention for any wound is irrigation — flushing with large volumes of clean water under pressure to physically remove bacteria, debris, and devitalized tissue. A 20-milliliter syringe with an 18-gauge needle or a squeeze bottle with a small tip generates sufficient pressure. The volume matters: a laceration should be irrigated with at minimum 100-200 milliliters of clean water, more for heavily contaminated wounds. Hydrogen peroxide and iodine damage tissue and impair healing — they should not be used inside wounds. Clean water is more effective and less harmful.

Wound closure should not be rushed. Wounds contaminated with soil, debris, or animal material, and wounds more than six to eight hours old, carry high infection risk if closed. Delayed closure — leaving the wound open, packed loosely with moist gauze, and closing it three to five days later once clean — is often the correct approach. Signs of wound infection require immediate attention: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, or pain after the first 48 hours; purulent discharge; red streaks extending from the wound toward the body (lymphangitis — a serious sign of spreading infection); fever.

**Bleeding control** follows a clear priority sequence. Direct pressure — firm, sustained, uninterrupted pressure using a cloth or dressing held firmly against the wound — is always the first intervention. Pressure must be maintained for at least ten to fifteen minutes without lifting to check; checking interrupts clot formation. For deep wounds that do not respond to surface pressure, wound packing — pushing gauze firmly into the wound cavity and applying pressure from above — controls bleeding that direct surface pressure cannot. For limb bleeding that does not respond to pressure, a tourniquet applied two to three inches above the wound and tightened until bleeding stops is correct and life-saving. The time of application must be noted. Tourniquets cause tissue damage if left in place for extended periods — they are a bridge to definitive care, not a final solution. The division should stockpile commercial tourniquets (CAT or SOFTT-W) and train community health workers in their use.

**Oral rehydration therapy (ORT)** is the most important acute care intervention for diarrheal disease and one of the most significant medical advances of the 20th century. Most people do not know how it works or why it is more effective than simply drinking water. Diarrheal illness causes secretory loss of fluid and electrolytes from the gut — the intestinal wall actively pumps sodium and water out. Glucose actively co-transports sodium back across the intestinal wall through a separate pathway that remains functional even during secretory diarrhea. The WHO oral rehydration solution formula exploits this mechanism: one liter of clean water, six level teaspoons of sugar, and half a level teaspoon of salt. This solution is absorbed far more efficiently than plain water or sports drinks. It has saved tens of millions of lives globally. Every community health worker should be able to prepare it from memory and recognize when it is indicated. Signs of dehydration requiring ORT: dry mouth, decreased urination, dark urine, sunken eyes, and poor skin turgor (skin that remains tented when pinched rather than snapping back). Diarrheal illness that includes blood in the stool, high fever, signs of dehydration that don't respond to ORT, or duration beyond ten days requires escalation.

**Fever management** requires understanding what fever is. Fever is a physiological defense response — not the disease itself, but the body's attempt to create a less hospitable environment for pathogens. A moderate fever in an adult (under 103°F/39.4°C) without other serious symptoms may not require treatment. Fever above 103°F warrants acetaminophen or ibuprofen and monitoring; above 104°F (40°C) is a serious concern requiring aggressive management and escalation if it does not respond. Cooling measures — removing clothing, wet cloths to the neck and armpits, increased ventilation — supplement medication. Aspirin should not be given to children or adolescents with fever due to the risk of Reye's syndrome.

**Burns** are assessed by depth and area. Superficial burns (reddening only) and partial-thickness burns (blistering, painful) covering small body surface areas can be managed at community level. Full-thickness burns — white or charred skin, painless due to nerve destruction — and any burns covering large body surface areas, involving the face, hands, feet, genitals, or joints, or caused by chemicals or electricity require escalation. The first treatment for any burn is cool running water for twenty minutes — not ice, not butter, not toothpaste. Cool water limits tissue damage; everything else either adds harm or does nothing. The burn area can be estimated using the rule of nines: the head is 9% of body surface, each arm is 9%, the chest is 18%, the back is 18%, each leg is 18%, and the perineum is 1%.

**Anaphylaxis** is a life-threatening allergic reaction requiring immediate treatment. Signs include sudden onset of hives, throat tightening, difficulty breathing, rapid heartbeat, and falling blood pressure following exposure to an allergen — typically food, insect sting, or medication. Epinephrine is the only effective treatment and must be administered immediately — not after antihistamines, not after observation. The division must stockpile epinephrine auto-injectors and train community health workers in their use. After epinephrine administration, the person must be monitored for a minimum of four to six hours — biphasic reactions, in which symptoms return hours after initial resolution, occur in up to 20% of cases.

**Musculoskeletal injuries** — sprains, strains, and suspected fractures — are among the most common acute care presentations. Sprains and strains are managed with rest, ice (20 minutes on, 20 off), compression, and elevation. Suspected fractures should be splinted in the position found — forcing a limb into anatomical position risks additional injury — and the circulation, sensation, and movement below the injury checked before and after splinting. An open fracture (bone visible through skin) is a surgical emergency and must be referred immediately if any referral pathway exists.

**Heat illness** exists on a spectrum. Heat exhaustion — heavy sweating, weakness, cool and pale skin, rapid pulse, nausea — is managed by moving the person to a cool environment, removing excess clothing, and giving fluids. Heat stroke is a medical emergency: hot and often dry skin, confusion or loss of consciousness, and body temperature above 104°F. Aggressive cooling — immersion in cold water, ice packs to the neck, armpits, and groin, fanning with wet skin — must begin immediately and continue until temperature falls below 101°F. Heat stroke kills.

**Hypothermia** occurs when core body temperature drops below 95°F (35°C). Mild hypothermia presents as shivering, confusion, and clumsiness; severe hypothermia as cessation of shivering (a dangerous sign — the body has exhausted its warming mechanism), severe confusion, and eventually loss of consciousness. Treatment is removal of wet clothing, insulation from the ground and environment, and warming the core — not the extremities. Applying heat to the extremities drives cold blood to the core and can worsen the condition. Warm, sweet liquids if the person is conscious and can swallow. Hypothermia victims who appear dead should be rewarmed before death is declared — a person is not dead until they are warm and dead.

**Pain management** at community level relies primarily on acetaminophen and ibuprofen, used correctly. Acetaminophen addresses pain and fever but not inflammation; ibuprofen addresses pain, fever, and inflammation but should be avoided in dehydrated patients and used with caution in those with kidney or stomach concerns. Alternating the two at appropriate intervals provides more sustained analgesia than either alone. For dental pain specifically, clove oil — which contains eugenol, a natural anesthetic — applied directly to the affected tooth or socket provides meaningful temporary relief. Willow bark, which contains salicin (the precursor to aspirin), has real analgesic activity and has been used for centuries; it is less potent than pharmaceutical aspirin but a legitimate option when pharmaceuticals are unavailable.

## Chronic Disease Management

Chronic disease management is the division's most consequential function in extended disruption. The acute emergencies get attention — wounds, infections, fractures — but the members who will be most immediately endangered by loss of institutional healthcare are those managing ongoing conditions that require consistent medication, monitoring, and lifestyle support. Diabetes, hypertension, asthma, epilepsy, and thyroid disease do not pause for a crisis. The division must have a plan for every member with a chronic condition before that plan is urgently needed.

**The division should maintain a chronic disease registry** — a record of which members have which conditions, what medications they require, current dosages, and prescribing physicians. This is the foundation of the division's chronic disease function. Without it, the division cannot anticipate needs, cannot help members stockpile appropriately, and cannot identify who is at acute risk when supply chains are disrupted. The registry should be maintained with the same care as the Mortuary Division's advance planning records, with the same expectation of privacy.

**Diabetes** is the most complex chronic condition the division will manage. Type 1 diabetes requires insulin to survive — there is no dietary or lifestyle substitute. Type 2 diabetes can often be managed substantially through diet, exercise, and oral medication, with insulin requirements reduced or eliminated in some cases. The division must understand which members have which type.

Insulin storage is a non-obvious critical detail. Unopened insulin should be refrigerated at 36-46°F (2-8°C), where it remains effective until its expiration date. Once opened or unrefrigerated, insulin remains usable for approximately 28 days at room temperature below 77°F before significant degradation occurs. This window is the planning horizon for power disruptions — a member who loses refrigeration has roughly four weeks of effective insulin remaining, not days. In hot climates or prolonged heat, that window shrinks. A clay pot evaporative cooler (two nested clay pots with wet sand between them) can maintain temperatures 20-30°F below ambient — a meaningful intervention for insulin storage without electricity.

Blood glucose monitoring requires test strips, which expire, degrade in heat and humidity, and cannot be substituted. The division should assist diabetic members in maintaining an adequate strip supply. Hypoglycemia — blood sugar too low — presents as shaking, sweating, rapid heartbeat, confusion, and hunger, and is treated with 15-20 grams of fast-acting sugar: four ounces of juice, regular soda, or glucose tablets. This must be available to every diabetic member at all times. Severe hypoglycemia with loss of consciousness is a medical emergency.

**Hypertension** is common, largely asymptomatic, and dangerous precisely because members may not prioritize its management when they feel well. Uncontrolled hypertension causes stroke, heart attack, and kidney failure — none of which announce themselves in advance. The division should monitor blood pressure across its membership as a standing function and treat hypertension management as non-discretionary for affected members. Lifestyle interventions — weight management, exercise, sodium reduction, and a diet emphasizing vegetables, fruits, whole grains, and low-fat dairy — meaningfully reduce blood pressure and reduce medication requirements. Many antihypertensive medications are inexpensive generics that can be stockpiled at low cost. Hypertensive crisis — blood pressure above 180/120 accompanied by severe headache, visual changes, or chest pain — requires immediate escalation.

**Asthma** management depends on distinguishing rescue medication from maintenance medication. Rescue inhalers (albuterol and similar short-acting bronchodilators) stop an acute attack; maintenance inhalers (inhaled corticosteroids) prevent attacks over time. Members who use only rescue inhalers without maintenance medication are not optimally managed — the division should support members in obtaining and using both. Trigger identification and avoidance — dust, mold, smoke, animal dander, pollen — reduces attack frequency. A peak flow meter allows members to monitor their lung function objectively and recognize declining control before an acute attack. An asthma attack that does not respond to rescue inhaler after two to three doses, or that produces cyanosis or inability to speak, is a life-threatening emergency.

**Epilepsy** presents particular risk in disruption because seizure medications must not be stopped abruptly. Abrupt discontinuation of anti-epileptic drugs can trigger status epilepticus — continuous or rapidly recurring seizures that are immediately life-threatening. This is not a gradual or manageable risk; it is acute. The division must treat epilepsy medication supply as a critical priority and assist affected members in maintaining the largest stockpile their prescribers will support. Seizure first aid for community health workers: protect the person from injury by clearing the area, do not restrain them, do not put anything in their mouth, position them on their side, and time the seizure. A seizure lasting more than five minutes, or a person who does not regain consciousness between seizures, requires emergency escalation.

**Thyroid disease** — particularly hypothyroidism — requires ongoing medication (levothyroxine) but is relatively manageable if medication is available. Levothyroxine is inexpensive, stores well under appropriate conditions (cool, dry, away from light), and is one of the most commonly prescribed medications in the United States. Members with hypothyroidism should maintain a multi-month supply. Untreated hypothyroidism produces fatigue, cold intolerance, cognitive impairment, and eventually cardiovascular complications. Hyperthyroidism is more difficult to manage without pharmaceutical intervention and warrants particular attention in the division's chronic disease registry.

**Psychiatric medications** managed as chronic medications include antidepressants, antipsychotics, mood stabilizers, and anxiolytics. Abrupt discontinuation of SSRIs and SNRIs produces discontinuation syndrome — dizziness, nausea, sensory disturbances, and flu-like symptoms — that is distressing but generally not life-threatening. Abrupt discontinuation of benzodiazepines (such as diazepam, lorazepam, or alprazolam) can produce life-threatening withdrawal including seizures. Lithium, used for bipolar disorder, has a narrow therapeutic window and requires monitoring — toxicity and deficiency are both dangerous. The division should identify members on these medications and treat their supply with the same urgency as insulin and anti-epileptic drugs.

**The community health worker's role in chronic disease** is regular, structured engagement with affected members — not waiting for them to present with a problem, but proactively checking in. This includes monitoring vital signs (blood pressure, blood glucose where applicable), assessing medication supply and adherence, identifying early signs of decompensation, and coordinating with the division when supply issues are emerging. The community health worker who visits a diabetic member monthly and catches a dwindling insulin supply with two weeks remaining is doing exactly what this function requires.

## Maternal and Reproductive Health

Maternal health is among the most consequential areas where community-level care can prevent death. Most maternal and infant mortality is not inevitable — it results from the absence of basic monitoring and skilled attendance at birth. The division's maternal health function is not about replacing obstetric medicine; it is about ensuring that the most common and most serious complications are recognized early, managed correctly where possible, and escalated immediately when they are not.

**Prenatal care begins before pregnancy is visible.** Folic acid supplementation — 400 to 800 micrograms daily — in the first trimester, ideally beginning before conception, prevents neural tube defects including spina bifida. This is one of the highest-impact single nutritional interventions in medicine. The division should ensure folic acid is part of its pharmaceutical stockpile and that members of reproductive age know to begin supplementation immediately upon suspecting pregnancy. Iron supplementation addresses the anemia that affects a majority of pregnant women and contributes to maternal mortality from hemorrhage — a woman who is already anemic tolerates blood loss poorly. The division should maintain prenatal vitamins with adequate iron.

**Blood pressure monitoring during pregnancy is non-negotiable.** Preeclampsia — hypertension combined with organ involvement developing after 20 weeks of pregnancy — is one of the leading causes of maternal death worldwide and is detectable through blood pressure monitoring before it becomes life-threatening. Blood pressure above 140/90 in pregnancy is abnormal and requires escalation. Warning signs that indicate imminent danger: severe headache, visual disturbances (flashing lights, blurred vision), upper right abdominal pain, sudden severe swelling of the face and hands. These signs indicate eclampsia risk — a condition that can progress to seizures and death. Any pregnant member presenting with these symptoms requires immediate referral to emergency obstetric care if it exists.

**Fetal position** can be assessed by an experienced attendant through Leopold maneuvers — a systematic palpation of the abdomen that identifies which part of the baby is presenting at the pelvis. A breech presentation (buttocks or feet first) significantly complicates vaginal delivery and warrants obstetric referral if available. The skill of identifying fetal position before labor begins is not technically complex and should be part of training for members who will serve as birth attendants.

**The physiology of normal labor** is something every birth attendant must understand. Labor progresses through three stages: the latent phase (contractions establishing and cervix dilating to 6 centimeters), the active phase (rapid dilation from 6 to 10 centimeters), and the pushing phase followed by placental delivery. Most labors progress without intervention. The birth attendant's primary role is presence, support, monitoring, and recognition of the point at which things are not progressing normally.

**Position during labor and birth** matters more than most people realize. Upright positions — standing, kneeling, squatting, hands and knees — use gravity to assist descent and generally shorten labor. The supine lithotomy position (flat on the back, feet in stirrups) standard in hospital births actually works against gravity and narrows the pelvic outlet; it exists for provider convenience, not maternal benefit. The division's birth attendants should support members in using positions that work with physiology.

**Delayed cord clamping** — waiting at least one to three minutes after birth before clamping and cutting the umbilical cord — allows transfer of 80 to 100 milliliters of additional blood from the placenta to the newborn, reducing risk of iron deficiency anemia in infancy. This requires nothing beyond knowledge and a willingness to wait.

**Postpartum hemorrhage** is the leading cause of maternal death worldwide and can occur rapidly after birth. Risk factors include prolonged labor, large baby, twins, and prior hemorrhage. Management begins immediately after birth: uterine massage (firmly massaging the fundus through the abdomen to stimulate contraction), immediate breastfeeding (which triggers oxytocin release and uterine contraction), and monitoring blood loss. The division should stockpile misoprostol — a prostaglandin medication on the WHO essential medicines list that is inexpensive, heat-stable at room temperature for up to two years, and highly effective in preventing and treating postpartum hemorrhage. 600 micrograms placed under the tongue immediately after delivery of the placenta reduces postpartum hemorrhage risk significantly. Hemorrhage that does not respond to these measures is a life-threatening emergency.

**Newborn care in the first minutes of life** requires warmth, airway clearance, and feeding. Newborns lose heat rapidly — hypothermia is a leading cause of neonatal death and sets in far faster than most people expect. Skin-to-skin contact with the mother immediately after birth is the most effective warming method and simultaneously supports the initiation of breastfeeding. Breastfeeding should be initiated within the first hour. A newborn who does not breathe spontaneously at birth should be dried and stimulated; if no response, basic rescue breathing is indicated.

**Complications requiring immediate escalation:** cord prolapse (the umbilical cord descends before the baby — a true obstetric emergency requiring the presenting part to be held off the cord until surgical delivery); prolonged active labor without progress (more than four hours without advancement in a woman who has given birth before, or six hours in a first-time mother); signs of infection in labor or postpartum (fever above 100.4°F, uterine tenderness, foul-smelling discharge); and any convulsion in a pregnant or recently delivered woman, which indicates eclampsia and is immediately life-threatening.

**Postpartum depression** must be distinguished from the baby blues — the normal tearfulness and emotional lability of the first two weeks postpartum. Postpartum depression is persistent (beyond two weeks), involves inability to care for the baby, hopelessness, and withdrawal, and requires active support and intervention. Postpartum psychosis — rare but serious — presents as confusion, hallucinations, or rapid cycling mood changes in the days after delivery and is a psychiatric emergency. The division's mental health function should maintain particular awareness of postpartum members.

**Reproductive health more broadly** includes family planning knowledge and access. When pharmaceutical contraception is unavailable, barrier methods (condoms) provide reliable pregnancy prevention and also reduce transmission of sexually transmitted infections. Fertility awareness methods — tracking cycle length and basal body temperature to identify fertile windows — require consistent practice to be effective and should be understood as a complement to barrier methods rather than a replacement. The division should not treat reproductive health as a specialized topic separate from general health — it is central to the wellbeing of a significant portion of the community.

## Mental Health

Mental health in extended crisis conditions is not a peripheral concern. Prolonged stress, loss, uncertainty, and disruption of normal life create significant psychological burden across a community — not as individual pathology, but as a predictable collective response to abnormal conditions. The division's mental health function addresses both the acute end (members in crisis) and the sustained end (maintaining community psychological health over time). Neither is optional.

**Resilience is the norm, not the exception.** The starting point for community mental health is not a presumption of fragility. Most people who experience trauma — even severe trauma — do not develop lasting psychiatric illness. Strong social connection, a sense of purpose and agency, honest communication, and community ritual are the most powerful protective factors available, and the community's structure provides all of them. The division's mental health function should be built around strengthening these factors, not around treating the community as a collection of patients waiting to break.

**Psychological First Aid (PFA)** is the evidence-based framework for supporting people in the immediate aftermath of crisis. It is not counseling, not debriefing, and not asking people to process trauma before they are ready. Its principles are: ensure safety, support calming, promote connection to others, support self-efficacy (the sense that a person can manage their situation), and instill realistic hope. A community health worker trained in PFA knows how to be present with a person in acute distress — listening without rushing to fix, helping them identify immediate needs, and connecting them to support — without causing harm. Critical incident stress debriefing, which encourages people to recount traumatic experiences in group settings shortly after the event, has been shown to be ineffective and in some cases harmful; it should not be used.

**Acute psychiatric crisis** — a member who is acutely suicidal, experiencing psychosis, or in a mental health emergency — requires immediate response. For suicidal crisis, the first and most important skill is asking directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?" Asking does not increase risk — this is a persistent and consequential myth. A direct question creates permission to answer honestly and is the entry point for effective support. Risk factors that increase urgency include a specific plan, access to lethal means, prior attempts, recent loss, and social isolation. The most effective immediate intervention after safety assessment is reducing access to lethal means — firearms, medications, other means — which is one of the most evidence-supported suicide prevention strategies available.

**Means restriction** is not a comfortable conversation, but it is a necessary one. When a member is at elevated suicide risk, reducing access to lethal means — temporarily securing firearms with another trusted community member, removing or securing medications — significantly reduces risk. The method matters because most suicidal crises are time-limited; a person who survives a crisis usually does not make another attempt. Means restriction saves lives not by resolving the crisis but by extending time through it.

**Grief** is not a mental health disorder, and it should not be treated as one. Loss — of people, of stability, of the life a person expected — is a legitimate and necessary experience. The community's role is to hold space for it, not to rush it toward resolution. Community rituals of mourning — memorial services, shared acknowledgment of loss — serve a genuine psychological function that the Mortuary Division helps to provide. The division's mental health function should resist any impulse to move bereaved members too quickly toward recovery and should maintain sustained contact and support well past the point where external attention typically fades.

**Trauma and post-traumatic stress** in a community experiencing sustained disruption may be collective as well as individual. An entire community can share a trauma — a major loss, a violent event, prolonged extreme hardship — and the responses to it. Collective trauma is addressed through collective means: honest community communication, shared acknowledgment of what has happened, maintained ritual and social structure, and the preserved sense that the community is still itself. The division should support community leadership in providing these things rather than attempting to manage collective trauma through individual clinical intervention.

**Substance use** increases under stress and in communities experiencing disruption, and the division must be prepared for the consequences. Alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal are potentially life-threatening — both can cause fatal seizures. A community member who has been drinking heavily daily and abruptly stops is at significant medical risk; the division must know this and treat it as a medical situation requiring monitoring, not simply a personal matter. Opioid withdrawal is extremely uncomfortable but rarely directly life-threatening in otherwise healthy adults. Any member who discloses heavy, daily use of alcohol or benzodiazepines should be flagged to the division's medical knowledge base and monitored through any period of reduction or cessation.

**Children's mental health** requires particular attention. Children manifest stress differently than adults — behavioral regression, somatic complaints (stomach aches, headaches with no physical cause), sleep disturbances, and changes in behavior. Children are more resilient than adults often assume — especially children who maintain stable relationships with caring adults. Shielding children from difficult realities is less helpful than honest, age-appropriate communication. A child who is told what is happening, in language they can understand, and who sees trusted adults managing difficulties with competence, is in a far better position than one who senses fear and confusion without explanation.

**Structural protective factors** should be understood as part of the division's mental health program. Regular community gatherings and maintained rituals preserve the social fabric that buffers against psychological deterioration. Structured daily routine provides psychological stability. Meaningful contribution — people who have real work and real roles in the community's functioning — cope significantly better than those in passive roles. Physical activity has a robust protective effect against depression and anxiety. Sleep is both a symptom and a driver of mental health: disrupted sleep worsens nearly every mental health condition and impairs function in ways that compound under stress.

**The community health worker's mental health role** is not therapy — they are not therapists, and the division should not position them as such. It is regular contact, active listening, identification of members in crisis, and connection to whatever support the community can provide. The community health worker who checks in on an isolated member weekly, who notices behavioral changes and responds to them, and who knows when a situation exceeds what they can handle and escalates accordingly is doing precisely what this function requires.

## Pharmaceutical Management

The division's pharmaceutical function ensures that medications and medical supplies are available to the community when normal supply chains are disrupted. This requires advance planning, correct storage, an accurate picture of member needs, and an honest understanding of what pharmaceutical alternatives can and cannot provide. It is not about stockpiling everything — it is about stockpiling the right things, storing them correctly, and knowing what they can do.

**Medication expiration dates are not what most people think.** The printed expiration date is the date through which the manufacturer guarantees full potency under ideal storage conditions — not the date the medication becomes unsafe or ineffective. The FDA's Shelf Life Extension Program, which tested large stockpiles of military pharmaceuticals, found that the vast majority of medications remained at least 90% potent years beyond their labeled expiration dates. Most solid medications — tablets and capsules — remain effective well past expiration if stored properly. The division should not discard expired solid medications during a supply disruption without understanding this.

Genuine exceptions exist and must be known. **Insulin** degrades meaningfully once opened or unrefrigerated and should not be used significantly past expiration. **Epinephrine** in auto-injectors degrades and the solution changes color — a yellow or brown tint indicates degradation; clear solution with some color change may still provide meaningful dose but at reduced potency. **Nitroglycerin** is highly volatile and loses effectiveness quickly once the bottle is opened. **Liquid medications** — including liquid antibiotics — degrade faster than solids and should be treated more conservatively. **Tetracycline antibiotics** are historically associated with degradation to toxic compounds; while modern formulations may behave differently, caution is warranted.

**Storage conditions are the primary determinant of medication longevity.** Heat, humidity, and light degrade most medications faster than time does. The bathroom medicine cabinet is one of the worst storage locations — heat and humidity from showers accelerate degradation. Medications should be stored in a cool (below 77°F), dry, dark location: a closet, a dedicated storage box kept away from heat sources, or in cool seasons, a cool pantry. Refrigeration is required for insulin, certain liquid antibiotics, and vaccines. Freezing damages many medications, including insulin, and should be avoided.

**The division's pharmaceutical stockpile** should address predictable community needs across three categories: general medical supplies, over-the-counter medications, and chronic disease medications.

*General medical supplies:* tourniquets (CAT or SOFTT-W); hemostatic dressings (QuikClot or equivalent); irrigation syringes; sterile gauze and bandages in multiple sizes; medical tape; examination gloves; N95 respirators; thermometers; blood pressure cuffs; pulse oximeters; blood glucose meters with test strips; epinephrine auto-injectors; misoprostol (for postpartum hemorrhage); oral rehydration salts; prenatal vitamins with folic acid and iron.

*Over-the-counter medications:* acetaminophen and ibuprofen (in adequate quantity — these are foundational to the acute care function); diphenhydramine (antihistamine, useful for allergic reactions and as a sleep aid); loperamide (antidiarrheal — note: loperamide is contraindicated in dysentery with bloody diarrhea, where it traps pathogens in the gut); antacids; docusate sodium (stool softener); clotrimazole (topical antifungal); hydrocortisone cream; povidone-iodine solution for wound area preparation; topical antibiotic ointment; basic vitamins including vitamin C, vitamin D, and a B-complex for nutritional gap coverage.

*Chronic disease medications:* the division assists affected members in maintaining a minimum 90-day supply of every chronic disease medication they depend on. This requires member cooperation with their prescribers and the division's active support in navigating that process. The division should identify and maintain awareness of which chronic medications are available as inexpensive generics — most are — since cost is often the barrier to adequate stockpiling.

**Antibiotic stewardship** matters even — especially — when supply is limited. Unnecessary antibiotic use drives resistance that makes antibiotics ineffective for everyone. Most upper respiratory infections are viral and do not benefit from antibiotics. The division must train community health workers to distinguish infections that require antibiotics from those that do not: bacterial pneumonia (fever, productive cough, rapid breathing, one-sided chest pain) is very different from a common cold. Signs that suggest bacterial infection and may warrant antibiotics: symptoms worsening rather than improving after five to seven days, localized infection spreading (cellulitis extending, lymphangitis), high fever persisting beyond three to five days, purulent discharge from a wound that is not resolving. The division should treat its antibiotic supply as finite and irreplaceable for as long as resupply is uncertain.

**Herbal and traditional medicine alternatives** have a real but limited role and must be represented honestly. Clove oil (eugenol) provides genuine topical dental anesthesia. Willow bark contains salicin, which metabolizes to salicylate — the basis of aspirin — and provides real though modest analgesic and anti-inflammatory activity. Honey has genuine antimicrobial properties due to its low water activity, hydrogen peroxide content, and in the case of Manuka honey, methylglyoxal; it is appropriate for use on wound surfaces in some situations. Garlic has documented antimicrobial activity in laboratory settings that does not translate reliably to clinical practice. The division should be straightforward about what the evidence supports and what it does not. Herbal alternatives should be understood as complements to and partial substitutes for pharmaceuticals in specific situations — not as equivalent replacements.

**Controlled substances** — opioids, benzodiazepines — cannot be stockpiled by the division without legal authorization. Members who depend on these medications are among the most acutely vulnerable to supply disruption. The division should identify these members through its chronic disease registry and support them in maintaining the largest prescription supplies their providers will authorize, while being transparent about the limits of what the division can do if supply is interrupted.

## Community Health Worker Program

The community health worker program is the division's delivery mechanism — the structure through which health knowledge and basic care reach every member household, not just those who seek the division out. It is also the clearest expression of the knowledge recovery mission: every human community maintained lay health workers before medicine was professionalized. The barefoot doctors of rural China, the promotores de salud in Latin American communities, the traditional birth attendants and herbalists that every village had — these were not inadequate substitutes for real medicine. They were real medicine, adapted to what communities actually needed and what was actually available. The community health worker program recovers that function.

**The CHW model works on household assignment.** Each community health worker is responsible for a defined set of households — typically ten to twenty, depending on the worker's capacity and the community's density and health needs. The CHW makes regular scheduled visits to each household, not just responding to acute needs. The relationship built through consistent contact is what makes the model effective: a CHW who knows a household's baseline recognizes deviation from it. A CHW who notices that a diabetic member seems more fatigued than usual at a routine visit, or that a hypertensive member's blood pressure is trending upward, or that a household has been less present than normal, is catching things that would not surface through reactive care.

**Training should be organized around knowledgeable community members teaching others.** Physicians, nurses, paramedics, midwives, experienced first responders, and community members with relevant specialized knowledge are the teachers — not formal curricula imported from outside the community, though those can supplement. The division should identify who in the community holds health knowledge and build its training program around them. A retired nurse is a resource. A paramedic is a resource. A member who has managed a child's Type 1 diabetes for fifteen years is a resource. The division's role is to identify, organize, and sustain the teaching relationships that distribute knowledge broadly.

**Core competencies for every community health worker:**

- Vital signs measurement and interpretation — respiratory rate, heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, AVPU consciousness scale
- Wound care — irrigation, closure decisions, signs of infection, dressing changes
- Bleeding control — direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet application
- Oral rehydration therapy — preparation, indications, signs of dehydration, when to escalate
- Recognizing and responding to red flags — the conditions and signs that require immediate escalation beyond the CHW's scope
- Chronic disease monitoring — routine visits for members with diabetes, hypertension, asthma, epilepsy, and other ongoing conditions; tracking medication supply, vital signs, and early signs of decompensation
- Maternal home visits — prenatal monitoring, blood pressure checks, danger sign recognition, birth preparation, postpartum support
- Mental health first aid — psychological first aid principles, suicide risk recognition and response, de-escalation, active listening
- Medication management support — understanding what members are taking, storage conditions, supply levels, and when to alert the division
- Hygiene and vector control education — handwashing, safe water handling, sanitation practices, local vector risks

**CHWs must know their limits as clearly as they know their skills.** The ability to recognize when a situation exceeds CHW-level care is not a gap in the program — it is a core competency. A CHW who attempts care beyond their training causes harm; a CHW who recognizes the boundary and escalates correctly saves lives. The division should train this skill explicitly and create clear escalation pathways so that CHWs always know who to contact and how when they reach the edge of what they can manage.

**Record keeping at the household level** should be simple enough to sustain under field conditions and complete enough to be useful. Each CHW maintains a basic record for every household they cover: members, chronic conditions, current medications and supply levels, most recent vital signs for those being monitored, and any concerns or open issues. These records are not for bureaucratic compliance — they are the CHW's working tool and the division's early warning system. Patterns visible in aggregate across the CHW's households — or across CHWs reporting to the division — are what allow the division to catch emerging problems before they become crises.

**Supervision and ongoing support** must be a standing division commitment. CHWs are not independent operators — they are part of a structured program that includes regular gatherings for case discussion, shared learning, and community among the CHWs themselves. These gatherings serve multiple purposes: continuing education, supervision of complex cases, and prevention of the burnout and secondary trauma that are real risks for lay health workers embedded in their communities and bearing witness to hardship without the professional distance that clinical training provides. The division should treat CHW wellbeing as an operational priority — a burned-out or overwhelmed CHW is a gap in coverage.

**Coverage equity** must be designed into the program from the start. The households most likely to be missed by conventional healthcare — those with language barriers, physical limitations, social isolation, or distrust of formal systems — are also the most likely to be missed by a program that relies on self-identification of need. CHW household assignments should be reviewed for coverage gaps, and the division should prioritize assigning CHWs who share language and cultural background with the households they serve wherever possible.

## Division Relationships

**Mortuary**
The Healthcare Division and Mortuary Division are each other's closest partners. Healthcare members are the most likely to determine or confirm cause of death — a determination with direct consequences for how remains are handled. The two divisions must coordinate immediately on any death where communicable disease is suspected, and the Mortuary Division's death pattern surveillance is a primary input into the Healthcare Division's outbreak detection. Information flows in both directions continuously.

**Water Security**
Safe water is a health prerequisite. Waterborne illness — diarrheal disease, hepatitis A, typhoid, cholera — is directly preventable through clean water and safe handling, and directly treated through the ORT and acute care protocols the division maintains. The Healthcare Division should treat water quality as a standing concern and coordinate with Water Security on any illness pattern that suggests a water source issue.

**Sanitation**
The fecal-oral disease transmission chain that Sanitation exists to break is the mechanism behind many of the illnesses the Healthcare Division treats. The two divisions share a common purpose: Sanitation prevents disease at the source; Healthcare catches what gets through. The divisions should coordinate on hygiene promotion across the community and on any illness pattern that may indicate a sanitation failure.

**Food Security**
Nutrition is preventive medicine. Nutritional deficiencies — vitamin C, iron, B vitamins — produce clinical illness that the Healthcare Division will need to recognize and treat. The division should advise Food Security on the nutritional adequacy of community food supplies and escalate early when dietary patterns suggest deficiency risk. Conversely, Food Security's distribution function is a channel through which the Healthcare Division can reach households during routine operations.

**Housing Security**
Shelter is a health prerequisite. Exposure, overcrowding, inadequate heating or cooling, and housing instability all produce direct health consequences — hypothermia, heat illness, accelerated disease transmission, and the chronic stress and mental health effects of insecurity. The Healthcare Division should maintain close coordination with Housing Security and treat housing stability as a health variable, not a separate domain.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on building its community health worker program, completing the chronic disease registry, maintaining preventive health practices, and developing pharmaceutical and supply stockpiles. As conditions deteriorate and institutional healthcare becomes less accessible, acute care, chronic disease support, and the CHW household visit program become more central. The division does not change what it does — it scales what it does.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Provide acute care for illness and injury at community level
- Monitor and support members with chronic disease
- Maintain the CHW household coverage program across the community
- Identify and respond to emerging disease patterns before they become outbreaks
- Support maternal health through prenatal, birth, and postpartum care
- Respond to mental health crises and maintain community psychological health

In an epidemic or mass casualty scenario, all of these functions must operate simultaneously and at elevated scale. The knowledge, personnel, supplies, and relationships to do so must be in place before they are urgently needed.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Build the chronic disease registry**
The division's first task is identifying every member with a chronic condition, understanding their medication requirements, and assessing their current supply situation. This is the most acute vulnerability the division faces — members who will be endangered fastest by loss of institutional healthcare are those already depending on it. The registry is the foundation of the pharmaceutical management function and must be completed before it is urgently needed.

**2. Train the first cohort of community health workers**
The division should identify members with health knowledge — clinical or experiential — and begin training the first CHW cohort before household assignments are formalized. Training does not need to be comprehensive before it begins to be useful. Community health workers in the field providing basic monitoring and preventive health education are delivering value while their training continues.

**3. Stockpile pharmaceutical and medical supplies**
With the chronic disease registry in hand and the first CHW cohort developing, the division builds its pharmaceutical stockpile — general supplies first, chronic disease medications in coordination with affected members, and specialized items (epinephrine, misoprostol, ORT materials) as standing priorities.

**4. Establish full household coverage**
CHW assignments are formalized across the membership, with each household assigned to a specific worker. The regular visit schedule begins. The division establishes its record-keeping system and starts aggregating household-level data into division-level awareness.

**5. Operate community programs**
Preventive health education, disease surveillance, maternal home visits, and chronic disease monitoring become ongoing programs rather than startup efforts. The CHW program matures from first cohort to a structured system with supervision, continuing education, and clear escalation pathways.

## Division Documents

The Healthcare Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt.

**Community Health Worker Training Guide** — a comprehensive instructional resource covering all core CHW competencies: vital signs, wound care, bleeding control, ORT, triage and red flags, chronic disease monitoring, maternal home visits, mental health first aid, and medication management. Designed to support both initial training and ongoing reference.

**Household Health Record Template** — a standardized record maintained by each CHW for every assigned household, capturing member roster, chronic conditions, current medications and supply levels, vital sign baselines, and open concerns. The working tool of the CHW household visit program.

**Chronic Disease Registry** — a division-level record of all members with chronic conditions, their medications, dosages, prescribing relationships, and current supply status. Maintained with appropriate privacy protections and reviewed regularly.

**Pharmaceutical Stockpile Guide** — an inventory framework and reference document covering what to stockpile, in what quantities, correct storage conditions, expiration date guidance, and appropriate use for each item. Includes guidance on herbal alternatives where evidence supports them.

**Maternal Health Home Visit Protocol** — a structured protocol for prenatal and postpartum home visits: what to assess at each stage, danger signs requiring escalation, birth preparation checklist, postpartum monitoring schedule, and breastfeeding support guidance.

**Triage and Red Flags Reference** — a quick-reference document for community health workers covering the red flag conditions that require immediate escalation, vital sign thresholds, and the escalation pathway for each category of emergency.
