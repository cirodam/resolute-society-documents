# Climate and Meteorology Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Climate and Meteorology Division exists to ensure that the Resolute Society understands the environment it inhabits — what the weather is doing now, what it is likely to do, how the climate is shifting over years and decades, and what the living world is signaling about conditions that instruments alone cannot fully capture. It does this through continuous observation, careful record-keeping, instrument maintenance and construction, and the interpretation of what it observes into a public record available to every member and every division.

The Division does not make decisions. It makes observation available. Agriculture decides when to plant. Water Security decides when to increase storage. The Watch decides when to shelter members. The Division provides what those decisions depend on — an honest, current, and historically grounded picture of environmental conditions — and holds that record in trust for the whole Society.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for four things:

- That the Society maintains continuous weather observation and short-term forecasting capacity, independent of external services and internet-connected infrastructure
- That long-term climate and phenological records are kept with sufficient rigor and continuity to be useful across decades
- That the Division maintains functioning instruments and holds the knowledge to construct them from locally available materials when manufactured instruments fail
- That all observations, records, and interpretations are maintained in a form accessible to every member and every division that needs them

## Core Functions

- **Weather Observation and Forecasting** — continuous monitoring of current conditions and short-term forecasting using instruments, traditional indicators, and pattern recognition
- **Climate Record-Keeping** — systematic long-term recording of temperature, precipitation, wind, humidity, and extreme weather events, building a picture of local climate behavior over time
- **Phenological Observation** — tracking the seasonal timing of biological events in the living world — plant flowering, leaf-out, and senescence; bird arrival and departure; insect emergence; animal behavior — as both a climate record and a source of predictive signal
- **Instrument Maintenance and Construction** — keeping observation instruments in working order and holding the knowledge and materials to build replacements from local resources
- **Record Maintenance and Access** — organizing and maintaining all Division records in forms that are durable, navigable, and genuinely accessible to anyone who needs them

## Weather Observation and Forecasting

Short-term forecasting — what is the weather likely to do over the next hours and days — is the Division's most time-sensitive function. It requires continuous observation, pattern recognition built from local experience, and the discipline to communicate forecasts honestly, including their uncertainty.

**Instrument-based observation** is the foundation. The Division maintains a weather station at each hub — at minimum a thermometer, barometer, rain gauge, wind vane, and hygrometer. These instruments together provide the readings from which local forecasts are derived. The Division should also maintain an anemometer for wind speed and a maximum-minimum thermometer to capture temperature extremes between observation periods. Instruments at multiple hubs allow the Division to observe spatial variation across the Society's geographic area, which matters for understanding approaching systems and local microclimatic differences.

**The barometer is the most important single instrument for short-term forecasting.** Atmospheric pressure and its rate of change carry more predictive information than any other single reading. A falling barometer indicates approaching unsettled weather; a rapidly falling barometer indicates it is approaching fast. A rising barometer after a storm indicates clearing. The Division should ensure that every observation station records barometric readings and trend, not just current pressure, and that observers understand what rate of change means for the near-term forecast.

**The non-obvious value of multiple readings per day** is that trend matters more than absolute value. A temperature of 85°F at noon tells you little. A temperature of 85°F at noon that was 78°F two hours ago tells you something about the air mass moving through. The Division should establish a standard observation schedule — minimum twice daily, ideally four times — and maintain it consistently so that trend data is available.

**Pattern recognition from local experience** is the complement to instrument readings that develops over years of observation. General meteorological principles describe how weather systems behave broadly. Local knowledge describes how they behave in this specific geography — how the Appalachian terrain to the northwest shapes incoming systems, how urban heat islands affect local temperature and precipitation, how the timing of sea breezes from the coast modifies summer afternoon conditions in coastal areas, which cloud formations reliably precede the severe convective storms that characterize Georgia summers. This knowledge is not available from a textbook. It accumulates through years of watching, recording, and comparing what was predicted against what arrived. The Division builds it deliberately.

**Traditional and ecological indicators** extend observation beyond what instruments capture directly. Certain plant behaviors, animal activities, and atmospheric phenomena are reliable short-term weather signals that have been observed and recorded across cultures for centuries. Ring around the moon indicates high cirrus cloud — often a precursor to an approaching warm front. Swallows flying low indicate falling atmospheric pressure. Morning red sky has genuine meteorological basis in the behavior of dust particles in dry versus humid air. The Division should build and maintain a local inventory of traditional indicators that have proven reliable in the Society's specific geography, understanding that these are probabilistic signals rather than certainties and should be weighed alongside instrument readings.

**Communicating forecasts honestly** requires naming uncertainty explicitly rather than presenting forecasts as predictions. The Division communicates what the observations suggest, how confident that interpretation is, and what conditions would change the forecast. A forecast that says "rain likely tomorrow, probability decreasing if the barometer holds" is more useful than one that simply says "rain tomorrow." Members and divisions making decisions based on Division forecasts need to know how much weight to place on them.

**Georgia-specific weather patterns** the Division should develop deep familiarity with include: the severe convective storm season from spring through early fall, which produces tornadoes, large hail, and damaging straight-line winds with limited warning; the tropical weather season from June through November, which brings the possibility of tropical storms and hurricane remnants producing extreme rainfall and flooding; the ice storm vulnerability of late winter, when warm moist air from the Gulf meets cold continental air and produces freezing rain that is more disruptive than snow; and the summer drought patterns driven by the Bermuda High, which can produce extended dry periods with serious agricultural implications. Each of these patterns has observable precursors the Division should know.

## Climate Record-Keeping

Climate is not weather. Weather is what is happening now. Climate is the pattern of what happens over decades. A Society that knows its local climate — not from published averages but from its own continuous records — has an asset that no outside source can provide: ground truth for this specific place, this specific microclimate, accumulated over the Society's own history.

**Standard climate variables** the Division records at each observation point include: daily maximum and minimum temperature, total daily precipitation, daily barometric pressure readings, wind direction and speed, humidity, and cloud cover. These are the variables from which climate summaries, trend analyses, and anomaly detection are derived. Consistency matters more than precision — a thermometer that reads one degree low but does so consistently across years produces useful trend data. An inconsistently calibrated instrument produces noise.

**Extreme event recording** is a distinct obligation. The Division maintains a record of every significant weather event — hard freezes, late frosts, drought periods, flood events, severe storms, extended heat events — with dates, severity, duration, and observed effects. This record becomes increasingly valuable over decades as the frequency and intensity of extreme events can be assessed against the historical baseline.

**Record format and durability** require deliberate attention. Digital records are searchable and easy to analyze but vulnerable to hardware failure, software obsolescence, and power disruption. Physical records are durable but require organized storage and consistent format to remain navigable over decades. The Division should maintain both — digital for current accessibility, physical as the archival record — and should use standardized formats that will be legible to members who were not present when the records were created. A climate record that cannot be read or interpreted by future members has not served its purpose.

**The value of long records compounds over time.** A one-year record tells you what this year's weather was. A ten-year record tells you something about variability. A thirty-year record tells you what the climate of this place actually is — its averages, its extremes, its seasonal patterns, and how reliably those patterns hold. A fifty-year record begins to reveal long-term trends. The Division should understand that it is building something for future members as much as for present ones, and should treat the integrity and continuity of the record as an obligation to those who will use it decades from now.

**Climate change is an active variable, not a background condition.** Georgia's climate is shifting — average temperatures are rising, precipitation patterns are becoming less predictable, the timing of seasons is changing, and the frequency of extreme events is increasing. The Division's long-term records are the instrument through which the Society tracks these changes in its specific location rather than relying on regional or national averages that may not capture local conditions. The Division should maintain explicit awareness of how observed local patterns compare to historical baselines and should communicate significant departures to the General Assembly and relevant divisions as they emerge.

## Phenological Observation

Phenology is the study of the timing of recurring biological events — when plants flower and leaf out, when birds arrive and depart, when insects emerge, when animals breed, when leaves turn and fall. These events are exquisitely sensitive to temperature and climate conditions. Their timing, recorded consistently over years and decades, constitutes one of the most sensitive climate records available — and one that requires no instrument beyond attentive observation and a notebook.

**Why phenology belongs in this Division** is not only that it produces climate data. It is that sustained phenological observation requires the Division's members to be present in the living world in a particular way — attending carefully and consistently to what is happening in the land around them, season after season. That quality of attention is itself valuable, independent of the data it produces. It is ecological restraint made into a practice.

**Standard phenological observations** the Division should maintain records of include: first and last frost dates; first flowering dates for key native species (redbud, dogwood, tulip poplar, black locust, goldenrod, aster); leaf-out and fall color timing for deciduous species; arrival and departure dates for migratory bird species; first emergence dates for key insect species including pollinators; and breeding activity timing for amphibians, which are among the most sensitive biological indicators of temperature change. The Division should develop a standardized observation list appropriate to the Society's specific geography and ecology, and should maintain it consistently across years.

**Phenological records have direct agricultural value.** The timing of plant flowering and insect emergence tells farmers when pollination windows open, when pest pressure begins, and when planting conditions are appropriate. The relationship between lilac bloom and the emergence of the European pine shoot moth, or between forsythia bloom and crabgrass germination, are examples of the phenological correlations that experienced growers learn and use. The Division's phenological records, accumulated over years, become a locally calibrated planting and management guide more accurate than any generic published calendar.

**The relationship between phenological and climate records** is mutually reinforcing. Phenological timing can be explained by climate records — a warm February that triggers early flowering is visible in both the temperature record and the bloom dates. Climate trends can be detected through phenological records — the consistent advance of spring arrival dates across multiple species is one of the clearest biological signals of warming temperatures. The Division should analyze its phenological and climate records together, not in isolation.

**Phenological observation is distributed work.** Unlike instrument readings, which require specific equipment at specific locations, phenological observation can be performed by any member who is paying attention. The Division should actively engage the broader membership in observation — establishing simple reporting protocols so that members who notice a first bloom or a first migrant can contribute to the record. This distributes the observational network across the Society's full geographic area and draws members into a relationship with the living world that the Division's own staff cannot maintain alone.

## Instrument Maintenance and Construction

The Division's observational capacity is only as reliable as its instruments. Manufactured instruments will eventually fail, wear, or become unavailable. The Division holds the knowledge to maintain what it has and to construct replacements from materials that can be sourced or produced locally.

**Maintenance** requires regular calibration against known standards, inspection for damage or drift, cleaning and protection from the elements, and documentation of any instrument's known error so that readings can be corrected. Each instrument at each observation station should have a maintenance record and a calibration schedule. The Division should maintain a stock of common repair materials — replacement glass tubing for thermometers, calibration weights, oil for moving parts — against the need for field repair.

**The barometer** can be constructed from locally available materials in two forms: the mercury barometer, which requires glass tubing and mercury; and the aneroid barometer, which uses a sealed metal capsule that flexes with pressure change. The mercury barometer is simpler in concept and more straightforwardly constructible, though it requires careful handling of mercury. The Division should hold the knowledge to construct and calibrate both.

**The thermometer** in its simplest form is a sealed glass tube containing a liquid whose volume changes predictably with temperature — alcohol being preferable to mercury for most applications because it remains liquid at temperatures below mercury's freezing point. Calibration against ice water (0°C / 32°F) and boiling water (100°C / 212°F at sea level) provides two fixed reference points from which a scale can be established. The Division should be able to construct a functional thermometer and calibrate it against these reference points.

**The rain gauge** is among the simplest instruments to construct — a straight-sided container of known diameter, with a measuring tube of smaller diameter to amplify small rainfall amounts for easier reading. The relationship between the collection area and the measuring tube diameter determines the multiplication factor. Construction requires only basic materials and accurate measurement.

**The wind vane and anemometer** require moving parts and a reference orientation to magnetic or true north. A wind vane can be constructed from sheet metal or carved wood. A cup anemometer — the rotating cup design — can be built from lightweight materials and calibrated against a known wind speed through comparison with a reference instrument or through calculation from cup geometry. The Division should hold construction knowledge for both.

**The hygrometer** in its simplest traditional form uses human hair, which changes length predictably with relative humidity, connected to a needle indicator. More robust options use wet-bulb and dry-bulb thermometer pairs — the difference in their readings, consulted against a standard table, gives relative humidity. The Division should maintain competency in wet-bulb/dry-bulb measurement as the most reliable locally constructible humidity measurement method.

## The Public Record

Every observation, record, and analysis the Division produces is maintained as a public record accessible to any member. The Division does not filter or restrict access. It does not decide which divisions need the information and which do not. It maintains the record and makes it available.

**The record should be navigable.** Accessibility is not merely availability. A record that exists but cannot be found or understood by someone who was not present when it was created is not genuinely accessible. The Division should organize its records with future users in mind — clear labeling, consistent formats, explanatory context that allows someone approaching the record for the first time to understand what they are reading.

**Physical and digital copies** should be maintained simultaneously. Physical records at each hub give local access without requiring network connectivity. The Division's digital holdings allow search and analysis that physical records cannot support. Neither alone is sufficient.

**Periodic summaries** translate the raw record into forms useful for planning. Annual climate summaries, multi-year trend analyses, seasonal forecasts based on accumulated pattern knowledge, and phenological calendars derived from the Division's records are all outputs that make the raw data accessible to members and divisions who need its conclusions but not its detail. The Division should produce these summaries on a regular cycle and make them available alongside the raw record.

## Division Relationships

**Agriculture**
The closest operational relationship. Agricultural planning depends on frost dates, planting windows, rainfall patterns, drought risk, and extreme weather timing — all of which the Division's records directly address. The Division should maintain particular attention to the variables most relevant to agricultural decision-making and should ensure that Agricultural division officers know how to access and interpret Division records.

**Water Security**
Rainfall records, drought pattern analysis, and seasonal precipitation forecasting directly inform water storage, sourcing, and treatment decisions. Extended dry periods visible in the Division's records should be communicated to Water Security as part of the Division's routine record availability.

**Housing**
Extreme weather event records, wind patterns, flood history, and temperature extremes inform construction standards, site selection, and maintenance planning. The Division's long-term records are relevant to any significant housing infrastructure decision.

**Education**
The Division's phenological records and instrument construction knowledge are natural curriculum material — particularly for the ecological and practical formation components of the Society's curriculum. The Education Division should draw on Division expertise in developing curriculum related to ecological observation and traditional weather knowledge.

**All Divisions**
The Division's record is a commons. Any division planning activities sensitive to weather or climate — from harvest timing to construction schedules to event planning to emergency preparation — benefits from Division records and forecasts. The Division should communicate clearly how its records are organized and how to access them, so that members across all divisions can use them without requiring Division staff to interpret on request.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division maintains its observation schedule, updates its records, produces periodic summaries, calibrates and maintains instruments, and develops its local pattern knowledge through accumulated experience. This is continuous work — the value of the record depends on its continuity, and gaps in observation are gaps in the record that cannot be filled retrospectively.

As conditions deteriorate and external forecasting services become unreliable or unavailable, the Division's own observation and forecasting capacity becomes the Society's only source of weather intelligence. The transition should be invisible — the Division's capacity does not depend on external services and does not degrade when they fail.

At the floor, the Society can still read the sky. Members trained in traditional weather indicators, instrument-independent observation, and phenological signals retain meaningful forecasting capacity without any infrastructure. This is the oldest human relationship with the weather, predating every instrument, and it functions when nothing else does. The Division builds this knowledge into its members and into the broader Society as a standing practice, not an emergency fallback.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Establish observation stations at hubs**
Acquire or construct the core instrument set — thermometer, barometer, rain gauge, wind vane, hygrometer — at each hub. Establish the observation schedule and begin recording from day one. The record's value begins with its first entry.

**2. Develop local pattern knowledge**
Identify members with existing local weather knowledge — long-term residents, farmers, hunters, anyone who has been paying attention to local conditions for years. Their knowledge is the starting point for the Division's pattern library. Document it explicitly before it is lost.

**3. Establish the phenological observation network**
Develop the standardized observation list for the Society's geography. Train observers across the membership. Begin the phenological record alongside the climate record.

**4. Build instrument construction knowledge**
Identify or develop members with the knowledge to construct and calibrate each instrument type. Document construction processes in coordination with the Education Division. Acquire materials for at least one complete backup instrument set.

**5. Establish the record format and access system**
Determine the format for both digital and physical records. Establish the hub-based physical record holdings. Produce the first periodic summary within the first year.

## Division Documents

**Observation Log** — the primary daily record maintained at each hub observation station: date, time, temperature, pressure, precipitation, wind, humidity, cloud cover, and observer notes. Standardized format maintained consistently across all stations and all time.

**Extreme Event Record** — a separate log of significant weather events with dates, observed conditions, duration, and effects. Maintained alongside the observation log and cross-referenced with it.

**Phenological Record** — the annual record of first and last dates for each species and event on the Society's standardized observation list, with observer notes on conditions at time of observation.

**Local Pattern Library** — a living document accumulating the Division's knowledge of local weather patterns, traditional indicators proven reliable in this geography, and the relationships between observed conditions and subsequent weather. Updated as knowledge develops and tested against experience.

**Instrument Construction Manual** — construction and calibration instructions for each instrument type the Division maintains, with materials lists, tool requirements, and calibration procedures. Held in physical form and maintained in coordination with the Education Division.

**Annual Climate Summary** — a yearly synthesis of the observation record: monthly averages and extremes, precipitation totals, notable events, phenological highlights, and comparison to prior years. The primary document through which the raw record becomes accessible to members and divisions using it for planning.
