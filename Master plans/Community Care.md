# Community Community Care Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Community Care Division exists to ensure that no member of the Resolute Society goes without daily assistance and genuine human connection because of age, disability, or circumstance — and that the Society extends that same care to anyone in the surrounding community who needs it.

The division operates on two equal and inseparable fronts: practical task assistance and relational presence. A member who is fed, transported, and housed but who has no genuine connection to community life has been partially cared for. A member who is surrounded by people but cannot manage the basic tasks of daily living is in the same incomplete situation. The division treats both as real needs that require real response.

The axioms ground this plainly. Human dignity does not diminish with age or disability — a member who requires assistance does not become less worthy of the community's full attention and effort. Community is constitutive of the person — isolation is not merely unpleasant but genuinely harmful, damaging something essential to what a person is. And the generation that is now old built much of what the present community uses and stands on — care is not charity but obligation, the completion of a reciprocal relationship that spans time.

The Community Care Division is not a service department. It is the community organized around the fact that persons at every stage of life and every level of capacity have equal standing, and that a community that fails to act on that premise has failed something central to what it is.

The division's scope is defined by the baseline. Ordinary household life — cooking, cleaning, managing one's own affairs, maintaining social relationships — is a person and household responsibility. The division exists specifically for members who cannot meet that baseline because of age, disability, or circumstance. It does not absorb what capable households should handle themselves; it serves the members for whom the household tier is not sufficient.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no member who requires daily assistance goes without it, regardless of whether they seek it out
- That no member lives in sustained isolation from genuine community connection
- That the Society's care extends to anyone in the surrounding community who needs it, as capacity allows
- That care is delivered in ways that preserve and support the autonomy and dignity of those receiving it — not as charity dispensed to dependents, but as mutual obligation fulfilled between equals

## Core Functions

- **Needs Assessment** — identifying which members require assistance or are at risk of isolation, and understanding the specific nature of their needs
- **Daily Task Assistance** — practical help with the tasks of daily life: meals, transportation, errands, household tasks, and home maintenance within the division's capacity
- **Companion Program** — structured, sustained relationships between members and community companions who provide regular presence, conversation, and genuine connection
- **Community Integration** — active effort to keep members receiving care participating in the full life of the community, not cordoned off in a care track separate from everyone else
- **Crisis and Emergency Response** — ensuring that members who live alone or have limited mobility are reached first and reached reliably when conditions deteriorate
- **Coordination with Healthcare** — continuous communication with the Healthcare Division's community health workers, who share the household visit function with a different focus

## Needs Assessment

The division cannot respond to needs it does not know about. Needs assessment is how the division builds and maintains a complete picture of who requires what — not once, but continuously, as circumstances change.

**The assessment begins with the roster.** The division maintains a current record of all members who have identified as disabled, elderly, or otherwise requiring daily support, along with a summary of their specific needs and current situation. This record is not built from self-identification alone — many people who need help do not ask for it, and the division must not design a system that reaches only those willing to ask. Community health workers, neighbors, and members themselves are all sources of identification.

**The assessment covers both dimensions.** For each member the division serves, the assessment addresses practical needs — what tasks of daily living require assistance and at what frequency — and relational needs — how connected is this person to genuine community life, how often do they have meaningful contact with others, what risks of isolation are present. A member who has robust family and neighbor relationships but needs help with transportation has a different profile than a member who manages physical tasks adequately but has no one visiting them.

**Needs change.** A member recovering from surgery has temporary needs. A member with a progressive condition has changing needs. A member who loses a spouse may become isolated in ways that were not previously apparent. The division revisits its assessment for every member it serves regularly — not waiting for the member to report a change — and updates its support accordingly.

**The division extends its awareness to the surrounding community.** The Society's obligation to help anyone is not conditional on division capacity in any given week — it is a standing obligation that the division acts on as capacity allows and tracks honestly when it cannot. The division maintains awareness of elderly and disabled individuals in the surrounding community, prioritizes those who are most isolated or at most risk, and provides what assistance it can while being transparent about limits.

## Daily Task Assistance

Task assistance is the practical dimension of the division's work. It covers the concrete things that daily life requires — the things that become difficult or impossible for members who face age-related limitation, disability, or recovery — and provides them as a matter of standing obligation, not exceptional charity.

**Meals.** For members who cannot prepare their own food reliably, the division coordinates meal preparation and delivery. This may be organized through the Food Security Division's existing infrastructure or maintained by the Community Care Division directly. What matters is reliability — a member who depends on the division for food must receive it consistently, not as a function of whether a volunteer happened to be available that day. The division treats meal delivery as a structured commitment, not a best-effort service.

**Transportation.** Members who cannot drive or use public transportation independently need help getting to medical appointments, community gatherings, markets, and wherever else they need to go. The division coordinates transportation from within the member community — people who have vehicles and capacity — and treats isolation caused by lack of mobility as seriously as physical isolation.

**Errands and household tasks.** Grocery runs, prescription pickup, mail, and other routine errands that require mobility or physical capacity the member lacks. Household tasks — cleaning, laundry, minor home maintenance — that fall within the division's capacity and that the member cannot manage alone. The division does not do for members what they can do with minimal support; the goal is enabling the member to manage their life, not replacing their agency with division management.

**Home environment.** The division assesses the home environment of members it serves and coordinates with the Housing Security Division on modifications that would support independence — grab bars, ramps, reorganization of living spaces — rather than defaulting to increasing the level of human assistance when a physical change would serve the member better and preserve more of their autonomy.

**Pacing and dignity.** Task assistance is delivered at the pace of the person receiving it, not the schedule of the person providing it. A companion rushing through a meal delivery or a home task to get to the next stop is doing less than the work requires. The division trains its members in the practical and relational dimensions of task assistance together, because how care is given is part of what care is.

## Companion Program

The Companion Program is the division's response to isolation — not as an add-on to the practical work but as an equally primary function. Sustained social isolation is a documented health harm, not a mere quality-of-life concern. The axioms do not permit the Society to treat it as secondary.

**Companion assignments.** Each member identified as at risk of isolation — whether because they live alone, have limited mobility, have lost close relationships, or are otherwise cut off from regular human connection — is assigned a companion. The companion is a Society member who commits to regular, sustained contact: at minimum a weekly visit, more for members with greater isolation risk. The companion is not a visitor performing an obligation — they are a person in genuine relationship with the member they are assigned to.

**What companionship is.** A companion visit is not a wellness check with conversation as a side effect. It is time spent together as people — talking, shared activity, shared silence, the texture of actual relationship. The division trains companions in what genuine presence means and what it does not mean: it does not mean managing the member's emotions or providing informal therapy; it means showing up consistently, paying attention, and treating the person visited as a full human being whose thoughts, history, and preferences matter.

**Matching.** Companion assignments are made thoughtfully, with attention to shared interests, background, and temperament. A companion who has nothing to say to the person they visit will not sustain the relationship. The division invests time in matching rather than simply filling slots.

**Reciprocity.** Members receiving companions are not passive recipients. Where capacity allows, the relationship has reciprocal dimensions — a member who cannot leave their home may nonetheless have knowledge, skills, or experience that the companion can genuinely benefit from. The division actively seeks these dimensions and builds them into companion relationships where they exist, because reciprocity protects the dignity of the person receiving care and sustains the motivation of the companion providing it.

**Group connection.** Individual companion relationships are supplemented by structured group connection — regular gatherings that include members receiving care as full participants, not as a separate population being visited. The division coordinates with the Culture Division and community governance to ensure that community events, meals, and gatherings are physically and practically accessible to members with mobility or sensory limitations.

## Community Integration

The division's deepest failure mode is creating a parallel track — a care population whose daily lives are organized around the division and who are effectively separated from the full community life the Society offers. Community integration is the active work of preventing that failure.

**Members receiving care are full members.** They vote, they participate in governance, they are present at community gatherings, they contribute what they can in the forms they can. The division ensures that the practical barriers to this participation — transportation, physical access, communication — are addressed, and actively pushes back against any cultural drift toward treating members receiving care as a special category with lesser standing.

**The community's role.** Community integration is not solely the division's responsibility. The obligation to care for members who need it is the Society's obligation, expressed through and coordinated by the division but not confined to it. Neighbors, friends, and fellow members all carry part of this obligation. The division supports and structures that distributed care rather than attempting to concentrate it within the division alone.

**Visibility.** Members receiving care should be visible in community life — present at meals and gatherings, participating in decision-making, known by name to the broader membership. Invisibility is the precondition of neglect, and the division treats the visibility of those it serves as an operational concern, not an incidental outcome.

## Crisis and Emergency Response

Members who live alone, have limited mobility, or require daily assistance are the most vulnerable when conditions deteriorate rapidly — a severe weather event, a health crisis, a supply disruption, a communication failure. The division's crisis function ensures these members are not the last to be reached when conditions change.

**The division maintains a current emergency contact list** for every member it serves: who to contact if the member cannot be reached, what their specific medical and practical needs are, and what would constitute a crisis for this individual that might not be obvious to someone who doesn't know them.

**Check-in protocols are established before they are needed.** Every member living alone or at significant risk has an established check-in cadence with a specific division contact or companion. If check-in fails, escalation is immediate. The division does not wait to see if the member surfaces — it sends someone.

**The division coordinates with Healthcare** to ensure that crisis response accounts for medical needs. A member who requires insulin, who has seizures, or who has a condition that deteriorates rapidly under stress has those facts known by both divisions and by the companion or neighbor most likely to reach them first.

## Coordination with Healthcare

The Community Care Division and the Healthcare Division share the household visit function — the CHW visits to monitor health, the companion visits to sustain connection and assist with daily tasks. These are distinct functions performed by distinct people, but the information flows between them must be continuous and clear.

**Cross-referral is standing practice.** A CHW who observes signs of isolation, social withdrawal, or unmet practical needs during a health visit flags it to the Community Care Division. A companion who observes health changes — new symptoms, medication problems, declining physical function — flags it to the Healthcare Division's CHW assigned to that household. Neither division waits to be asked.

**Shared scheduling where possible.** Where CHW and companion visits are both occurring at a household, the divisions coordinate scheduling to avoid overwhelming the member with visits while also avoiding gaps. The goal is reliable, appropriate presence — not excessive management.

**The division does not provide healthcare.** Companions are not health workers and the division does not train them as such. When a companion encounters a health situation, they contact the CHW or Healthcare Division immediately. The line is clear: the companion is present for the person; the CHW is present for the health.

## Division Relationships

**Healthcare**
The primary coordination relationship. Shared household coverage, continuous cross-referral, and coordinated crisis response. The two divisions should meet regularly at the division level to review shared members, flag concerns, and address gaps in coverage.

**Housing Security**
Home environment and physical accessibility directly affect what level of daily assistance a member requires. The Community Care Division coordinates with Housing Security on modifications that increase independence, and alerts Housing Security to conditions in members' homes that require attention.

**Food Security**
Meal preparation and delivery for members who cannot manage food independently may run through Food Security's existing distribution infrastructure. The divisions coordinate on which members require meal support and at what frequency.

**Culture**
Community gatherings, events, and shared activities are the primary venue for community integration. The Community Care Division works with the Culture Division to ensure events are accessible and to actively facilitate the participation of members who would otherwise be unable to attend.

**Courier**
Transportation and errand coordination may overlap with the Courier Division's function depending on how each Society structures those services. Where overlap exists, the two divisions coordinate to avoid duplication and close gaps.

## Operational Posture

The division's work is continuous and does not follow a crisis cycle. Task assistance and companion visits happen whether conditions are stable or disrupted — and become more critical as disruption increases, precisely when they are hardest to maintain.

In stable conditions, the division focuses on building complete needs assessment, establishing companion relationships, and ensuring that no member requiring assistance is unaccounted for. Task assistance and companion visits operate on their regular schedule.

As conditions deteriorate, the division's crisis function activates. Check-in protocols become more frequent. Transportation and meal delivery are maintained as priority functions. The division accounts for every member it serves before turning attention elsewhere.

The companion relationship is the division's most fragile asset under stress. Companions are community members with their own circumstances and pressures. The division actively monitors companion capacity and distributes the work of caring for isolated members broadly enough that no companion is carrying more than they can sustain.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete the needs assessment**
Before any other function is operational, the division must know who it serves. This means identifying every member who requires daily assistance or is at risk of isolation — not waiting for self-identification — and building a complete record of their needs. This is the foundation on which everything else is built.

**2. Establish companion assignments**
With the assessment complete, the division makes initial companion assignments. Matching takes time; the division begins with the members at greatest isolation risk and builds outward. A companion relationship established before it is urgently needed is worth far more than one established in a crisis.

**3. Structure task assistance as a committed service**
Meal delivery, transportation, and errand support are organized as standing commitments with specific responsible members, not as ad hoc volunteer efforts. The division identifies who will do what for whom and on what schedule, and builds redundancy into every commitment so that a single person's absence does not leave a member without assistance.

**4. Establish Healthcare coordination**
The division establishes its working relationship with the Healthcare Division early — shared roster review, cross-referral protocols, and scheduling coordination. This relationship is most valuable when it exists before a specific coordination need arises.

**5. Extend awareness to the surrounding community**
The division begins building its awareness of elderly and disabled individuals in the surrounding community and identifies initial ways to extend assistance beyond the membership. Capacity limits what is possible; transparency is required about what the division can and cannot do.

## Division Documents

**Member Care Registry** — a current record of all members receiving division services: their practical and relational needs, current companion assignment, task assistance commitments, emergency contacts, and relevant health information shared by the Healthcare Division with appropriate consent.

**Companion Assignment Record** — a record of all active companion relationships: who is paired with whom, the visit schedule, and any open concerns flagged by the companion.

**Emergency Contact and Crisis Protocol** — for each member the division serves who lives alone or is at significant risk: emergency contacts, specific needs in a crisis, check-in cadence, and escalation steps if check-in fails.

**Community Outreach Record** — the division's record of individuals in the surrounding community who have been identified as potentially needing assistance, what contact has been made, and what the current status of any assistance being provided is.

**Coordination Log with Healthcare** — a standing record of cross-referrals and shared concerns between the Community Care Division and the Healthcare Division, reviewed at regular joint meetings.
