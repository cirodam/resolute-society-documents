# Construction Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Construction Division exists to build and maintain the community's built environment — ensuring that structures are safe, weather-tight, and structurally sound, and that the community has the capacity to construct new buildings when members need them. It does this by maintaining a clear picture of the community's structural condition, performing repairs and construction that exceed household capacity, and distributing building knowledge broadly enough that households can maintain their own structures without division involvement.

The technical standards that govern all community construction are defined in the Structural Integrity Regulation and the Fire Safety Regulation. The Construction Division does not repeat those standards — it organizes the community's building capacity around them.

The built environment is the frame inside which all other community life occurs. A roof that fails in winter is a healthcare emergency. A floor that cannot bear load is a safety emergency. A community that cannot build new housing for members who need it has a constraint on its capacity to grow and care for its members. Construction is not a peripheral function — it is the physical substrate of everything else.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That the community's existing structures are maintained in safe and habitable condition, and that structural problems are identified and addressed before they become emergencies
- That the community can build new structures — housing, food storage, community buildings, infrastructure — when members need them
- That all community construction meets the standards defined in the Structural Integrity Regulation and the Fire Safety Regulation
- That building knowledge is held broadly enough across the community that households can maintain their own structures, and that the division is not the only repository of construction skill

## Core Functions

- **Structural Assessment** — maintaining a current picture of the condition of all community structures, identifying problems before they become failures
- **Major Repair** — performing structural repairs, roof replacement, foundation work, and other repairs that exceed household capacity
- **New Construction** — building new dwellings, community buildings, food storage, and infrastructure
- **Liberty House Program** — designing, building, and deploying the community's standardized simple dwelling for members in housing need
- **Community Infrastructure** — maintaining paths, drainage, fencing, and shared structures
- **Natural Building** — developing and maintaining competency in construction methods that use local materials with minimal supply chain dependence
- **Tool and Material Management** — maintaining a community tool library, managing material supply, and salvaging usable materials from structures being removed or damaged
- **Skills and Teaching** — distributing building knowledge across the membership so that households hold the baseline they are responsible for

## Structural Assessment

The division maintains a current picture of the structural condition of every community structure. A structure that deteriorates without being noticed is a structure that eventually fails — and structural failure is rarely gradual. What appears as minor damage can be the visible surface of significant hidden degradation, particularly in the moisture-related decay pathways that the Structural Integrity Regulation addresses in detail.

**The annual inspection cycle.** Every structure used by community members — dwellings, food storage, common buildings, infrastructure — is inspected on an annual cycle. The inspection applies the assessment criteria from the Structural Integrity Regulation: visual inspection of all accessible structural elements for sagging, bowing, cracking, and rot; inspection of the foundation perimeter for settlement cracks, water staining, and heaving; inspection of the roof for sagging ridgeline, damaged roofing, and blocked drainage; inspection of connections for separation, missing fasteners, and corrosion.

**Priority inspection for high-consequence structures.** Community buildings with assembly occupancy — shared kitchens, meeting spaces, medical facilities — are inspected semi-annually and after any significant weather event. These structures carry higher occupancy than private dwellings and their failure has consequences for many people simultaneously. The Structural Integrity Regulation establishes higher safety factor requirements for assembly occupancy; the inspection program verifies these requirements are being maintained.

**The structural condition record.** Every inspection produces a written record: date, inspector, findings, and any corrective actions required. The record distinguishes between observations requiring immediate action, items requiring action within a defined timeline, and items to monitor. An inspection that produces a record but no follow-up on findings is not a functioning inspection program.

**Member-reported concerns.** Members who observe structural concerns in their dwellings — unusual settling, new cracks, doors and windows that have stopped fitting properly, soft spots in floors — should have a clear way to report them to the division. The division evaluates reported concerns promptly. Many structural problems are caught first by occupants noticing something has changed; this is valuable early warning the division should be organized to receive.

**Structural hazard response.** When an inspection or report reveals a condition that poses immediate occupancy risk, the division acts quickly. The appropriate response may range from a repair that can be completed in days to a temporary occupancy restriction while structural work is arranged. A structure that is known to be dangerous and that continues to be occupied because the division has not organized a response has not been assessed — it has been observed and ignored. The division does not allow that gap.

## Major Repair

Major repair is work that exceeds what households are responsible for managing themselves. The household document establishes the baseline: stop a leak, patch a wall, replace a broken window, weatherize. Everything beyond that threshold — structural repair, roof replacement, foundation work, repair of systems that affect habitability — is division territory.

**Roof work** is the most common major repair the division will perform. Roofs fail through neglect, storm damage, and the cumulative wear that makes flashing fail and decking rot. A roof that leaks is a structure that is actively deteriorating — moisture intrusion degrades structural members, insulation, and finishes in ways that compound over time. The division should treat roof repair as a time-sensitive priority and should not allow known roof problems to age without response.

Roof-to-wall connections are the most critical structural element in most roofs and the most consistently underbuilt in folk construction. The Structural Integrity Regulation's Section 1.10 covers this in detail — toenail connections that are standard in many roofs provide minimal uplift resistance. The division should identify structures with inadequate roof-to-wall connections during inspection and prioritize adding hurricane ties or equivalent connections as a standing improvement program.

**Foundation work** is technically demanding and the most consequential structural repair. Settlement, moisture damage, and inadequate original design are the most common foundation problems. Foundation repair — whether masonry, stone, or concrete — is Masonry Division work. The Construction Division's role is to identify the structural symptoms: settling, racking, floors that are out of level, doors and windows that no longer fit their frames. When these symptoms appear, the division engages the Masonry Division for evaluation and repair of the foundation itself, then coordinates the above-grade structural repair that follows. The two divisions should maintain a clear protocol for this handoff so that foundation problems are addressed at both levels rather than only above grade.

**Structural repair** covers the full range of repairs to load-bearing elements: floor joists and beams weakened by rot or overload, bearing walls with inadequate connections, columns and posts that have deteriorated at their bases. The Structural Integrity Regulation's coverage of wood rot (Section 1.12) is directly applicable — the nonlinear relationship between visible section loss and actual strength reduction means that what looks like minor rot may represent significant structural deficiency. Every structural repair should restore full load-carrying capacity, not approximate it.

**Lateral bracing additions** are among the highest-value repairs the division can perform on existing structures. The Structural Integrity Regulation's Section 1.9 establishes that lateral forces are the most consistently underestimated structural concern in folk construction, and that adding diagonal bracing to existing structures is straightforward and dramatically improves wind and seismic resistance. The division should treat lateral bracing improvement as a standing program for all community structures that were built without adequate lateral resistance.

**Coordination with Housing Security.** Members whose housing requires major structural repair may need temporary housing while work is performed. The division coordinates with Housing Security on the timeline and scope of major repairs so Housing Security can plan accordingly. A member displaced by necessary structural repair should not fall through a gap between divisions.

## New Construction

New construction is the division's most resource-intensive function. It requires organized labor, material supply, tool availability, and sufficient skill in the membership to execute the work correctly. The division builds this capacity continuously rather than assembling it when a project is underway.

**What the community builds** reflects the community's needs at the time. Dwellings are the most common new construction need, followed by food storage infrastructure, community buildings, and specialized facilities for divisions with facility needs (a covered work area for Textiles, cold storage for Food Security, a dedicated medical space for Healthcare). The division should maintain awareness of what facility needs exist across all divisions and what new housing need exists through Housing Security, so that construction priorities reflect actual community need.

**Site selection and preparation** precede every construction project. The Structural Integrity Regulation's Section 1.5 establishes site assessment standards — soil bearing capacity, drainage, and slope stability must be evaluated before any foundation is placed. A structure built on an unsuitable site cannot be made adequate by good construction above grade. The division applies these assessment criteria to every proposed construction site and declines to build on sites that are inappropriate, regardless of other pressures.

**The construction process** follows the load path logic the Structural Integrity Regulation establishes: foundation first, then structure, then enclosure, then finish. The division does not rush this sequence — a structure enclosed before its structure is sound is a structure that conceals its own problems. Each stage is inspected and confirmed adequate before the next stage begins.

**Labor organization.** Construction requires more labor than most households can provide, which is why it is division-level work. The division organizes construction labor from the membership — both members with construction skills and members without who can perform the unskilled labor that every construction project requires. Work parties organized for construction are among the most productive collective labor events a community can undertake, and the social dimension of members building together is not incidental.

**Documentation.** Every completed construction project produces a record: what was built, where, what materials were used, what the structural design is, and any as-built conditions that differ from the plan. This record serves the inspection program — the inspector who knows how a structure was built can assess it more accurately than one who doesn't — and survives the people who built it.

## Liberty House Program

The Liberty House is the community's standardized simple dwelling: a single-room structure of 400 to 800 square feet, designed to be built by community members from available materials, and intended to provide private, habitable shelter for a member or small household. It is the Construction Division's primary delivery mechanism for housing need identified by the Housing Security Division.

**The design is fixed, not custom.** A standardized design can be refined over time, built from templates, and executed by members with moderate building skill rather than requiring skilled design work for each unit. The division maintains the Liberty House design documentation — drawings, material quantities, and construction sequence — so that any member with basic construction experience can execute the build with division guidance. Repeated builds of the same design produce efficiency and quality that custom construction cannot match.

**The design must be buildable with available materials and tools.** A Liberty House design that requires commercial lumber in specific sizes, factory-made windows, and power tools is a design that cannot be executed when supply chains are stressed. The design should be developed with deliberate attention to what can be substituted, what can be built from timber that is locally sourced, and what the build looks like when power is unavailable. The division maintains variants of the design for different material conditions — not one design that assumes ideal supply.

**Liberty Houses cluster.** Where multiple units are needed, they should be sited together — the density of clustered units reduces the energy demand of each, supports the Transit Division's ability to serve them, and creates the conditions for residents to know each other. Isolated Liberty Houses should be the exception, not the pattern.

**Accessory dwelling units.** The Liberty House design should be adaptable as an accessory dwelling unit on an existing household's property — a backyard structure that creates a multigenerational or additional household without requiring a new site. This use supports the multigenerational housing model the Housing Security Division identifies as a positive resilience strategy.

**The division maintains build capacity.** A Liberty House can be built in a concentrated push of organized labor — the division should know how many person-days a build requires and maintain the realistic ability to organize that labor when Housing Security identifies a need. Build capacity is assessed annually and compared against the community's likely need.

## Community Infrastructure

Infrastructure maintenance covers the built elements that serve the entire community rather than specific households — paths, drainage, fencing, shared structures, and the facilities that divisions use.

**Paths and access.** Maintained paths and access routes make transit possible, allow rapid response by Fire and Watch, and enable members with limited mobility to participate in community life. The division maintains community paths at walkable condition year-round, addresses drainage problems that create muddy or impassable stretches, and ensures that the access route standards the Fire Safety Regulation requires for fire response are maintained.

**Drainage.** Water management at community scale — drainage channels, culverts, swales — requires coordination and maintenance that exceeds what individual households can manage. The division coordinates with the Agricultural Division's earthworks and water management function and ensures that drainage infrastructure is maintained before wet seasons rather than repaired after floods.

**Shared structures.** Covered work areas, community storage, and division-specific facilities require ongoing maintenance. The division applies the same inspection standards to shared structures as to dwellings, and treats deferred maintenance on shared structures as a division failure mode — shared structures tend to have diffuse ownership and fall through the maintenance gap that clear household ownership prevents.

**Division facility coordination.** Divisions that need dedicated facilities — food storage, medical space, tool storage, textile work areas — bring those needs to the Construction Division. The division assesses the need, designs a response that fits within the community's construction capacity, and builds and maintains the facility. It does not design facilities for divisions without understanding how those divisions will use them.

## Natural Building

Natural building — construction using locally available materials rather than commercial supply chain products — is the division's long-term resilience investment in wood-based and fiber-based construction methods. The Masonry Division holds the parallel competency in earthen, stone, and lime-based methods; the two divisions' natural building programs complement each other and should be developed in coordination.

**Straw bale** uses compressed straw bales as the wall structure, with earthen or lime plaster providing the moisture protection and structural skin. Straw bale construction produces walls of excellent insulating value — better than most other natural building methods — making it particularly appropriate in climates where heating energy is the primary concern. The structural behavior of straw bale construction requires careful attention to moisture management; a straw bale wall that gets wet fails quickly.

**Timber framing** uses heavy timber joined with traditional mortise-and-tenon and other wood joinery rather than dimensional lumber and metal connectors. It is more demanding of carpentry skill than light frame construction but produces structures that can be built from locally milled or hand-hewn timber and that last centuries when properly built and maintained. The division should develop at least basic timber framing competency, since it is the natural building method most directly applicable when commercial lumber is unavailable.

**The natural building principle:** develop each method through actual building, not only study. A division member who has built a timber-framed structure understands timber framing structurally, practically, and in its limitations. A division member who has only read about it does not.

## Tool and Material Management

Construction requires tools and materials in quantities and variety that individual households cannot reasonably maintain. The division manages these as community resources.

**The community tool library** holds the tools needed for construction work and makes them available to members performing division work and to households performing the household-level maintenance they are responsible for. The library includes hand tools — levels, squares, saws, hammers, chisels, planes, pry bars, drills — and larger shared equipment: ladders, scaffolding components, mixing equipment. Power tools are held in the library when power is available; the library also maintains the hand tool equivalents for when it is not.

Tool maintenance is a standing library function. A tool that is returned dull, damaged, or missing parts is a tool that fails the next user. The division establishes and enforces clear expectations for tool care and return condition, and performs regular maintenance — sharpening, cleaning, part replacement — as a standing function rather than an emergency response to failure.

**Material management** covers both new materials and salvaged materials. New material — lumber, fasteners, roofing material, insulation, concrete — should be maintained in a community stockpile at quantities sufficient to respond to repair needs without waiting for procurement. Material quantities should be assessed annually against likely repair needs and adjusted accordingly.

**Salvage** is the division's most resilient material source. Structures being removed or damaged in storms contain usable lumber, hardware, roofing, windows, and doors that can be recovered for community use. The division should establish salvage as a standing program — identifying salvage opportunities, organizing recovery labor, assessing salvaged material for quality, and incorporating it into the community material inventory. Salvaged lumber that is structurally sound, salvaged windows that are weathertight, and salvaged hardware in working condition reduce the community's dependence on commercial supply chains for repair and construction material.

**Material substitution knowledge.** The division should hold working knowledge of what can be substituted for commercial materials that may become unavailable. What species of locally available timber can substitute for commercial dimensional lumber? What can serve as roofing when commercial roofing is not available? This knowledge should be documented and held across multiple division members, not only the most experienced. The Masonry Division holds the parallel knowledge for masonry and earthen materials.

## Skills and Teaching

Construction skill is unevenly distributed in most communities, concentrated in members with professional or extensive experiential backgrounds. The division's teaching function works to distribute that skill more broadly — not to make every member a builder, but to ensure that the household-level maintenance baseline is achievable by every household, and that the division's own capacity is not concentrated in too few people.

**Household baseline training.** The household document establishes that households are responsible for basic structural maintenance: stopping a leak, patching a wall, replacing a window, weatherizing adequately. Not every household member knows how to do these things. The division should offer regular instruction in these specific skills — not a general construction course, but targeted teaching of the exact competencies households need. Members who complete this instruction are less likely to let small problems become large ones, and less likely to call the division for work that is properly their own.

**Division skill distribution.** The division should never be in a position where a single member holds a skill that no one else does. Structural assessment, roof work, and natural building methods should be held by enough division members that a departure or illness does not leave a gap. The Transmission principle from the Education Division applies here: knowledge held in one person is one generation from being lost.

**The apprenticeship model.** The most effective construction teaching is work-based — an experienced builder working alongside a member who is developing the skill, in real conditions on real projects. Division construction projects are teaching opportunities, not just production events. The division should organize its projects with this in mind, assigning less experienced members to work alongside experienced ones rather than sorting members by skill level and assigning each to different work.

**Teaching the Structural Integrity Regulation.** The regulation is detailed and technically rich. The division is responsible for ensuring its principles — load paths, connection requirements, lateral bracing, moisture management — are understood by every division member and by the community more broadly. Members who understand why structures fail make better decisions about structures they encounter, whether or not they are actively involved in construction.

## Division Relationships

**Forestry**
The division's primary source for locally produced lumber and timber. The Forestry Division mills logs to the dimensions Construction specifies; Construction's planning must account for the milling and seasoning lead time — lumber needed in eight months needs to be milled now. The two divisions should maintain a standing communication on anticipated lumber demand, species preferences, and timber framing stock requirements. Where Construction is sourcing salvaged lumber, Forestry provides the knowledge of wood species and structural condition assessment.

**Masonry**
The division that provides foundations for every structure Construction builds. The two divisions must maintain clear coordination on the foundation-to-structure handoff, on site drainage requirements, and on any structural masonry that is part of a building Construction is otherwise framing. The two divisions share assessment records for structures with both masonry and wood-frame elements. When structural symptoms in a wood-frame structure suggest a foundation problem, Construction is responsible for identifying the symptoms and engaging Masonry for the foundation-level response.

**Housing Security**
The primary driver of new construction demand. Housing Security identifies members who need housing; the Construction Division builds it. The two divisions must maintain close coordination on Liberty House readiness — Housing Security should know what the division's current build capacity is and the division should know what Housing Security anticipates needing. Coordination is also needed when major structural repair requires temporary displacement of occupants.

**Fire Division**
All community construction must meet the Fire Safety Regulation's standards. The Fire Division's inspection program and the Construction Division's build and repair program must be coordinated — construction that does not meet fire safety standards, or repair that inadvertently creates fire safety problems, must be caught before it is completed. The two divisions should have a standing review relationship so that new construction is reviewed against fire safety standards before it is finished.

**Energy**
Building envelope performance directly affects energy demand. The Construction Division and Energy Division should coordinate on insulation, weatherization, and passive design in all new construction and major retrofit. A Liberty House that is well-insulated and well-sited reduces the Energy Division's load on the members it houses. The Energy Division's Settlement Pattern section and the Construction Division's siting and clustering approach should be developed in alignment.

**Agriculture**
The Agricultural Division needs structures: covered storage for harvested crops, processing space, greenhouse structures, animal housing. The Construction Division builds and maintains these. The two divisions should coordinate on what facility needs Agriculture anticipates so the Construction Division can plan accordingly. Agriculture's earthworks work — swales, berms — and the Construction Division's drainage work overlap and should be coordinated rather than duplicated.

**Community Care**
Members served by Community Care often need physical modifications to their homes to support independence — grab bars, ramps, wider doorways, reorganization of living spaces. The Construction Division performs these modifications as a standing service coordinated with Community Care. A physical modification that allows a member to manage their own daily tasks is more resilient than an equivalent increase in the human assistance the Community Care Division provides.

**Education**
The Construction Division is a primary site for the apprenticeship model that the Education Division's Transmission section establishes as the deepest form of knowledge transfer. The two divisions should coordinate on identifying members who are developing construction skills and ensuring that appropriate apprenticeship relationships are in place. The Construction Division's teaching function and the Education Division's knowledge preservation function both apply to structural knowledge.

**Textiles**
The Textile Division's work — particularly weaving and spinning — benefits from a covered, sheltered work area that protects both workers and materials from weather. The Construction Division should understand what facility conditions the Textile Division's work requires and maintain those conditions.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions, the division focuses on the inspection cycle, preventive maintenance, incremental natural building skill development, tool and material inventory management, and household baseline instruction. New construction proceeds as need is identified by Housing Security and other divisions.

As conditions deteriorate and supply chains become unreliable, the division's emphasis shifts toward salvage-based material sourcing, natural building methods, and repair that preserves existing structures rather than assuming replaceable components. The division's natural building competency and salvage capacity become load-bearing rather than supplementary. Structures that were deferred maintenance candidates under normal conditions may become urgent priorities when replacements are not available.

The division should never be in a position where it is learning a building method for the first time because the usual method has become unavailable. The skills must exist before the need is acute. This is the operational logic that governs natural building development and material substitution knowledge in stable conditions.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete the structural condition survey**
Before any other function is operational, the division needs a complete picture of every community structure's condition. This assessment identifies what requires immediate repair, what requires attention within a defined timeline, and what is in acceptable condition. It is the foundation of the inspection program and the starting point for the repair program.

**2. Establish the tool library**
A community tool library should be organized before the first major repair or construction project, not assembled from whatever is at hand when a project starts. Inventory what tools exist across the membership, establish what the community needs to acquire, define the library's location and check-out protocol, and perform initial maintenance on tools that need it.

**3. Address immediate structural hazards**
The condition survey will likely identify structures with conditions requiring prompt attention. These are addressed before routine inspection cycle work or new construction.

**4. Develop the Liberty House design**
The Housing Security Division needs a buildable, documented Liberty House design before a housing emergency creates the need for it. The division should develop and refine this design early — ideally by building the first unit — so that the design is tested and the build sequence is understood.

**5. Launch household baseline instruction**
Regular instruction in household-level structural maintenance should begin as soon as the division has capacity to offer it. This is the single highest-leverage teaching function the division performs — households that can manage their own maintenance reduce the division's repair load and catch problems earlier.

**6. Begin natural building development**
Complete at least one timber framing or straw bale project before that capacity is needed. The project scale does not need to be large — a small outbuilding, a storage shed, a covered work area — but the project should be real and completed, so the division holds practical rather than theoretical competency. The Masonry Division is responsible for the parallel development of earthen and lime-based methods.

## Division Documents

**Structural Condition Register** — a current record of every community structure: last inspection date, inspector, findings by category (immediate action required / action within timeline / monitor), corrective actions assigned and their status. The primary instrument of the inspection program.

**Construction Project Record** — for every completed construction project: location, what was built, materials used, structural design basis, as-built conditions, and any deviations from the plan. Maintained permanently as the structural record of the community's built environment.

**Liberty House Design Package** — the complete design documentation for the standardized Liberty House: plans and elevations, material quantities, structural design basis (per the Structural Integrity Regulation), construction sequence, and variants for different material conditions. Updated when the design is refined through actual builds.

**Tool Library Inventory** — a current record of all community-held tools: what they are, their condition, their location, and their maintenance status. Includes check-out records so the location of tools in use is known.

**Material Inventory** — a current record of community-held construction materials: quantities, condition, and storage location. Includes salvaged material with assessment notes on quality and appropriate uses.

**Structural Knowledge Guide** — an instructional document covering the core structural concepts from the Structural Integrity Regulation in terms accessible to community members without engineering background: the failure modes, the load path concept, the moisture management principles, and the specific improvements most valuable for community structures. Used in household baseline instruction and division member training.
