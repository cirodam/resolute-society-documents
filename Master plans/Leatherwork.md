# Leatherwork Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Leatherwork Division exists to convert raw hides from community livestock into durable leather goods — footwear, belts, straps, harness, bags, and the full range of goods that leather uniquely provides — and to maintain the tanning and fabrication knowledge that allows the community to meet these needs from its own animals and its own land. It does this by receiving hides from slaughter, processing them through the full tanning sequence, and producing and repairing goods in priority order.

Leather is not a luxury material. Footwear fails. Draft animal harness fails. Forge bellows fail. The tools and containers that leather uniquely provides fail, and without the knowledge to replace them, communities dependent on those things lose capacity. A community that can tan its own hides and construct its own footwear and harness holds a supply chain that runs from its own pasture through its own shop — one that does not pass through any commercial system.

Historically, the leather trades were among the most subdivided of all crafts — tanners, curriers, cordwainers, cobblers, saddlers, harness makers, and glovers were distinct guilds that rarely overlapped. The division does not try to fully separate these trades, but it does recognize that tanning and fabrication are genuinely different skills, and that the division must develop both.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no hide from community slaughter is wasted — every usable hide is processed through tanning and returned to the community as usable leather
- That the community's footwear stock is actively maintained through repair, and that the division holds the knowledge and capacity to produce basic footwear from community-tanned leather
- That draft animal harness and working leather goods are maintained in serviceable condition, and that the division can replace them when they fail
- That tanning and fabrication knowledge is held broadly enough across the division that it cannot be lost through the departure of a single member

## Core Functions

- **Hide Acquisition and Preparation** — receiving hides from Agriculture at slaughter, fleshing, dehairing, and preparing them for the appropriate tanning method
- **Tanning** — processing hides through brain tanning, vegetable tanning, alum tawing, or rawhide preparation depending on the intended use
- **Currying and Finishing** — oiling, softening, and finishing tanned leather to prepare it for fabrication
- **Footwear** — constructing new shoes and moccasins from community-tanned leather and repairing existing footwear
- **Harness and Saddlery** — constructing and maintaining draft animal harness, collars, riding equipment, and working straps
- **Leather Goods** — producing belts, bags, pouches, sheaths, aprons, gloves, bellows, and other goods from available leather
- **Tool Management** — maintaining the division's fleshing knives, drawknives, beam, tanning vats, awls, lasting pliers, and fabrication tools
- **Skills and Teaching** — distributing tanning and fabrication knowledge across the membership

## Hide Acquisition and Preparation

The division's raw material comes from Agricultural Division slaughter. Every animal slaughtered for food produces a hide; hides left unprocessed deteriorate within days. The division should have a standing relationship with the Agricultural Division so that hides are delivered promptly at slaughter and the division is ready to receive them.

**Receiving hides.** A fresh hide should reach the division within hours of slaughter, before it begins to spoil. If processing cannot begin immediately, hides can be preserved short-term by salting — rubbing the flesh side thoroughly with coarse salt and folding or stacking. A well-salted hide can be stored for weeks before tanning. Freezing is also effective where freezer space is available. Dried hides — stretched flat and allowed to air dry — can be stored almost indefinitely but require rehydration before tanning.

**Fleshing.** The first processing step is removing all flesh, fat, and membrane from the hide's flesh side. This is done on a fleshing beam — a smooth, rounded log or post — using a fleshing knife or drawknife worked from the center of the hide outward. Thorough fleshing is not optional: any flesh or fat left on the hide will rot during tanning, producing an unusable result. This is skilled, physical work that takes time to do correctly.

**Dehairing.** For most tanning methods, hair must be removed. The traditional method is liming: soaking the hide in a solution of water and wood ash lye or slaked lime for several days until the hair loosens and can be scraped off with a dull knife or scraper. The timing varies with temperature and lime concentration — the division should develop a feel for when hair is ready to slip rather than relying on fixed time tables. Over-limed hides lose strength; under-limed hides require damaging effort to scrape.

Brain-tanned hides are dehaired differently — the hair is scraped off a wet, fresh hide before any chemical treatment, when the hair roots are still embedded in the grain layer and can be loosened with moisture and mechanical work.

Rawhide requires neither dehairing nor tanning — the hide is simply stretched under tension and allowed to dry. Hair can be left on or removed depending on intended use.

**The brain.** Brain tanning uses the animal's own brain as the tanning agent. The folk saying — that every animal has exactly enough brain to tan its own hide — is approximately true. The brain is collected at slaughter, emulsified with water (boiled or blended), and worked into the dampened hide. The division should establish that brain collection at slaughter is routine and that brains are delivered to the division along with the hide.

## Tanning Methods

The division maintains competency in four distinct approaches. Each produces leather suited to different applications; the choice of method should be made at the time the hide is received, based on what the community most needs from it.

**Rawhide** is not tanning — it is simply drying a cleaned hide under tension. The result is a hard, stiff material with remarkable strength. Rawhide lashing shrinks significantly as it dries, drawing joints together with force that approaches a mechanical fastener. Rawhide wrapped around a tool handle produces a grip that is nearly impossible to split. Rawhide drums, mallet heads, and snowshoe frames are products that wet-tanned leather cannot replicate. The division should treat rawhide as a distinct material with its own applications, not as an inferior substitute for tanned leather.

**Brain tanning** produces the softest, most supple leather available — the leather of buckskin shirts, moccasins, and fine bags. The process: the dehaired hide is dampened, emulsified brain is worked thoroughly into every surface and fiber, the hide is allowed to dry, rewetted and worked again, and this cycle repeated until the hide is completely penetrated and pliable. The final step is smoking: the hide is suspended over a smoldering fire of rotted wood for hours to days, which polymerizes the oils and makes the leather permanently water-stable. An unsmoked brain-tanned hide will stiffen when wet; a smoked one remains soft. Smoking is not optional. Brain-tanned leather cannot be produced in large batches or hurried — it is labor-intensive and requires judgment developed through experience.

**Vegetable tanning** uses tannins extracted from plant material — primarily the bark of oaks, hemlock, chestnut, or sumac — to chemically bind the hide's collagen fibers. It produces firm, dense leather suited to footwear soles, belts, straps, harness, and any application requiring durability and structure. The process is slow: hides progress through a series of tanning pits, moving from weak to progressively stronger tannin solutions over weeks to months depending on hide thickness. Rushing vegetable tanning produces case-hardened leather — tanned on the outside, raw inside — which cracks and delaminates in use. The division must build and maintain tanning pits, establish a reliable tannin source in coordination with the Forestry Division, and plan production across a multi-month cycle.

Tannin extraction: bark is broken up and soaked in water, which takes on the brown color and astringency of the tannins. The liquor is concentrated by simmering if needed. A simple float test — a piece of hide in the liquor should feel firmer than it did before — confirms tannin activity. Spent bark goes to Agriculture as a soil amendment.

**Alum tawing** uses alum (potassium aluminum sulfate) and salt rather than organic tannins. The result is white, pliable leather — historically used for fine gloves, book bindings, and decorative goods. Alum is a commercially sourced mineral that is not available from local materials the way bark tannins are, which limits this method's supply-chain independence. The division should understand alum tawing as a technique but should not plan primary production around it.

## Currying and Finishing

Tanned leather — whether vegetable- or brain-tanned — is not yet ready for fabrication. Currying is the preparation step between the tannery and the shop: oiling to restore flexibility, working to soften and even the grain, and splitting or skiving to the appropriate thickness.

**Stuffing and oiling.** Vegetable-tanned leather loses moisture and flexibility during tanning. Restoring it requires working oils and fats into the leather while it is still slightly damp from the final tanning bath. Tallow, neatsfoot oil (rendered from cattle leg bones), and lard are the traditional stuffing fats. The oil is worked in by hand or tool on the flesh side, and the hide is allowed to absorb it thoroughly before finishing. Leather that is not properly stuffed dries brittle and cracks in use.

**Working and softening.** Heavy working — pulling the hide over a stake, pulling it repeatedly across a beam edge, working it by hand — breaks up stiffness and produces more even softness. The amount of working needed depends on the intended use: sole leather for shoes is used stiff and needs less working; strap leather for harness needs to be supple enough to flex without cracking.

**Skiving.** Many applications require leather thinner than the full hide — lacing, thin straps, pocket linings. Skiving is the process of shaving the flesh side to reduce thickness. It requires a sharp skiving knife and a firm, flat surface. The skill is keeping the cut even; uneven skiving produces weak spots.

## Footwear

Footwear is the division's most critical production function. Shoes fail faster than any other leather good under hard use, are irreplaceable by any other material the community produces, and — in cold or wet conditions — are a health and safety necessity. The division treats footwear production and repair as its standing first priority.

**Cobbling — repair first.** The most valuable footwear skill in the near term is repair, not new construction. Resoling a worn boot extends its life by years. Patching a torn upper prevents structural failure. Replacing a broken lace or worn insole is trivial in labor and extends comfortable wear significantly. The division should establish boot and shoe repair as a standing service — members bring worn footwear, the division repairs it — and treat this as higher priority than new construction until a member genuinely cannot be shod from existing stock.

Resoling requires: removing the old sole (a lasting pincers or awl and patience), cutting a new sole from thick vegetable-tanned leather, cementing or stitching it in place, and trimming to the welt. Welt construction — a strip of leather that joins the upper to the sole and allows the sole to be replaced without disturbing the upper — is the feature that makes quality shoes repairable; the division should understand welt construction when evaluating footwear for repair feasibility.

**Moccasin construction** is the most accessible new footwear construction method. A simple one-piece moccasin — a single hide piece gathered around the foot and seamed at the toe and heel — requires minimal tools, minimal skill, and a few hours of work. It is appropriate for indoor use and light outdoor use. The puckered-toe moccasin of Eastern woodlands tradition, with a separate vamp piece, is slightly more complex and significantly more durable. The division should be able to produce both patterns in a range of sizes and should maintain pattern templates for each.

**Turn-shoe construction** is the traditional European method through the medieval period: the shoe is sewn inside-out — upper to sole — then turned right-side-out. The seam is on the inside, producing a smooth outer surface. Turn-shoes require no last, can be fitted well with careful pattern work, and are appropriate for light to moderate outdoor use. They are the appropriate next step beyond moccasins for a division building footwear competency.

**Lasted construction** uses a last — a foot-shaped wooden form — around which the upper is stretched and nailed before the sole is attached. This produces the best-fitting and most durable shoe construction but requires a set of lasts in a range of sizes, more tools, and more skill. The division should develop lasted construction as a long-term goal. Wooden lasts can be carved locally; the Machining and Metallurgy Division can produce metal hardware — tacks, eyelets, buckles — that lasted construction uses.

## Harness and Saddlery

If the community keeps draft animals — horses, mules, or oxen — harness is load-bearing infrastructure. A broken collar or trace is equipment downtime; a community that cannot repair its own harness is dependent on a supply chain for the capacity of its farm and transit operations.

**The draft collar** is the most critical and most technically demanding piece of heavy harness. It must fit the individual animal — a poorly fitted collar causes sores that put the animal out of work — and it takes a beating under sustained draft load. Traditional horse collars are stuffed with rye straw or other packing and covered in leather; the division must understand collar fitting as well as collar construction. A collar that fits the animal well and is maintained in good condition can last decades.

**Traces** are the straps that transmit draft force from the collar to the load. They must be made from heavy, full-thickness vegetable-tanned leather and inspected regularly — a broken trace under load is dangerous. The division should treat trace inspection as a standing function rather than waiting for visible wear.

**Bridles and reins** are lighter work than draft harness but are used daily and subject to wear and breakage. The division should maintain a standing supply of finished bridle leather and be able to produce replacement parts quickly.

**Riding saddles** are the most complex fabrication the division attempts. A functional saddle requires a tree (the wooden or synthetic frame) — which the Construction or Machining and Metallurgy divisions would produce — and leather work covering it. The division should develop saddle repair competency before new saddle construction, since repair is more likely to be needed and teaches the relevant skills.

## Leather Goods

Beyond footwear and harness, leather serves a wide range of community functions. The division produces these goods in priority order based on community need.

**Bellows** for the forge are a critical output for the Machining and Metallurgy Division. A forge bellows — two leather panels joined at the edges, with a valve that allows air in on the draw and forces it through the nozzle on the compression stroke — is not technically complex but requires supple, airtight leather and durable construction. Bellows failure stops smithing; the division should treat forge bellows maintenance as a standing service for Machining and Metallurgy.

**Belts and straps** are constant-consumption goods. Work belts, tool belts, pack straps, and load-bearing straps are among the most wear-prone leather goods in daily use. The division should produce belts and straps from vegetable-tanned leather as a standing output, in common widths and lengths that serve the most common needs, maintaining a small inventory rather than producing only on demand.

**Bags and pouches** serve storage and carrying needs that no other division's output meets as well. Leather bags are durable, water-resistant, and long-lasting. The division should produce a range of working bag sizes — small belt pouches, mid-size work bags, larger field bags — as capacity allows.

**Sheaths and scabbards** protect cutting tools and are needed wherever knives, axes, and other edged tools are used. Every tool the Machining and Metallurgy Division produces needs a way to be carried and stored safely; leather sheaths are the standard solution.

**Gloves** are protective equipment for hands doing hard or hot work. Work gloves in heavy leather protect hands in Agriculture, Construction, and Forestry operations. The division should be able to produce basic work gloves as a standing product.

**Aprons** protect workers in the forge, the kitchen, and other environments where heat and abrasion are concerns. A leather apron is simple in construction and high in daily use value.

## Material Sourcing

The division's material independence depends on two sourcing relationships: hides from Agriculture and tanbark from Forestry.

**Hides.** Every community livestock slaughter produces a hide. The division should establish with Agriculture that every hide is preserved and delivered — no hide should be discarded because the division is not ready to receive it. A hide salted at slaughter can wait weeks; a hide left in the sun is lost in days. The division should maintain salt in sufficient quantity to preserve hides through periods when processing capacity is backed up.

Species matter for leather quality. Cattle hides are the workhorse of the leather trades — thick, large, and suited to heavy goods, sole leather, and harness. Deer hides, which come from hunting, are thinner and best suited for brain tanning and soft goods. Sheep and goat hides are thin and appropriate for fine goods and garments. The division should track what hides are available and what each is best suited for.

**Tanbark.** The Forestry Division manages oak and other tannin-rich bark as part of its harvest program. The Leatherwork Division specifies what bark it needs — species, quantity, timing — and coordinates with Forestry on harvest. Bark is best stripped in spring when sap is flowing, which causes bark to slip cleanly from the wood. This seasonal constraint means tanbark supply must be planned in advance rather than requested on demand. Spent bark from exhausted tanning liquor can go to Agriculture as a soil amendment.

**Alternative tannin sources.** Where oak bark is scarce, other locally available plants provide tannin: hemlock bark, chestnut bark (where chestnut survives), sumac leaves and bark, black walnut hulls, and gallnuts from oak galls. The division should know what tannin sources are available locally and have tested at least one alternative beyond oak bark.

## Tool Management

The leatherwork tool set is divided between the tannery and the fabrication shop. Both require maintenance and are not interchangeable with any other division's tools.

**Tannery tools:** fleshing beam and fleshing knife or drawknife, dehairing scraper, liming vessels, tanning pits or vats (wooden barrels, lined stone or concrete), stirring paddles, a bark grinder or hammer for breaking up tanbark, and a hydrometer or float test materials for checking tannin concentration.

**Fabrication tools:** a cutting board and sharp straight-edged cutting knife, skiving knife, stitching awls in multiple sizes, harness needles, waxed linen thread, edge beveler, edge slicker or bone folder, stitching groover (to recess thread below the surface so it does not wear through), stitching chisels or pricking irons for marking stitch holes, lasting pliers and tacks for shoe construction, a cobbler's last and heel iron for repair work, and a sharpening setup sufficient to maintain all cutting tools at working sharpness.

**Sharpness is the non-negotiable.** A dull fleshing knife tears rather than cuts, damaging the hide. A dull skiving knife produces uneven thickness and wandering cuts. A dull awl crushes leather fibers rather than piercing them, creating weak points in seams. Every cutting and piercing tool in the division should be maintained at a standard where it performs its function without force. The division should hold sharpening competency as a standing skill.

## Skills and Teaching

The historical separation of tanning from fabrication reflects a real skill distinction that takes years to master in each domain. The division should not expect that any member becomes fully proficient in both quickly. The teaching program should develop tanning and fabrication as parallel tracks, with members developing depth in one or both based on aptitude and interest.

**Priority skills for early development:**
- Boot and shoe repair (immediate value, lower skill threshold than new construction)
- Brain tanning (can be learned and practiced on small hides; a deer hide is a good starting project)
- Moccasin construction (the most accessible new footwear construction)
- Vegetable tanning setup and management (slower to develop but foundational for durable goods)
- Basic strap and belt fabrication

**The apprenticeship model.** Leather skills are transmitted most effectively through direct supervised work — a skilled tanner working alongside someone developing the skill. The judgment calls in tanning (when is the hide ready to advance? is the fleshing complete?) and in fabrication (how tight should this stitch be? is this skive too thin?) are learned through practice and correction, not from instruction alone.

**Teaching the full sequence.** Members who understand only one stage of the hide-to-goods sequence are more fragile as a knowledge base than members who understand all of it. The division should teach the complete sequence — from receiving the fresh hide to finished product — to every member, even those who will specialize. A fabricator who has never tanned does not understand why the leather behaves the way it does; a tanner who has never sewn does not understand what fabricators need from the leather they produce.

## Division Relationships

**Agriculture**
The primary upstream relationship. Hides and brains come from slaughter; the division coordinates with Agriculture on slaughter schedules so it can receive hides promptly. The division should also coordinate on draft animal harness — any community keeping horses, mules, or oxen depends on functional harness, and the division should understand what animals are in use and what harness condition they are working with.

**Forestry**
The tanbark supply chain. The Forestry Division harvests oak and other tannin-rich bark as a standing output for the Leatherwork Division. The division specifies what species, quantities, and timing it needs; Forestry plans bark harvest accordingly. The seasonal constraint — bark strips best in spring — means this coordination must happen in advance. Spent bark returns to Agriculture through Forestry.

**Machining and Metallurgy**
Two significant connections. First, bellows: the forge depends on functional bellows, and bellows maintenance is a Leatherwork Division function; the two divisions should treat this as a standing service relationship. Second, hardware: buckles, rivets, eyelets, tacks, and D-rings are metal goods that Machining and Metallurgy produces and Leatherwork incorporates into harness and goods. The two divisions should coordinate on what hardware the Leatherwork Division needs and maintain a standing inventory of the most common items.

**Transit**
Where the community uses draft animals for transport, harness is Transit's operational dependency. A broken collar or failed trace is downtime. The Leatherwork Division should understand what Transit's harness inventory looks like, when equipment was last inspected, and what is approaching end of serviceable life — and address it before failure rather than after.

**Textiles**
Both divisions serve the domain of things people wear and carry. Plant-fiber cordage remains a Textiles function; leather lacing and rawhide lashing are Leatherwork. The two divisions share the upstream Agricultural relationship — Textiles for fiber, Leatherwork for hides — and should coordinate on what the Agriculture Division is producing so neither division is surprised by what is available. Members learning basic mending and garment construction in Textiles may find natural extension into leather fabrication; the two divisions should treat their teaching programs as complementary.

**Construction**
Leather lashings, tool handle wrappings, and rawhide joints appear throughout construction work. The division should maintain a supply of rawhide lashing available to Construction and should understand what Construction's leather needs are.

**Education**
Tanning and leatherwork are knowledge the community cannot recover quickly if lost. The Education Division's Transmission principle applies directly: the full sequence from hide to finished goods must be held by enough members that departure or illness does not leave a gap. The division coordinates with Education on apprenticeship placement.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions, the division focuses on boot repair as the immediate community service, building brain tanning and vegetable tanning competency through regular practice hides, maintaining harness in good condition, and producing a standing inventory of common goods — belts, straps, pouches — from available leather.

As conditions deteriorate and commercial leather goods become unavailable, the division's production shifts from supplementary to essential. Footwear replacement — community members whose shoes have worn out with no commercial replacement available — becomes the most urgent function. Harness production for expanded draft animal use supports the Agriculture and Transit divisions' shift away from fuel-dependent equipment. The division's tanning infrastructure, built and practiced in stable conditions, handles this load; a division that has not maintained active tanning practice will not be able to scale quickly when the need arrives.

The division should never be in a position where it processes its first batch of vegetable-tanned leather after commercial leather has become unavailable. Vegetable tanning takes months; if the first batch starts when the need is acute, it will not be ready until the crisis has deepened. The tanning program must be continuous in stable conditions.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Establish hide collection at slaughter**
Before any tanning begins, the division needs a reliable stream of hides from Agriculture. Coordinate with Agriculture to establish the expectation that every hide is preserved and delivered. Stock salt for preservation. This is the prerequisite for everything else.

**2. Complete the first brain-tanning project**
Brain tanning is the fastest path to community-produced leather and teaches the fundamentals of hide assessment, preparation, and finishing. A deer hide from the first hunting season is the appropriate starting project. Brain tanning a deer hide takes several days of active work and produces immediately usable soft leather.

**3. Establish boot and shoe repair as a standing service**
Set up a cobbling capability — a last, lasting pliers, a supply of sole leather from the first vegetable-tanned batch or commercially sourced, and waxed linen thread. Begin repairing community footwear. This is the division's highest-leverage immediate function and the one that produces the most visible community value.

**4. Build the tanning vats and begin vegetable tanning**
Construct tanning pits or barrels, establish the tannin source with Forestry, and begin the first batch of vegetable-tanned leather. Accept that this batch will take months. Start it before it is urgently needed.

**5. Produce and inventory harness condition**
With the Agricultural Division, assess the condition of all working harness. Identify what is approaching end of life. Produce replacement traces and straps from available leather as the first fabrication priority after footwear repair.

**6. Establish bellows maintenance with Machining and Metallurgy**
Inspect forge bellows condition and establish a standing maintenance relationship with Machining and Metallurgy. The forge depends on this; it should not be allowed to become a crisis.

## Division Documents

**Hide Register** — a log of every hide received: date, species, source animal, preservation method, assigned tanning method, and current status in the tanning sequence. Every hide in process should have an entry so none are lost track of during the months-long vegetable tanning cycle.

**Tanning Production Record** — for each batch of vegetable-tanned leather: start date, hide species and count, tannin source and concentration schedule, progress through pits, finish date, and quality assessment of the finished leather. Accumulated across batches to refine timing and produce consistent results.

**Footwear and Harness Repair Log** — a running record of repair work completed: what was repaired, for whom, what materials were used, and what the condition was before and after. Tracks division capacity and identifies where production is most needed.

**Goods Inventory** — a current count of finished leather goods held by the division: belts, straps, pouches, shoes in progress, harness components. Allows the division to respond to needs from stock rather than starting from raw material every time.

**Pattern Library** — pattern templates for the division's standard goods: moccasin sizes, turn-shoe sizes, common belt widths and lengths, harness components. Maintained so any member can cut to pattern without redrafting.
