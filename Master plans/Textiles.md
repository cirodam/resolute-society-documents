# Textile Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Textile Division exists to ensure that every member of the Resolute Society is adequately clothed — warm enough in winter, protected from the elements, and supplied with the basic textiles that daily life requires. It does this by maintaining the community's existing clothing stock through repair, building the fiber-to-fabric skills that allow the community to produce what it cannot acquire, and distributing clothing to members who lack it.

The skills this division works to recover — spinning, weaving, knitting, sewing, mending — were universal human capacities until very recently. Every community in human history clothed itself. The industrial revolution concentrated textile production into factories at a scale and efficiency that made home and community production economically irrational, and within two generations those skills largely disappeared from ordinary life. They are not gone. They are recoverable. The division exists to recover them before the need is acute.

Clothing is a life-safety issue in cold climates. A member who lacks adequate warm clothing in winter is in genuine danger. The division treats cold-weather clothing coverage as its most critical function, in the same way that the Energy Division treats heat and the Healthcare Division treats medication supply.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no member lacks clothing adequate for the climate — particularly warm clothing in cold seasons
- That the community's existing clothing stock is actively maintained and extended through repair and mending
- That textile skills — fiber processing, spinning, knitting, weaving, sewing — are held broadly enough across the community that they cannot be lost
- That the community has the fiber sources, tools, and knowledge to produce clothing when supply chains cannot supply it

## Core Functions

- **Assessment and Inventory** — maintaining a current picture of the community's clothing stock, textile tools, fiber sources, and skill distribution
- **Repair and Mending** — extending the life of existing clothing through skilled repair, the highest-leverage function the division performs
- **Fiber Acquisition and Processing** — sourcing, cleaning, and preparing fiber from animal and plant sources for spinning
- **Spinning** — converting fiber to yarn, the foundational skill from which all fabric production follows
- **Fabric Production** — knitting, weaving, and felting to produce cloth and finished goods from yarn
- **Clothing Construction** — cutting, sewing, and pattern work to produce and modify garments
- **Leather and Cordage** — tanning hides for leather goods and producing cordage from plant fiber
- **Distribution** — ensuring clothing reaches members who need it

## Assessment and Inventory

The division begins with a clear picture of what the community already has and what it can already do. Clothing production is slow. A member who discovers in October that they lack adequate winter clothing cannot wait for the division to spin, weave, and sew a coat — they need what exists to reach them now. The assessment function ensures the division knows what exists and where the gaps are before they become crises.

**Clothing inventory.** The division maintains awareness of the community's clothing stock — not an item-by-item count, but a working understanding of whether members are adequately clothed for the climate. The assessment identifies members who lack warm coats, adequate footwear, or sufficient cold-weather layering. Children are flagged specifically, because children grow out of clothing faster than adults and their needs change on a different timeline.

**Textile tool inventory.** The tools of textile production — sewing machines and their needles and thread, knitting needles and crochet hooks, spinning wheels and drop spindles, looms, darning mushrooms, leather tools — represent community capital. The division maintains an inventory of what tools exist among the membership, their condition, and who holds them. Tools that are community-owned should be accessible to any member learning a skill. Tools that are member-owned should be known to the division so they can be called on when capacity is needed.

**Skill distribution.** The division should know who in the community can do what. Sewing, knitting, spinning, weaving, mending, and pattern drafting are distinct skills. A community with ten members who can sew but none who can spin has a different profile from one with the reverse. Skills should be identified — including skills members may not think to offer, because many people who knit or sew do not think of it as a community asset — and the division should actively work to fill gaps through teaching.

**Fiber sources.** The division identifies what fiber sources are available in or accessible to the community: whether members keep sheep, angora rabbits, or fiber goats; what plant fibers can be grown locally; what salvage sources exist. This is coordinated with the Agricultural Division for animal fiber and with the Agricultural Division's seed library for plant fiber crops.

## Repair and Mending

Repair and mending is the division's most immediately valuable function and the one that yields the most return for the least investment. A repaired garment is clothing that didn't need to be produced. At community scale, a culture of repair extends the life of the clothing stock for years — which is years the division has to build production capacity before the stock genuinely runs out.

**The repair-first principle.** The division should treat every garment that could be repaired as a resource that has not yet been exhausted. Clothing thrown away because of a worn hem, a broken zipper, a torn seam, or a hole is clothing that was discarded prematurely. The division cultivates a community norm that repair is the default response to clothing failure, not disposal.

**Darning.** Darning — reweaving worn areas of knitted fabric, most commonly socks — is the single highest-impact textile repair skill in a resilience context. Socks are the clothing item that fails fastest under heavy use. A pair of socks that can be darned indefinitely is a pair that never needs to be replaced. Darning requires only a darning needle, yarn, and a darning mushroom or improvised substitute. It can be learned in an afternoon and practiced anywhere. The division should teach darning broadly and treat it as a basic adult skill the community holds universally.

**Seam and structural repair.** Torn seams, detached buttons, broken zippers, torn pockets, and fraying cuffs are all straightforward repairs that significantly extend garment life. Hand sewing these repairs requires minimal equipment — a needle, thread, and basic stitches. The division should teach basic hand sewing to every member who does not already know it. Machine sewing is faster and stronger for many repairs but requires a functioning machine and electricity; hand sewing works everywhere.

**Patching.** Worn-through areas in non-knitted fabric — the knees of trousers, the elbows of jackets, the seat of work pants — can be patched to restore function. Decorative visible mending, which patches with contrasting fabric intentionally, has made patching culturally acceptable in ways it was not a generation ago; the division can build on this without apology. A patched garment that is warm and functional is better than an unpatched garment with a hole.

**Repurposing.** Clothing that is beyond repair in its original form often has material value. A wool sweater too worn to wear becomes felt when fulled — washed hot to shrink and mat the fibers — and felt can be cut for patches, insoles, or small goods. Jeans too worn to wear become canvas for heavy-duty patches or bags. Sheet fabric too thin for sheets becomes interfacing or rags. The division should think of worn-out clothing as raw material, not waste.

**Community mending circles.** Regular community mending sessions — members gathering to do hand sewing and mending together — serve both a practical and a social function. They normalize repair, create a teaching environment for members learning basic skills, and provide the division with ongoing repair capacity without requiring a dedicated facility. The mending circle is among the easiest division programs to start and maintain.

## Fiber Acquisition and Processing

Producing fiber from raw sources is slower and more labor-intensive than any other stage of textile production. The division should maintain realistic expectations: fiber production at community scale supplements existing clothing stock, it does not replace supply chains in the short term. It is the long-term investment the division makes while repair and existing stock carry the near-term load.

**Wool** is the most practically important natural fiber for most of the continental United States. It is warm even when wet — the only common natural fiber with this property — felts readily, and can be processed with simple hand tools. Wool comes from sheep but also from angora rabbits, which can be kept at smaller scale than sheep and are practical even in suburban settings. Goat fiber — cashmere from cashmere goats, mohair from angora goats — is finer and warmer than sheep wool but requires the same processing. The division coordinates with the Agricultural Division to access fiber from community-kept animals, compensating Animal keepers for shearing and fiber with credits or other exchange.

Wool processing proceeds in stages. Raw fleece is skirted — the dirty edges and heavily soiled portions removed — then washed (scoured) in hot water with a small amount of dish soap or wool wash to remove lanolin and vegetable matter. The critical rule of scouring is: hot water, no agitation. Agitation in hot water felts wool, producing an unusable tangled mass. Fleece is added to hot water, left to soak, then gently lifted out — never wrung or squeezed — and rinsed at the same temperature. Temperature shock also felts wool; rinse water should match wash water temperature. Washed fleece is dried flat before carding.

Carding aligns fiber for spinning using a pair of flat paddles covered with wire tines (hand cards) or a drum carder. The result is a rolag — a soft roll of aligned fiber ready to spin. Combing, done with coarse-toothed combs rather than cards, produces a straighter, more parallel fiber alignment suited to worsted spinning; carding produces a fluffier alignment suited to woolen spinning. Both methods produce spinnable fiber; the division should teach both.

**Plant fibers** require more processing than wool but produce fiber in climates where sheep are impractical. Linen from flax is the most important plant fiber for temperate climates. Flax grows readily across most of the United States, can be grown at garden scale, and produces both fiber and edible seed. Processing flax — retting (water soaking to loosen fiber from the stem), breaking (crushing the woody stalk), scutching (beating out woody debris), and hackling (combing to align fine fibers) — is labor-intensive but requires no specialized equipment beyond tools the community can make. The result is linen fiber that spins into strong, durable yarn.

Hemp fiber is processed similarly to flax and produces coarser, stronger fiber suited to cordage, canvas, and heavy fabric. Its cultivation is legal with appropriate registration and it grows vigorously in most climates. Nettles — wild stinging nettles — produce a fiber comparable to linen and grow without cultivation in disturbed soils across much of the country. The division should identify what grows locally and what can be grown, and develop processing capacity for the most practical local sources.

**Salvage fiber** is the division's most immediately accessible non-commercial fiber source. Worn-out wool sweaters, blankets, and fabric can be unraveled for reusable yarn or processed into fiber for spinning. Thrift stores and community donations are sources of fiber-containing textiles that would otherwise be discarded. The division should establish a textile salvage program as a standing function — collecting fabric and worn clothing that can be repurposed, reused as material, or unraveled for fiber.

## Spinning

Spinning is the conversion of loose fiber into usable yarn. It is the bottleneck skill in textile production — without yarn, neither knitting nor weaving is possible — and the skill the division should prioritize teaching after repair.

**The drop spindle** is the most accessible spinning tool and the correct starting point for community spinning education. A drop spindle is a weighted disc on a short stick, costing a few dollars to buy or easily made from a dowel and a wooden disc. It produces yarn more slowly than a spinning wheel but requires no infrastructure, can be learned in an afternoon, and can be used anywhere. A community in which many members can spin with a drop spindle has distributed spinning capacity that does not depend on any single piece of equipment.

The mechanics of spinning are simple: fiber is drafted — pulled thin — and twisted simultaneously. The twist locks the fibers together into a continuous strand. The spindle hangs from the forming yarn and its weight keeps tension on the fiber while its spin adds twist. With practice, the hands develop a feel for the right ratio of draft to twist that produces consistent yarn.

**Spinning wheels** produce yarn significantly faster than drop spindles and are the appropriate tool for sustained production. The division should acquire and maintain community spinning wheels — traditional Saxony wheels or modern Ashford or Schacht wheels, all of which are functional and repairable — available for member use. Members who learn to spin on a drop spindle transition to a wheel naturally; the mechanics are the same and the wheel simply automates the spindle's rotation.

**Yarn weight and twist.** The weight of yarn — the thickness — is determined by how much fiber is drafted per unit of twist. More draft produces thinner yarn (fingering, lace weight); less draft produces thicker yarn (bulky, worsted weight). Twist angle determines whether yarn is hard and strong (more twist, suited to warp yarn in weaving) or soft and lofty (less twist, suited to knitting). The division's spinning instruction should develop these judgments, not just the mechanical skill.

**Plying** — twisting two or more singles together in the opposite direction from which they were spun — produces balanced, stronger, more even yarn. Single-ply yarn is faster to produce; plied yarn is more durable and better suited to most uses. The division should teach both and help members understand when each is appropriate.

## Fabric Production

Yarn becomes fabric through knitting, weaving, or felting. Each produces different fabrics suited to different uses. The division should develop capacity in all three, recognizing that each requires different tools and skills and that different community members will be drawn to each.

**Knitting** is the fastest path from yarn to finished garment for most community members, requires the least equipment, and produces elastic, warm fabric well-suited to cold-weather clothing. Knitting needles — straight, circular, or double-pointed — are inexpensive and nearly indestructible. Many members already knit. The division should build on existing knitting knowledge in the community, identify experienced knitters willing to teach, and treat the expansion of knitting capacity as a standing priority.

The items with the most resilience value to produce by knitting, in order: socks (wear out fastest, most needed), mittens and gloves (critical for cold-weather work), hats (significant heat loss prevention), sweaters and vests (warmth layer), and blankets (slower to produce but durable). The division should maintain a queue of priority items based on identified community needs.

Fair Isle, stranded colorwork, and other patterned techniques produce doubly-thick fabric with greatly improved warmth. They are not more difficult than plain knitting — they require managing two yarns simultaneously — but produce garments significantly warmer than plain single-layer knitting at the same yarn weight. The division's knitting instruction should include stranded techniques as a warmth-optimization tool.

**Weaving** produces flat fabric that can be cut and sewn into any garment or textile. It requires a loom — ranging from a simple frame loom built from scrap lumber to a four-shaft floor loom — but produces fabric at a rate significantly faster than knitting once the warp is set up. Woven fabric is the appropriate production method for high-volume flat goods: yardage for clothing construction, blanket fabric, canvas for bags and tarps, and toweling.

A simple rigid heddle loom or frame loom can be built from available lumber and produces usable fabric. The division should build at least one community loom before acquiring commercial equipment, because the building process teaches how a loom works and makes repair and adaptation practical. As production capacity grows, the division should acquire or build floor looms capable of producing wider fabric at greater speed.

Warp preparation is the most skilled step in weaving. The warp — the lengthwise threads held under tension — must be measured to consistent length, wound onto the loom beam evenly, and sleyed (threaded) through the heddle or reed correctly. An incorrectly prepared warp produces uneven fabric or breaks during weaving. The division should identify members willing to specialize in warp preparation and treat this skill as a division asset.

**Felting** converts raw wool or loosely spun wool yarn directly into fabric without spinning fine yarn first. Wet felting — applying hot water, soap, and agitation to wool fiber or fabric — causes the scales on individual wool fibers to lock together, producing a dense, non-fraying material. Needle felting uses barbed needles to mechanically entangle fibers without water. Felted fabric cannot be knit or woven, but it can be cut to any shape without fraying and is extremely warm and wind-resistant.

Felting is the fastest fiber-to-fabric process available and the one that requires the least skill and equipment. Its products are limited — flat panels and simple three-dimensional shapes — but those products are genuinely useful: boot liners, insoles, hats, mittens, simple slippers, insulating pads. The division should treat felting as a high-speed production capacity for simple warm goods when speed matters more than flexibility.

## Clothing Construction

Spinning and weaving produce fiber and fabric. Clothing construction turns fabric into garments. These are distinct skills and the division should develop all three rather than assuming they travel together.

**Pattern making and modification.** Clothing construction begins with a pattern — a template for cutting fabric pieces that, when sewn together, produce a garment of the right size and shape. Commercial patterns exist for virtually every garment type and are available cheaply. The more resilient skill is drafting and modifying patterns directly from measurements, which allows production without depending on commercial pattern availability and produces better-fitting garments. The division should teach both: using commercial patterns for rapid production, and drafting basic patterns from body measurements for long-term independence.

**Hand sewing.** All clothing can be constructed by hand. It is slow — a hand-sewn shirt requires many hours — but it requires no equipment and produces durable results. The division should teach the basic hand stitches sufficient for clothing construction: the running stitch, backstitch, whip stitch, slip stitch, and blanket stitch. A member who can hand-sew can produce and repair clothing without any powered equipment.

**Machine sewing** dramatically accelerates production and produces stronger seams than most hand sewing. The division should maintain community sewing machines in working condition, ensure that members who sew have access to them, and maintain a supply of needles, thread, and bobbins. Treadle sewing machines — foot-powered, requiring no electricity — are the most resilient option and were standard equipment until the mid-20th century. The division should acquire at least one functional treadle machine as a no-electricity backup.

**Priority garments for production.** The division should maintain a clear priority order for what it produces based on community need. Cold-weather essentials come first: outer layers, warm underlayers, hats, mittens, and socks. Children's clothing — particularly for children who are actively growing — is a standing priority because children cycle through sizes faster than clothing can be replenished. Work clothing that protects against physical labor — heavy trousers, work aprons, sturdy gloves — comes next. Clothing for formal and community occasions is a lower priority than survival warmth but not an unimportant one; the Community Care Division's social connection function depends partly on members being able to participate in community life without feeling poorly dressed.

**Children's clothing.** Children present a specific planning challenge that adults do not. They grow continuously and unpredictably. A coat that fits a six-year-old in October does not fit the same child in March. The division should maintain an actively managed children's clothing exchange — a pool of children's clothing in a range of sizes that families contribute to as their children grow out of items and draw from as their children need them. This is more efficient than producing children's clothing to demand, because the exchange recirculates what already exists before production is needed.

## Cordage

Plant-fiber cordage — rope, twine, and string made from twisted plant fiber — is needed across virtually every division and falls within the Textile Division's scope as a fiber craft. The process is simple: fiber is twisted in one direction to form strands, then strands are twisted together in the opposite direction to form cordage. The twist-direction reversal is what locks the structure together; a rope twisted all in one direction unravels under tension. Cordage can be made from nettles, dogbane, milkweed, cattail leaves, inner bark of basswood or cedar, and many other locally available plants. The division should teach cordage making as a basic skill and identify what fiber sources are available in the community's region.

Leather goods — footwear, harness, belts, bags — are the Leatherwork Division's domain. The two divisions share the upstream Agricultural relationship and should coordinate, but tanning and fabrication are distinct trades from fiber processing and garment construction.

## Division Relationships

**Agricultural Division**
The primary upstream relationship. Wool comes from Agricultural Division animals; flax and hemp are grown in Agricultural Division gardens and fields. The two divisions should maintain standing coordination on what fiber is available and when, so the Textile Division can plan processing capacity accordingly. The Agricultural Division's seed library should include fiber crop varieties — flax, hemp — as a standing component.

**Leatherwork**
Both divisions serve the domain of things people wear and carry. Plant-fiber cordage is a Textile function; leather goods are Leatherwork's. The two divisions share the upstream Agricultural relationship — Textiles for fiber and fleece, Leatherwork for hides — and should coordinate so neither division is surprised by what Agriculture produces. Members developing textile skills may find natural extension into leather fabrication; the two divisions should treat their teaching programs as complementary rather than competing.

**Community Care Division**
Members who are elderly or disabled may have difficulty maintaining their clothing, performing repairs, or obtaining what they need. The Textile Division is a standing resource for the Community Care Division's daily assistance function — helping members mend, alter, or obtain clothing as part of care visits. The children's clothing exchange should be accessible to families receiving Community Care support.

**Healthcare Division**
Wound dressing, bandaging, and protective garments all require textile materials. The Textile Division should coordinate with Healthcare on material needs and should treat production of healthcare textiles — clean bandaging fabric, wound packing material — as a standing priority alongside clothing production.

**Housing Security**
Adequate warm clothing reduces heating requirements. A member who is well-clothed in thermal layers can tolerate a lower indoor temperature safely, which reduces fuel requirements in winter. The two divisions share an interest in cold-weather member safety and should coordinate during cold events on whether members lack adequate clothing or adequate heat — and address whichever gap is easier to close in the moment.

**Education**
Textile skills should be part of the community's standing education program, not restricted to the Textile Division. Spinning, knitting, basic hand sewing, and mending are skills that every member benefits from holding. The division should work with the Education Division to integrate textile instruction into regular community learning.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the division focuses on repair and skill building. Mending circles run regularly. Members learn spinning, knitting, and sewing. The tool inventory grows and is maintained. The children's clothing exchange operates continuously. The division identifies fiber sources and begins building relationships with Agricultural Division members who keep fiber animals.

As conditions deteriorate and commercial clothing becomes scarce or unavailable, active production becomes central. The division shifts toward meeting identified gaps in the community's clothing stock — cold-weather essentials first, children's clothing second, work clothing third. Fiber processing, spinning, and weaving operate at sustained pace rather than learning-oriented pace.

The division should never be in a position where it is learning to spin for the first time because supply chains have failed. The skills must exist before the need is acute. This is the operational principle that governs everything in stable conditions: build capacity now, because there is no time to build it later.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete the clothing and skill assessment**
Identify members who lack adequate cold-weather clothing immediately. Identify members with existing textile skills. Inventory tools available across the membership. This picture determines everything that follows.

**2. Start the mending circle**
A regular mending gathering requires no infrastructure and no equipment beyond what members bring. It produces immediate value through repair, builds community around a shared practice, and creates the social context in which textile skills spread naturally. Start this before anything else is organized.

**3. Establish the children's clothing exchange**
Set up the exchange as a standing community resource. It addresses a specific ongoing need that production alone cannot efficiently meet and requires only organization, not production capacity.

**4. Identify and develop spinning capacity**
Acquire drop spindles — they can be made or cheaply purchased — and identify members willing to teach spinning. Run the first spinning sessions. Spinning is the bottleneck that everything else depends on.

**5. Establish fiber source relationships with the Agricultural Division**
Coordinate on what fiber animals exist in the community and when shearing and slaughter occur. Establish the relationship before fiber is available so the processing response is organized when it arrives.

**6. Build weaving and sewing capacity**
Acquire or build a community loom. Ensure community sewing machines are functional. Identify members who can teach weaving and sewing and begin regular instruction.

## Division Documents

**Clothing and Skill Assessment** — a current record of the community's clothing coverage, textile tool inventory, and skill distribution across the membership. Updated annually and whenever significant changes occur.

**Children's Clothing Exchange Registry** — the current inventory of the children's clothing exchange: sizes available, items in circulation, and families participating.

**Fiber Source and Processing Calendar** — a schedule of when fiber is available from Agricultural Division sources — shearing dates, slaughter dates — and the division's processing plan for each source.

**Tool Inventory and Maintenance Record** — a current record of all community-owned and member-committed textile tools, their location, condition, and maintenance status.

**Pattern Library** — a collection of basic garment patterns in a range of sizes, including patterns drafted directly from measurements, maintained and freely available to all members engaged in clothing production.

**Repair and Production Log** — a running record of what the division has repaired and produced, by item type and quantity. Used to assess capacity and identify where production effort is most needed.
