# Transit Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Transit Division exists to move people. Members traveling between hubs, workers getting to where they are needed, elderly or disabled members who cannot move independently, and the entire membership when emergency evacuation is required — all of this moves through the Division. Where the Courier Division moves objects on scheduled routes, the Transit Division moves people, on schedule where routes justify it and on demand where they do not.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for three things:

- That every member can move through the Society's geography reliably — to community gatherings, work, healthcare, and daily life — regardless of whether they own a vehicle or can drive one
- That members who cannot move independently due to age, disability, or circumstance have access to the same mobility as everyone else
- That the Society can move its entire membership quickly and safely when emergency evacuation is required

## Core Functions

- **Scheduled Routes** — fixed timetable service between hubs and key community locations where demand and capacity justify a regular run
- **On-Demand Transport** — arranged trips for members whose needs do not fit a scheduled route, including regular service for members supported by the Community Care Division
- **Emergency Evacuation** — moving the entire membership, or any part of it, quickly and safely when conditions require rapid departure
- **Fleet and Mode Management** — maintaining the Division's vehicles and developing human-powered transport capacity alongside motorized operations

## Scheduled Routes

Scheduled routes operate where demand is consistent enough to justify a fixed timetable. A scheduled route is a commitment — members plan their lives around it, and a route that runs unreliably is worse than no route at all because it creates false confidence.

**Route establishment** follows demonstrated demand. Before a route is set, the Division assesses how many members need to travel between two points regularly and at what times. A route is established when demand is sufficient to justify committing a vehicle and operator to a fixed schedule. Routes are not established speculatively and then run empty — this depletes fuel and operator capacity without serving members.

**Route frequency** reflects actual use. A route connecting a residential hub to a community kitchen or healthcare facility may run multiple times daily. A route between two outlying hubs may run twice weekly. The Division reviews route frequency regularly and adjusts as demand changes. A route whose ridership has dropped consistently is reassessed — the capacity may be better deployed as on-demand service.

**Schedules are public and stable.** Every scheduled route's timetable is posted at each stop on the route and at every hub. When schedules must change, members are notified through the Communications Division with as much lead time as possible. Unannounced schedule changes erode trust in the service faster than any other operational failure.

**Stops** are established at hubs and at locations with consistent boarding demand — healthcare facilities, community kitchens, gathering spaces, work sites. Stops should be known, marked, and safe to wait at. Members should never need to guess where to board.

**Transfers** between routes are designed into the network where possible so that members can reach any point in the Society's geography with a predictable number of connections. The Division maps transfer points and publishes connection times so that members can plan multi-leg trips.

## On-Demand Transport

On-demand transport serves members whose needs do not fit a scheduled route — irregular trips, destinations off established routes, and members who require dedicated assistance rather than shared transit.

**Request and scheduling.** Members request on-demand trips through whatever communications channel is currently functioning. The Division maintains a current schedule of pending on-demand trips and assigns operators and vehicles accordingly. Requests should be acknowledged promptly with a confirmed time — a member waiting for transport with no confirmation is not being served.

**Priority for members with mobility limitations.** The Community Care Division maintains a roster of members who require transport assistance as a standing need — medical appointments, community gatherings, errands. The Transit Division treats these as recurring scheduled commitments rather than ad hoc requests, coordinating directly with the Community Care Division to build them into the weekly schedule. A member who needs transport to a medical appointment every Tuesday does not submit a new request every week; the Division holds that slot.

**Operator discretion on safety.** An operator who determines that road, weather, or security conditions make a trip unsafe may decline or defer it. This judgment is not overridden by request urgency alone — an operator and vehicle lost to an avoidable incident eliminates capacity that the Division and its members depend on.

**Combining trips.** Where multiple on-demand requests overlap in timing and geography, the Division combines them into a single run where members consent and timing allows. This extends the Division's effective capacity and should be standard practice rather than the exception.

## Emergency Evacuation

Emergency evacuation is the Division's most demanding function and the one that most directly determines whether the community survives a rapidly deteriorating situation. The capacity to evacuate must exist before it is needed — a Division that is planning its evacuation response during an evacuation is already behind.

**The evacuation roster.** The Division maintains a current roster of all members, their locations, and their mobility status. Members who can self-evacuate — who have vehicles and can move independently — are distinguished from members who require Division transport. The roster is reviewed and updated regularly, not only when evacuation is imminent.

**Members who cannot self-evacuate are the first priority.** Elderly members, disabled members, members without vehicles, and members with children too young to travel independently are the Division's first responsibility in any evacuation. These members are assigned to specific vehicles and specific operators before any evacuation is called. When evacuation is ordered, those operators go directly to their assigned members. No member requiring Division transport waits to see if someone arrives.

**Evacuation routes are mapped and maintained.** The Division identifies primary and alternate evacuation routes out of each area of the Society's geography, assesses their likely condition under different emergency scenarios, and ensures that operators know all routes without relying on digital navigation. A route that is passable under normal conditions may be impassable in a flood, fire, or infrastructure failure — the Division maps alternatives for each scenario rather than maintaining a single route.

**Staging and assembly.** Evacuation does not mean every member independently going somewhere different. The Division establishes designated assembly points where members gather to be transported, and where self-evacuating members can collect those who need rides. Assembly points are known to all members in advance — not announced during the emergency.

**Vehicle capacity accounting.** The Division maintains a current count of total vehicle seating capacity available for evacuation and compares it against the number of members requiring Division transport. If capacity is insufficient, the gap is identified and a plan to close it — through mutual aid agreements with sister Societies or the surrounding community — is in place before it is needed.

**Coordination with Healthcare.** Members with medical equipment, who require temperature-controlled transport for medications, or who cannot be moved without specific precautions are identified in advance and their transport requirements communicated to evacuation operators. The Healthcare Division provides this information and updates it when member needs change.

**Drill.** Evacuation capacity that has never been tested is unknown capacity. The Division conducts periodic evacuation drills — not full activations every time, but exercises that verify roster accuracy, operator route knowledge, and vehicle readiness. Gaps identified in a drill are addressed before the next one.

## Fleet and Mode Management

The Division's transport capacity is only as reliable as the vehicles and modes it can actually field. Fleet management is not a background administrative function — it is the operational foundation on which everything else depends.

**Motorized vehicles** are the Division's primary capacity under current conditions. The Division maintains a current inventory of all vehicles available for transit operations — Society-owned vehicles and member vehicles whose owners have committed them to Division use — including their seating capacity, fuel type, current condition, and maintenance status. A vehicle that is not maintained is not available capacity.

**Fuel.** Motorized operations depend on fuel. The Division coordinates with the Energy Division on fuel availability and maintains a reserve adequate for emergency evacuation of all members requiring Division transport. The fuel reserve is not a general-purpose reserve — it is held for evacuation and emergency use and is not drawn down for routine operations without replenishment.

**Human-powered transport** is developed continuously alongside motorized operations, not as a fallback for when vehicles fail. Cycling extends the Division's reach on shorter routes and operates without fuel. The Division maintains serviceable bicycles and trains operators in their use. Routes that are realistic for cycling are identified and incorporated into the route network. A Society that has developed no human-powered transport capacity is fully dependent on motorized options — a vulnerability the Division does not accept.

**Walking** is the floor of the Division's capability. Any route the Division operates should be walkable, even if it is not normally walked. Operators should know walking times and paths for all routes so that no trip fails entirely when vehicles are unavailable.

**Maintenance.** Vehicles that are not maintained fail at the worst times. The Division establishes a maintenance schedule for all Society-owned vehicles and tracks the maintenance status of member-committed vehicles. The Division coordinates with whatever mechanical capacity exists in the community — members with vehicle maintenance skills are a Division asset whether or not they are Division operators.

## Division Relationships

**Community Care Division**
The closest operational relationship. The Community Care Division's transport commitments — regular trips for members who cannot move independently — are built into the Transit Division's weekly schedule as standing runs, not ad hoc requests. The two divisions coordinate directly and review their shared schedule regularly.

**Healthcare**
Members traveling to medical appointments, divisions requiring transport of health workers to member locations, and the evacuation requirements of members with medical needs all connect Healthcare to Transit. The Healthcare Division communicates its regular transport needs to the Division and updates member medical transport requirements as they change.

**Courier**
The Courier and Transit Divisions share some infrastructure — routes overlap, vehicles may be shared, and hub locations serve both. The two divisions coordinate to avoid conflicts and identify opportunities to combine capacity without compromising the reliability of either service.

**Communications**
On-demand trip requests, schedule publications, and evacuation notifications all flow through Communications. The Transit Division coordinates with Communications on how members reach the Division to request trips and how the Division reaches members when schedules change or evacuation is ordered.

**Energy**
Fuel availability directly constrains motorized transit capacity. The Division coordinates with Energy on fuel supply, maintains its evacuation reserve, and adjusts operational tempo when fuel availability is constrained.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division runs its scheduled routes, fulfills on-demand commitments, maintains its fleet, and develops human-powered capacity alongside motorized operations. The emphasis is on reliability — members who cannot depend on the Division to show up will stop depending on it.

As conditions deteriorate and fuel or vehicle availability becomes constrained, the Division shifts progressively toward shorter routes, consolidated on-demand trips, and human-powered alternatives. This shift should not be an emergency improvisation — the human-powered capacity is built before it is needed so the transition is a change in proportion rather than a scramble.

When evacuation is ordered, all routine operations cease and the full capacity of the Division is directed to moving members. Operators go to their pre-assigned members. Self-evacuating members are directed to assembly points. The Division does not improvise its evacuation response — it executes the plan it has already built.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Build the evacuation roster**
Before any scheduled route is established, the Division must know who can self-evacuate and who cannot, where every member is located, and what transport they require in an emergency. This is the Division's most critical document and the starting point for everything else.

**2. Assess demand and establish initial routes**
Survey member travel needs — where people go regularly, at what times, in what numbers. Establish scheduled routes where demand justifies them. Identify on-demand needs that routes will not cover.

**3. Inventory and commit fleet capacity**
Identify all vehicles available to the Division — Society-owned and member-committed. Assess seating capacity against evacuation requirements. Identify gaps. Establish the fuel reserve.

**4. Coordinate standing commitments with Community Care**
Work directly with the Community Care Division to identify all members requiring regular transport assistance. Build these into the weekly schedule as committed runs before the on-demand request system is handling them one at a time.

**5. Develop human-powered capacity**
Identify routes suitable for cycling. Acquire and maintain serviceable bicycles. Identify operators willing to run cycle routes. Begin integrating human-powered operations into the route network from the start.

**6. Conduct the first evacuation drill**
Before the Division considers itself operational, it drills its evacuation response. The drill verifies roster accuracy, operator assignments, route knowledge, and vehicle readiness. Gaps found in the drill are addressed before the Division declares the function ready.

## Division Documents

**Member Transport Roster** — a current record of all members, their locations, their mobility status, their evacuation assignment (self-evacuating or Division-transported), and any specific transport requirements. Updated whenever member circumstances change.

**Route Schedule** — the complete current schedule of all scheduled routes: stops, times, frequency. Posted at every stop and hub and published through Communications.

**On-Demand Request Log** — a running record of all on-demand trip requests: member, origin, destination, requested time, confirmed time, operator assigned, and completion status.

**Fleet Inventory** — a current record of all vehicles available to the Division: owner, seating capacity, fuel type, maintenance status, and evacuation assignment.

**Evacuation Plan** — the complete evacuation protocol: operator assignments, member assignments, assembly points, primary and alternate routes for each area, vehicle capacity accounting, fuel reserve status, and coordination protocols with Healthcare and sister Societies.

**Evacuation Drill Record** — a record of each drill conducted: date, scenario, participants, gaps identified, and corrective actions taken.
