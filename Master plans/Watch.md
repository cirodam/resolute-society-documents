# The Watch — Master Plan

## Mission

The Watch exists to respond to acute safety emergencies within the Society — situations where harm is actively occurring or immediately imminent and where the response cannot wait. It is the community's capacity to stabilize a dangerous situation, protect members from harm, and create the conditions in which the Judicial Division can do its work.

Like the Fire Division, the Watch is primarily relevant when existing institutions are unavailable or non-responsive. When professional emergency services can respond in time, they should be called and the Watch supports rather than replaces them. When they cannot — because institutions have failed, because response time is inadequate, because the Society is operating in conditions where professional services no longer function — the Watch is what the community has.

The Watch acts to ensure safety. The Judicial Division acts to address what happened and what it requires. This boundary is the Watch's most important operational principle. The Watch does not investigate, does not adjudicate, does not determine fault, and does not impose consequences. It stabilizes. Everything else belongs to the Judicial Division.

## Mandate

The Watch is responsible for three things:

- That acute safety emergencies within the Society are met with a trained, organized response rather than improvised reaction
- That the response is the minimum intervention necessary to secure safety — no more
- That once safety is secured, the situation is transferred to the Judicial Division cleanly and completely

## Core Functions

- **Acute Response** — showing up quickly and with clear purpose when a safety emergency is reported
- **De-escalation** — the primary tool; verbal and relational intervention that reduces the intensity of a situation before physical safety becomes the question
- **Stabilization** — separating parties, containing a situation, and holding it until it is safe and until Judicial can receive it
- **Handoff** — transferring the situation to the Judicial Division with a complete account of what the Watch found, what it did, and what the current status is
- **Readiness** — maintaining the trained capacity to respond before it is needed

## What the Watch Does and Does Not Do

The boundary between the Watch and the Judicial Division must be clearly understood by every Watch member and by the membership at large. Blurring this boundary — the Watch attempting to adjudicate, or the Judicial Division attempting to do real-time crisis response — produces failures in both directions.

**The Watch does:**
- Respond to reports of acute safety emergencies
- Attempt de-escalation as the first and primary intervention
- Separate parties who are in active conflict
- Ensure that members who are being harmed are removed from immediate danger
- Contain a situation so that it does not escalate or spread
- Remain present until the situation is stable and the Judicial Division has been notified
- Provide the Judicial Division with a factual account of what the Watch observed and did

**The Watch does not:**
- Determine who is at fault
- Impose consequences or conditions
- Speak for the Judicial Division or predict its outcomes
- Use more intervention than the situation requires
- Treat a member in crisis as having forfeited their dignity or their standing in the Society
- Remain involved in a matter after safety is secured and handoff is complete

The Watch is not a punitive body. A member who has caused harm is still a member with equal dignity until the Judicial Division has addressed the matter through its process. A member in mental health or substance crisis is a member in need of help, not a threat to be neutralized. The Watch holds this orientation even — especially — in the hardest situations, when it is most difficult.

## De-escalation

De-escalation is the Watch's primary tool and the skill that determines most outcomes. The Watch member who can reduce the intensity of a situation through presence, voice, and relationship is more valuable than any other capacity the division can develop. Physical intervention is the Watch's last resort, not its method.

**Presence matters before words do.** A Watch member who arrives calm, unhurried, and clearly in control of themselves changes the character of a situation before they have said anything. Urgency, visible anxiety, or aggression in the responder amplifies rather than reduces the situation. Watch training should develop the capacity to arrive calm — not to suppress genuine response, but to act from a grounded place regardless of what is happening.

**Acknowledgment before direction.** A person in acute distress — whether that distress is expressed as violence, threat, breakdown, or withdrawal — is someone whose nervous system is overwhelmed. Immediately telling that person what to do, or demanding compliance, typically escalates rather than de-escalates. The first words from a Watch member should acknowledge what they see: that this person is in serious distress, that the Watch is here, that they are not in danger from the Watch. Only after the person has been acknowledged does direction have any traction.

**Slow down the situation.** Acute situations feel like they require immediate action. Usually they require slowing down. A Watch member who can slow the pace — speak more slowly, move more deliberately, resist the pressure to resolve things immediately — gives everyone in the situation, including themselves, more time to think and more room to de-escalate. Most situations that feel like they will boil over in the next thirty seconds will not if someone calm is present and the tempo drops.

**Reduce audience.** Situations escalate when there is an audience. A member who is acting out in front of other members has social dynamics operating on them that make de-escalation harder. Where possible, the Watch should clear non-essential bystanders from the immediate area — not to conceal what is happening, but to remove the social pressure that is amplifying it. This should be done quietly and without making the departure of bystanders itself a spectacle.

**Know when de-escalation has failed.** De-escalation is the first and primary tool. It is not always sufficient. A Watch member who continues attempting verbal de-escalation while members are being physically harmed has made a judgment error. The Watch should have a clear internal threshold — established through training rather than improvised in the moment — for when de-escalation has been attempted adequately and the situation requires a different response.

## Stabilization

Stabilization is what the Watch achieves when de-escalation has succeeded or when de-escalation alone was insufficient. A stabilized situation is one in which no member is being actively harmed, the immediate danger has been contained, and the situation can be held until the Judicial Division takes it over.

**Separation is the primary stabilization tool.** Parties in conflict who are separated from each other cannot continue to harm each other. The Watch's first physical priority, when de-escalation alone has not contained the situation, is to create physical separation between parties. Separation should be achieved with the minimum physical contact necessary — guiding rather than forcing, creating space rather than restraining, wherever the situation allows.

**Ensuring safety for the person harmed.** Before any other stabilization concern, the Watch ensures that the member who has been harmed is no longer in immediate danger. This may mean relocating them, staying physically between them and the person who caused harm, or having one Watch member attend to them while another addresses the situation. The person harmed should not be managed as a logistical problem — they should be spoken to, informed of what the Watch is doing, and given agency in what happens next wherever that is possible.

**Containment, not restraint.** The Watch aims to contain a situation — prevent it from spreading, prevent additional members from being drawn in, prevent further harm — not to restrain or control individuals beyond what safety requires. A member who is no longer in a position to harm others and who is not in immediate danger does not need to be physically controlled. Containment of the situation is the goal, not control of the person.

**Holding the situation.** Once immediate safety is achieved, the Watch holds the situation — maintains a calm, stable presence — until the Judicial Division is notified and ready to receive it. Holding is not passive. The Watch member holding a situation is actively maintaining the calm that has been established, attentive to any change, and in communication with the Watch Officer about status.

**Healthcare coordination.** When members have been physically injured, the Watch immediately involves the Healthcare Division. Physical safety and medical care are parallel priorities, not sequential ones. The Watch should never delay medical response in the name of managing the situation — a Watch member who can stay with the situation while another calls for Healthcare is managing two priorities simultaneously, not choosing between them.

## The Handoff to Judicial

The handoff from the Watch to the Judicial Division is the most operationally important moment in any response. Done well, it ensures that the Judicial Division has what it needs to begin its work without requiring members to re-experience the acute situation. Done poorly, it creates gaps in the record, double-handling, and confusion about what the Watch's response means for what comes next.

**Notify the Judicial Division as early as possible.** The Watch does not wait until the situation is fully resolved to contact the Judicial Division. As soon as the Watch is on scene and has a basic understanding of what has occurred, the Judicial Division is notified. This gives the Judicial Division time to prepare and ensures the handoff is not rushed.

**The Watch's account is factual, not interpretive.** What the Watch reports to the Judicial Division is what it observed and what it did — not who was at fault, not what the Watch believes happened before it arrived, not what consequences the Watch thinks are appropriate. The Judicial Division is the body that weighs and interprets. The Watch provides the factual substrate from which that weighing begins.

**The account covers:** when the Watch was notified and by whom; what it found on arrival; what actions the Watch took and in what sequence; the current status of all parties; and any medical involvement. This account is provided in writing to the Judicial Division as soon as practical after the response, not only verbally.

**The Watch does not make statements to members about what Judicial will do.** A Watch member who tells a member in distress "you'll be fine, nothing will happen to you" or tells a member who was harmed "they'll be dealt with" has overstepped. The Watch does not know what the Judicial Division will do and does not represent it. What the Watch can say: that the Judicial Division will be in contact, that its process is fair, and that the Watch's role is now complete.

**Clear withdrawal.** Once the Judicial Division has received the handoff and safety is no longer the primary concern, the Watch withdraws. Watch members do not linger to observe Judicial proceedings, do not continue to offer opinions about the situation, and do not discuss what happened with other members. The Watch's involvement in a specific matter ends at handoff.

## Types of Situations

The Watch is designed for acute internal emergencies. The situations it responds to vary, and the response must be calibrated to the situation rather than applied uniformly.

**Interpersonal conflict escalating to violence.** Two or more members in a conflict that has become or is becoming physical. This is the situation the Watch is most directly designed for. De-escalation is the first response; separation if de-escalation is not sufficient; stabilization until Judicial can receive the matter.

**Member in acute mental health crisis.** A member whose mental health crisis poses a safety risk — to themselves, to others, or both. This situation requires close coordination with the Healthcare Division, which holds the clinical function. The Watch's role is safety; Healthcare's role is care. The two functions operate in parallel. A member in mental health crisis has not forfeited their dignity or their membership standing. The Watch should never treat crisis as misconduct.

**Domestic or intimate partner situation.** Violence or threat of violence within a household relationship. This is among the most sensitive situations the Watch will encounter. The person being harmed may not want Watch involvement; may fear the consequences of Watch involvement for their relationship; may be ambivalent about what they want. The Watch's first obligation is to ensure the safety of the person being harmed. It does not require that person to take any particular action. It does not leave them in a situation where harm is actively continuing. It attends to their wishes within the bounds of what safety allows.

**Threat to community safety.** A situation in which a member poses an acute threat to the community broadly — not a specific interpersonal conflict, but a member whose behavior in the moment endangers others. The Watch responds to the immediate safety dimension. The Judicial Division addresses the broader matter, which may include the most serious outcomes available to the Society.

## Division Relationships

**Judicial Division**
The Watch's primary relationship. Every situation the Watch responds to becomes a Judicial matter once safety is secured. The two divisions must maintain ongoing communication about handoff protocols, account format, and how the Watch's involvement is recorded in Judicial proceedings. The Watch does not operate as a preliminary adjudicative body — it operates as the safety response that makes Judicial's work possible.

**Healthcare Division**
Physical injury and mental health crisis both require Healthcare involvement. The Watch and Healthcare should have standing coordination so that each knows how to reach the other quickly and what each needs from the other during a joint response. The Watch does not make medical judgments. Healthcare does not make safety judgments. Both operate simultaneously when both are needed.

**Community Care Division**
Members who are elderly, disabled, or otherwise vulnerable may be involved in situations the Watch responds to — as persons harmed, as members in crisis, or as witnesses. The Watch should have awareness of which members have specific vulnerabilities and coordinate with Community Care when their members are involved in a situation.

**Communications Division**
How the Watch is notified matters. Every member needs to know how to reach the Watch quickly when they observe an acute situation. The Communications Division establishes and maintains the notification channel, ensures every member knows it, and ensures that Watch members can be reached without delay. A Watch that cannot be reached is not a Watch.

## Operational Posture

The Watch operates in two modes.

**When professional services are functional:** The Watch's primary role is readiness. It maintains its trained capacity, its notification protocols, and its coordination with the Judicial Division. When an acute situation occurs and professional services can respond in time, the Watch supports — notifying services, maintaining calm until arrival, ensuring safety — without displacing professional response. The Watch does not activate its independent response function when professional services are available and responding.

**When professional services are unavailable:** The Watch activates its full response function. It is the Society's only acute safety capacity and must function as such. This mode may be temporary — a response gap during a specific event — or extended, if institutions are broadly unavailable. The Watch's readiness during the first mode determines how effectively it can function in the second.

**On-call rotation.** The Watch maintains a roster of trained members on-call at all times. The on-call structure ensures that the Watch can respond at any hour without requiring all Watch members to be available simultaneously. The on-call rotation distributes the burden across the Watch membership and ensures no member is carrying the function indefinitely.

**Watch Officer.** Every response has a designated Watch Officer who serves as incident commander. The Watch Officer makes real-time decisions, coordinates with Healthcare and Judicial during the response, and is responsible for the written account provided to the Judicial Division. Every trained Watch member should be capable of serving as Watch Officer, because the assigned Officer may not be the first to arrive.

---

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Establish the on-call structure and notification channel**
Before the Watch can respond to anything, every member must know how to reach it, and the Watch must be reachable at all times. The on-call rotation and notification channel are the Watch's infrastructure. They come first.

**2. Train the initial Watch membership in de-escalation and crisis response**
Watch members require specific training before they respond to situations. De-escalation, crisis intervention, physical separation techniques, and the handoff protocol are the core curriculum. The Watch should not respond to situations before its members have this training.

**3. Establish handoff protocols with the Judicial Division**
The Watch and Judicial Division should agree on the account format, notification process, and the division of roles before any specific situation requires them to work together. A handoff protocol established in advance is a handoff that works under pressure.

**4. Coordinate with Healthcare**
Establish the joint response protocol with the Healthcare Division so that Watch and Healthcare members know how to work alongside each other during a response that involves both safety and medical needs.

**5. Make the Watch known to the membership**
Every member should know the Watch exists, how to reach it, and what it will and will not do. The Watch's function depends on members calling it when they observe an acute situation. Members who do not know the Watch exists, or who do not trust what it will do, will not call.

**6. Conduct regular drills**
Like the Fire Division, the Watch's capacity degrades without practice. Scenario-based drills — de-escalation practice, joint response with Healthcare, handoff simulation with Judicial — maintain readiness and identify gaps before a real situation reveals them.

## Division Documents

**Incident Account** — the written factual record provided to the Judicial Division after every response: notification time and source, observations on arrival, actions taken in sequence, current status of all parties at handoff, and any Healthcare involvement. Standardized format maintained by the Watch.

**On-Call Roster** — the current rotation of Watch members on-call, updated as the membership and availability changes. Known to the Communications Division for notification purposes.

**Handoff Protocol** — the agreed procedure between the Watch and the Judicial Division for transferring a matter: what the Watch provides, in what form, and what the Judicial Division needs to receive it. Maintained jointly by both divisions and reviewed annually.

**Joint Response Protocol with Healthcare** — the agreed procedure for situations requiring both Watch and Healthcare response simultaneously: who does what, how they communicate during a response, and how each division's records relate to the other's.

**Training Record** — a record of all Watch member training completed, including de-escalation, crisis intervention, and drill participation. The Watch Officer confirms training is current before any member serves on the on-call rotation.
