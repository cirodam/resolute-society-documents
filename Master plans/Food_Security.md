# Food Security Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Food Security Division exists to ensure that every member of the Resolute Society has access to food — today and under whatever conditions tomorrow brings. It does this by maintaining the capacity to acquire, store, process, and distribute food at the community level, independent of any single supply chain or institutional structure.

The division also exists as an act of cultural recovery. Practical knowledge that was ordinary household competency two or three generations ago — preserving food, storing grain, cooking from raw staples — has eroded to near extinction. Each generation that lost a skill did so because a cheaper, more convenient substitute appeared. The cumulative result is a population genuinely dependent on systems it cannot replace. This division exists to recover that knowledge before it is gone, and to hold it in common.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around food security — an instrument of collective action, not a provider of services. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by what the community can do.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for three things:

- That no member goes without food due to inability to access or prepare it
- That the community maintains meaningful food reserves against disruption
- That the knowledge and infrastructure to store, process, and distribute food at community scale are actively developed, broadly held, and kept ready — not concentrated in the division, but distributed across the community

## Core Functions

- **Acquisition** — maintaining a reliable flow of food into the community through diverse sourcing channels
- **Storage** — maintaining community-level food reserves against disruption
- **Processing** — converting raw food inputs into usable form at community scale
- **Distribution** — ensuring food reaches every member who needs it
- **Household Food Preservation** — building preservation capacity and knowledge at the household level
- **Household Food Preparation** — building cooking capacity and knowledge at the household level

## Acquisition

The division is responsible for maintaining a reliable flow of food into the community. This does not require the division to grow food — the Agricultural Division is the primary internal source of cultivated produce — but the division must not depend on any single source. The division should at all times understand where its next inputs are coming from.

Acquisition channels exist on a spectrum from commercial to relational. The division should have active presence across all of them.

**Commercial purchasing** is the most accessible channel under normal conditions. Restaurant supply stores (Restaurant Depot, Sysco) offer bulk staples at significantly lower prices than retail and are accessible to organizations. Ethnic grocery stores frequently carry bulk rice, beans, and lentils at lower prices than mainstream grocery chains. Costco and Sam's Club are viable for shelf-stable bulk goods.

**Food bank networks** are a major underutilized resource. Local food banks are member agencies of the Feeding America network and distribute both donated food and USDA commodity goods. Access requires the division to register as a formal partner agency — this is not a walk-in relationship. The division should pursue this registration early. Through food banks, the division can also access **USDA TEFAP (The Emergency Food Assistance Program)**, a federal program that distributes commodity foods including grains, canned goods, and dairy to registered organizations. This is a significant and largely unknown channel.

**Gleaning** is the harvesting of surplus or unharvested crops from farms after commercial harvest. It can be a substantial source of fresh produce. The Society of St. Andrew coordinates gleaning nationally; local gleaning networks also exist. The key operational requirement is labor availability on short notice — gleaning windows open and close quickly based on harvest timing.

**Direct farm relationships** allow the division to source bulk grains and produce at below-retail prices. These relationships must be built before they are needed. A farmer who knows the division is a reliable, organized buyer is more likely to prioritize them when supply is tight.

**Caloric thinking** should inform all acquisition decisions. The division should think in terms of calories per dollar and calories per pound, not just volume of food. Cooking oil is the most calorie-dense food available — roughly 3,500 calories per pound — and is frequently overlooked in food storage planning. Rice and dried beans together form a complete protein, covering essential amino acids that neither provides alone. A baseline planning figure is 2,000-2,500 calories per person per day.

## Storage

The division maintains community-level food reserves sufficient to sustain members through periods of supply disruption. This includes dry goods and grain storage, preserved foods, and household-level storage support. Reserves should be kept on rotation to prevent spoilage and maintained against a target buffer the division defines and publishes.

Most people underestimate what viable long-term food storage actually requires. The three variables that determine whether stored grain survives or spoils are moisture, temperature, and oxygen.

**Moisture** is the most critical. Grain must be below 12-14% moisture content for long-term storage — above that threshold, mold and mycotoxins develop, and the grain becomes unusable. Moisture content cannot be assessed by looking at grain; the division needs a grain moisture meter.

**Temperature** directly affects shelf life. Cooler storage roughly doubles shelf life for every 10°F drop. A metal building that reaches 100°F in a Georgia summer will degrade grain far faster than a climate-stable interior space. Building selection must account for this.

**Oxygen** sustains insect life and causes oxidation. The standard for long-term grain storage is food-grade mylar bags sealed with oxygen absorbers, packed inside food-grade plastic buckets. This combination, in cool dry conditions, is what enables decade-scale storage. Regular plastic containers and improvised solutions do not perform comparably.

**What actually stores long-term** under proper conditions:
- Whole wheat berries: 25+ years
- White rice: 25-30 years
- Dried beans and legumes: caloric value holds indefinitely, but beans older than 5 years may never fully soften regardless of cooking time
- Whole oat groats: 20-30 years
- Honey, salt, white sugar: indefinite

**What does not store long-term:**
- Brown rice: 6-12 months — the bran layer contains oils that go rancid
- Whole wheat flour: 6-12 months for the same reason
- Rolled oats: significantly shorter than whole groats
- Cooking oils: 1-4 years depending on type; coconut oil lasts longest

The implication is that the division should store whole grains and mill them as needed — not store flour. Whole wheat berries last 25 years; the flour milled from them lasts months.

**Rotation** must be a standing operational protocol, not an afterthought. The division should operate on a first-in, first-out basis with scheduled inspection cycles. Stored food that is not actively monitored and rotated becomes a liability rather than an asset.

## Processing

The division maintains the equipment and knowledge to convert raw food inputs into usable form. This includes grain milling and food preservation methods such as canning, fermentation, and drying. Processing capacity is both a community function and a knowledge base — the division is responsible for ensuring these skills exist among its members.

**Grain milling** is the division's most critical processing capability, given that whole grains are the foundation of long-term food storage. The division should maintain both hand-powered and electric milling capacity — electric mills are far faster, but power cannot be assumed.

Hand-powered stone mills (the Country Living Grain Mill is the standard recommendation for community use, as it is robust and can be converted to motor power) produce roughly one pound of flour per five to ten minutes of hard cranking. A community of 50 people eating bread daily may need 25-50 pounds of flour per week. That is a significant labor commitment. The division should plan its milling capacity against actual community size.

**Grain dust is a fire and explosion hazard.** Flour dust suspended in air is flammable; commercial grain elevators have exploded for this reason. At community scale the risk is lower but real. Milling should always be done in ventilated spaces where dust cannot accumulate.

Freshly milled whole wheat flour contains oils from the bran and germ that cause it to go rancid within days at room temperature. It must be used quickly, refrigerated, or frozen. This is why the division stores whole grain rather than flour — and mills only what it needs.

**Food preservation** at community scale has different requirements than household scale. Three methods are most relevant:

**Fermentation** requires no equipment beyond salt and vessels. Lacto-fermentation — the process behind sauerkraut, kimchi, and traditional pickles — uses salt to create an anaerobic brine environment that preserves vegetables for months. No vinegar, no heat, no electricity. It is the most accessible preservation method available and should be a core division competency.

**Dehydration** can be done with solar dehydrators that require no electricity and can be built cheaply. Commercial dehydrators are faster but power-dependent. Solar dehydration is the appropriate baseline for an off-grid scenario.

**Root cellaring** is frequently overlooked. Potatoes, carrots, beets, turnips, winter squash, and cabbage can be stored for months in a cool, humid, dark space without any processing at all. The target environment is 32-40°F with 90-95% humidity. A basement, earth-sheltered space, or purpose-built root cellar can dramatically extend the life of fresh produce with zero energy input. The division should identify viable root cellaring spaces as part of its facility mapping.

**Nixtamalization** is a non-obvious but important process for corn storage. Treating dried corn with an alkaline solution — traditionally lime (calcium hydroxide) — before grinding dramatically increases its nutritional availability, releasing niacin and improving its amino acid profile. Without this step, corn as a dietary staple can cause pellagra, a niacin deficiency disease. The division should understand and teach this process if corn is part of its storage or distribution plan.

## Distribution

The division ensures food reaches members who need it. This includes operating community kitchens, supporting household food preparation, and maintaining dedicated delivery for members who cannot prepare or retrieve food on their own. Distribution is the most visible function of the division and the clearest measure of whether it is fulfilling its mission.

The division's primary distribution model is household-based. Members receive food from the division and prepare it in their own homes. Community kitchens exist for members who are genuinely unable to cook for themselves — due to disability, illness, acute crisis, or loss of facilities — not as a replacement for household cooking capacity.

**Regular distribution** should operate on a defined schedule — weekly or bi-weekly — at a consistent location. Predictability matters: members plan around it, and the division can plan inventory around it. Distribution should be tracked, both to ensure equity across households and to give the division accurate data on consumption rates for reserve planning. The division's distribution records should integrate with the society's broader ledger-keeping function.

**Portioning** requires caloric planning. The baseline figure is 2,000-2,500 calories per person per day. Distribution quantities should be calculated per household member, not per household. A 50-pound bag of white rice contains roughly 80,000 calories — enough to supply one person for about 40 days at 2,000 calories, but not as a sole food source. The division should distribute a balanced mix of staples, not single commodities.

**Special needs** must be accounted for in distribution planning. The division should maintain a registry of members with dietary restrictions, allergies, or medical dietary requirements. Critically, infants and young children cannot eat the same staples as adults — the division must have a specific plan for households with infants, including access to formula or appropriate food.

**Delivery** must exist as a parallel capability for members who cannot come to a distribution point. The division should maintain a current list of members requiring delivery — disabled members, those without transportation, those in acute health crisis — and treat delivery as a standing operational responsibility, not an ad hoc favor.

**Community kitchens** become more central as conditions deteriorate and more members lose the ability to cook independently — due to loss of utilities, housing instability, or other crisis conditions. The division should maintain the capacity to scale kitchen operations up without prior notice. Cooking at community scale requires attention to food safety practices that are less critical at household scale: temperature control, cross-contamination prevention, and volume-appropriate cooking methods. The division should also maintain a wood-fired cooking capability as a backup for grid-down scenarios.

## Household Food Preservation

The division's food preservation program exists to recover and redistribute knowledge that most households once held and have since lost. The goal is not a well-attended class — it is a community in which enough households practice preservation regularly that the knowledge becomes self-sustaining and cannot be lost again. The division organizes this recovery. The knowledge belongs to the community.

The division should maintain a lending library of preservation equipment as a bridge — lowering the barrier to entry for households building the skill before they have their own equipment. The end state is households owning and using their own equipment, not permanently depending on the division's. Instruction should be organized around knowledgeable community members teaching others — the division creates the conditions for that transmission, it does not need to be the source of the knowledge itself.

**The foundational distinction in canning** is acid level. Water bath canning is safe only for high-acid foods — fruits, jams, jellies, pickles, and tomatoes with added acid. Low-acid foods — vegetables, meats, beans, soups — require pressure canning to reach the temperatures necessary to destroy botulism spores. Botulism toxin is odorless and tasteless; there is no way to detect it without a lab. This distinction must be taught clearly and consistently, and never compromised.

**Pressure canners and pressure cookers are not the same thing.** A standard pressure cooker is not rated for canning and cannot reliably reach the temperatures required to kill botulism spores. The division's instruction program must address this directly — it is a common and dangerous mistake. The All American pressure canner (all-metal, no rubber gasket) is the recommended standard for community use due to its durability and reliability.

**Standard canning lids are single-use.** The sealing compound on Kerr and Ball lids degrades after one use. Jars are reusable indefinitely if undamaged; lids are not. The division's equipment lending library should stockpile lids as a consumable. Tattler reusable lids exist as an alternative but require different technique and should not be mixed with standard-lid instruction.

**Fermentation** is the most accessible entry point for most members and should be taught first. Lacto-fermentation requires only salt, vegetables, and vessels. The critical variables are salt ratio — typically 2% salt by weight of vegetables — and keeping vegetables fully submerged under brine to maintain an anaerobic environment. Common failures are too little salt (rot), too much salt (inhibited fermentation), and air exposure (surface mold). Fermentation speed is temperature-dependent: warmer temperatures ferment faster; cooler temperatures produce better flavor and longer shelf life.

**Dehydration** of vegetables requires blanching first — brief immersion in boiling water — to stop enzyme activity that causes off-flavors and color loss during drying. Meat dehydrated for jerky must reach an internal temperature of 160°F to be safe; dehydration alone does not guarantee this. Dehydrated food must be stored in airtight containers away from light; moisture reabsorption is the primary failure mode.

**Instruction should be sequenced by risk.** Members should learn fermentation first, then water bath canning, then pressure canning. Pressure canning must never be taught without hands-on demonstration — written instructions alone are insufficient given the consequences of error.

**The division's equipment lending library** should include at minimum: All American pressure canners, water bath canners, canning jar lifters and funnels, dehydrators, fermentation weights and crocks, and an adequate supply of canning jars and lids.

## Household Food Preparation

The division's food preparation program exists for the same reason as its preservation program — to recover practical knowledge that has been lost and return it to the community as a broadly held, self-sustaining capability. The ability to cook from raw staples was once ordinary. It is now rare. The division's role is to organize its recovery, not to cook on the community's behalf. As with preservation, instruction should be organized around knowledgeable community members teaching others — the division identifies who holds this knowledge and creates the conditions for it to spread.

In a disrupted environment, members may have access to bulk staples but lack the knowledge or equipment to turn them into meals. Cooking from scratch — especially from raw grains and dried legumes — is a distinct skill set from cooking with processed or pre-prepared ingredients, and one that most people do not currently have.

**Dried beans** must be soaked overnight in cold water, drained, and then simmered for one to two hours. Beans that are more than five years old may never fully soften regardless of cooking time — another reason storage rotation matters. Salt and acidic ingredients such as tomatoes or vinegar must not be added until beans are fully cooked; acid prevents the cell walls from softening. A pressure cooker reduces bean cooking time to 30-45 minutes and is one of the most valuable pieces of household equipment for cooking from storage.

**Kidney beans specifically require boiling.** Raw kidney beans contain phytohaemagglutinin, a toxic lectin that causes severe nausea and vomiting. Kidney beans must be boiled vigorously for at least ten minutes before simmering — slow cookers do not reach the temperature needed to neutralize this toxin. This is not widely known and must be explicitly taught.

**Whole grains** require significantly more water and time than processed grains. Wheat berries and oat groats benefit from overnight soaking, which reduces cooking time and improves digestibility. Most whole grains take 45-60 minutes of simmering. Members accustomed to instant or processed grains should be taught to plan meals accordingly.

**Bread without commercial yeast** is achievable through sourdough or flatbreads. A sourdough starter — a live culture of wild yeast and bacteria maintained in flour and water — can be kept indefinitely with regular feeding and requires no store-bought inputs. Flatbreads (tortillas, chapati) require only flour, water, salt, and heat, with no leavening at all. The division should maintain and distribute active sourdough starter as part of its food preparation program.

**Cooking without gas or electricity** is a core skill the division must teach. Wood fire cooking requires practice — temperature control is fundamentally different from a gas burner. Rocket stoves, which can be built from bricks or cinder blocks, burn small-diameter wood efficiently and produce a focused, controllable flame. Solar ovens can reach 250-350°F on a sunny day, sufficient for cooking beans, rice, and bread. Retained heat cooking — bringing food to a full boil, then placing the pot in a heavily insulated box — continues cooking for hours with no additional fuel and is one of the most fuel-efficient methods available.

**Fat is a nutritional requirement, not a preference.** A diet of lean protein and carbohydrates without adequate fat leads to protein poisoning — historically called rabbit starvation — characterized by nausea, diarrhea, and eventual organ failure. Cooking fat must be part of every household's food supply and every meal the division prepares or distributes.

**Nutritional completeness** should be a standing concern. Rice and beans together provide all essential amino acids that neither supplies alone. Fresh or fermented vegetables provide micronutrients that stored staples lack. Vitamin C deficiency causes scurvy within weeks of a diet with no fresh produce; fermented foods such as sauerkraut are a viable source when fresh produce is unavailable. The division's instruction program should teach members to think in terms of nutritional completeness, not just caloric sufficiency.

## Division Relationships

**Agriculture**
The Agricultural Division is the division's primary internal food source. Food Security receives cultivated yield and coordinates with Agriculture on harvest timing, volumes, and preservation needs. The two divisions should maintain ongoing communication, but Food Security's operational independence means it cannot wait on Agriculture to fulfill its mandate.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on building capacity, supporting members who lack access or ability, and maintaining reserves. As conditions deteriorate, those same functions become more urgent and more central — the division does not change what it does, it scales what it does.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Source food through whatever channels are available
- Maintain and draw on community reserves
- Operate community kitchens at whatever frequency members need
- Preserve and process food at community scale
- Deliver food to members who cannot retrieve it themselves

The division must be capable of sustaining all of these functions simultaneously and indefinitely. Infrastructure, skills, and supply relationships should be built with that standard in mind, not with the assumption that crisis is temporary.

## Establishment Priorities

The division's first work is not operational — it is foundational. Before the division can store, process, or distribute food reliably, it must understand the food environment it operates in and build the relationships that will sustain it under any condition.

**1. Map the food landscape**
The division's first task is to develop a clear picture of where the community's food currently comes from — commercial supply chains, local producers, food banks, mutual aid networks, and any other sources. This includes understanding vulnerabilities: what fails first, what holds longest, and where the gaps are for members who are already food insecure.

Mapping also includes the physical environment. The division should identify buildings in the community that could serve as grain storage or milling facilities if purpose-built infrastructure is unavailable. This means actively surveying candidate spaces — assessing whether they are dry, pest-resistant, accessible, and structurally adequate — so that if the need arises, the division is not starting from scratch.

**2. Build sourcing relationships**
Before infrastructure exists, relationships are the division's primary asset. The division should establish direct connections with local farmers, food banks, bulk suppliers, and mutual aid networks. These relationships must be maintained actively, not treated as a one-time task.

**3. Plan community infrastructure**
With a grounded understanding of the food landscape and sourcing capacity, the division plans the physical infrastructure it needs — grain storage, milling, preservation facilities — sized to the actual community and realistic about what can be built and maintained.

**4. Build capacity**
The division builds out planned infrastructure and develops the skills among its members to operate it. Preservation knowledge, grain processing, and food handling are all active competencies the division is responsible for cultivating.

**5. Operate community programs**
Community kitchens and regular distribution programs come online once the foundation is in place. These are the most visible face of the division but depend entirely on the work that precedes them.

## Division Documents

The Food Security Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt — instructional materials and planning frameworks that give members and division leadership the knowledge they need to operate effectively.

**Household Food Preservation Guide** — instructional guide covering fermentation, water bath canning, pressure canning, and dehydration. Includes safety guidance, equipment requirements, and sequenced instruction by risk level.

**Household Food Preparation Guide** — instructional guide covering cooking from bulk staples, alternative cooking methods, and nutritional completeness. Includes specific guidance on beans, whole grains, bread without commercial yeast, and cooking without gas or electricity.

**Community Kitchen Operations Guide** — operational framework for running a community kitchen, including food safety protocols, volume cooking methods, and wood-fired backup procedures.

**Storage and Reserve Planning Framework** — a general methodology for establishing and maintaining food reserves. Covers what to stock, in what ratios, caloric planning by household size, storage requirements, and rotation protocols. Designed to be applied locally regardless of community size or geography.

## Appendix: Core Staple Crops of the United States

A staple crop is one that provides the caloric and nutritional foundation of a diet — dense, storable, and producible at scale. What follows is a regional overview of the primary staple crops across the United States, organized by category. Every Resolute Society should understand which of these are viable in its local climate and soil conditions, and orient its storage and acquisition programs accordingly.

**Grains**

*Wheat* is the most widely grown grain in the country. Hard red winter wheat dominates the Great Plains and is planted in fall, overwintered, and harvested in early summer. Hard white wheat and soft wheat varieties extend into the Midwest, Mid-Atlantic, and Pacific Northwest. Wheat berries store for 25+ years under proper conditions and are the most calorie-dense and versatile grain available for long-term storage.

*Corn* grows across virtually the entire United States and is the most productive grain crop by volume. Dry field corn — not sweet corn — is what is stored and milled. It is the historical staple grain of the South, Midwest, and much of the Americas. Requires nixtamalization for full nutritional value (see Processing section).

*Oats* are well suited to the Northern states — the Midwest, Great Plains, and Pacific Northwest — and tolerate cool, wet conditions that other grains do not. Whole oat groats store for 20-30 years. They are among the most nutritionally complete grains available.

*Sorghum* is exceptionally drought tolerant and well suited to the South, Southern Plains, and arid regions where corn struggles. It can be eaten as a grain, fermented, or processed into sorghum syrup. It is an underutilized crop with significant resilience value.

*Rye* is cold-hardy and well suited to the Northeast and Northern Plains. It grows in poor soils where wheat cannot, making it valuable as a fallback grain in difficult conditions. Stores comparably to wheat.

*Rice* requires standing water and is primarily viable in the Gulf Coast region and parts of the Carolinas and California. It is listed here because of its exceptional storage life (25-30 years) and caloric density, making it valuable as a stored staple regardless of whether it can be grown locally.

**Legumes**

Legumes are the protein complement to grains. Together, grains and legumes provide all essential amino acids — neither does so alone. Every society's storage plan should include both.

*Dried beans* (pinto, navy, black, kidney, great northern) are widely adaptable across the US with different varieties suited to different climates. They are the most accessible and versatile legume for long-term storage.

*Cowpeas and field peas* (black-eyed peas, crowder peas) are exceptionally heat and drought tolerant, making them the primary legume for the South and Southeast. They fix nitrogen in the soil and produce reliably in conditions that stress other legumes.

*Lentils* are well suited to the arid inland Northwest — Eastern Washington and Oregon are major US lentil-producing regions. They are among the fastest-cooking legumes, requiring no soaking, and store for years.

*Soybeans* dominate the Midwest and provide both protein and oil. They require processing (cooking thoroughly — raw soybeans contain antinutrients) but are among the most calorie-dense and complete legumes available.

*Peanuts* are technically a legume and are among the most calorie-dense foods available — high in both fat and protein. They are well suited to the Southeast and parts of the South and Mid-Atlantic. They do not store as long as dried beans but are exceptionally valuable as a fat and protein source.

**Roots and Tubers**

*Potatoes* are one of the most calorie-dense and widely adaptable crops in existence. They grow across the entire country with peak production in the Pacific Northwest, Northern Plains, and Northeast. They store for 6-12 months in proper root cellar conditions (cool, humid, dark).

*Sweet potatoes* are better suited to warmer climates — the South, Southeast, and Mid-Atlantic — and are among the most nutritionally complete vegetables available, providing significant calories, vitamin A, and vitamin C. They store for 4-6 months in warm, dry conditions — notably different from regular potatoes.

*Turnips* and other root vegetables (beets, carrots, parsnips) are cool-season crops viable across most of the country. They store for months in root cellar conditions and provide important micronutrients during winter months when fresh produce is otherwise unavailable.

**Oilseeds**

Fat is a caloric necessity, and oilseeds are the primary means of producing it locally.

*Sunflowers* grow across the US, particularly the Plains states, and are among the most productive oilseed crops available to small-scale growers. Seeds can be eaten directly or pressed for oil.

*Peanuts* (also listed under legumes) are the most accessible fat source in the South and Southeast.

*Flaxseed* is cold-tolerant and well suited to the Northern Plains and Upper Midwest. It provides omega-3 fatty acids that are largely absent from grain and legume-based diets.

**The Three Sisters**

Corn, beans, and squash — the traditional companion planting system of Indigenous North American agriculture — together form a nutritionally complete and mutually reinforcing growing system viable across most of the United States. Corn provides carbohydrates and structure; beans fix nitrogen and provide protein; winter squash shades the ground to retain moisture and provides vitamins and minerals. This system represents centuries of accumulated agricultural knowledge and remains one of the most resilient and productive small-scale growing approaches available.
