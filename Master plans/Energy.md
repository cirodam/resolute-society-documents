# Energy Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Energy Division exists to ensure that every member of the Resolute Society has access to essential energy services — heating, cooking, lighting, and power for critical equipment — today and under whatever conditions tomorrow brings. It does this by building community-level capacity to produce, store, and manage energy independent of centralized infrastructure, and by ensuring that the knowledge and supplies to do so are held broadly across the community.

The division is not an institution that exists apart from the community it serves. It is the community organized around energy — an instrument of collective action, not a provider of services. Its success is measured not by what the division can do, but by what the community can do.

Human communities managed their own energy for the entirety of history before the electrical grid. Wood, water mills, windmills, draft animals, and fire met every essential need. The centralization of energy into a grid — convenient, powerful, and almost universally depended upon — has left most communities without any alternative when that grid fails. The knowledge and infrastructure to meet essential needs without it are recoverable. The division exists to recover them.

## Mandate

The division is responsible for four things:

- That no member goes without essential energy services — heat, cooking, light — due to infrastructure failure or inability to afford utility costs
- That the community maintains production and storage capacity for essential energy needs independent of any single supply system
- That critical fuel-dependent equipment — particularly agricultural machinery — has a reliable liquid fuel supply, because some equipment cannot be substituted or converted
- That energy knowledge — generation, storage, fuel management, efficient use — is actively held broadly enough across the community that it cannot be lost

## Core Functions

- **Assessment and Mapping** — understanding the community's current energy infrastructure, dependencies, consumption patterns, and failure modes
- **Power Generation** — maintaining solar, generator, and community-scale generation capacity for essential electrical loads
- **Energy Storage** — batteries and their specific constraints, capabilities, and maintenance requirements
- **Liquid Fuels** — storage, stabilization, rotation, and community-scale production of gasoline, diesel, propane, and biodiesel
- **Heating** — wood, passive solar, and insulation as the primary tools for meeting heating needs without grid dependence
- **Cooling** — passive cooling strategies and the health stakes of heat in warm and hot climates
- **Cooking Energy** — efficient low-fuel cooking methods that function without grid power or abundant fuel
- **Lighting** — low-power lighting options and their practical tradeoffs
- **Household Energy Planning** — building distributed energy knowledge and capacity at the household level across the community

## Assessment and Mapping

The division's first function is understanding the community's energy situation before disruption occurs — what infrastructure it depends on, how that infrastructure fails, who is most vulnerable when it does, and what assets already exist within the community that can serve as alternatives. Energy assessment is not a one-time task; infrastructure changes, members' situations change, and the division's understanding must stay current.

**The electrical grid fails as a system, not as individual components.** Most extended outages originate at transmission infrastructure — high-voltage lines and substations that serve large areas — rather than at the local distribution level. A community whose power is interrupted by a downed local line is likely to have power restored within hours or days. A community whose regional substation or transmission corridor is damaged faces a fundamentally different situation that can extend for weeks. The division should understand which transmission infrastructure serves its area, where the nearest substations are, and what historical outage patterns look like. County emergency management offices and the local utility typically publish this information or will share it.

**Natural gas infrastructure** has a different failure profile than the electrical grid. Gas compressor stations and pressure regulators along transmission pipelines often run on gas themselves, giving them some independence from electrical outages. However, local distribution infrastructure — the lines serving homes and businesses — can still fail, and gas supply can be interrupted by damage to pipelines or by deliberate shutoff. Members who heat with natural gas should understand that their supply is not guaranteed during extended grid disruption.

**Heating fuel dependency is the most urgent energy vulnerability to map.** Electric resistance heat and electric heat pumps fail immediately with the grid. Natural gas heat continues if gas supply is maintained. Propane heat continues as long as the tank is filled. Wood heat is independent of any supply network beyond the wood itself. The division should identify every member household's primary heating fuel and characterize its failure profile. Members dependent on electric heat in climates where winter temperatures are dangerous are the division's most acute vulnerability and should be prioritized in household energy planning.

**Medical equipment dependencies** require specific mapping. Members who depend on powered medical equipment — home oxygen concentrators, CPAP machines, powered wheelchairs, refrigerated medications — face life-safety consequences from power loss that other members do not. The division should identify these members and treat their power continuity as a critical planning priority, distinct from general household power planning.

**Community energy assets** — generators, solar installations, fuel storage, woodlots — represent capacity the division does not need to build. The assessment function should inventory what already exists: who has generators and how large, who has solar panels and whether they include battery storage, who has significant fuel stockpiles, and where accessible woodlots and firewood sources are located. Relationships with fuel suppliers — diesel distributors, propane companies, firewood vendors — are infrastructure the division should establish before they are urgently needed.

**Critical load identification** is the analytical core of the assessment function. The question is not how to replace the community's total energy consumption under disruption — that is not achievable — but which loads are essential and which are merely convenient. Essential loads include heating and cooling where temperatures are dangerous, cooking, lighting sufficient for safety, refrigeration for medications, water pumping where wells require it, and fuel for agricultural and critical vehicles. Everything else is a lower priority. The division should develop a clear picture of essential loads across the community and size its generation and storage planning against those loads, not total normal consumption.

## Power Generation

The division maintains capacity to generate electricity for essential loads when grid power is unavailable. Practical community-level generation centers on solar photovoltaics and generators — each with distinct strengths, limitations, and appropriate applications. The goal is not to replicate grid-scale power but to meet the critical loads identified in the assessment function.

**Solar photovoltaic panels** convert sunlight directly to electricity and represent the most practical long-term generation option for most communities. They have no moving parts, require minimal maintenance, produce no fuel consumption, and last 25-30 years under normal conditions. Their primary constraints are intermittency — no generation at night, reduced generation on cloudy days — and the need for battery storage to be useful beyond the hours of direct sun.

A non-obvious and important limitation: **grid-tied solar systems shut off during grid outages by default.** Anti-islanding protection, required by utility interconnection standards, causes grid-tied inverters to stop producing power when they detect that the grid is down — to prevent back-feeding energized lines that utility workers might be touching. A household with solar panels and no battery backup has no power during a grid outage despite having panels on the roof. Battery backup systems or off-grid configurations do not have this limitation. The division should ensure members with solar installations understand what they actually have.

**System sizing** requires matching generation to load. The planning sequence: identify essential loads in watt-hours per day (watts × hours of use); divide by local peak sun hours (a figure that accounts for realistic daily solar output in the region, typically 3-6 hours for most of the continental US); add 20-25% for system losses from wiring, charge controllers, and inverter inefficiency. A household running a refrigerator (50-100 Wh/day), lighting (20-40 Wh/day), phone charging (10-20 Wh/day), and a water pump (variable) has modest essential load that a small system — 400-800 watts of panels with appropriate battery storage — can realistically meet. Heating and cooling loads are orders of magnitude larger and generally impractical to meet with solar at household scale.

**Charge controllers** regulate power flow from panels to batteries. MPPT (maximum power point tracking) controllers are significantly more efficient than PWM (pulse width modulation) controllers, particularly in cooler temperatures and with LiFePO4 battery banks — the efficiency gain typically justifies the higher cost.

**Generators** provide on-demand power at higher output than most household solar systems but consume fuel continuously. A 5,000-watt gasoline generator consumes roughly 0.5 to 0.75 gallons per hour at moderate load — against a 50-gallon fuel stockpile, that is 65-100 hours of runtime. Generators are most appropriate as a bridge technology: covering loads that cannot wait for solar, running critical equipment during cloudy periods, or meeting temporary high-power demands. Running a generator at 50-75% of rated capacity is more efficient than running at full load or very low load.

**Transfer switches** are a safety requirement for connecting a generator to a home's electrical panel. Without a transfer switch, a generator feeding a home panel can back-feed the utility grid through the meter — energizing lines that utility workers believe are dead. This kills people. The division should treat transfer switch installation as a prerequisite for any generator connected to a panel, not an optional upgrade.

**Carbon monoxide** is the acute safety hazard of generator use. Generators must never be operated indoors, in garages, or within ten feet of any window, door, or vent — CO is colorless, odorless, and kills within minutes at high concentrations. The division should ensure every household with a generator has functioning CO detectors and understands this constraint. Generator-related CO poisoning is one of the most consistent causes of death during power outages.

**Generator maintenance** determines whether the equipment functions when needed. Gasoline left in a carburetor over months of non-use gums up and prevents starting. Generators should be run under load for 30 minutes monthly to keep seals lubricated, carburetors clear, and batteries charged. Oil should be changed per manufacturer schedule. Spark plugs and air filters should be inspected annually. A generator that has not been maintained and tested is not a reliable backup — it is a liability.

**Community-scale generation** options beyond aggregated household systems include micro-hydro and shared solar microgrids. Micro-hydro — where a stream with sufficient elevation drop (head) is available — produces continuous, reliable power independent of sun or wind and is among the most valuable generation assets a community can develop. A stream with 10 feet of head and modest flow can produce several hundred watts continuously with a small turbine, far more useful than equivalent peak solar capacity. Where micro-hydro is available, it should be treated as a priority resource. Community solar microgrids — multiple households sharing a central solar array and battery bank — allow pooling of generation and storage capacity that individual households cannot justify alone, and should be explored where community land and relationships support it.

## Energy Storage

Generation without storage is only useful during the hours it is generating. Storage is what makes solar practical through the night, what allows a generator to run intermittently rather than continuously, and what bridges the gap between when energy is available and when it is needed. For most communities, battery storage is the primary storage technology — but understanding what batteries can and cannot do is essential to planning realistically.

**Battery chemistry determines the practical performance of any storage system.** The two relevant chemistries for community energy storage are lead-acid and lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4).

Lead-acid batteries — including flooded, AGM, and gel variants — are inexpensive, widely available, and well-understood. Their critical limitation is usable capacity: discharging a lead-acid battery below 50% state of charge dramatically shortens its lifespan. A 100 amp-hour lead-acid battery provides roughly 50 usable amp-hours. Flooded lead-acid batteries also off-gas hydrogen during charging and require ventilation and periodic water top-off. They are a practical choice where cost is the primary constraint and maintenance is manageable.

LiFePO4 batteries are the current standard for new community energy storage installations. They provide 80-90% usable capacity, last 3,000-5,000 charge cycles (versus 300-500 for flooded lead-acid), maintain a more consistent voltage across the discharge curve, require no maintenance, and do not off-gas. Unlike other lithium chemistries, LiFePO4 does not experience thermal runaway — the fire and explosion risk associated with lithium-ion cells in consumer electronics. The higher upfront cost is offset by far lower cost per cycle over the life of the system. Any new storage installation should use LiFePO4.

**Battery capacity is measured in watt-hours** — the product of amp-hours and voltage. A 100 amp-hour 12V battery contains 1,200 watt-hours of theoretical capacity. Against a household essential load of 300-500 watt-hours per day (lighting, device charging, modest refrigeration), a properly sized LiFePO4 bank provides two to three days of autonomy without generation input — the practical planning target.

**Temperature dramatically affects battery performance.** A flooded lead-acid battery at 0°F delivers roughly half its rated capacity. LiFePO4 handles cold better but still loses capacity below freezing, and most LiFePO4 batteries should not be charged below 32°F — charging a cold lithium battery causes lithium plating that permanently damages cells. Many quality LiFePO4 batteries include internal heaters that address this; batteries without this feature should be kept in insulated, temperature-controlled spaces in cold climates. Battery storage location should be part of household energy planning in any region that experiences freezing temperatures.

**System voltage** — 12V, 24V, or 48V — determines how the battery bank is wired and what components are compatible. Higher voltage systems carry the same power at lower current, which reduces wire size and heat losses. Systems above roughly 1,500 watts of generation or load should use 24V or 48V rather than 12V. The division's household energy planning program should include voltage selection guidance, as retrofitting a system to higher voltage after installation is expensive.

**Non-battery storage** is often overlooked but practically significant. A full propane tank is stored energy. A cord of dry firewood is stored energy. A full diesel tank is stored energy. Thermal mass — the heat retained in masonry, water, or earth — stores heating and cooling energy in forms that require no maintenance and lose no capacity over time. A well-insulated home with significant thermal mass holds temperature far longer during a heating or cooling failure than a poorly insulated one. The division should treat fuel stockpiles and building thermal performance as components of the community's energy storage picture, not as separate considerations.

**The division's storage stockpile** should include: LiFePO4 batteries in standardized sizes that can serve multiple applications; MPPT charge controllers appropriate to the system voltages in use; pure sine wave inverters (required for sensitive electronics and most modern appliances — modified sine wave inverters damage some equipment); appropriate fusing and disconnect hardware; and cable in common gauges. A community that has these components available can build or repair household systems without waiting for supply chains to recover.

## Liquid Fuels

Liquid fuels are irreplaceable for certain applications. Tractors, combines, large generators, chainsaws, and heavy vehicles cannot practically be converted to run on electricity or wood — they run on diesel, gasoline, or propane, and the community's ability to maintain agricultural production, transport, and heavy equipment depends on maintaining an adequate liquid fuel supply. The division treats liquid fuel management as a standing operational priority, not a contingency.

**Gasoline** is the most common fuel for small engines and many vehicles but the least stable in storage. Gasoline begins to degrade within three to six months as light hydrocarbon fractions evaporate and oxidation produces gum and varnish that clogs carburetors and fuel systems. Ethanol-blended gasoline — the standard E10 and increasingly E15 sold at most pumps — degrades faster and introduces an additional failure mode: phase separation. Ethanol absorbs water from the atmosphere; once enough water accumulates, the ethanol and water separate from the gasoline and sink to the bottom of the container as a distinct layer. An engine that draws from this layer will not run. Ethanol-free gasoline, available at some marinas, small airports, and farm supply stores, stores significantly better and should be preferred for any fuel intended for stockpiling.

Fuel stabilizer (Sta-Bil or equivalent) added at the time of storage extends gasoline life to one to two years. Stabilizer cannot restore significantly degraded fuel, but PRI-G and similar products can partially revitalize fuel that has begun to degrade. All stored gasoline containers should be dated at filling and rotated on a first-in, first-out basis. Degraded gasoline has a distinctive sour smell and darker color — these are useful field indicators before attempting to use stored fuel.

**Diesel** is more stable than gasoline and the critical fuel for agricultural equipment, heavy vehicles, and large generators. Untreated diesel stores reliably for six to twelve months; with appropriate treatment, two or more years. Its primary storage failure mode is not chemical degradation but biological: diesel fuel supports microbial growth at the fuel-water interface within storage tanks. Bacteria and fungi consume the fuel, produce acids and sludge, and can completely foul a fuel system. Biocide treatment — Biobor JF is the standard — added at storage prevents microbial growth and should be considered mandatory for any diesel stored beyond three to six months.

Diesel also presents cold weather challenges. As temperature drops, wax crystals form in diesel fuel — first visible as cloudiness at the cloud point, then preventing flow at the pour point. Standard No. 2 diesel begins to cloud around 14°F and may not pour below 0°F. Winter-blended diesel (No. 1 diesel or winterized No. 2) and anti-gel additives lower these thresholds. The division should maintain anti-gel additive as a standard stockpile item in any climate where winter temperatures approach these thresholds, and should ensure agricultural equipment operators understand the risk of equipment failing to start in cold weather due to gelled fuel.

Off-road diesel — sold dyed red and tax-exempt — is legal for agricultural equipment, generators, and construction equipment but not for on-road vehicles. It is otherwise identical to on-road diesel. The division should establish accounts with diesel suppliers and understand which equipment qualifies for off-road fuel.

**Propane** is the most storage-stable liquid fuel available. Stored in sealed tanks, propane does not degrade — a full tank in storage today is as usable in twenty years as it is today. This makes propane uniquely valuable for long-term fuel planning. Propane is sold by weight (pounds) or volume (gallons); one gallon equals approximately 4.24 pounds. Its energy content is roughly 91,500 BTU per gallon, somewhat lower than diesel or gasoline, but its indefinite shelf life offsets this in most planning scenarios.

Propane has one critical safety characteristic: it is heavier than air and pools in low areas when it leaks. A propane leak in a basement, crawl space, or low-lying area accumulates to explosive concentration without dispersing. Any propane storage or use in enclosed or below-grade spaces requires careful attention to ventilation and leak detection. Propane detectors, positioned low where gas accumulates, should be standard in any space where propane is stored or used.

Many gasoline generators can be converted to dual-fuel operation — running on propane as well as gasoline — with relatively straightforward conversion kits. This significantly extends operational flexibility and allows the generator to run on the most available fuel at a given time. The division should evaluate dual-fuel conversion for community generators.

**Biodiesel** is the community's path toward partial liquid fuel independence. Biodiesel is produced through transesterification — a chemical reaction in which vegetable oil or animal fat reacts with methanol in the presence of lye (sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide) to produce biodiesel and glycerin as a byproduct. The reaction produces methyl esters — biodiesel — which most diesel engines run on without modification, typically blended at up to 20% with petroleum diesel (B20) for broad compatibility across climates and equipment.

The practical value of biodiesel for community resilience is that its primary feedstock — waste vegetable oil — is locally available from restaurants and food service operations, often at no cost. A community that collects waste vegetable oil and processes it into biodiesel has a renewable local fuel source for tractors, generators, and vehicles, partially decoupling agricultural production from petroleum supply chains. Community-scale biodiesel production requires a processor (a heated mixing vessel with appropriate plumbing), methanol, and lye — equipment and chemicals that are available and not technically complex to use, but that require training and appropriate safety precautions. Methanol is the limiting input that cannot be produced at community scale; stockpiling methanol is therefore a critical dependency for any community biodiesel program.

The glycerin produced as a byproduct of biodiesel production — approximately 10% of output by volume — is directly usable in soap production, creating a practical connection between the community's fuel production and the Sanitation Division's soapmaking program.

Cold weather is biodiesel's primary limitation. Pure biodiesel gels at temperatures well above petroleum diesel; B20 blends are workable in most climates, but higher biodiesel concentrations in cold weather require heated storage or blending with No. 1 diesel. The division should size its biodiesel program to what can be used in the relevant climate rather than producing more than conditions allow.

**Fuel safety** applies across all liquid fuels. Static electricity buildup during fuel transfer from container to container can ignite vapor — metal containers should be grounded before transfer by touching them to the destination container or a grounded surface. Fuel storage should be sited away from structures, with secondary containment to capture spills. No open flame or ignition sources near storage. All containers should be clearly labeled with fuel type and fill date. Fuel fires are among the most dangerous and preventable community emergencies.

## Heating

Heating is the most critical energy need in any climate that experiences cold winters. Loss of heat kills — hypothermia sets in faster than most people expect, and the elderly, infants, and medically vulnerable members are at risk within hours in sufficiently cold conditions. The division's heating function prioritizes three things in order: reducing heat loss through insulation and air sealing, maintaining wood heating capacity as the most resilient fuel source, and ensuring every member household has a concrete heating plan before it is needed.

**Insulation and air sealing are the highest-leverage heating interventions available.** Reducing heat loss is more cost-effective and more impactful than increasing heat production. A house that loses heat slowly requires far less fuel to keep warm than one that loses it quickly, regardless of what fuel is used. The division's household energy planning program should treat building envelope improvement as a first priority, before generator sizing or fuel stockpiling.

Air sealing matters more than insulation in most existing homes. Air infiltration — cold outside air entering and warm inside air escaping — accounts for 25-40% of heating loss in typical construction. The primary pathways are not obvious: gaps around ceiling light fixtures, plumbing penetrations, attic hatches, and the junctions between walls and floors move far more air than door gaps or window frames. Sealing attic bypasses — the many small penetrations through the ceiling plane where interior walls, pipes, and wires pass into the attic — is often the single most effective weatherization measure available. This requires access to the attic and spray foam or caulk, not specialized equipment.

Thermal curtains over windows add R-4 to R-6 per window in addition to reducing convective heat loss from cold glass surfaces. Windows are the weakest point in any building envelope — even double-pane windows achieve only R-2 to R-3, compared to R-13 or better for insulated walls. Closing thermal curtains at sunset and opening them on south-facing windows during daylight hours meaningfully reduces heating load.

**Wood is the most resilient heating fuel** — it is locally available in most of the continental United States, renewable, and requires no supply chain beyond the tools to harvest and process it. The division should facilitate wood heating capability across the community and maintain community woodlot access and harvesting capacity.

Firewood must be seasoned before burning. Green wood — freshly cut — contains 50% or more water by weight, produces significantly less heat per cord, burns incompletely, and generates far more creosote. Seasoning requires a minimum of six months of air drying, preferably twelve, with wood split, stacked off the ground, and covered on top while remaining open on the sides for airflow. A community that begins cutting and splitting firewood today for use next winter is operating on the correct timeline. A community that cuts it in October for use in December is not.

Creosote is the combustion byproduct that accumulates in chimney flues and causes chimney fires. It is produced by incomplete combustion — low-temperature, smoldering fires generate it rapidly; hot, efficient fires produce far less. Flue temperatures above 250°F substantially reduce creosote accumulation. Chimneys should be inspected and cleaned at least annually; a dirty flue is a fire risk that has destroyed many homes. The division should include chimney cleaning as part of its household heating preparation program.

**Rocket stoves** represent a meaningful advance over conventional open fireplaces and simple woodstoves. A rocket stove's J-shaped combustion chamber — a horizontal feed channel meeting a vertical riser — creates a powerful natural draft that draws air through the fire at high velocity, producing combustion temperatures that consume gases and particulates that conventional fires leave unburned. The result is dramatically more heat from dramatically less wood: rocket stoves use 75-80% less fuel than a conventional fireplace for equivalent heat output. They burn small-diameter wood — pencil to wrist thickness — rather than large logs, making fuel collection and processing easier. The design is simple enough to build from firebricks or clay, making it a practical community construction project.

**Masonry heaters** — traditional in northern Europe and Russia — take a different approach: a large combustion chamber surrounded by a massive thermal mass of brick, stone, or tile is fired intensely for one to two hours, then allowed to cool while the stored heat radiates into the living space over the following 12-24 hours. A single daily firing can maintain comfortable temperatures in a well-insulated space. Masonry heaters are among the most efficient solid fuel heating systems available, achieving 85-90% efficiency, and the thermal mass makes temperature swings far less pronounced than in a conventional wood stove. They are a long-term community infrastructure investment rather than an emergency measure, but represent the appropriate standard for communities planning permanent wood heating capacity.

**Space concentration** is the most immediately available heating strategy under disruption. Heating a large house is far more fuel-intensive than heating a single well-insulated room. Closing off unused spaces, gathering household members into the smallest practical area, and using the body heat of multiple people in a confined space reduces fuel requirements dramatically. A warm room — one small, well-insulated space that everyone sleeps and spends time in — is the appropriate response to fuel scarcity, not attempting to maintain whole-house comfort with limited supply. The division's household planning program should help every member identify their warm room before it is needed.

**Propane space heaters** provide a practical transitional heating option where wood heating is unavailable. Ventless propane heaters such as the Mr. Heater Big Buddy are widely available and effective but produce both carbon monoxide and water vapor — safe for temporary use in ventilated spaces, not for sustained use or sleeping without ventilation and CO detection. Vented propane heaters eliminate the CO concern but require installation. CO detectors are mandatory with any indoor combustion heating device.

## Cooling

Cooling is not a comfort issue in hot climates — it is a survival issue. Heat kills more Americans annually than any other weather event, and the deaths are concentrated among the elderly, infants, and those with cardiovascular disease or on certain medications. The 1995 Chicago heat wave killed over 700 people in five days, almost entirely elderly residents alone in hot apartments. Loss of grid power during a heat event removes air conditioning from the households that most need it and creates a mass casualty scenario that develops quickly. The division must take heat as seriously as cold.

**The physiological limit of human heat tolerance** is a wet bulb temperature of approximately 35°C (95°F at 100% relative humidity; higher temperatures at lower humidity). Above this threshold, the human body cannot cool itself through sweating — the mechanism fails because the atmosphere cannot accept more water vapor. At a wet bulb temperature of 32-35°C, healthy adults can survive with rest and shade but cannot perform physical work safely. These conditions are increasingly common in hot, humid regions and represent a hard ceiling on human habitation without cooling.

**Night recovery is as critical as daytime cooling.** The body recovers from heat stress during cooler nighttime hours. If nighttime temperatures do not drop sufficiently — below roughly 80°F — the body carries accumulated heat stress into the next day without recovery. This is why multi-day heat waves kill: not because any single day is unsurvivable, but because cumulative heat stress without nighttime recovery degrades the body's ability to cope. The division's cooling strategy must address nighttime temperature as specifically as daytime shade and hydration.

**Certain medications impair heat tolerance** in ways most members will not know. Diuretics reduce fluid reserves. Anticholinergic medications — including common antihistamines, some antidepressants, and bladder medications — reduce sweating, eliminating the primary cooling mechanism. Beta blockers impair the cardiovascular response to heat. Antipsychotics can disrupt thermoregulation entirely. The Healthcare Division's chronic disease registry should flag members on these medications as heat-vulnerable, and the division should coordinate with Healthcare on identifying and prioritizing these members during heat events.

**Shading is the most effective passive cooling intervention.** Exterior shading — overhangs, awnings, shutters, trees, or shade cloth — prevents solar radiation from entering the building in the first place. Interior shading (blinds, curtains) is far less effective because the solar energy has already been converted to heat inside the glass and building envelope before it reaches them. South and west-facing windows are the primary solar gain sources in summer; trees planted on the south and west sides of a building provide effective shading that also improves winter sun access if deciduous. Reflective radiant barriers installed under roof sheathing reduce attic temperatures by 20-30°F, significantly cutting heat gain through ceilings.

**Night flushing** exploits the diurnal temperature swing — the drop in temperature between day and night — to cool buildings without mechanical equipment. The strategy is simple: when outside temperature drops below inside temperature in the evening, open windows to flush accumulated heat out of the building and cool its thermal mass. Before the sun rises and outside temperature climbs above inside temperature, close everything and trap the cool. Buildings with significant thermal mass — concrete, brick, tile, stone — store more coolness and hold it longer into the day. In climates with a meaningful diurnal swing (15°F or more), this strategy can maintain comfortable daytime temperatures without any active cooling.

**Evaporative cooling** — swamp coolers, wet cloth on the skin, misting — is highly effective in dry climates and nearly useless in humid ones. The mechanism is evaporation of water, which absorbs heat. When the air is already near saturation (high dew point), it cannot accept more water vapor and evaporation stops. The practical threshold: when the dew point exceeds 65°F, evaporative cooling is limited; above 75°F dew point, it provides almost no relief. Communities in the arid West can rely heavily on evaporative coolers, which use 75% less energy than refrigerant-based air conditioning. Communities in the humid Southeast and Gulf region cannot.

**Underground spaces** exploit the thermal stability of the earth. Below three to four feet of depth, soil temperature stabilizes at approximately the mean annual air temperature of the region — typically 50-65°F across most of the continental United States. A basement, root cellar, or partially earth-sheltered space is naturally and reliably cooler than summer ambient temperatures without any energy input. The division should identify underground spaces within the community — basements, root cellars, cellars — and map them as cooling resources available to members who lack them.

**Community cooling centers** are the division's most important community-scale cooling intervention. A single building with reliable cooling — whether from better insulation and night flushing, underground space, evaporative cooling in appropriate climates, or generator-powered spot cooling — can serve as a refuge for vulnerable members who lack access to cool spaces at home. The cooling center model, operated proactively during heat events rather than reactively after deaths occur, addresses the social isolation that kills people in heat waves as much as the temperature itself. The division should identify a cooling center location, establish the operational protocol for activating it during heat events, and maintain a list of members who should be contacted proactively when the center opens.

**Low-power cooling** extends the range of what is practical on limited generation capacity. Ceiling fans consume 15-75 watts against 1,000-3,500 watts for a window air conditioning unit — they do not lower room temperature but significantly increase the effective cooling from skin evaporation. A single small window unit cooling one designated cool room uses a fraction of the power of whole-house cooling and can make the difference for a medically vulnerable member. The division should guide households toward spot cooling of a single critical space rather than attempting whole-house cooling with limited generation.

## Cooking Energy

Cooking energy is distinct from space heating and power generation in one important respect: it is a daily, non-negotiable need. A household can tolerate reduced lighting or intermittent power, but it cannot stop cooking. The division's cooking energy function ensures that every member household can cook reliably without grid power, using fuel sources that are available and sustainable over an extended period.

**Fuel efficiency at the cooking fire matters more than fuel quantity.** Most traditional open-fire and simple woodstove cooking wastes 85-95% of the energy produced as heat that bypasses the pot. Small improvements in combustion and heat transfer have dramatic effects on how long any fuel supply lasts. The division should teach efficient cooking practices as part of its household energy program — not as an emergency measure but as standard practice.

Covering pots during cooking is the simplest and most impactful efficiency measure. Steam carries enormous heat energy; an uncovered pot loses it continuously to the air. A tight-fitting lid keeps that energy in the pot and can reduce cooking time and fuel use by 30% or more. Matching pot size to the heat source — using a small pot over a small fire rather than a large one — prevents heat from escaping around the sides. Bringing water to a boil quickly with high heat, then reducing to the minimum needed to maintain a simmer, uses less fuel than maintaining a uniform medium heat throughout.

**Pressure cookers** are among the highest-value cooking tools for a community operating without reliable fuel. By cooking at elevated pressure, they raise the boiling point of water to approximately 250°F, dramatically reducing cooking times: dried beans that require 90 minutes of simmering cook in 25-30 minutes; whole grains in 15-20 minutes; tough cuts of meat in under an hour. Pressure cooking reduces fuel consumption by 50-75% for long-cooking foods. The division should treat pressure cookers as a standard household supply item, not a specialty tool.

**Retained heat cooking** — also called fireless cooking or haybox cooking — extends the fuel savings further. The principle is straightforward: bring food to a full rolling boil on any heat source, then immediately transfer the covered pot to a heavily insulated container — a box packed with blankets, a sleeping bag, a commercial thermal cooker — where it continues cooking in its own retained heat for two to four hours without any additional fuel. Grains, legumes, stews, and soups all cook successfully this way. Combined with a pressure cooker, retained heat cooking can reduce the fuel required to cook beans or whole grains by 80-90% compared to conventional simmering. The insulated container requires no equipment beyond what any household already has.

**Rocket stoves** — introduced in the Heating section — are equally applicable to cooking. Their efficient J-channel combustion produces a hot, controllable flame from small-diameter wood that is well-suited to cooking. A rocket stove cooking surface can bring a pot to boil in minutes on fuel that would barely warm an open fire. Rocket stoves designed specifically for cooking are lower to the ground and optimized for pot support rather than room heating, but the combustion principle is identical. The division should include rocket stove construction in both its heating and cooking training programs.

**Solar ovens** provide cooking capacity without any fuel consumption during daylight hours with sufficient sun. Box solar ovens — an insulated box with a glass or plastic lid and reflective panels that direct additional sunlight into the box — reach temperatures of 250-350°F and can bake bread, cook rice and beans, prepare casseroles, and pasteurize water. They are slow (cooking times are roughly twice conventional) and weather-dependent, but they consume nothing, are safe enough for unsupervised operation, and can be built from cardboard, aluminum foil, and a piece of glass. Parabolic solar cookers concentrate sunlight to a focal point and reach 400-700°F — hot enough to fry and boil quickly — but require more attention and periodic adjustment to track the sun. Solar cooking is most effective from roughly 10am to 2pm when the sun is high. A community in a sunny climate that integrates solar cooking into its daily practice can dramatically reduce its wood and fuel consumption.

**TLUD gasification stoves** (Top Lit Updraft) represent a more advanced cooking technology with a significant byproduct benefit. A TLUD burns wood fuel from the top down rather than from the bottom up; pyrolysis gases from the partially burned fuel below are drawn upward through the hot char layer and combust cleanly above it, producing very high temperatures with low smoke output. The material remaining after combustion is not ash but biochar — a stable, porous carbon that is a highly effective soil amendment. The biochar produced by cooking connects directly to the Agricultural Division's soil program, closing a loop between daily food preparation and long-term soil fertility. TLUD stoves are more complex to build and operate than rocket stoves but are well-documented and buildable at community scale.

**Charcoal production** extends the community's solid fuel capacity and enables applications that wood alone cannot support well. Charcoal is produced by the same pyrolysis process as biochar — wood heated in low-oxygen conditions — but optimized for fuel rather than soil amendment: higher temperatures, longer burn times, denser feedstock. The result is a fuel that is more energy-dense than wood, burns at higher and more consistent temperatures, produces less smoke, and is easier to control for precision cooking tasks. For food processing that requires sustained, steady heat — canning, rendering, large-batch cooking — charcoal is a better fuel than wood. It is also the historical fuel of metalworking and blacksmithing; any community developing fabrication or repair capacity will need it.

The most practical small-scale production method is the **oil drum retort**: a metal barrel with a controlled air intake and a vent pipe at the top. Wood is loaded inside the barrel, a small fire started underneath or at the base, and the barrel sealed. As internal temperature rises, the wood pyrolyzes — gases and tars vent through the pipe and can be burned off at the vent to heat the retort further, reducing fuel waste. When smoke from the vent turns thin and blue (indicating most volatiles have been driven off), the vents are sealed and the retort allowed to cool completely before opening. Opening a hot retort admits air and ignites the charcoal. A standard 55-gallon drum produces a useful batch of charcoal in four to six hours and requires no materials beyond the drum, some pipe fittings, and wood feedstock.

The **pit burn** (earth clamp) is the lower-technology alternative: wood is piled in a mound, covered with earth or turf leaving controlled air holes at the base, and allowed to burn slowly over one to three days before the holes are sealed and the pile allowed to cool. It is less consistent than a retort and loses more material to ash, but requires no fabricated equipment. The traditional charcoal-making knowledge embedded in this method is genuinely worth recovering — communities produced charcoal this way for millennia before the fossil fuel era made it unnecessary.

The distinction between charcoal (fuel) and biochar (soil amendment) is worth maintaining clearly. Both come from the same pyrolysis process, but charcoal is produced from dense wood feedstock at higher temperatures to maximize energy content, while biochar is produced from a wider range of organic materials at lower temperatures to maximize porosity and surface area for soil use. The TLUD stove produces biochar as a cooking byproduct; dedicated charcoal production inverts the priority. Both have a place in the community's energy and agricultural programs.

**The masonry heater as the cooking center** of a small dwelling integrates heating and cooking into a single structure. Traditional designs incorporating a cooking surface on top, a baking oven in the side, and sometimes a water heating coil in the flue represent the most thermally efficient integrated approach available. The Liberty House design should treat the masonry heater as its energy heart precisely because it consolidates heating, baking, cooking, and hot water into one daily firing. A Liberty House built around a central masonry heater with a cooking surface requires no separate cooking fuel beyond the wood that heats the home. This integration must be designed in from the start — the foundation load of a masonry heater is 1,500 to 3,000 pounds or more and cannot be accommodated as a retrofit.

## Lighting

Lighting is the most easily solved energy problem at household scale and the one most immediately addressed by small solar systems. A household that can reliably light its living and working spaces at night — for food preparation, medical care, child supervision, reading, and basic security — has met one of its most fundamental energy needs at very low power cost. The division's lighting function focuses on ensuring every household has adequate, fuel-efficient lighting capacity before it is needed.

**LED technology has made low-power lighting practical in ways that were not possible a generation ago.** A 10-watt LED produces roughly the same light output as a 60-watt incandescent bulb — a 75-80% reduction in power consumption for equivalent illumination. More importantly for off-grid applications, LED bulbs designed for 12-volt DC operation run directly from battery banks without an inverter, eliminating conversion losses and simplifying system design. A small battery bank that cannot power a refrigerator or a heat source can easily power LED lighting for an entire household for many hours. The division's household energy planning program should standardize around 12V DC LED lighting for off-grid applications wherever possible.

**Lumens measure light output; watts measure power consumption.** Most people size lighting by watts, which is a measure of what something uses, not what it produces. The relevant planning unit is lumens. Task lighting — reading, food preparation, medical care — requires 300-500 lumens at the work surface. Ambient room lighting requires 800-1,600 lumens depending on room size. A single 10-watt LED bulb producing 800 lumens adequately lights a small room for less power than a single traditional nightlight.

**Solar lanterns** are practical, self-contained lighting units that charge via small integrated panels during daylight and provide three to eight hours of light at night. Widely used in off-grid communities globally, they require no battery bank, no charge controller, and no wiring — a member who has a solar lantern has functional lighting at no ongoing cost. The division should treat solar lanterns as a standard household preparedness item and maintain a community supply for members who lack them.

**Oil lamps** provide lighting entirely independent of electricity and remain functional indefinitely as long as fuel is available. Kerosene and lamp oil are practical stockpile items that store well. Vegetable oil — including ordinary cooking oil — burns in simple wicked lamps and produces adequate light for close tasks; olive oil is the historical standard, but any liquid vegetable oil works. A functional oil lamp can be made from a glass jar, cotton wick material, and cooking oil — this is genuinely low-technology and genuinely functional. Open-flame lighting requires attention to fire safety and ventilation; it should not be left unattended, and CO production, while modest from small wicked flames, is a consideration in poorly ventilated spaces.

**Headlamps** are among the most practical individual lighting tools available. They free both hands for work, direct light precisely where it is needed, and require very little power. Every member household should have at least one headlamp with rechargeable batteries. In medical care situations, adequate hands-free lighting is a functional requirement — the Healthcare Division's supply stockpile should include headlamps as a standard item.

**Light discipline** may be a relevant consideration in some disruption scenarios. Light visible from outside a building at night communicates occupation and activity. Blackout curtains or improvised window coverings that contain interior light are a straightforward precaution that the division's household planning program should address.

**Critical lighting priorities** should be established in every household plan: which spaces need light reliably (food preparation area, sleeping area for infant or medical needs, latrine path), which can be managed with minimal light, and what the lighting plan is in priority order. A household that has made these decisions in advance and pre-positioned appropriate equipment is not scrambling to find candles in the dark.

## Household Energy Planning

The division's household energy planning program ensures that every member household understands its energy situation, has identified its critical needs, and has pre-positioned the knowledge and equipment to meet those needs before disruption occurs. As with water, food, and sanitation, the goal is capacity held broadly across the community — organized by the division, owned by the households themselves.

**Every household needs an energy plan.** That plan should answer five questions: what is this household's current energy infrastructure and what are its failure modes; what are the household's critical energy needs in priority order; what alternative energy sources and equipment does the household have; how long can the household sustain itself on current supplies; and what are the household's most acute vulnerabilities. The division should assist members in developing written plans and maintain awareness of which households are prepared and which are not.

**Critical needs must be prioritized explicitly.** The planning sequence follows the same logic as the assessment function: heating and cooling where temperatures are dangerous come first; cooking comes second; lighting comes third; power for medical equipment is elevated to critical regardless of where it would otherwise fall. Everything else — device charging, entertainment, convenience loads — is acknowledged as desirable and planned for after critical needs are secured. A household plan that attempts to maintain normal energy consumption rather than identifying and protecting critical loads will fail.

**Understanding current infrastructure** is the foundation of the household plan. Many members do not know whether their home heating system can operate without electricity (most gas furnaces cannot — they require an electric igniter and blower), whether their water supply depends on an electric pump, or what their utility's historical outage profile looks like. The division's planning program should help every member answer these questions. A household on electric-only heat has a fundamentally different failure profile than one with a gas furnace or a wood stove, and the plan must reflect that difference.

**Pre-positioned equipment and supplies** should be in place before they are needed. At minimum, every household should have: a reliable alternative lighting source (solar lantern plus headlamp); an alternative cooking capability (rocket stove, camp stove with fuel, solar oven); a minimum fuel supply for heating appropriate to the climate and the household's backup heating method; and a 72-hour supply of cooking fuel independent of the grid. Members with medical equipment dependencies require power continuity planning specific to their equipment — the Healthcare Division's chronic disease registry should inform which households require priority attention.

**Home energy assessment** — evaluating insulation quality, air sealing, window performance, and heating system type — should be part of every household plan, not a separate initiative. A household that improves its building envelope reduces its critical heating and cooling loads, which in turn reduces the generation and fuel storage requirements to sustain them. The division should develop relationships with members who have weatherization skills and facilitate household-level improvements, coordinating with the federal Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) and similar resources while they are available.

**Vulnerable households require proactive outreach.** The households most at risk from energy disruption are those with electric-only heat in cold climates, those with members dependent on powered medical equipment, those with infants or elderly members sensitive to temperature extremes, and those with limited financial resources to pre-position equipment. These households are also the least likely to complete energy planning independently. The division should identify them through its assessment function and prioritize them — not wait for them to seek help.

**Fuel rotation and maintenance** should be standard household practice, not an emergency response. Gasoline stored without rotation degrades into an unusable asset. A generator that has not been tested in eighteen months is not a reliable backup. The division's household planning program should establish simple maintenance routines — monthly generator test runs, annual fuel rotation, seasonal equipment checks — and support members in following them. Community-scale maintenance events, where members test and service their equipment together, are an effective way to make this routine rather than exceptional.

**The household plan should be written and reviewed annually.** Energy situations change — members move, equipment ages, fuel supplies are consumed, household composition shifts. A plan written once and never revisited reflects conditions that may no longer exist. The division should build annual review into its household program and treat plans as living documents.

## Division Relationships

**Agriculture**
Liquid fuel for tractors, combines, irrigation pumps, and transport vehicles is a direct agricultural input — without it, food production capacity collapses regardless of how well the Agricultural Division has developed its other capacity. The two divisions must coordinate on diesel and biodiesel supply as a standing priority. The biodiesel production program connects them further: waste vegetable oil from food processing becomes fuel for farm equipment, and the biochar byproduct of TLUD cooking stoves becomes a soil amendment. These loops should be made explicit and managed jointly.

**Healthcare**
Power continuity for medical equipment is a life-safety priority that the Energy Division must coordinate with Healthcare. The Healthcare Division's chronic disease registry identifies which members depend on powered equipment; the Energy Division's household planning program ensures those households have appropriate generation and storage in place. Temperature extremes — both heat and cold — are health emergencies that require coordinated response. The community cooling and warming center functions connect the two divisions operationally during heat and cold events.

**Food Security**
Cooking energy is inseparable from food security. Stored food that cannot be cooked is a partial resource. The division should coordinate with Food Security on the cooking energy requirements of the community's food program — particularly for bulk staples like grains and legumes that require sustained cooking — and ensure that cooking energy planning accounts for the actual foods the community is preparing. Cold storage for food preservation may also require power, which the division's generation program must account for.

**Housing Security**
The built environment is the Energy Division's primary leverage point — a well-insulated, well-sited structure requires dramatically less energy to heat and cool than a poorly performing one. The two divisions should coordinate on the Liberty House design standards, weatherization programs, and any community construction that creates long-term energy obligations. The masonry heater as the heart of the Liberty House is a design decision with structural, fuel, and construction implications that both divisions must own together.

## Operational Posture

The division's responsibilities exist on a spectrum. In stable conditions, the division focuses on household energy planning, weatherization improvements, fuel stockpiling, and building community generation and storage capacity. As grid reliability deteriorates, active fuel management, community warming and cooling center operations, and critical load prioritization become more central. The division does not change what it does — it scales what it does.

At any point on that spectrum the division may be called on to:

- Support households in transitioning to alternative energy sources when grid power is unavailable
- Manage community fuel supplies and coordinate distribution to priority users
- Operate community warming or cooling centers during temperature emergencies
- Ensure agricultural equipment maintains fuel access as a food security priority

The division must be capable of sustaining all of these functions simultaneously and indefinitely. Fuel stockpiles, generation equipment, battery storage, and community relationships must be built and maintained with that standard in mind.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Complete the energy assessment and vulnerability map**
The division's first task is understanding the community's energy situation — who has what infrastructure, which households face the most acute failure risk, where medical equipment dependencies exist, and what community assets are already available. This map is the foundation of every subsequent priority.

**2. Build household energy plans for vulnerable households first**
Households with electric-only heat, medical equipment dependencies, elderly or infant members, and limited resources to self-prepare cannot wait for a general rollout. The division should work through these households first, helping them identify and pre-position alternative heating, cooking, and power equipment before it is urgently needed.

**3. Establish liquid fuel stockpiles**
With agricultural equipment fuel needs identified through the Agriculture Division, the division builds and manages a community liquid fuel stockpile — diesel and propane as the primary long-duration fuels, gasoline for smaller engines, and the methanol supply needed to sustain biodiesel production. Fuel rotation schedules and biocide treatment protocols should be established at the same time.

**4. Build community generation and storage capacity**
Solar installations, battery storage, and generator capacity — sized to the critical loads identified in the assessment — come online as the household planning program matures. The community biodiesel processor, if developed, is established during this phase.

**5. Operate community programs**
Household energy planning becomes an ongoing program as membership grows and situations change. Annual plan reviews, fuel rotation events, generator maintenance days, and the cooling and warming center protocols become standing operational responsibilities.

## Division Documents

The Energy Division is responsible for producing and maintaining the following documents. These are general-purpose resources any Resolute Society can adopt.

**Household Energy Plan Template** — a fill-in document each member household completes identifying current energy infrastructure and failure modes, critical loads in priority order, alternative energy equipment in place, current fuel supplies, and vulnerabilities requiring division support.

**Liquid Fuels Storage and Management Guide** — an operational reference covering storage requirements, stabilization and treatment procedures, rotation schedules, safety requirements, and fuel-specific degradation timelines for gasoline, diesel, propane, and biodiesel.

**Biodiesel Production Guide** — a community-scale production manual covering feedstock collection and preparation, the transesterification process, methanol and lye handling and safety, quality testing, glycerin byproduct management, and cold weather blending guidance.

**Efficient Cooking Guide** — an instructional guide covering rocket stove construction and operation, retained heat cooking procedures, solar oven construction and use, pressure cooker techniques, and fuel efficiency practices applicable across cooking methods.

**Solar PV and Battery Storage Guide** — a system design and installation reference covering load calculation, panel sizing, battery selection and configuration, charge controller and inverter selection, 12V DC wiring, and basic maintenance and troubleshooting.

**Community Warming and Cooling Center Protocol** — operational procedures for activating and running a community temperature refuge during heat or cold events: activation thresholds, staffing, member notification, vulnerable member outreach, and coordination with Healthcare.
