# Education Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Education Division exists to ensure that the Resolute Society never loses what it knows, and that what it knows is held in people, not only in documents. It does this through two inseparable functions: preserving and maintaining the body of knowledge the Society depends on, and coordinating the teaching of that knowledge to members across every stage of life and every domain of practice.

These functions cannot be separated without degrading both. A Division that preserves knowledge without teaching it produces documents that outlast the people who can use them. A Division that teaches without preserving produces capable members whose knowledge dies with them. The Division holds both obligations simultaneously, understanding that knowledge lives fully only when it exists in people and is continuously renewed through transmission.

The Division is an obligated Division. A Society that has committed to the cultivation of every member as a condition of dignity cannot dissolve the institution through which that commitment is honored.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for four things:

- That the Society's accumulated knowledge across every domain is collated, maintained, and made accessible to members
- That adult members are actively taught across the full range of formation the Society's principles require
- That the capacity to educate children is maintained and activated when external institutions are inadequate or unavailable
- That knowledge is transmitted continuously across generations so that what the Society knows today is not lost to the departure or death of those who currently hold it

## Core Functions

- **Knowledge Preservation** — collating, organizing, maintaining, and updating the Society's body of knowledge across every domain, in forms that are accessible, durable, and independent of any single infrastructure
- **Adult Formation** — coordinating the delivery of the Society's curriculum to adult members, including foundational literacy and numeracy, cognitive restoration, practical skills, and the full range of formation the telos requires
- **Child Education** — maintaining the capacity to educate children within the Society when external institutions are insufficient, and activating that capacity when conditions require it
- **Transmission** — ensuring that knowledge passes between members and across generations through apprenticeship, mentorship, and direct instruction — not through documents alone

## Knowledge Preservation

The Society's knowledge is its most durable asset. Physical infrastructure can be rebuilt. Supply chains can be reestablished. Knowledge that is lost cannot be recovered — it must be relearned from scratch, at great cost, often in the worst possible conditions. The Division treats knowledge preservation as a standing emergency: not because crisis is imminent, but because the loss of knowledge is irreversible and the conditions that cause it arrive without warning.

**What the Division preserves** spans every domain the Society operates in: water sourcing and treatment, food production and preservation, healthcare and community health practice, construction and maintenance, energy production, communications, printing and bookbinding, governance and conflict resolution, ecology and land stewardship, and the full range of practical skills on which community life depends. The Division does not generate this knowledge — the divisions that operate in each domain generate it. The Division collates it, maintains it, and ensures it is accessible. Each division is responsible for documenting what it knows. The Education Division is responsible for ensuring that documentation exists, is current, and is held in a form that survives the failure of the division that produced it.

**The form of preserved knowledge matters as much as its content.** Knowledge stored only in digital form is vulnerable to power failure, hardware failure, and platform failure. Knowledge stored only in physical documents is vulnerable to fire, flood, and physical destruction. Knowledge stored only in the minds of a few specialists is vulnerable to illness, departure, and death. The Division maintains knowledge in all three forms simultaneously — digital for accessibility and searchability, physical for resilience, and human for genuine transmission — and treats the absence of any one form as a preservation gap requiring active attention.

**Physical knowledge holdings** are produced in coordination with the Printing Division, which supplies the paper, ink, and binding for physical documents. The Division should maintain a complete physical reference library covering every domain of Society operation. This library should exist in at least two physical locations within the Society's area, so that the loss of one location does not mean the loss of the physical holdings. Each hub should hold at minimum a basic reference collection covering the skills and knowledge most relevant to that hub's geographic area and membership.

**Currency is an ongoing obligation.** Knowledge that was accurate three years ago may be outdated today — particularly in domains like healthcare, where practices evolve, and agriculture, where local conditions shift. The Division should establish a regular review cycle with each division, ensuring that documented knowledge reflects current practice rather than the practice of the founding generation.

**The non-obvious preservation failure** is not the dramatic loss — fire, flood, collapse. It is the quiet drift: documents that exist but are never consulted, knowledge that is technically preserved but practically inaccessible because no one knows it exists or can navigate to it. The Division's knowledge function is not fulfilled by the existence of documents. It is fulfilled by the genuine accessibility of what those documents contain.

## Adult Formation

Adult formation is the Division's most active and most visible function. It encompasses everything from foundational literacy instruction for members who lack basic reading and writing skills to the full range of cognitive, practical, and civic formation the Society's telos describes.

**The foundational programs** address capabilities that are prerequisites for full participation in Society life. A member who cannot read cannot access the Society's written knowledge, cannot fully participate in governance, and cannot transmit knowledge in written form. A member without basic numeracy cannot manage resources, interpret data, or contribute to planning. Foundational Literacy and Foundational Numeracy are therefore not enrichment programs — they are access programs. The Division should treat them as standing obligations and should actively identify and serve members who need them, without stigma and without making participation contingent on disclosure.

**The cognitive formation programs** address the damage the attention economy has done to members' capacity to think, reason, and engage with each other honestly. Cognitive Restoration rebuilds attentional capacity degraded by algorithmic systems. Practical Reasoning rebuilds the capacity to work through difficult problems. Practical Epistemology rebuilds the capacity to evaluate evidence and form honest beliefs. Interior Capacity addresses the psychological resilience, identity, and emotional literacy that genuine community participation requires. Presence and Understanding addresses the capacity for genuine conversation that the current media environment has systematically eroded. These programs are not remedial — they address damage that has been done to nearly everyone in the current era, and they are as relevant to the Society's most accomplished members as to its newest.

**The practical formation programs** build the material capabilities that genuine agency requires. Manual Skills develops household capability without infrastructure dependence — the ability to accomplish what machines currently do, through direct knowledge of the underlying processes. The Community Health Worker curriculum builds the health knowledge and practice the Division of Health depends on being distributed broadly across membership. Living Well addresses the interior and practical orientation required to live with purpose in difficult conditions rather than being paralyzed by them or numbing to them.

The Society's competency documents — *What a Person Should Be Able to Do* and *What a Household Should Be Able to Do* — define the practical baseline that the Division's formation programs are designed to achieve and maintain across the membership. These documents are not aspirational; they describe what the Division is responsible for ensuring every member and household can do. Where a member or household falls short of that baseline, the Division's formation programs are the primary mechanism for closing the gap.

**The relationship between programs matters.** The courses are not independent — they build on each other and reinforce each other. Attentional capacity developed in Cognitive Restoration is the substrate on which Practical Reasoning and Practical Epistemology operate. Interior Capacity and Presence and Understanding develop the relational conditions in which honest deliberation becomes possible. The Division should think about member formation as a whole rather than as a menu of independent options, and should guide members toward sequences that build coherently rather than leaving curriculum navigation entirely to individuals.

**Formation does not happen only in courses.** The formal curriculum is one instrument of formation, not the whole of it. The Society's governance structures form civic courage through practice. Common work forms care and accountability through shared stakes. Mentorship and apprenticeship form practical wisdom through direct transmission. The Division coordinates formal instruction but understands that the community's common life is itself the primary site of formation — and that the Division's job is to support and augment that formation, not to substitute for it.

**The guide is not a teacher in the conventional sense.** Every curriculum document the Division operates emphasizes this. The guide holds structure, poses questions, models the capacities being developed, and witnesses the group's progress honestly. The guide does not lecture, does not perform expertise, and does not position themselves as having solved what the group is working on. This is not a facilitation style — it is a philosophical commitment. Formation that passes through genuine human relationship produces something different from, and more durable than, formation that passes through instruction.

## Child Education

The Division maintains the capacity to educate children within the Society at all times. Whether that capacity is actively deployed depends on the state of external institutions.

Under normal conditions, where public schools and other educational institutions are functioning adequately, the Division's child education function operates in a supporting role — enriching what external institutions provide with the Society's own formation commitments, offering supplementary instruction in areas external institutions do not cover, and ensuring that children growing up in the Society are not strangers to its knowledge, values, and practices.

As external institutions deteriorate — through underfunding, ideological capture, physical collapse, or the general institutional fragility the polycrisis produces — the Division scales its child education function to meet the gap. The threshold is not perfection: the Division does not activate full child education simply because external schools are imperfect. The threshold is adequacy — whether children in the Society's membership are receiving an education sufficient to develop genuine capability and prepare them for full participation in Society life. When external institutions fall below that threshold, the Division steps in.

**The Division should be prepared to operate full child education at any point.** This means maintaining curriculum for children across age ranges, identifying members with the knowledge and disposition to teach, and having a clear operational plan that can be activated without a lengthy startup period. A Division that does not prepare for child education until it is urgently needed will not be ready when it is urgently needed.

**Child formation has a different character than adult formation.** Children are in the process of becoming — their capacities, identities, and orientations are actively forming rather than being restored or developed against a prior state. This requires methods, pacing, and relational conditions appropriate to development rather than simply scaled-down versions of adult programs. The Division should hold this knowledge and should not assume that competence in adult formation translates directly to competence in child education.

**The Society's own formation commitments apply to child education.** Children educated within the Society are not educated merely to be economically productive. They are educated to become fully oriented persons — people who can think clearly, act wisely, relate genuinely, and contribute to the community's common life. The telos that orients adult formation orients child education as well.

## Transmission

Preservation and instruction are necessary but not sufficient. The deepest obligation of the Division is transmission — the passage of knowledge from those who hold it to those who will carry it forward, across time and across generations, through human relationship rather than documents alone.

**A manual is not a mentor. A curriculum is not a College.** Documents record what people know. They do not transmit the judgment that comes from applying knowledge in real situations, the feel for when a principle applies and when it doesn't, the wisdom that accumulates through practice and error over years. That form of knowledge lives in people and passes between them only through direct relationship — through the apprentice working alongside the master in actual conditions, through the student watching how an experienced person navigates a genuinely difficult situation, through the mentee seeing how someone carries knowledge they have not yet developed.

**The apprenticeship model** is the Division's primary instrument for this deeper transmission. In every domain of Society practice, the Division supports relationships between members who hold deep competency and members who are developing it — not as a formal program separate from actual work, but as the way actual work gets done. The person who knows teaches the person who is learning by doing the thing together, in real conditions, with real stakes. The Division identifies where these relationships are missing and works to establish them.

**Transmission must be deliberate.** Knowledge does not flow automatically from those who hold it to those who need it. It flows when someone takes responsibility for the transfer — when the person who knows makes the effort to teach rather than simply to do. The Division holds this responsibility institutionally: it is not enough for the Society to have members who know things. The Division must ensure that what members know is actively being passed on, and must identify and address gaps where knowledge is concentrated in too few people or is not being transmitted to the next generation.

**The two-generation rule** is a useful frame for assessing transmission risk. Any piece of knowledge held by only one generation of members — not yet transmitted to anyone younger — is one generation away from being lost. The Division should maintain awareness of where knowledge is concentrated generationally and should treat single-generation concentration as a standing transmission priority.

## Division Relationships

**All Divisions**
The Education Division is the institutional memory of the entire Society. Every division generates knowledge through its operations — knowledge about what works, what fails, what local conditions require, what the founding generation got wrong. The Education Division is responsible for ensuring that knowledge is documented, preserved, and accessible. This relationship runs in both directions: the Education Division depends on operating divisions to generate and document current knowledge, and operating divisions depend on the Education Division to ensure that knowledge is not lost when the people who hold it are gone.

**Printing**
The Printing Division produces the physical materials the Education Division's knowledge preservation function depends on — the paper, ink, and binding for physical document holdings and curriculum materials. The two divisions should maintain a close working relationship, with the Education Division communicating its production needs to Printing as standing requirements and Printing treating Education as a primary obligation.

**Health**
The Community Health Worker curriculum is an Education Division program that directly serves the Health Division's mandate. The two divisions should coordinate on curriculum content to ensure that what the Education Division teaches reflects current Health Division practice.

**All Obligated Divisions**
The Education Division's formation mandate directly serves every obligated division. Food security depends on members who know how to grow, preserve, and prepare food. Housing depends on members who know how to build and maintain structures. Health depends on members who carry health knowledge. The Watch depends on members who understand and are committed to the Society's safety and justice framework. Education is not a separate domain from these — it is the process by which the knowledge each domain requires gets into the people who need it.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division runs its adult formation programs on a regular cycle, maintains and updates its knowledge holdings, supports apprenticeship relationships across the Society, and coordinates with external educational institutions to supplement rather than duplicate what they provide for children.

As conditions deteriorate, the Division increases the intensity of practical formation — prioritizing the skills and knowledge most immediately relevant to changing conditions — and activates child education capacity in proportion to the failure of external institutions. It also intensifies its knowledge preservation function, ensuring that what the Society knows is in durable physical form and held across enough people that localized disruption cannot eliminate it.

At the floor, the Society can always transmit knowledge. Members who know things can teach members who don't, directly, without curriculum or infrastructure. The oral tradition, the apprenticeship, the mentor alongside the student in the work — these are the oldest and most resilient forms of transmission, and they function when nothing else does. The Division builds on them rather than replacing them.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Assess the membership**
Before any formal program is launched, the Division should develop a clear picture of what the founding membership knows and what it doesn't — where knowledge is concentrated, where it is absent, where single-generation concentration creates transmission risk. This assessment shapes everything else.

**2. Launch foundational programs**
Foundational Literacy and Foundational Numeracy are the access programs that determine who can participate fully. These launch first and run continuously. No member should remain without these capabilities for want of instruction.

**3. Establish the knowledge preservation function**
Begin collating existing knowledge holdings from each division. Establish coordination with the Printing Division for physical document production. Identify gaps in documentation and begin filling them.

**4. Launch cognitive and practical formation programs**
The full curriculum rolls out in sequence as guides are identified and prepared. Priority should be given to programs that develop the capacities most immediately needed by a new Society — Cognitive Restoration, Practical Reasoning, Manual Skills — before moving to the programs that depend on those foundations.

**5. Establish apprenticeship relationships**
Map where deep competency exists in the membership and where it needs to be transmitted. Establish formal apprenticeship relationships in the highest-priority domains — the areas where loss of a single member would significantly degrade the Society's capability.

**6. Assess child education readiness**
Within the first year, the Division should assess the state of external educational institutions and the Society's own capacity to supplement or replace them. Do not wait for an emergency to discover the gap.

## Division Documents

**Knowledge Holdings Inventory** — a current catalog of the Society's preserved knowledge across every domain, organized by division, indicating what is documented, in what form, where it is held physically and digitally, and when it was last reviewed for currency. The primary instrument through which the Division manages its preservation function.

**Curriculum Guide** — an overview of the Society's full formation curriculum: what each program addresses, what it requires in terms of guide preparation and group conditions, how programs relate to each other and build on each other, and how to guide members toward sequences that develop coherently. Used by Division officers and prospective guides.

**Transmission Risk Register** — a standing record of knowledge domains where transmission to the next generation is inadequate: single-generation concentration, underdocumented practice, domains where the departure of one or two members would constitute significant loss. Updated continuously and reviewed by the Division officer as a standing operational priority.

**Child Education Readiness Assessment** — a periodic assessment of external educational institution adequacy and the Society's own child education capacity, used to determine whether and to what degree the Division's child education function should be activated.
