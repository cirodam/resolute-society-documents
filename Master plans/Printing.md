# Printing Division — Master Plan

## Mission

The Printing Division exists to ensure that the Resolute Society can produce physical documents, books, and written materials from locally available resources — independent of supply chains, corporate infrastructure, or grid power. It does this by holding and practicing the knowledge and craft of papermaking, ink production, press operation, hand-copying, and bookbinding, and by producing the paper, ink, and bound materials the Society needs from what the land provides.

The Division is a production operation. Its outputs are physical goods — paper, ink, binding materials, and finished documents — not services rendered on request. It holds a craft tradition and keeps it alive through practice, produces materials the Society depends on, and transmits its knowledge to each generation of members so that the capability cannot be lost to the death or departure of any individual.

## Mandate

The Division is responsible for three things:

- That the Society can produce paper, ink, and binding materials from locally available raw materials, under any conditions
- That this capability is held in practice across enough members that it cannot be lost and does not depend on any single person
- That the Society's production capacity is sufficient to meet the document and material needs of all divisions

## Core Functions

- **Papermaking** — processing plant fiber into usable writing and printing paper using locally sourced and cultivated materials
- **Ink Production** — producing writing and printing inks from local sources including iron gall, carbon-based, and plant-derived formulations
- **Bookbinding** — producing durable bound documents from leather, cloth, board, and thread sourced or produced locally
- **Press Operation** — maintaining and operating hand-powered printing equipment that functions without grid power or manufactured consumables
- **Knowledge Transmission** — ensuring that every process the Division depends on is actively taught and practiced broadly enough across membership that it cannot be lost

## On Modern Printing Equipment

Laser printers, inkjet printers, and their consumables — toner cartridges, ink cartridges, drum units — are the product of highly specialized global supply chains. They are extraordinarily fragile. Cartridges have expiration dates, dry out in storage, and cannot be refilled indefinitely with acceptable results. Toner is a precision-engineered powder that cannot be meaningfully produced outside an industrial context. Printers themselves require specific replacement parts that become unavailable as models age.

Under normal conditions these tools are convenient and the Division may use them. Under supply disruption they will fail within months. The Division does not build any part of its core capability around them. They are mentioned here only to name clearly what they are — the starting point that will not last — so that the Society is not caught assuming they will.

Everything that follows is the capability that remains when they are gone.

## Papermaking

Paper is fiber. Almost any plant fiber can be made into paper with sufficient processing. The knowledge of which local plants yield the best fiber, how to process them, and how to form and dry sheets is the core of the Division's papermaking capability.

**Local fiber sources** vary by region but in the southeastern United States include kudzu, cattail, hemp, nettles, iris leaves, daylily stalks, and corn husks. Kudzu in particular is abundant to the point of invasiveness across Georgia and produces excellent long-fiber paper with good writing qualities. It represents a local resource of extraordinary scale that the Division should know how to use thoroughly. Cotton and linen rags — when available — produce the highest quality paper and should be collected and saved rather than discarded. The Agriculture Division should be coordinated with to deliberately cultivate high-yield fiber crops where possible.

**The basic process** runs from raw plant material through retting — soaking to break down the plant structure — followed by cooking in an alkaline solution to separate the cellulose fibers from lignin and other plant material. Wood ash lye, produced from burned hardwood, is a readily available alkaline agent that has been used for this purpose for centuries and can be produced anywhere trees are burned. The cooked fiber is beaten — historically by hand or with a mallet, later with a stamping mill — to further separate and hydrate the fibers into a pulp called half-stuff. The half-stuff is diluted in a vat of water, and a mould and deckle — a framed screen — is dipped through it, lifting an even layer of fiber that drains and dries into a sheet. Sheets are pressed to remove water and hung or laid flat to dry.

The quality of paper depends on fiber choice, pulp preparation, and forming technique. The Division should develop competency across this full range rather than optimizing for a single fiber or process — different materials and conditions call for different approaches, and the Division's resilience comes from range, not specialization.

**Equipment** for papermaking is simple and can be produced locally. Moulds and deckles are wooden frames with woven wire or fabric screens — constructible by any member with basic woodworking skill. Vats can be any watertight container of appropriate size. Presses are simple weighted frames or screw presses, buildable from wood. None of this requires manufactured parts that cannot be replaced locally.

**Storage** of finished paper requires protection from moisture and pests. Paper stored in dry, sealed conditions lasts centuries. The Division should maintain a standing stock of finished paper sufficient to meet the Society's needs for at minimum six months, so that production disruption does not immediately affect the Society's ability to produce documents.

## Ink Production

Ink is a pigment or dye suspended in a carrier that flows onto a surface and dries. The history of ink is the history of finding local materials that do this reliably. The Division should hold competency in at minimum three formulations: iron gall ink, carbon ink, and at least one plant-derived ink appropriate to local sources.

**Iron gall ink** has been the primary writing ink of Western civilization for over a thousand years. It is produced from oak galls — the hard round growths that form on oak trees in response to wasp larvae — combined with ferrous sulfate (iron vitriol), water, and a small amount of gum arabic as a binder. Oak galls are abundant wherever oak trees grow. Ferrous sulfate can be produced by dissolving iron in dilute acid — vinegar will serve — or can be stockpiled in quantity as it stores indefinitely. Gum arabic, derived from acacia trees, is the traditional binder; other plant gums serve adequately as substitutes. Iron gall ink is waterproof when dry, extremely durable, and bonds chemically with paper rather than simply sitting on the surface. It is the best available ink for documents intended to last.

The non-obvious constraint with iron gall ink is that it is mildly acidic and will slowly degrade paper over decades — this is the cause of the deterioration seen in many historical manuscripts. For documents intended for long-term preservation, the Division should understand this trade-off and consider it in decisions about what is written in iron gall versus more neutral inks.

**Carbon ink** — lampblack or charcoal suspended in a binder — is among the oldest inks in existence and is chemically stable and non-acidic. Lampblack is the fine carbon soot collected from the incomplete combustion of oils, resins, or pitch. Pine pitch and linseed oil are the traditional sources. The pigment is ground and mixed with a binder — gum water, egg white, or boiled linseed oil depending on the application — to produce an ink with good flow and excellent permanence. Carbon ink is the appropriate choice for documents and printed materials intended for archival preservation.

**Plant-derived inks** — from pokeberry, black walnut hull, elderberry, and many other local sources — are easier to produce but generally less durable. They serve well for everyday writing and notice production where longevity is less critical. The Division should develop a working knowledge of locally available dye plants and their suitability as inks, understanding that most will fade over years of light exposure and are not appropriate for archival use.

**Printing ink** for use with a press differs from writing ink in viscosity — it must be thick enough to transfer reliably from type or plate to paper rather than flowing freely. Historically, printing inks were produced from linseed oil boiled to increase viscosity combined with lampblack pigment. This formulation is producible entirely from local materials and has been in continuous use since Gutenberg. The Division should maintain competency in printing ink production alongside writing inks.

## Bookbinding

A loose stack of paper is not a book. Binding transforms paper into a durable object that can survive handling, storage, and transmission across time. The Division holds bookbinding as a complete craft, from folding and sewing signatures through covering in leather, cloth, or board.

**Structures** the Division should maintain competency in include: the pamphlet stitch for short documents, the Coptic stitch and long stitch for books that open flat, the case binding for durable hardcover books, and the limp binding in leather or vellum for books intended to survive rough conditions. Each structure has different material requirements and different strengths — the Division should be able to choose the appropriate structure for what is being bound.

**Covering materials** in order of durability and local availability: leather tanned from locally available hides is the most durable covering material available without industrial inputs, waterproof, and producible entirely within the Society's own processes in coordination with the Food and Agriculture Divisions. Cloth covers — linen or canvas — are lighter and adequate for books not subject to hard use. Boards for hardcover cases can be produced from laminated waste paper or from thin-split wood. Thread for sewing signatures can be linen, cotton, or other natural fiber. Paste and adhesive can be produced from flour and water.

**The non-obvious skill** in bookbinding is not the covering — it is the folding and sewing of signatures, which determines the structural integrity of the book's spine. A book whose signatures are improperly sewn will fall apart with use regardless of how well it is covered. The Division should ensure that sewing structures are practiced and taught as the foundation of the binding curriculum, not treated as a detail.

## Press Operation

A hand press capable of producing printed pages requires no power beyond human effort and no consumables that cannot be produced locally once the Division's ink production capability is established. The Society should own and maintain at minimum one hand press and should hold the knowledge to build another from local materials if the existing one is damaged or destroyed.

**The screw press** — the direct descendant of Gutenberg's original design — is the simplest and most buildable press type. It consists of a flat platen driven down onto the printing surface by a wooden or metal screw. The mechanism can be built by any competent woodworker and produces adequate pressure for relief printing. A screw press built from hardwood with a threaded wooden or salvaged metal screw requires no manufactured parts that cannot be sourced or produced locally.

**Relief printing** — printing from a raised surface that receives ink and transfers it to paper under pressure — is the most accessible printing method available without industrial inputs. Type can be carved from wood or linoleum. Designs can be carved as woodcuts. Text can be set from salvaged or cast metal type. The Division should maintain competency in hand-setting type and in cutting relief printing blocks, so that the press can be used for both text and image production.

**Stencil and screen methods** are simpler than press printing and require minimal equipment. A stencil cut from oiled paper or thin leather, inked with a brush or pad, produces adequate results for notices and single-page documents. Screen printing from fabric stretched over a frame extends this capability to higher-volume production and larger formats. These methods should be part of the Division's practical range, particularly for hub notice production where speed and simplicity matter more than the precision of press printing.

**Hand-copying** is the floor beneath the press. When all other methods are unavailable or insufficient, a literate member with ink and paper can produce any document. This is not a failure state — for millennia it was the only state. The Division should maintain an awareness of calligraphic traditions and hand-copying practices, and should ensure that the knowledge of how to produce a legible, durable hand-copied document is not lost simply because it has become unnecessary under normal conditions.

## Knowledge Transmission

Every process described in this plan exists as a capability only if it is practiced. The Division's most important obligation is not to produce paper and ink — it is to ensure that enough members know how to produce paper and ink that the knowledge cannot be lost.

The Division maintains a College of members who hold deep competency across the full range of its craft. New members of the College are trained through direct apprenticeship — working alongside experienced members in actual production, not in simulated exercises. The College establishes standards of competency for each process and certifies members who meet them.

The non-obvious requirement is that production must happen regularly, not only when materials are needed. A member who last made paper three years ago has not maintained the skill. The Division should schedule regular production cycles — making paper, producing ink, binding books — as standing practice rather than on-demand response. The materials produced through regular practice cycles stock the Division's inventory. Practice and production are the same activity.

The Division should also ensure that foundational process knowledge is held in the broader membership beyond the College. Not every member needs deep craft competency, but every member should know the basics of what local plants can be made into paper and ink, and why. This is part of the Society's broader commitment to holding practical knowledge widely rather than concentrating it in specialists.

## Material Sourcing and the Agriculture Relationship

The Division's raw materials come from the land. Some will be foraged from what grows naturally. Some should be deliberately cultivated. The Division should work with the Agriculture Division to ensure that high-value fiber and ink plants are grown within the Society's agricultural capacity — not as a luxury but as a standing supply chain the Society controls.

Priority cultivation targets for a southeastern Society include hemp and flax for long-fiber paper, oak trees managed for gall production, and flax for linseed oil used in printing ink. These are crops with multiple uses across divisions — hemp for fiber in both paper and textile, flax for both paper fiber and linseed oil — making them efficient uses of agricultural capacity.

The Division should maintain a current inventory of locally available wild sources for all critical materials and should map seasonal availability, so that production cycles align with material availability rather than discovering gaps when materials are needed.

## Division Relationships

**Agriculture**
The primary material relationship. Fiber crops, oil crops, and oak management for gall production all require coordination with the Agriculture Division. The Division should communicate its material needs to Agriculture as standing cultivation priorities, not one-time requests.

**Food**
Leather for bookbinding comes from hides produced through the Food Division's animal husbandry and slaughter operations. Tallow and lard have historical uses in ink formulation. Flour for paste adhesive is a shared resource. The Division should maintain a standing relationship with Food around these material flows.

**Communications**
The Communications Division depends on the Printing Division for the physical materials that make Tier 4 communications function — notice board content, governance records posted at hubs, emergency reference cards. The two divisions should coordinate on production standards for hub notices and the cadence at which notice materials are produced and distributed.

**Education**
The Division of Education is the primary consumer of the Printing Division's output. Curriculum materials, reference guides, and the physical compendium all require paper, ink, and binding. The Education Division should communicate its production needs to Printing as standing requirements, and the Printing Division should treat Education's material needs as a core planning input.

**All Divisions**
Each division that produces reference documents, operational guides, or training materials depends on the Printing Division for physical production. Division officers should communicate their document production needs to Printing as part of their operational planning.

## Operational Posture

In stable conditions the Division maintains standing production cycles, stocks paper and ink against future need, trains new College members, and develops its material sourcing relationships. Production is not reactive — the Division produces continuously so that materials are available when any division needs them.

As conditions deteriorate and modern consumables become unavailable, the Division's locally-sourced production capability becomes the Society's only production capability. The transition should be invisible — not a scramble to establish something new but a shift in volume of something already functioning. This is only possible if the lower-tier production methods are practiced continuously rather than held in reserve.

At the floor, the Society can always produce documents. Members with knowledge of local materials, basic equipment, and practiced craft can make paper, produce ink, and bind books from what the land provides. This capability does not depend on any supply chain, any grid connection, or any corporate infrastructure. It is the floor the Division has been built to maintain.

## Establishment Priorities

**1. Identify and practice with local fiber sources**
The first task is material knowledge — what grows locally, what it yields, how to process it. This requires no equipment beyond basic tools and produces the foundational knowledge everything else depends on. A Society in Georgia should be making paper from kudzu within its first year.

**2. Establish ink production**
Iron gall ink from local oak galls and ferrous sulfate, carbon ink from lampblack and linseed oil. Both are achievable with minimal equipment. Stockpile oak galls and ferrous sulfate as early production resources.

**3. Acquire or build a hand press**
A screw press built from hardwood by a competent woodworker. Establish relief printing capability alongside it — type setting or block cutting. Begin producing printed materials.

**4. Establish bookbinding practice**
Train College members in signature sewing and at least two covering structures. Begin producing bound books. Establish leather sourcing relationship with the Food Division.

**5. Build the College**
Formalize the College of members holding deep competency. Establish apprenticeship relationships. Set production cycles that maintain practice across all processes continuously.

## Division Documents

**Local Materials Guide** — a field reference identifying fiber plants, ink sources, and binding materials available in the Society's specific geography, with seasonal availability, processing notes, and quality assessments. Specific to the Society's location and updated as the Division's material knowledge develops.

**Production Process Manual** — step-by-step documentation of every production process the Division maintains: papermaking from each fiber source, each ink formulation, each binding structure, press operation and maintenance. Held in physical bound form within the Division. Ironically, this is among the most important documents the Division produces — the manual that enables the Division to continue functioning if its experienced members are lost.

**Material Inventory and Production Schedule** — a standing record of current paper, ink, and binding material stocks against projected divisional needs, with a production schedule that maintains adequate inventory. Updated continuously.

**College Competency Standards** — the criteria for competency in each process the Division maintains, used to assess and certify College members and to guide apprenticeship training.
