# Death and Body Handling

---

## 1.1 The Biology of Death and Decomposition

**Standard:** This standard exists as a foundational rule because the fear of dead bodies significantly exceeds the actual biological hazard they present in most circumstances — and because misunderstanding the actual hazard leads to errors in both directions. Communities that are too afraid of bodies delay burial, handle them with unnecessary panic, and fail to document and prepare adequately. Communities that are insufficiently careful about infectious disease deaths or burial siting expose themselves to genuine harm. Understanding what actually happens to a body after death enables proportionate response.

**What happens after death:**

The body begins to change immediately after the heart stops. Two processes drive decomposition: autolysis and putrefaction.

**Autolysis:**

- Cells begin digesting themselves within minutes of death — enzymes that were confined within cell membranes release into surrounding tissue
- Autolysis progresses fastest in organs with high enzyme content: the pancreas, liver, and brain begin breaking down within hours
- Structural tissue — bone, cartilage, fibrous connective tissue — resists autolysis and may remain intact for years

**Putrefaction:**

- Bacteria from the intestinal tract — primarily anaerobes that were already present — migrate through the intestinal wall into surrounding tissue, typically within hours of death
- These bacteria produce gases (hydrogen sulfide, methane, ammonia, carbon dioxide) that cause the bloating and characteristic odor associated with decomposition
- The gut is the primary engine of decomposition — a body's own intestinal bacteria decompose it from the inside out

**Temperature is the primary variable:**

- Above 80°F: active decomposition begins within 24 hours; significant odor and bloating within 24-72 hours; burial within 24 hours strongly indicated
- 60-80°F: decomposition begins 48-72 hours after death; burial within 48-72 hours appropriate
- Below 40°F: decomposition dramatically slowed; a body at sustained below-40°F temperatures may remain relatively intact for weeks
- Below 32°F: decomposition essentially halted; frozen bodies are preserved indefinitely
- Cold is the community's primary tool for extending the time available for burial preparation

**What pathogens survive in a body and for how long:**

Most pathogens that contributed to death die rapidly after the host dies, because they require the living host's physiology — blood flow, nutrient delivery, temperature regulation — to survive. The biological hazard from most bodies is low.

Notable exceptions:

- Hepatitis B: viable in dried blood and body fluids for up to 7 days after death — the most significant blood-borne pathogen risk in body handling
- Tuberculosis (Mycobacterium tuberculosis): survives in lung tissue post-mortem for extended periods; aerosolization during body manipulation creates inhalation risk
- Anthrax (Bacillus anthracis): forms spores that survive in soil for decades; requires specialized handling protocol (see 1.8)
- Prion diseases (Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease): essentially indestructible by any community-level decontamination; require outside specialized handling
- HIV: dies rapidly outside the living host; not a significant post-mortem hazard beyond immediate fluid exposure in the first hours after death

**The actual contamination pathways:**

- Direct contact with blood and body fluids — the primary handler risk
- Groundwater contamination from burial leachate — decomposing tissue produces cadaverine, putrescine, ammonium compounds, and residual pathogens that migrate through permeable soil toward groundwater; the burial setback standards in 1.6 address this pathway
- Insect and scavenger vectors — exposed unburied bodies attract flies that can carry pathogens to food sources and surfaces; this is why timely burial is a public health measure, not only a cultural one

**Rationale:** The history of epidemic management is full of examples where communities fled their dead, abandoned them, or buried them so hastily and carelessly that contamination of water sources followed. The plague responses of medieval Europe — bodies in wells, mass graves without adequate depth, flight from communities leaving unburied dead — are extreme versions of panic-driven body management that consistently worsened outbreaks by creating the contamination pathways the panic was trying to escape. Conversely, communities that established orderly body handling, adequate burial, and handler precautions experienced dramatically lower secondary mortality even in the absence of any understanding of germ theory. The Islamic requirement for burial before the next sundown, the Jewish requirement for burial within 24 hours, the Hindu practice of cremation within 24 hours — these are not merely spiritual requirements. They are the accumulated practical discovery that rapid, careful disposal of the dead reduces disease transmission in ways that were observable before anyone understood why.

The temperature variable is emphasized because it is the community's primary practical tool. A community that understands that cold slows decomposition can make rational decisions about burial timing — proceeding immediately in summer heat, taking additional time in winter cold for preparation, ceremony, and waiting for absent family members. A community that does not understand this may bury hastily when haste is not required, or delay when delay is dangerous.

## 1.2 Confirming Death

**Standard:** A body shall not be treated as deceased until death is confirmed by specific criteria. Premature declaration of death and burial is an irreversible error. People can appear dead under conditions including severe hypothermia, opioid or barbiturate overdose, cardiac arrhythmia, and certain neurological states. The confirmation criteria below shall be applied before any body is prepared for burial.

**Conditions that can mimic death:**

- Severe hypothermia: the body's metabolic rate drops dramatically under profound cold; pulse and respiration may be undetectable without careful examination; a hypothermic individual is not confirmed dead until rewarmed
- Opioid overdose: respiratory depression can reduce breathing to undetectable levels; pulse may be very faint
- Lightning strike and electrocution: cardiac arrest that may be reversible with immediate response

**Signs required for confirmation of death:**

All of the following shall be present:

- Absent carotid pulse: no pulse felt at the carotid artery on both sides of the neck for a minimum of 60 full seconds each
- Absent respiration: no chest movement and no breath sounds heard or felt at the mouth and nose for 2 full minutes
- Unresponsive to stimulation: no response to firm sternal rub (knuckles pressed firmly and rubbed against the sternum)
- Fixed and dilated pupils: pupils do not change size in response to light (cover one eye, shine light in the other; a living pupil constricts)

**Confirmatory signs — not required but definitive when present:**

- Rigor mortis: stiffening of muscles beginning 2-6 hours after death, fully developed by 12 hours, releases over 24-72 hours. A body in full rigor is unambiguously dead.
- Livor mortis: blood pools in the lowest points of the body after circulation stops, producing fixed purple-red discoloration on dependent surfaces. Begins forming within 30 minutes of death, fixed (does not shift with position change) by 6-12 hours. Fixed livor mortis is unambiguous confirmation.
- Algor mortis: the body cools at approximately 1.5°F per hour until ambient temperature is reached. A body cold throughout is dead.

**The hypothermia exception:**

- Do not confirm death in an individual who is severely hypothermic until the body has been rewarmed to near-normal core temperature and still shows no vital signs
- "Not dead until warm and dead" — this principle has saved documented lives
- Rewarming may require transport to outside medical care; evaluate that option before abandoning resuscitation in a cold individual

**Who confirms:**

- The most medically knowledgeable community member available
- In the absence of medical training: two community members independently applying the above criteria and reaching the same conclusion
- The time of confirmed death shall be documented

**When in doubt:**

- Err toward resuscitation effort rather than declaration of death
- The cost of resuscitation effort on a person who is truly dead is zero
- The cost of declaring dead a person who is not is irreversible

**Rationale:** The confirmation criteria are presented in detail because the conditions that mimic death are not rare. Opioid overdose is a common cause of apparent death in communities across the country. Hypothermia in winter months, particularly in outdoor workers, is realistic. The signs of death — rigor mortis, fixed lividity, cold throughout — are unmistakable once present and eliminate any ambiguity. The practice of waiting for these confirmatory signs before burial, or of applying all confirmation criteria carefully before proceeding, costs almost nothing and prevents an irreversible error.

The hypothermia exception deserves emphasis because the instinct when confronting a cold, unresponsive person is to treat them as dead. In extreme cold, the body's metabolic requirements drop dramatically — people have survived hours of clinical death from hypothermia who were subsequently rewarmed and resuscitated. The documented cases of survival from apparent cold-death are not medical anomalies; they reflect the genuine protective effect of hypothermia on brain tissue under conditions of cardiac arrest.

## 1.3 Handler Safety

**Standard:** All persons handling a body shall use universal precautions — treating all blood and body fluids as potentially infectious regardless of known cause of death. Universal precautions protect against the full range of blood-borne pathogens without requiring knowledge of the specific infectious status of the deceased, which is often unknown. Enhanced precautions apply in the specific circumstances identified in 1.8.

**Required precautions for all body handling:**

- Gloves: nitrile or latex, intact, covering hands and wrists for all direct body contact
- Eye protection: required whenever there is risk of fluid splash — moving a body, bathing a body, handling trauma deaths
- Skin coverage: arms and torso covered; no open skin wounds exposed during handling
- Dedicated clothing: clothing worn during body handling shall be laundered before wearing again; an apron or coverall that can be removed and washed is preferred

**After handling:**

- Remove gloves by turning inside out and dispose of without touching the outer surface
- Wash hands with soap and water for minimum 20 seconds; do not rely on hand sanitizer alone when hands may have contacted body fluids
- Launder clothing that contacted the body before wearing again
- If eyes, nose, or mouth were exposed to body fluids: flush with clean water for 15 minutes

**Who should not handle bodies:**

- Immunocompromised individuals — those undergoing chemotherapy, with HIV/AIDS, or with other significant immune suppression — shall not handle bodies unless no alternative exists
- Individuals with open wounds on hands or arms unless double-gloved with intact gloves covering all exposed wounds
- Pregnant women where cause of death included possible cytomegalovirus, toxoplasmosis, or unknown infectious illness

**Minimizing handler exposure:**

- Limit handlers to the minimum necessary for safe body movement — typically two or three people
- Designate specific handlers in advance; more people present does not reduce individual exposure if all are in contact with the body
- Assign a non-contact coordinator who documents, directs, and manages logistics without direct body contact
- Document the names of all individuals who directly handled the body (required for contact tracing if infectious disease is subsequently identified)

**Rationale:** Universal precautions emerged from the HIV epidemic as a systematic response to the problem of unknown infectious status — the policy of treating all blood and body fluids as infectious regardless of assumed status was validated by outbreak investigations that showed that known-status assumptions were wrong often enough to cause documented transmission events. The same logic applies here. Cause of death is frequently not known precisely at the time of body handling. Hepatitis B status, tuberculosis status, and the status of other blood-borne pathogens that survive post-mortem are generally unknown. Universal precautions eliminate the need for these determinations to be made correctly before safe handling can proceed.

The documentation of handlers is included not as bureaucratic requirement but as a practical disease surveillance tool. If a handler becomes ill in the days to weeks following body handling, documentation of who handled whom makes the exposure linkage possible. Without this documentation, a case of hepatitis B or tuberculosis in a community member weeks after they handled a body may not be connected to the exposure at all, preventing appropriate medical follow-up and contact tracing.

## 1.4 Body Care in the Immediate Period

**Standard:** The period between death and burial requires specific actions that preserve dignity, slow decomposition to allow adequate preparation time, and contain fluids. Temperature and time are the governing variables. Decisions made in the first hours after death — including body position and cooling — are either impossible or much more difficult to reverse later.

**Immediate actions (first 30-60 minutes):**

These actions become impossible once rigor mortis sets — typically 2-6 hours after death:

- Lay the body flat on its back on a firm, flat surface
- Close the eyes — place a damp cloth over them if they will not stay closed
- Support the jaw closed — a folded cloth under the chin and tied over the head, or a jaw wrap, maintains closure before rigor sets
- Remove medical equipment (tubes, IVs, catheters) using gloves; these are not needed post-mortem and complicate preparation
- Straighten the limbs if possible before rigor sets

**Time limits by temperature:**

- Above 80°F: burial within 24 hours strongly indicated; 36 hours maximum before advanced decomposition
- 60-80°F: burial within 48-72 hours appropriate under most circumstances
- Below 60°F: burial can generally be extended to 72-96 hours; monitor daily
- Below 40°F: significant extension possible; body may remain in appropriate condition for days to weeks

**Cooling the body when burial must be delayed:**

- Moving the body to the coolest available location significantly extends the time available
- Packing with ice dramatically slows decomposition — place ice around the torso, particularly the abdominal area where gut bacteria drive decomposition fastest
- Ice melt water shall be disposed of away from any food preparation areas, food storage, or potable water sources — treat it as potentially contaminated
- A body does not need to be refrigerated to be preserved for a reasonable period — shade, ventilation, and ice achieve meaningful temperature reduction even in warm climates

**Shrouding:**

- Shroud the body in a natural fiber cloth (cotton, linen, wool) before transport to the burial site
- Shrouding contains fluids, reduces handler exposure during transport, and is fully appropriate as a burial wrap
- Secure the shroud so it will not open during transport
- Do not use synthetic materials — polyester and nylon do not biodegrade appropriately for soil burial

**When burial will be significantly delayed:**

- A location below 40°F or with adequate ice is required if burial will exceed the temperature-appropriate window
- The holding location shall be a minimum 50 feet from occupied structures and 100 feet from any food production or storage areas
- The location shall be clearly marked and documented
- The body shall not be placed in any location where fluids could contaminate water sources

**Rationale:** The time limits by temperature are derived from the decomposition biology in 1.1 — they are not arbitrary but reflect the actual progression of decomposition under typical conditions. The intent is not to rush burial inappropriately but to give communities clear decision thresholds. A community that does not know whether they have 24 hours or 72 hours may either rush unnecessarily or allow decomposition that creates public health risks and makes handling substantially more difficult and hazardous.

The jaw closure and eye closure actions are performed in the first hour not for cosmetic reasons — though dignity matters — but because rigor mortis will lock the body in whatever position it is in when rigor sets. A body in rigor with mouth open and eyes open is significantly more difficult to prepare and may cause distress to community members who see it during ceremony. These simple actions, taken immediately, preserve options.

The ice melt water warning is included because it represents a genuine contamination pathway that is easy to overlook. The instinct when managing a body is to focus on the body; the fluids that collect and drain from an ice-packed body are an afterthought that can contaminate food or water storage if not consciously managed.

## 1.5 Documentation and Community Records

**Standard:** Every death within the community shall be documented before burial. Documentation serves three purposes: disease surveillance, community historical record, and the ability to locate remains if needed. The documentation requirement is not waived by circumstances, including difficult deaths, mass casualty events, or community distress. Under pressure, designate documentation as a specific role so that it is not lost in the urgency of other tasks.

**Required information for each death:**

- Full name of the deceased
- Date of birth or approximate age
- Date and time of confirmed death (as accurately as known)
- Location where death occurred
- Circumstances of death: where found, what was known of their condition beforehand, whether death was expected or unexpected
- Known medical conditions and current medications
- Symptoms in the days and weeks before death — essential for disease surveillance
- Likely cause of death, with acknowledgment that this is community assessment, not medical certification
- Names of individuals present at the time of death
- Names of individuals who directly handled the body
- Date of burial or cremation
- Location of burial: GPS coordinates if available, or triangulated distance and bearing from a minimum of two permanent landmarks (a specific named tree, building corner, rock formation)
- Burial depth
- Any items buried with the remains

**Community death registry:**

- A designated physical document (not stored only digitally) maintained in a secure, known location
- A backup copy stored in a separate location
- Accessible to community leadership and the community health officer
- Retained permanently — death records are among the most important community historical documents

**Disease surveillance threshold:**

- If a person dies of unknown cause after an acute illness: notify the community health officer immediately for enhanced surveillance of other community members
- If two or more people die of similar presentations within a 14-day period: treat as a potential outbreak, document aggressively, and activate the community outbreak response protocol
- Multiple deaths with similar cause is the most important signal available to a community for detecting an outbreak in progress

**When documentation is difficult:**

- In mass casualty events, designate one person specifically for documentation before other activities begin — documentation quality degrades under stress and this role must be protected
- If full documentation is not possible at the time of death, document what is known immediately and supplement as information becomes available
- Identification before burial — do not bury unidentified remains if any means of identification is available, including photographs, distinctive clothing, or physical description recorded in the registry

**Rationale:** The documentation requirement is placed before the burial standards because documentation is time-sensitive in a way that burial standards are not — information known at the time of death may be unavailable or harder to verify after burial has occurred. The names of people present, the specific symptoms of the final illness, the circumstances of death — these become less reliable as time passes and memory fades. Documentation at the time is categorically more accurate than reconstruction afterward.

The disease surveillance role of cause-of-death documentation deserves emphasis. A death registry that includes symptom information from the days before death is a genuine epidemiological tool. Communities that maintained good death records historically have repeatedly identified outbreak patterns — the same symptom complex appearing in multiple death records within days of each other — that would not have been recognized without documentation. The Broad Street cholera outbreak of 1854, which produced the foundational work in epidemiology, was identified through systematic death records that allowed John Snow to map cases and identify the contaminated pump. A community with good death records is a community with outbreak surveillance capacity.

## 1.6 Burial Standards

**Standard:** Burial shall meet minimum depth and setback requirements that prevent contamination of groundwater and surface water, exclude scavengers, and provide appropriate weight and temperature stability for decomposition. These standards are derived from the contamination physics of burial leachate — not from tradition or convention — and shall be applied to every burial on community land.

**Minimum burial depth:**

- Minimum 5 feet of soil cover over the top of remains, measured from the soil surface to the top of the body or container
- In practice: a grave approximately 6 feet deep at minimum, with remains placed at the bottom and 5 feet of soil above
- The 5-foot standard provides: adequate weight to resist disturbance by scavengers; sufficient soil depth for filtration of decomposition leachate; temperature stability that moderates decomposition; and protection against surface erosion exposing remains over time
- Where bedrock or hardpan prevents a 6-foot grave: consult the alternatives in this section before compromising depth

**Setback from water sources:**

- Wells and springs: minimum 100 feet in loam, clay-loam, or heavy soils; minimum 300 feet in sandy, gravelly, or highly permeable soils
- Surface water (streams, rivers, ponds, lakes): minimum 100 feet
- Community cisterns and water storage: minimum 150 feet
- The soil type distinction is critical: decomposition leachate migrates through sandy or gravelly soil with minimal filtration and can reach groundwater sources at distances that would be safe in clay-rich soil. When soil type is unknown, apply the 300-foot setback.

**Setback from other land uses:**

- Occupied structures: minimum 10 feet
- Active food gardens and crop production areas: minimum 50 feet
- Animal pasture: minimum 25 feet
- No burial within or adjacent to active agricultural areas where crop roots would contact remains

**Groundwater considerations:**

- The bottom of the grave shall be minimum 2 feet above the seasonal high water table — the highest level the water table reaches during wet seasons
- If water is encountered while digging, that site is not appropriate for burial — the water table is too close to the surface
- In areas with seasonally high water tables: burial mounds constructed with imported fill soil can achieve the required depth above the water table even when in-ground depth is limited
- Low-lying areas, areas that pond after rain, and areas adjacent to seasonal streams are likely to have high water tables and should be avoided for burial

**Site characteristics:**

- Level or gently sloping ground prevents erosion that could expose remains over time
- Avoid topographic low points that collect surface water or experience seasonal flooding
- Avoid known or potential future well locations — a community expanding its water infrastructure should not be constrained by burial locations chosen without forethought
- A designated community burial area is strongly preferred over dispersed individual burials — it simplifies setback compliance, enables good record-keeping, and creates a maintained community space

**Container standards:**

- No container is required — burial in a natural fiber shroud only (natural burial) is fully appropriate and produces faster, more complete decomposition
- If a container is used: it shall be biodegradable — untreated wood, wicker, cardboard, or other natural materials
- Sealed metal or plastic containers are not appropriate for soil burial: they prevent decomposition for years to decades, can accumulate gases and deform catastrophically, and eventually corrode and collapse rather than integrating into the soil
- Embalming fluids shall not be used in any burial on community land — formaldehyde and the chemical mixture used in commercial embalming contaminate soil and groundwater and persist for extended periods. Embalming also serves no purpose when burial occurs within the timeframes specified in 1.4.

**Burial site records:**

- Location recorded in the community death registry with GPS coordinates or triangulated distance and direction from two or more permanent landmarks
- A permanent marker at the head of the grave
- Community burial area mapped and the map retained with the death registry
- Burial locations shall be considered when planning community infrastructure — water lines, buildings, gardens — to ensure they are not inadvertently disturbed

**Rationale:** The depth and setback standards are grounded in the contamination pathway identified in 1.1. Decomposing tissue produces leachate containing cadaverine, putrescine, ammonium compounds, and residual pathogens. This leachate migrates downward and laterally through soil at rates determined primarily by soil permeability. In permeable sandy or gravelly soils, leachate can travel hundreds of feet and reach groundwater sources; in clay-rich soils, filtration and adsorption substantially reduce migration distance and pathogen load. The soil type distinction in the setback standards reflects this physical reality — a standard appropriate for clay soil is insufficient for sand.

The prohibition on sealed metal or plastic containers deserves specific rationale because the intuition runs the other way — a sealed container seems more protective than an open one. The problem is long-term: a sealed metal coffin prevents decomposition for decades, then corrodes and collapses, often creating a subsidence depression at the surface and releasing partially decomposed material into the soil simultaneously rather than in the gradual, well-managed way that open burial produces. Wood and natural materials biodegrade alongside the remains, integrating into the soil without this deferred failure mode. Natural burial also produces faster, more complete integration — the biological reality is that decomposition is not something to prevent but something to manage safely.

The embalming prohibition is included because embalming chemicals are genuinely toxic soil contaminants. Formaldehyde is a carcinogen; the full embalming chemical mixture includes additional preservatives, dyes, and surfactants that persist in soil and can leach to groundwater. The commercial funeral industry embalms primarily for cosmetic presentation and transport requirements — neither of which applies to community burial conducted within the timeframes in 1.4. Embalming provides no safety benefit to handlers or community members that universal precautions do not already provide.

## 1.7 Cremation

**Standard:** Cremation is an appropriate alternative to burial when community members choose it, when burial site conditions are inadequate, or when infectious disease deaths require alternative disposal. Complete cremation requires temperatures substantially higher than an open campfire can achieve. This standard establishes the requirements for community-conducted cremation that achieves adequate combustion rather than partial combustion that creates a worse outcome than burial.

**What adequate cremation requires:**

- Sustained temperatures of 1,400-1,800°F for an extended period
- An open campfire achieves 900-1,100°F — insufficient for complete combustion of a human body
- A traditional open-air funeral pyre using dry hardwood can achieve adequate temperatures with sufficient fuel and adequate air supply to the base of the fire
- Complete cremation produces calcined bone fragments — calcium phosphate — that are what is commonly called "ashes." These are not the gray powder of wood ash but white to gray bone fragments, often requiring manual grinding. They are essentially sterile and pose no infectious hazard.

**Building an adequate cremation structure:**

- An elevated pyre with air circulation beneath dramatically increases combustion temperature — ground-level fires lose heat to the earth and restrict air flow
- A raised platform of green logs or non-combustible material (stone, brick) supports the body above a fire bed
- A surround of clay, brick, or stone on three sides reflects heat back to the body and concentrates combustion
- Fuel requirement: approximately 600-700 pounds of dry hardwood for a complete adult cremation. Softwood burns hotter initially but does not sustain adequate temperatures — dry hardwood is required. Green or wet wood dramatically reduces combustion temperature.
- Duration: a complete cremation with adequate fuel takes 4-8 hours

**Site requirements:**

- Minimum 100 feet from occupied structures
- Downwind of occupied areas based on prevailing wind direction — smoke from cremation contains combustion products that are respiratory irritants
- Minimum 50 feet from dry vegetation (fire spread risk)
- A cleared area of minimum 10 feet radius around the pyre, free of combustible material
- Water or sand available on site for fire control
- A designated fire safety observer present throughout

**What shall not be cremated:**

- Pacemakers and implantable defibrillators: the lithium battery can explode at cremation temperatures with significant force. A pacemaker shall be removed before cremation — this requires a small incision in the chest wall over the device, which can be performed with basic equipment and gloves. The device location is almost always visible as a small rectangular outline beneath the skin on the left upper chest.
- Large orthopedic metal hardware (joint replacements, long bone rods): generates toxic metal fumes when heated to cremation temperatures. Where possible, avoid cremation for individuals with significant metal implants, or accept incomplete combustion of the metallic components.
- Sealed pressurized containers: will explode

**Handling cremated remains:**

- Calcined bone fragments from complete cremation are sterile — they pose no infectious or chemical hazard
- They may be buried without setback requirements (they are not a contamination source), scattered on land or water, or retained by family
- Where cremation was incomplete — fuel exhausted, conditions inadequate — partially combusted remains shall be buried in place with minimum soil cover requirements from 1.6 applied

**Incomplete combustion:**

- If fuel runs short or conditions prevent adequate temperature, combustion will be incomplete
- Partially combusted remains must be treated as burial material — bury in place with a minimum of 3 feet of soil cover, or transport to a burial site if the pyre location does not meet setback requirements
- Do not attempt to transport partially combusted remains without containment — use a shroud and handle with the same precautions as a body

**Rationale:** The fuel quantity requirement — approximately 600-700 pounds of dry hardwood — is included because it is almost always underestimated by communities considering cremation. A cord of dry hardwood weighs approximately 2,000-3,000 pounds depending on species; a complete cremation requires roughly one-quarter to one-third of a cord. This is a significant fuel investment that requires advance planning. A community that begins a cremation with insufficient fuel will have incomplete combustion, which produces a worse outcome than a properly conducted burial — partially combusted remains that must still be disposed of, with handlers who have already been exposed to smoke and combustion products.

The pacemaker removal requirement is included because pacemaker explosions during cremation are documented in the funeral industry and represent a genuine hazard to fire tenders. The procedure for removal — a small incision, typically no more than 2 inches, over the device — is well within the capability of community members with basic first aid training and appropriate equipment. The device is immediately beneath the skin and straightforward to locate. Performing this step before cremation eliminates a hazard that is otherwise not visible or anticipated.

## 1.8 Infectious Disease Deaths — Enhanced Protocols

**Standard:** When death occurs during or within 14 days of an acute illness of potentially infectious nature, or when cause of death is unknown, enhanced precautions shall be applied throughout body handling and burial. The specific precautions depend on the suspected or known pathogen. When the pathogen is unknown, apply the maximum precautions until the situation is clarified.

**Conditions that trigger enhanced protocols:**

- Death during or within 14 days of fever-based acute illness
- Unknown cause of death in a previously healthy person
- Death during a known or suspected community disease outbreak
- Known or suspected exposure to a high-hazard pathogen (tuberculosis, hepatitis B, anthrax)
- Multiple deaths with similar presentation in a short period (potential outbreak)

**Enhanced PPE requirements:**

- N95 respirator or equivalent (not a surgical mask) — required whenever aerosol generation is possible (moving the body, turning, undressing)
- Eye protection mandatory — goggles preferred over glasses
- Double gloves — two layers of nitrile or latex
- Gown or dedicated outer clothing that fully covers arms and torso
- Boot covers if floor or ground is contaminated with body fluids

**Disease-specific considerations:**

**Tuberculosis:**

- Mycobacterium tuberculosis survives in lung tissue post-mortem and can be aerosolized during body manipulation
- Minimize movement and turning of the body — handle once, with purpose, not repeatedly
- N95 is mandatory throughout all handling
- All handlers are under enhanced symptom surveillance for 8 weeks following handling — TB incubation period
- Bury rather than cremate — cremation aerosol risk is higher than burial leachate risk for TB

**Hepatitis B:**

- Primary risk is blood and body fluid contact — blood-borne transmission, not airborne
- Double gloves, eye protection, and skin coverage are the critical precautions
- Individuals who have not received hepatitis B vaccination and who handled the body of a confirmed or likely hepatitis B case should seek post-exposure evaluation if available

**Anthrax (Bacillus anthracis):**

- Anthrax is rare but requires specific protocol because it does not behave like other pathogens
- B. anthracis forms spores that survive in soil essentially indefinitely — spores present in remains contaminate the burial site permanently
- Do not perform any procedures that open body cavities
- Do not cremate — cremation disperses spores
- Burial with minimum depth of 6 feet and application of a lime layer directly over the body before filling the grave reduces spore migration
- Suspected anthrax deaths require outside notification and expert guidance — do not proceed on community protocols alone if anthrax is genuinely suspected

**Prion diseases (CJD, variant CJD):**

- No community-level decontamination method inactivates prions — they are resistant to bleach, heat, UV radiation, and all standard disinfection methods
- Do not perform any body preparation that involves cutting or abrasion of tissue
- Handle only with double gloves; dispose of all contact materials
- Require outside specialized handling — contact outside medical authorities
- Standard burial (no cutting, no abrasion of tissue, adequate depth) is appropriate; cremation is not recommended due to aerosol concerns

**Limiting and documenting handlers:**

- Limit direct body contact to two people where possible
- Document the full name of every person who directly handled the body — this documentation is essential for disease surveillance and may be needed for contact tracing and post-exposure evaluation
- These individuals are under enhanced symptom surveillance for the incubation period of the suspected pathogen

**Burial timeline:**

- Expedite burial in infectious disease deaths — do not allow social or logistical delays to extend the time the body is in the community
- The primary exposure risk to handlers and community is during the period the body is present and unburied
- Full documentation must still occur, but rapidly — designate a documenter who works simultaneously with preparation

**Surface and textile decontamination:**

- Bedding, clothing, and surfaces that contacted the body or fluids: wash with hot water and soap, followed by bleach solution (1 part bleach to 10 parts water) for non-porous surfaces
- Textiles that cannot be washed above 140°F and are heavily soiled: bag and bury with the body or burn separately if safe to do so
- Handlers' clothing: launder separately at the highest available temperature before wearing again

**Rationale:** The tuberculosis airborne precaution is included because tuberculosis is common enough in the populations the Resolute Society may serve that it will realistically be encountered, and because the post-mortem hazard is specifically from aerosol generation during body manipulation — a hazard that differs from the blood-borne hazards that universal precautions address. A handler who uses gloves and eye protection but does not use respiratory protection while turning a TB body is protected from the hazards they can see (fluids) but not from the hazard they cannot see (aerosolized bacteria from lung tissue). The N95 is the specific intervention for this specific pathway.

The anthrax protocol is included despite its rarity because the consequences of mismanagement are severe and permanent — B. anthracis spores in a burial site contaminate that site indefinitely. Anthrax is not a realistic threat in most community contexts but it is a realistic threat in communities keeping livestock, particularly in regions where anthrax is historically present in soil (much of the western and central United States). A community that knows the protocol — particularly that cremation is contraindicated and that lime application reduces spore migration — avoids a specific, catastrophic, irreversible error in the event that a case occurs.

## 1.9 Mass Casualty Events

**Standard:** When multiple deaths occur simultaneously or in rapid succession — from disaster, epidemic, or violence — the normal death handling process must be compressed without eliminating its essential elements. Documentation, identification, and safe disposal remain mandatory. The order of priority in a mass casualty event: identify, document, then bury. A body whose identity is lost before burial cannot be recovered; documentation lost under pressure cannot be reconstructed; but burial can be expedited safely if the first two steps are completed.

**Immediate priorities:**

- Designate a documentation coordinator before other activities begin — documentation degrades severely under stress and this role must be protected from being absorbed into physical tasks
- Identify before burial — photograph each body, record distinctive features, clothing, and location found; do not allow burial to proceed for unidentified remains if any means of identification is available
- Do not rush identification for burial speed — a permanently unidentified burial is a failure that cannot be corrected

**Handler management:**

- More bodies do not require more simultaneous handlers — they require rotation
- Rotate handlers through body contact duty; continuous handling of multiple bodies is psychologically damaging and degrades care quality
- Maintain minimum PPE compliance throughout — under pressure, precautions are the first thing to slip

**Temporary holding when burial capacity is exceeded:**

- If the pace of deaths exceeds the community's burial capacity, bodies may be held temporarily
- Temporary holding requirements:
  - Cool location — below 60°F preferred, below 40°F strongly preferred
  - Minimum 50 feet from occupied structures
  - Minimum 100 feet from food storage or preparation areas
  - Covered against insects, weather, and scavengers
  - Each body marked with identification if known, or with a sequential number that links to the documentation record
  - Maximum holding time: 48 hours at temperatures above 60°F; 72 hours at 40-60°F; longer acceptable below 40°F
  - Location and contents documented in the community record

**Disease surveillance in mass casualty events:**

- Multiple deaths with similar presentation within a short period is the most important outbreak signal available — the documentation coordinator tracks cause-of-death observations and reports patterns to the community health officer immediately
- If deaths appear to follow a common cause (similar symptoms, common exposure), activate outbreak response in parallel with body handling — the two processes must proceed simultaneously, not sequentially
- External notification: if multiple deaths with unknown cause occur in a short period, seek outside information through any available communication channel — this may indicate a hazard (infectious, chemical, environmental) that requires outside expertise to identify

**Psychological dimensions:**

- Mass death events create acute psychological crisis that is not separate from the operational response — it is part of it
- Individuals assigned to body handling in mass casualty events shall be relieved of this role as quickly as rotation allows; sustained body contact duty without relief causes lasting harm
- Ceremony and ritual around the dead serve psychological functions that are not optional — they are necessary for community cohesion and psychological recovery. The sanitary requirements of burial are compatible with meaningful ceremony; they constrain it but do not preclude it. Time for ceremony shall be protected.
- Debriefing for body handlers following a mass casualty event — acknowledgment of what they did, space to process it — is a community obligation, not a courtesy

**Rationale:** The identification-before-burial priority is stated explicitly because the pressure in a mass casualty event runs in the opposite direction — there is urgency to clear bodies, burial feels like progress, and identification slows the process. The long-term consequence of unidentified burials is severe and permanent: families without closure, community historical record with gaps that cannot be filled, and in some circumstances legal and inheritance consequences that fall on surviving community members. The pressure to expedite burial shall not override identification.

The psychological dimension of mass casualty body handling is included in a regulation document because it is not only a humanitarian concern — it is an operational one. Handlers who are psychologically overwhelmed make more mistakes, fail to maintain precautions, and cannot sustain the work. Mass casualty events in which body handling breaks down into panic, where protective measures are abandoned, where documentation is lost, and where burial is hasty and inadequate, consistently produce secondary health crises from the same failures the protocols are designed to prevent. The psychological management of handlers is therefore a public health measure as well as a community obligation.
