# Sanitation Standards — Human Waste Management

---

## 1.1 Why Human Waste Management Is the Primary Public Health Intervention

**Standard:** This standard exists as a foundational rule because sanitation is the single most effective public health intervention in human history — more lives have been saved by adequate human waste management than by any medical treatment, vaccine, or other public health measure. A community that understands why this is true will maintain sanitation systems with the urgency they deserve rather than treating them as infrastructure afterthoughts.

**The historical evidence:**

The connection between human waste and disease was not understood until the mid-19th century. Before that understanding, sanitation was managed primarily for aesthetic reasons — waste smelled bad and was unpleasant to live near. The consequences of this ignorance were catastrophic:

- Cholera killed tens of millions of people in 19th century pandemic waves — the disease was carried in contaminated water, and contaminated water came from human waste entering drinking water supplies
- Typhoid fever killed approximately 30,000 Americans per year at the turn of the 20th century — almost entirely from fecal contamination of water and food
- Dysentery was the leading cause of death in military campaigns throughout history — armies defeated by diarrhea more consistently than by enemy action
- Infant mortality rates in 19th century cities exceeded 50% — the majority of deaths from waterborne and foodborne disease attributable to inadequate sanitation

The introduction of sewage systems, pit latrines, and basic waste containment in the late 19th and early 20th centuries produced the most dramatic reduction in infectious disease mortality in human history — a reduction larger than that produced by all vaccines and antibiotics combined.

**The fecal-oral transmission pathway:**

The mechanism by which human waste causes disease is the fecal-oral pathway — pathogens present in human feces reach the mouths of other people through a chain of transmission that always involves inadequate waste management at some point.

The transmission chain:

- Infected person defecates — feces contains pathogens at concentrations of millions to billions per gram
- Feces contaminates the environment — soil, water, flies, hands, food preparation surfaces
- Contaminated environment contacts food or water
- Food or water reaches another person's mouth
- New infection established

The fecal-oral pathway can be broken at any point — containment of feces prevents environmental contamination, hand washing prevents hand-to-mouth transfer, water treatment prevents waterborne transmission. But the most effective intervention is the earliest one — keeping feces out of the environment in the first place. A pathogen that never leaves the waste containment system cannot travel the fecal-oral pathway regardless of hand washing compliance or water treatment quality.

**The pathogens in human waste:**

Human feces from a community without universal sanitation contains a wide range of pathogens whose concentration depends on the prevalence of infection in the community:

- Vibrio cholerae — cholera. Produces profuse rice-water diarrhea causing death from dehydration within hours. Infectious dose as low as 1,000 organisms
- Salmonella typhi — typhoid fever. Systemic infection with sustained fever, abdominal pain, and potential intestinal perforation. Infectious dose 1,000-1,000,000 organisms
- Shigella — bacillary dysentery. Bloody diarrhea, fever, severe abdominal cramping. Infectious dose as low as 10 organisms — the lowest of any common fecal-oral pathogen
- E. coli O157:H7 and other pathogenic E. coli — bloody diarrhea, hemolytic uremic syndrome in children
- Hepatitis A virus — liver infection, jaundice, prolonged illness
- Norovirus — explosive vomiting and diarrhea, highly contagious
- Cryptosporidium and Giardia — protozoal parasites causing persistent diarrhea, highly resistant to chlorine disinfection
- Intestinal helminths — roundworm, hookworm, tapeworm — eggs shed in feces that infect through soil contact or food contamination

**The dose-response relationship:**

Not every exposure to fecal pathogens causes disease — infection depends on the dose (number of organisms) reaching a susceptible person. Lower doses may be cleared by the immune system without causing illness. Higher doses overwhelm immune defenses and establish infection.

This dose-response relationship has important implications for sanitation system design:

- Systems that reduce pathogen concentration in the environment — even if they do not eliminate it — reduce disease burden proportionally
- Partial sanitation coverage reduces disease burden significantly even though it does not eliminate it
- Pathogen die-off over time in contained waste reduces the hazard of older waste relative to fresh waste

**Containment versus treatment:**

A distinction that shapes the entire sanitation standards document:

Containment — keeping waste isolated from the environment so that the fecal-oral pathway cannot be completed. A properly sited and maintained pit latrine contains waste effectively — the waste is isolated from water sources and from people, and the pathogens in it die over time without causing disease.

Treatment — actively reducing the pathogen load in waste before it re-enters the environment. Composting, anaerobic digestion, and constructed wetlands treat waste — they reduce pathogen concentrations through biological processes before the treated product is applied to land.

In degraded infrastructure contexts, containment is almost always achievable — it requires only a hole in the ground in the right location. Treatment requires infrastructure and management capacity that may not be available. A community that cannot achieve treatment can always achieve containment — and containment alone provides most of the public health benefit.

**Rationale:** The historical mortality data is presented at the beginning of this document — rather than as supporting detail in subsequent sections — because it establishes the stakes in a way that abstract microbiology cannot. A community member who knows that inadequate sanitation killed more people in the 19th century than all wars combined, and that the introduction of basic waste containment produced the largest reduction in human mortality in history, has a fundamentally different relationship to sanitation infrastructure than one who understands it only as an aesthetic or regulatory concern.

The Shigella infectious dose of 10 organisms is included because it reframes the concept of sanitation adequacy. When the infectious dose for a common pathogen is 10 organisms — a quantity invisible to the naked eye and present in any environmental contamination from human feces — "good enough" sanitation is not a meaningful standard. Either the fecal-oral pathway is broken — feces is contained, hands are washed, water is treated — or exposure to these pathogens is essentially certain in a community where the infection is circulating. The implication is that partial sanitation measures, applied inconsistently, provide far less protection than their coverage would suggest — a community with 70% sanitation coverage has near-universal pathogen exposure because the 30% without containment contaminates the shared environment.

The containment versus treatment distinction is included because it defines a realistic goal for degraded infrastructure contexts. Treatment is valuable — a composting toilet that destroys pathogens before the product is used as fertilizer is better than a pit latrine. But the community that delays building any sanitation system until treatment infrastructure is available is a community with no sanitation — and no sanitation kills people. Containment is achievable immediately with minimal resources. The standards in this document establish containment as the minimum baseline and treatment as the aspiration.

## 1.2 The Fecal-Oral Pathway — Breaking the Chain of Transmission

**Standard:** The fecal-oral pathway can be interrupted at multiple points — each intervention reduces disease transmission and the interventions compound each other. This standard establishes the hierarchy of interventions and their relative effectiveness, enabling communities to prioritize limited resources toward the highest-impact sanitation improvements.

**The F-diagram — mapping the transmission pathway:**

The F-diagram is the foundational conceptual tool of sanitation public health — it maps all the routes by which feces reaches mouths and identifies intervention points.

Primary transmission routes:

- Fluids (water): feces contaminates water sources, contaminated water is consumed
- Fields (soil): feces deposited in the open environment contaminates soil, soil contacts crops, hands, and feet
- Flies: flies breed in feces and carry pathogens on their bodies to food and food preparation surfaces
- Fingers (hands): hands contaminated with feces during defecation or environmental contact transfer pathogens to food and mouth
- Food: food contaminated through any of the above routes is consumed

**Intervention points:**

**Point 1 — Containment (highest impact):**

- Prevent feces from entering the environment at all — pit latrines, flush toilets, composting toilets
- This intervention addresses all five transmission routes simultaneously — contained feces cannot contaminate fluids, fields, flies, fingers, or food
- A community with universal containment has eliminated the primary transmission source regardless of hand washing compliance, water treatment, or food handling practices

**Point 2 — Handwashing:**

- Clean hands at critical times — after defecation, before food preparation, before eating
- Interrupts the fingers-to-food-to-mouth route regardless of environmental contamination
- Handwashing with soap reduces diarrheal disease by approximately 40% in populations with existing contamination — significant but not equivalent to containment

**Point 3 — Water treatment:**

- Treatment at the source or point of use reduces pathogen load in drinking water
- Effective against waterborne pathogens — does not address other transmission routes
- Reduces disease burden by 25-40% in populations with existing contamination

**Point 4 — Food safety:**

- Cooking food to safe temperatures, proper storage, and safe handling practices
- Effective against foodborne transmission — does not address waterborne, soil contact, or fly transmission routes

**The compound effect:**

- A community with containment only: removes the primary source of environmental contamination — approximately 70-80% reduction in fecal-oral disease
- A community with containment plus handwashing: approximately 85-90% reduction
- A community with containment plus handwashing plus water treatment: approximately 90-95% reduction
- The compound effect demonstrates that containment provides the largest individual gain, and that the other interventions provide their full benefit only on top of containment — handwashing in a community with open defecation re-contaminates the hands immediately after washing

**Open defecation — the baseline hazard:**

Open defecation — defecating in the open environment rather than in a designated facility — is the worst-case sanitation condition and the starting point from which all sanitation improvement is measured.

In communities practicing open defecation:

- Feces is distributed throughout the environment — fields, roadsides, water source margins, and shared open spaces
- Every rainfall event washes fecal pathogens into surface water
- Every fly that lands on feces and then on food creates a direct transmission event
- Soil in the community environment contains viable fecal pathogens for weeks to months after deposition
- Children playing in soil are in continuous contact with pathogen-contaminated material
- The infectious dose for Shigella — 10 organisms — is achievable by any environmental contact in a community practicing open defecation with even low disease prevalence

**The minimum standard — no open defecation:**

The first and most important sanitation standard for any community is the elimination of open defecation. Any contained sanitation facility — however simple — is categorically better than open defecation. A hole in the ground with a covering slab is an improvement. A pit latrine with a basic superstructure is a significant improvement. The hierarchy of facility quality matters — but any facility is better than none.

**Rationale:** The F-diagram framework is included because it provides a mental model for evaluating any sanitation situation — not just the specific situations described in subsequent sections. A community member who understands that flies breeding in open defecation carry pathogens to food preparation areas, and that this pathway exists independently of water contamination, understands why food safety standards alone cannot protect a community without sanitation coverage. A community member who understands that handwashing re-contaminates within minutes in an open defecation environment understands why handwashing promotion without containment produces limited benefit. The F-diagram converts sanitation from a collection of unrelated rules into a coherent system with identifiable leverage points.

The 40% diarrheal disease reduction from handwashing alone deserves specific explanation because it is both an impressive effect and a context-dependent one. The studies producing this estimate were conducted in communities with existing levels of environmental contamination from inadequate sanitation — in these conditions, handwashing provides a meaningful personal protection by interrupting hand-to-mouth transfer even when the hands are rapidly recontaminated from the environment. In communities with adequate containment, the baseline contamination is lower and the absolute benefit of handwashing is smaller — but the combined effect of containment plus handwashing is greater than either alone. The 40% figure is a persuasive argument for handwashing but not an argument that handwashing can substitute for containment.

## 1.3 Siting and Setbacks — The Non-Negotiable Location Standards

**Standard:** The location of sanitation facilities relative to water sources, dwellings, food production areas, and community spaces is the most critical sanitation design decision — a correctly sited simple latrine is safer than a poorly sited sophisticated system. Siting errors that allow fecal contamination to reach water sources or food production areas cannot be corrected by any subsequent management measure. These standards shall be applied before any sanitation facility is constructed.

**Water source setbacks:**

**Horizontal setbacks — minimum distances:**

- Surface water (rivers, streams, ponds, lakes): 30 meters (100 feet) minimum from any sanitation facility
- Shallow wells (hand-dug, less than 15 meters deep): 30 meters minimum
- Boreholes and deep wells (greater than 15 meters deep): 15 meters minimum
- Springs: 30 meters minimum, measured upslope from the spring source
- Rainwater collection surfaces: 15 meters minimum

Why these distances:

- Pathogens in soil move with groundwater flow — horizontally away from the contamination source in the direction of groundwater gradient
- Horizontal movement rate in typical soils: 1-10 meters per day depending on soil permeability and hydraulic gradient
- At 30 meters from a latrine, typical soil conditions reduce pathogen concentration by 3-6 log units (1,000-1,000,000 fold) compared to the source — below infectious dose for most pathogens
- Sandy soils transmit pathogens farther and faster — increase setbacks to 50 meters in sandy soil conditions
- Clay soils transmit pathogens less far — minimum setbacks still apply because seasonal cracking and preferential flow paths can carry pathogens unexpectedly

**Vertical setbacks — groundwater depth:**

- The bottom of any pit latrine or sanitation system shall not penetrate the water table
- Minimum distance from the base of the pit to the seasonal high water table: 1.5 meters
- In areas where the water table rises seasonally to within 1.5 meters of the surface: pit latrines are not appropriate — surface-based systems (composting toilets, container-based sanitation) are required
- Determining water table depth: observe nearby wells — the water level in the well at its highest seasonal point is the seasonal high water table. If no wells exist: a hand-dug test hole that fills with water indicates the water table depth

**Slope and drainage:**

- Sanitation facilities shall be downslope of water sources — water flows downhill, carrying any leachate away from water sources rather than toward them
- Siting upslope of a water source is prohibited regardless of horizontal setback — water flowing from the latrine area toward the water source eliminates the protection that horizontal distance provides
- In flat terrain where slope direction is unclear: site sanitation facilities in the direction of prevailing groundwater flow. Where groundwater flow direction is unknown: maintain maximum setbacks in all directions from water sources

**Flood risk:**

- Sanitation facilities in flood-prone areas are catastrophically dangerous — floodwater entering latrines and septic systems distributes fecal pathogens throughout the flooded area, contaminating water sources, food crops, and living areas simultaneously
- No sanitation facility shall be sited in areas subject to annual flooding or in areas within 1 meter of elevation above the 10-year flood level
- Where flooding is unavoidable: sealed container-based systems that can be removed before flooding, elevated platform latrines, or emergency procedures for emptying systems before floods are the appropriate approaches

**Setbacks from dwellings and food production:**

- Minimum distance from any latrine to a dwelling: 6 meters — close enough for convenient use (reducing the temptation of open defecation) but far enough to reduce fly transmission to food preparation areas within the dwelling
- Maximum distance from a dwelling to a latrine: 50 meters — latrines that are inconveniently distant are not used consistently, particularly at night and by elderly and disabled community members
- Minimum distance from any latrine to a food production area (garden, field): 10 meters minimum, 30 meters preferred. Agricultural reuse of waste requires treatment — see Section 1.9
- Minimum distance from a latrine to a food preparation or eating area: 10 meters

**Community latrine siting:**

- Community latrines serving multiple households shall be sited to be accessible from all served households within 50 meters maximum
- Community latrines in markets, community centers, and gathering spaces shall be accessible from all areas of the facility within 100 meters
- Community latrines shall have separate facilities for men and women where community norms require it — mixed-use facilities that are not culturally appropriate are not used consistently

**Rationale:** The flood risk prohibition is the most absolute siting standard in this section and the one with the most dramatic historical consequence. Flooding events that overtop sanitation systems are consistently associated with cholera outbreaks, typhoid epidemics, and other fecal-oral disease surges. The 2010 Haiti earthquake was followed by a cholera outbreak that killed approximately 10,000 people and infected 800,000 — introduced into the environment by inadequate sanitation in a UN peacekeeping camp whose waste management was overwhelmed. The subsequent flooding events distributed cholera through the water system throughout the country. A sanitation system that fails during flooding — the time of highest disease transmission risk because flooding contaminates water supplies — provides no protection when it is most needed. Siting outside flood risk areas is the only reliable protection.

The dwelling distance optimization — 6 meters minimum, 50 meters maximum — reflects behavioral sanitation research rather than purely technical standards. The technical minimum for fly transmission reduction and odor management is approximately 6 meters. The behavioral maximum is approximately 50 meters — beyond this distance, particularly at night and for elderly, disabled, and very young community members, the latrine is effectively not used and open defecation occurs instead. A latrine that is technically perfect but behaviorally unused provides no public health benefit. The distance standard balances the technical minimum with the behavioral maximum to maximize actual use.

## 1.4 Pit Latrines — Design, Construction, and Maintenance

**Standard:** The pit latrine is the most widely deployed sanitation technology in the world and the appropriate baseline sanitation technology for most community contexts. It requires no water supply, no mechanical components, no energy, and minimal materials. When correctly sited, constructed, and maintained, it provides effective pathogen containment and dramatically reduces fecal-oral disease transmission. When incorrectly sited or maintained, it is a source of contamination rather than containment.

**Basic pit latrine design:**

**The pit:**

- Depth: minimum 2 meters. Shallower pits fill too quickly and do not allow adequate time for pathogen die-off before the pit must be emptied or a new pit dug
- Diameter: 1-1.5 meters for a circular pit, 1 meter × 1.5 meters for a rectangular pit — adequate for family use at a rate of accumulation of approximately 40-60 liters per person per year
- Pit lining: the lower portion of the pit shall remain unlined to allow liquid to drain into the surrounding soil — lining the entire pit prevents drainage and accelerates filling. The upper 0.5-1 meter of the pit wall shall be lined or cased to prevent collapse at the surface
- Lining materials: brick, stone, concrete rings, timber — any material that maintains the pit wall above the collapsing soil level
- Pit capacity estimation: a family of 5 generating 40 liters per person per year fills approximately 200 liters per year. A 1-meter diameter, 2-meter deep pit has approximately 1,500 liters of usable volume — providing approximately 7-8 years of use before the pit needs to be emptied or a new pit dug

**The slab:**

- A slab covering the pit is the single most important component of a functional latrine — an uncovered pit is a fly breeding site and an accident hazard
- The slab shall cover the entire pit opening with only a defecation hole — typically 250mm × 300mm (10 × 12 inches) — and a squat platform or a seat
- Slab materials: reinforced concrete is most durable, but heavy timber, prefabricated fiber-reinforced concrete, or locally available materials that can span the pit and support a person's weight without risk of collapse are acceptable
- The slab shall be smooth and cleanable — rough surfaces harbor pathogens and are difficult to keep clean
- The defecation hole shall have a cover — a removable lid that seals the hole when not in use. The lid dramatically reduces fly emergence from the pit and odor release

**The superstructure:**

- Privacy — walls and a door or curtain — is required for consistent use, particularly by women and girls
- Minimum height: 1.8 meters — tall enough that a standing person cannot see over the walls from outside
- The superstructure does not need to be permanent or high-quality — a simple frame with fabric, thatch, or salvaged material provides adequate privacy for consistent use
- Roof: a roof reduces rainwater entering the pit (which accelerates filling and can cause overflow) and provides weather protection

**Ventilated improved pit (VIP) latrine:**

The VIP latrine addresses the two primary user complaints about basic pit latrines — odor and flies — through passive ventilation.

VIP design elements:

- A vent pipe: a pipe 100-150mm in diameter extending from the pit to a height at least 500mm above the superstructure roof, with a fly screen at the top
- The vent pipe location: on the sunny side of the latrine — solar heating of the pipe creates an updraft that draws air from the pit interior up through the pipe and out through the fly screen
- Dark interior: the superstructure interior shall be dark — flies that emerge from the pit are attracted to light and fly toward the light-colored vent pipe opening. They exit through the fly screen and cannot return. Flies that enter through the door are also attracted upward toward the vent pipe
- The mechanism: odorous air rises through the vent pipe and exits at the top. Flies emerging from the pit fly toward the light at the top of the vent pipe and are trapped against the fly screen. Fresh air enters through the superstructure door

VIP latrine effectiveness:

- Odor reduction: approximately 80% reduction in perceived odor compared to basic pit latrine
- Fly reduction: significant reduction in flies emerging from the pit — the fly screen traps them before they can exit and contact food
- The VIP latrine requires no additional materials beyond a length of pipe and fly screen — it is an easily achievable upgrade from a basic pit latrine

**Twin-pit latrine:**

The twin-pit latrine solves the problem of what to do when the pit fills — instead of digging a new pit or emptying the old one, it provides a second pit that is used while the first pit rests and the waste decomposes.

Twin-pit design:

- Two pits of equal size, connected to the latrine superstructure by a diverter that directs use to one pit at a time
- When the first pit is nearly full, the diverter is switched to the second pit
- The first pit rests for a minimum of 6-12 months — pathogen die-off during this rest period reduces the pathogen load significantly
- After the rest period, the first pit can be emptied manually — the rested material is significantly safer to handle than fresh waste and can be used as soil amendment
- Minimum rest period: 6 months in warm climates, 12 months in cold climates — temperature affects pathogen die-off rate

**Pit latrine maintenance:**

Ongoing maintenance:

- Keep the slab and defecation area clean — regular washing with water and ash, lime, or other available cleaning agents
- Maintain the cover on the defecation hole when not in use — this is the single most important maintenance action for fly control
- Add a small amount of ash, dry soil, or lime after each use — this reduces odor, absorbs moisture, and creates an inhospitable environment for flies at the pit surface
- Inspect the superstructure regularly for structural damage — a collapsed latrine is not used

When the pit is full:

- Indicators: the waste surface is within 0.5 meters of the slab — the latrine is approaching full
- Options: dig a new pit and move the superstructure (the simplest option where space permits), empty the pit (requires appropriate equipment and disposal method), or convert to a twin-pit system going forward

Pit emptying:

- Mechanical emptying: vacuum tanker trucks — where available and accessible
- Manual emptying: with appropriate personal protective equipment — rubber gloves, rubber boots, aprons, and face protection. Never enter a pit to empty it — methane and hydrogen sulfide produced by anaerobic decomposition create an asphyxiation and explosion hazard in enclosed pits
- Emptied material: shall not be discharged near water sources or in areas where people and animals have access. Burial at minimum 0.5 meter depth in an area with no water source within 30 meters, or treatment per Section 1.9

**Rationale:** The pit cover requirement is the most operationally critical maintenance standard and the one most commonly neglected. A pit latrine without a cover on the defecation hole is a fly breeding facility — the pit provides the warm, moist, organic environment that blowflies require for egg laying and larval development, and the open hole allows adult flies to emerge directly into the superstructure and then into the surrounding environment. A single adult female blowfly can lay 150-200 eggs per batch, and larval development from egg to adult takes 10-14 days at warm temperatures. A latrine without a cover produces thousands of flies per week that carry pathogens from the pit contents to food, food preparation surfaces, and eating areas throughout the community. The cover — a simple lid that fits over the defecation hole — eliminates most fly emergence at essentially zero cost. It is the highest-impact, lowest-cost sanitation improvement available for an existing latrine.

The confined space warning for pit emptying addresses a hazard that kills latrine cleaners globally with tragic regularity. Anaerobic decomposition of human waste produces methane (CH4) and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) — both of which displace oxygen in enclosed spaces. A person entering a pit to clean it breathes these gases instead of air, loses consciousness within seconds, and dies before they can be rescued. Rescuers who enter without proper equipment to save the unconscious person also die — multiple fatalities from a single pit-entry incident are common in the global record. The rule is absolute: never enter a pit. Emptying is accomplished by reaching down from above with buckets, by mechanical pumping, or by waiting for the twin-pit system to provide time for safe emptying of the rested pit.

## 1.5 Septic Systems — Design, Sizing, and Maintenance

**Standard:** Septic systems — underground chambers that receive sewage from flush toilets and separate solids from liquids before distributing the liquid effluent to a drain field — are appropriate for community contexts where water supply is available for flush toilets and where soil conditions allow adequate liquid infiltration. They require more infrastructure than pit latrines but provide better user experience and can serve multiple households from a single system.

**Septic system components:**

**The septic tank:**

- A watertight underground chamber — concrete, fiberglass, or brick — that receives all wastewater from the connected structure
- Function: separates waste into three layers: a floating scum layer of fats, oils, and light solids at the top; a middle layer of clarified liquid (effluent); and a settled sludge layer at the bottom
- Anaerobic bacteria in the tank digest the sludge and scum — reducing the volume of solids over time
- Effluent from the middle layer flows out of the tank to the drain field — the tank retains solids while passing liquid

**Tank sizing:**

- Minimum tank volume: 1,000 liters (approximately 265 gallons) for a single household of up to 5 people
- Add 200 liters per additional person above 5
- Community septic systems serving multiple households: total volume calculated at 200 liters per person per day × 2-day retention time = liters per person of tank volume
- Larger tanks are preferable — a tank that is too small does not provide adequate retention time for solid separation and passes solids to the drain field, which clogs it

**Tank construction:**

- Inlet pipe: enters the tank below the scum layer — typically via a T-fitting that directs incoming waste downward below the scum layer surface, preventing scum disturbance
- Outlet pipe: exits the tank below the scum layer — typically via a T-fitting that draws effluent from the middle liquid zone, preventing scum and sludge from exiting to the drain field
- Access ports: minimum two — one above the inlet for inspection and cleaning, one above the outlet for inspection and cleaning
- Watertightness: the tank shall be completely watertight — leaks allow raw sewage to contaminate the surrounding soil. Test watertightness by filling the tank with water and observing for 24 hours before use

**The drain field (leach field):**

- A network of perforated pipes buried in gravel-filled trenches — the effluent from the septic tank flows through the perforated pipes and infiltrates into the surrounding soil
- Soil treatment: as effluent passes through the soil, pathogens are filtered and die off — the soil is the treatment medium
- Trench dimensions: 0.5-1 meter wide, 0.5-0.75 meter deep, with 0.3 meters of gravel below and around the pipe
- Pipe: perforated pipe (50mm diameter minimum) laid level or with minimal slope in the gravel trench
- Distribution: a distribution box after the septic tank distributes effluent equally to multiple drain field laterals — equal distribution prevents one lateral from being overloaded while others are underused

**Drain field sizing:**

Based on soil percolation rate — the rate at which water infiltrates into the soil

Percolation test: dig a test hole 300mm × 300mm × 300mm in the drain field area. Fill with water and allow to pre-soak for 24 hours. Refill to 150mm depth and measure the time for water level to drop 25mm.

Required drain field area per person based on percolation rate:

- Fast percolation (1 min/inch): 2 square meters per person
- Moderate percolation (5 min/inch): 4 square meters per person
- Slow percolation (30 min/inch): 12 square meters per person
- Very slow percolation (above 60 min/inch): soil is not suitable for a drain field — alternative system required

**Soils unsuitable for conventional drain fields:**

- Heavy clay soils with percolation rate above 60 min/inch — effluent does not infiltrate, pools at the surface
- Saturated soils where the water table is within 0.6 meters of the drain field — effluent contacts groundwater directly without adequate soil treatment
- Fractured rock — effluent travels through fractures without soil treatment
- For these conditions: mound systems (drain field elevated above natural grade in imported fill), constructed wetlands, or composting toilets are alternatives

**Septic system maintenance:**

Pumping schedule:

- Septic tanks require periodic pumping to remove accumulated sludge that the bacterial digestion process cannot fully decompose
- Pumping frequency: every 3-5 years for a properly functioning system at normal loading
- Signs that pumping is overdue: slow drains throughout the building (not just one fixture — which indicates a local clog), sewage odors from the drain field, wet spots or lush vegetation over the drain field, sewage backup into the building
- Pumping shall remove all sludge and scum from the tank — partial pumping leaves material that accelerates sludge re-accumulation

**What not to put in a septic system:**

- Garbage — the solid particles clog the drain field
- Grease and cooking oil — accumulate in the tank and drain field, reducing treatment capacity
- Chemical solvents, paint, and cleaning products — kill the bacteria that digest solids in the tank
- Medications — pass through to the drain field and contaminate groundwater
- "Flushable" wipes and feminine hygiene products — do not break down in the tank and clog the system

**Drain field protection:**

- Do not drive vehicles or heavy equipment over the drain field — compaction reduces soil infiltration capacity
- Do not plant trees or shrubs in the drain field — roots seek moisture and infiltrate and block the perforated pipes
- Do not build over the drain field — access for maintenance must be maintained

**Community septic systems:**

Shared systems:

- A single septic system serving multiple households reduces the total infrastructure required compared to individual systems
- Community septic systems require formal maintenance responsibility assignment — a system with multiple users and no designated maintenance responsibility receives no maintenance
- A community sanitation officer shall be designated with specific responsibility for shared septic system maintenance — inspection, pumping scheduling, and system protection

Grease traps for community kitchens:

- Community kitchens produce large quantities of cooking grease that would overwhelm a standard septic tank
- A grease trap — a small settling chamber before the main septic tank — removes grease from kitchen wastewater before it enters the tank
- Grease traps require monthly cleaning — accumulated grease shall be removed and disposed of per Section 2.16 grease management standards

**Rationale:** The percolation test requirement is included in full procedural detail because it is the foundational soil assessment for drain field design — and a drain field designed without a percolation test is a guess that may fail within years. A drain field in soil with poor infiltration backs up — effluent pools at the surface or backs up into the building — creating a sanitation failure that is more visible and more problematic than the original inadequate sanitation it was intended to replace. The 24-hour pre-soak is the element most commonly skipped in informal percolation testing — it is essential because dry soil absorbs water rapidly regardless of its steady-state infiltration capacity. A pre-soak that saturates the soil produces a percolation rate that reflects actual long-term performance. A percolation test without pre-soak produces an optimistic rate that will not be achieved in practice.

The prohibited substances list for septic systems addresses a common misunderstanding — that a septic system is simply a containment vessel for anything that can be flushed. The bacterial ecosystem in a septic tank that digests solids is a living system that can be killed by the same chemical products that kill bacteria elsewhere. A single significant dose of bleach, paint thinner, or chemical cleaner can kill the tank's bacterial population — without bacteria, solids accumulate without digestion, the tank fills rapidly, and solids pass to the drain field and clog it. The drain field cannot be un-clogged — it must be replaced at significant cost and effort. Protecting the bacterial ecosystem in the tank by controlling what enters it is the most important septic system maintenance practice.

## 1.6 Composting Toilets — Aerobic Decomposition and Safety

**Standard:** Composting toilets decompose human waste through aerobic microbial processes, producing a stabilized end product that is safer to handle and more valuable as a soil amendment than raw waste. They are appropriate for contexts where water is scarce (they require no flush water), where soil conditions are unsuitable for pit latrines or septic systems, and where the community has the management capacity to maintain an aerobic system. They require more active management than pit latrines but produce a more useful and safer end product.

**How composting toilets work:**

**Aerobic decomposition:**

- In the presence of oxygen, aerobic bacteria decompose organic material — including human waste — into carbon dioxide, water, and stabilized organic matter
- Aerobic decomposition is faster and produces less odor than anaerobic decomposition (the process in pit latrines)
- The heat produced by aerobic decomposition — thermophilic composting reaches 55-70°C (130-160°F) — kills pathogens including most bacteria, viruses, and parasite eggs

**The thermophilic kill:**

- Sustained temperature above 55°C (130°F) for 3 days kills essentially all fecal pathogens
- Above 62°C (145°F) for 1 hour achieves equivalent pathogen kill
- This temperature-time relationship is the safety foundation of composting — adequate temperature sustained for adequate time produces a safely treated end product

**Requirements for effective composting:**

**Carbon to nitrogen ratio:**

- Human waste is high in nitrogen — too high for optimal aerobic decomposition. Adding carbon material balances the ratio
- Optimal C:N ratio for composting: 25-30:1
- Carbon additions after each use: sawdust (C:N approximately 400:1), wood ash (primarily mineral, minimal C:N effect), dry leaves (C:N approximately 60:1), straw (C:N approximately 80:1)
- Practical rule: add approximately one cup of dry carbon material (sawdust preferred) after each use

**Moisture:**

- Aerobic decomposition requires moisture — completely dry material does not compost
- Optimal moisture: material should feel like a wrung-out sponge — moist but not wet. Excess moisture excludes oxygen and creates anaerobic conditions
- Urine separation: many composting toilet designs separate urine from feces before the composting chamber. Urine is high in nitrogen and moisture — removing it from the composting chamber improves the C:N ratio and moisture balance dramatically. Separated urine is handled separately per Section 1.9

**Aeration:**

- Oxygen must reach all parts of the composting material — without oxygen, aerobic decomposition ceases and anaerobic decomposition (with odor) begins
- Passive aeration: the composting chamber shall have air inlets at the base and a vent pipe at the top — air flows upward through the material
- Active aeration: periodic turning or stirring of the composting material ensures oxygen reaches all zones — required in systems where passive aeration is insufficient

**Temperature monitoring:**

- The composting material shall reach thermophilic temperatures (55°C minimum) to achieve pathogen kill
- Temperature measurement: a long-stem compost thermometer inserted into the center of the composting material — the center is the last part to reach temperature and the first to fall if conditions are inadequate
- If the center temperature does not reach 55°C: the system is not achieving adequate pathogen kill. Investigate: C:N ratio, moisture, aeration, and loading rate

**Composting toilet types:**

**Batch composting:**

- The composting chamber is filled over a period of weeks to months, then sealed and rested for a composting period (6-12 months minimum) while a second chamber is used
- After the rest period, the first chamber is emptied and the composted material inspected before use
- Advantages: simple management, clear completion of the composting process before use
- Disadvantages: requires two chambers, requires a rest period that limits throughput

**Continuous composting:**

- Fresh material enters at the top of the composting chamber continuously while composted material is removed from the bottom
- The material moves through the chamber over time — from fresh at the top to composted at the bottom
- Advantages: continuous operation without rest periods
- Disadvantages: requires careful management to prevent fresh material from mixing with composted material at the bottom

**End product management:**

Inspection before use:

Composted material from the lower chamber of a batch system after the rest period shall be inspected before use:

- Appearance: dark brown to black, crumbly, no recognizable waste material
- Odor: earthy smell like garden soil — not fecal odor. Any fecal odor indicates incomplete composting
- Temperature: the material should be at ambient temperature — if still warm it is still actively composting and needs more time

If composting is incomplete:

- Do not use material that fails the inspection criteria — return it to the composting process for an additional 3-6 months
- Incomplete composting indicates inadequate conditions — investigate and correct C:N ratio, moisture, or aeration before the next batch

Application:

- Composted human waste can be applied to non-food crops, fruit trees, and ornamental plants without restriction — the composting process has reduced pathogens to safe levels
- Application to food crops: composted material that has achieved thermophilic temperatures for the required time may be applied to food crops following the waiting periods in Section 1.9
- Never apply incompletely composted material to any crop — the visual and odor inspection is not sufficient to guarantee pathogen elimination without confirmed thermophilic temperatures

**Rationale:** The urine separation design feature is included as a standard rather than an option because it is the single most important design improvement for composting toilet function. Urine constitutes approximately 75-80% of the total volume of human excreta but only 10-15% of the organic matter — it is primarily water with dissolved urea. Adding this volume of liquid to the composting chamber creates moisture conditions that suppress aerobic decomposition and produce odors. Urine-diverting designs that route urine separately from feces into a dedicated collection vessel produce dramatically better composting conditions — lower moisture, better C:N ratio, and less odor — with no increase in complexity beyond a diverting insert in the toilet seat. The urine collected separately is nearly sterile (healthy persons' urine contains no fecal pathogens) and is an excellent nitrogen-rich liquid fertilizer diluted 10:1 with water for direct crop application.

The temperature monitoring requirement is included because it is the only reliable indicator that pathogen kill has occurred — the visual and odor indicators that compost appears finished are necessary but not sufficient for safety confirmation. A compost pile that looks and smells finished but never reached thermophilic temperatures has not achieved pathogen kill regardless of its appearance. The pathogens are present but not visible. A long-stem compost thermometer is an inexpensive instrument that provides the only direct measurement of whether the critical safety process — thermophilic heating — has occurred. Community composting toilet systems shall include thermometer monitoring as a routine management practice.

## 1.7 Greywater Management

**Standard:** Greywater — wastewater from sinks, showers, baths, and laundry, excluding toilet waste — requires management distinct from blackwater (toilet waste) because it has different pathogen content, different volume, and different reuse potential. Correctly managed greywater can be reused productively for irrigation and other non-potable uses. Incorrectly managed greywater creates breeding sites for disease vectors, contaminates water sources, and produces odors that indicate ongoing environmental contamination.

**Greywater characteristics:**

**Volume:**

- Greywater constitutes approximately 65-75% of total household wastewater volume — the majority of household water use exits as greywater rather than toilet waste
- Per capita greywater generation: 50-150 liters per day depending on water availability and use patterns

**Pathogen content:**

- Greywater is not sterile — it contains pathogens from body washing, food preparation, and laundry
- Kitchen sink greywater is the most contaminated — it contains food residue, cooking waste, and potentially fecal organisms from washing hands after toilet use and from washing raw meat and vegetables
- Bathroom sink and shower greywater: moderate contamination from skin bacteria, soap, and occasional fecal contamination from hand washing after toilet use
- Laundry greywater: contamination from soiled clothing including potentially fecal contamination from washing underwear and children's clothing
- Greywater pathogen content is significantly lower than blackwater — but it is not safe for consumption or direct contact with food crops

**Greywater source separation:**

Highest pathogen content — requires more careful management:

- Kitchen sink wastewater — food residue, potential raw meat washing
- Laundry wastewater — soiled clothing

Lower pathogen content — simpler management:

- Shower and bath wastewater
- Bathroom sink wastewater (hand washing, tooth brushing)

**Greywater management systems:**

**Direct irrigation — the simplest approach:**

- Greywater from low-pathogen sources (shower, bath, bathroom sink) can be diverted directly to subsurface irrigation — buried or covered channels that deliver greywater to the root zone of plants without surface contact
- Not appropriate for kitchen greywater — food residue and higher pathogen content require more management
- Not appropriate for surface irrigation — greywater on plant surfaces, soil surfaces, or in areas where people walk creates pathogen contact opportunities

**Greywater mulch basins:**

- A shallow basin (0.3 meters deep, 1-2 square meters) filled with wood chips or straw — greywater is distributed into the basin and the mulch filters and biologically treats the wastewater as it infiltrates
- The mulch provides both physical filtration and a microbial environment that degrades organic matter and reduces pathogens
- Mulch basins shall be covered — an open mulch basin is a fly breeding site and produces odors

**Three-chamber greywater system:**

- For kitchen greywater and other higher-contamination greywater: a three-chamber settling and treatment system
- Chamber 1: grease trap — removes floating fats and oils before they clog subsequent components
- Chamber 2: settling chamber — suspended solids settle out
- Chamber 3: biological filter — sand, gravel, and biological media remove remaining pathogens before discharge
- Effluent from a three-chamber system is suitable for subsurface irrigation of fruit trees and ornamental plants

**Constructed wetlands:**

- A gravel-filled bed planted with emergent aquatic plants (reeds, cattails) that treats greywater through biological processes — plant roots provide oxygen to aerobic bacteria in the gravel, which degrade organic matter and reduce pathogens
- Appropriate for community-scale greywater treatment — they are low-energy, low-maintenance, and highly effective for the volumes a small community generates
- Effluent from a well-functioning constructed wetland is suitable for irrigation of any crop using subsurface or drip irrigation

**Prohibited greywater practices:**

- Discharge to surface water — even treated greywater adds nutrient and pathogen load to surface water
- Surface flooding irrigation — greywater on soil surfaces creates pathogen contact opportunities for people and animals
- Discharge near water sources — per the setback standards in Section 1.3
- Storage of greywater for more than 24 hours without treatment — stored greywater develops significantly elevated pathogen loads as bacteria multiply in the warm, nutrient-rich water

**The 24-hour storage limit:**

- Greywater is a warm, nutrient-rich liquid — ideal conditions for bacterial growth
- Bacteria in greywater double approximately every 20-30 minutes at room temperature — a liter of greywater with 1,000 bacteria per milliliter at collection has 1,000,000 per milliliter after 3 hours of storage at room temperature
- Greywater stored more than 24 hours is significantly more contaminated than fresh greywater and shall be treated as blackwater for management purposes

**Rationale:** The kitchen sink greywater higher-risk classification addresses a distinction that is not widely understood — the assumption that because greywater doesn't contain toilet waste it is uniformly lower-risk than blackwater. Kitchen sink water that has been used to wash raw chicken, that contains scraps of raw meat and vegetables, or that comes from handwashing after using the toilet carries pathogen loads approaching those of diluted blackwater. A greywater system that treats all sources equivalently — routing kitchen sink water directly to surface irrigation as it would route shower water — creates a significant food crop contamination pathway. The source-separation approach — treating kitchen greywater with more caution than shower greywater — reflects the actual pathogen content difference rather than treating all greywater as equivalent.

The 24-hour storage limit is derived from the same bacterial growth mathematics used for food safety throughout this document — and it applies with equal force to greywater. A greywater collection tank that is emptied daily maintains moderate pathogen loads in the effluent. A tank that fills over several days and is then used for irrigation has bacterial counts many orders of magnitude higher than fresh greywater — the storage period converts a moderately contaminated waste stream into a highly contaminated one. Community greywater systems that cannot be managed with daily use should use flow-through designs — where greywater moves through the treatment system continuously rather than being stored — to avoid the accumulation problem.

## 1.8 Emergency Sanitation — When No System Exists

**Standard:** Emergency sanitation addresses the period between the onset of a crisis — natural disaster, conflict, displacement, infrastructure failure — and the establishment of permanent sanitation systems. This period is when the risk of fecal-oral disease is highest and when sanitation failures most reliably produce epidemics. Emergency sanitation shall prioritize the immediate cessation of open defecation as the primary life-safety measure.

**The emergency sanitation timeline:**

**Immediate phase (0-72 hours):**

- Focus: eliminate open defecation in the immediate vicinity of the community, establish minimum privacy for defecation
- Approach: designated defecation areas at the correct distance from water sources and dwellings, with basic coverage

**Short-term phase (72 hours — 3 weeks):**

- Focus: provide adequate facilities for all community members, establish handwashing points
- Approach: emergency latrines — trench latrines, drum latrines, or equivalent

**Medium-term phase (3 weeks — 3 months):**

- Focus: improve facility quality and user acceptance, establish maintenance systems
- Approach: improved emergency latrines, sanitation management structure

**Transition to permanent systems (3 months onwards):**

- Focus: replace emergency facilities with permanent systems per Sections 1.4-1.7
- Emergency facilities shall not become permanent — they are designed for temporary use and deteriorate rapidly

**Immediate phase — designated defecation areas:**

When no facilities exist at all, designated defecation areas (DDA) are the minimum first response.

DDA establishment:

- Select an area that meets the siting requirements of Section 1.3 — correct distance from water sources, food areas, and dwellings
- Mark the area clearly — physical boundaries (rope, stakes, vegetation) and verbal communication to all community members
- The DDA shall be downwind of the community and away from footpaths and public areas
- Designate separate male and female areas — consistent use is higher when privacy is provided

DDA management:

- Each use shall be covered with a layer of soil — community members carry a small tool (stick, trowel) for this purpose. Covering waste immediately prevents fly access and reduces odor
- A supply of covering material (dry soil, ash, or sand) shall be maintained at the DDA
- The DDA shall be moved when it becomes saturated — typically every 3-7 days depending on community size

**Trench latrines:**

A trench latrine is a long, narrow excavation used as a communal latrine — it is the standard emergency sanitation facility for large numbers of people.

Trench dimensions:

- Width: 30 cm — narrow enough to squat over
- Depth: 1.5-2 meters
- Length: 1 meter per 6-8 people for a facility used during daylight hours. 1 meter per 4-5 people for overnight use
- Example: a community of 60 people requires a trench approximately 10 meters long

Trench siting:

- All siting requirements of Section 1.3 apply — particularly the water source setback
- Trench orientation: perpendicular to prevailing wind — reduces odor impact on the community

Trench management:

- Soil from excavation placed alongside the trench — users cover each deposit with a thin layer of excavated soil
- Cover the trench with timber planks or equivalent when not in use — reduces fly access and accident risk
- Establish designated handwashing point adjacent to the trench — water and soap or ash for handwashing after use
- Abandon the trench when it is within 0.5 meters of being full — fill completely and compact the cover. Mark the location to prevent future excavation in that area

**Drum or container latrines:**

Where excavation is not possible (hard rock, high water table, temporary flooring), container-based sanitation provides emergency coverage.

Container latrine design:

- A container (drum, barrel, or purpose-built container) with a toilet seat or squat plate on top — waste is collected in the container
- Container capacity: approximately 200 liters provides approximately 1 week of use for a family of 5
- Containers shall be sealed — a tight-fitting lid prevents fly access and odor escape
- Containers shall be emptied regularly — before they become full — into a designated disposal area meeting siting requirements

**Handwashing infrastructure:**

A latrine without handwashing provision is significantly less effective at interrupting the fecal-oral pathway than a latrine with handwashing — the hands contaminated during defecation carry pathogens directly to food.

Minimum handwashing provision:

- Water supply adjacent to every latrine — a container of clean water with a tap or pour spout
- Soap or ash — ash is an effective handwashing agent in the absence of soap, reducing bacterial counts on hands comparably to soap in field conditions
- The handwashing station shall be directly at the latrine exit — not nearby, not in a separate location. The handwashing must be the first thing encountered when leaving the latrine

**Minimum per capita requirements for emergency sanitation:**

These are the Sphere Standards — the internationally established minimum standards for humanitarian response to sanitation crises:

- Maximum 20 people per toilet or latrine — at higher ratios, facilities are not adequately maintained and use decreases
- Maximum 50 meters from any dwelling to the nearest facility
- Separate facilities for men and women where cultural norms require
- At least 1 liter of water per person per day for handwashing at sanitation facilities

**Rationale:** The covered-immediately standard — covering each deposit immediately with soil — is the most important management practice for designated defecation areas and trench latrines and the one most reliably omitted when it is not explicitly established as a standard. An uncovered deposit in a DDA or trench latrine is immediately accessible to flies — a blowfly detects the deposit within minutes and begins oviposition. A covered deposit is not accessible to flies — the soil layer provides both a physical barrier and a hostile environment for oviposition. The tool requirement — each community member carries a small digging or covering tool — converts an abstract standard into a specific behavioral expectation. Communities that have implemented this practice consistently report significantly lower fly nuisance and improved acceptance of the DDA system compared to those that did not cover deposits.

The Sphere Standards minimum distances and ratios are included because they represent the accumulated learning from decades of humanitarian response experience — they are the thresholds below which sanitation facilities reliably fail from overuse and under-maintenance. A latrine serving 50 people rather than the 20-person maximum receives 2.5 times the use its maintenance is designed for — it fills faster, is less clean, produces more odor, and is used less consistently by community members who find it unacceptably unhygienic. The 20-person maximum is not arbitrary — it is the ratio at which volunteer maintenance by the served households remains manageable and at which facility condition remains acceptable to users.

## 1.9 Agricultural Reuse — Nutrient Value and Safety

**Standard:** Human waste is a valuable agricultural resource — it contains nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and organic matter that are the primary components of commercial fertilizer. Safe reuse of treated human waste in agriculture reduces the need for external fertilizer inputs and closes the nutrient cycle. Unsafe reuse — applying untreated or inadequately treated waste to food crops — contaminates those crops with fecal pathogens and is a direct cause of foodborne disease. The standards below establish the conditions under which reuse is safe and the waiting periods that reduce pathogen loads to acceptable levels.

**The nutrient content of human waste:**

**Urine:**

- Contains approximately 80-90% of the nitrogen and 50% of the phosphorus excreted by humans
- Nearly sterile from healthy individuals — carries no fecal pathogens
- Can be applied directly to crops when diluted 1:10 with water — undiluted urine is too concentrated and can damage plants
- Application timing: urine shall be applied to the soil or crop base at least 1 month before harvest — not to the edible portion of the plant directly

**Feces:**

- Contains approximately 10-20% of the nitrogen and 50% of the phosphorus excreted
- Contains the majority of the organic matter
- Contains significant quantities of fecal pathogens — requires treatment before safe reuse
- After adequate treatment (composting, anaerobic digestion, or extended storage), feces becomes a valuable soil amendment equivalent to commercial compost

**Treatment requirements before agricultural reuse:**

**Composting:**

- Thermophilic composting that achieves 55°C throughout the material for minimum 3 days, or 50°C for minimum 7 days
- Vermicomposting (worm composting) does not reliably achieve thermophilic temperatures — vermicomposted human waste shall be treated as partially treated for reuse purposes

**Anaerobic digestion:**

- Biogas digesters that maintain hydraulic retention time of minimum 30 days at temperatures above 20°C
- The digested slurry (digestate) is significantly lower in pathogen content than raw waste but is not sterile — apply with the waiting periods below

**Extended storage (passive treatment):**

- Pit latrine contents stored for minimum 1 year in warm climates, 2 years in cold climates — pathogen die-off over time reduces concentration to levels acceptable for restricted reuse
- Requires no energy or management beyond keeping the storage sealed and dry

**WHO waiting periods for safe reuse:**

These waiting periods are applied between the last application of waste to a field and the harvest — they provide time for pathogen die-off in the soil environment before people contact the crop.

**Treated waste (thermophilically composted or digested):**

- Root crops where the edible portion contacts the soil (carrots, potatoes, beets): 1 month
- Crops where the edible portion does not contact the soil (corn, tomatoes, beans): no waiting period required for thermophilically treated waste
- Leafy vegetables eaten raw: 1 month

**Partially treated waste (digested, extended storage):**

- Root crops: 3 months
- Above-ground crops eaten raw: 3 months
- Above-ground crops cooked before eating: 1 month

**Untreated waste:**

- Shall not be applied to any food crop
- May be applied to non-food crops (timber, fiber, ornamental plants) with appropriate handling precautions

**Application methods:**

**Subsurface application:**

- Incorporating waste into the soil before planting — tilling the material into the top 15-20 cm of soil
- Reduces surface contact with crops and people
- Most appropriate for composted material applied before crop establishment

**Drip or subsurface irrigation with liquid waste:**

- Liquid digestate or diluted urine applied through buried drip irrigation systems
- No surface contact with crops or soil surface — the most protective application method
- Appropriate for continuous application during the growing season for the safe waste sources above

**Prohibited application methods:**

- Overhead or sprinkler irrigation with any waste — droplets contact the edible portions of crops and people working in the field
- Surface application to growing leafy vegetables — direct contact between waste and edible leaf surface
- Application to standing water or drainage channels — creates surface water contamination

**Urine-diverting systems and direct reuse:**

Urine from urine-diverting toilets:

- Collected urine from healthy individuals is essentially sterile — it can be applied directly to crops more liberally than feces-containing waste
- Dilution: 1 part urine to 10 parts water for application to growing crops. Undiluted urine may be applied to bare soil before planting
- Storage before application: urine stored for 1 month in a sealed container undergoes additional pathogen reduction from the ammonia produced by urea hydrolysis — stored urine is safer than fresh urine for crop application
- Application to leafy vegetables eaten raw: 1 month before harvest minimum. Application to crops cooked before eating: no specific waiting period

**Rationale:** The urine nutrient content data is presented specifically because it reframes urine from a waste product to a resource — a reframing that changes the management priorities of sanitation systems. A community that understands that urine contains 80-90% of the nitrogen it needs to return to agricultural production designs its sanitation systems to capture and use urine rather than to dispose of it. Urine-diverting toilet designs that separate urine from feces before the composting chamber are not just better for composting — they are recovering the majority of the agricultural value of human excreta in a form that requires minimal treatment before use. The nitrogen in a community's urine, applied to its food production, can significantly reduce or eliminate external fertilizer requirements — closing a nutrient cycle that industrial agriculture has broken.

The WHO waiting periods are included as the primary reuse safety standard because they represent the internationally established evidence-based consensus on the time required for pathogen die-off in soil under real-world conditions. They are conservative — they include safety margins that account for variability in environmental conditions, application rates, and pathogen concentrations. A community that applies these waiting periods consistently to agricultural reuse of treated waste achieves pathogen reduction to levels that do not produce measurable disease risk in the populations consuming the crops. The waiting periods are the bridge between the agricultural value of human waste and its safe application to food production — and they require only time, not infrastructure or energy.

## 1.10 Vector Control in Sanitation Systems

**Standard:** Sanitation systems that are not designed and maintained to exclude vectors — flies, mosquitoes, and rodents — amplify rather than contain disease transmission. A pit latrine that breeds flies and delivers them to food preparation areas has not improved on open defecation — it has concentrated the pathogen source and provided a fly factory at its center. Vector control is not an optional enhancement to sanitation systems — it is an integral component of their public health function.

**Flies — the primary sanitation vector:**

**The fly transmission mechanism:**

- House flies and blowflies breed in human waste — they lay eggs in fresh feces and develop from egg to adult in 10-14 days at warm temperatures
- Adult flies feed on both feces and food — they carry pathogens on their body surfaces, in their gut, and in their regurgitate and feces deposited during feeding
- A single fly can carry millions of pathogenic bacteria on its body and can transfer these to food within minutes of leaving a latrine
- Fly populations in communities with inadequate sanitation are dramatically higher than in communities with adequate containment — the sanitation system is the primary fly breeding habitat

**Fly control in latrines:**

**Covering the defecation hole:**

- The most important fly control measure — a cover on the defecation hole when not in use prevents flies from entering the pit and prevents flies emerging from the pit from accessing the superstructure and the surrounding environment
- Cover materials: a simple lid that fits over the hole, a hinged cover, or a self-closing flap. The cover must be used consistently — a cover that is present but not used provides no protection

**VIP latrine fly trapping:**

- The VIP latrine fly trap mechanism (Section 1.4) captures flies emerging from the pit before they can exit — a significant reduction in fly population from a latrine that is otherwise identical to a basic pit latrine

**Keeping the pit dry:**

- Liquid in the pit — urine, water added to the pit, rainwater — increases fly breeding success. Urine-diverting designs and keeping rainwater out of the pit reduce moisture and fly breeding

**Pit surface treatment:**

- A thin layer of ash, lime, or dry soil on the pit surface after each use creates an inhospitable environment for fly oviposition — flies prefer moist, warm surfaces for egg laying

**Fly screens on ventilation openings:**

- All ventilation openings in latrine superstructures shall be screened with fine mesh — 1.5 mm mesh or finer — that excludes flies while maintaining airflow
- Fly screens require regular inspection and repair — any opening large enough for a fly to enter defeats the screening purpose

**Mosquito control in sanitation systems:**

**Mosquito breeding in sanitation systems:**

- Mosquitoes breed in standing water — open-topped latrines, septic system inspection ports, greywater collection containers, and poorly drained areas around sanitation facilities
- Anopheles mosquitoes (malaria vectors) breed in clean water — they are not associated with latrine breeding. Culex mosquitoes (arbovirus vectors including West Nile and others) breed in polluted water including latrine overflow and greywater pools
- Septic system inspection ports and ventilation openings that allow water accumulation are significant mosquito breeding sites

**Mosquito prevention in sanitation systems:**

- All openings in septic tanks, inspection ports, and storage chambers shall be sealed or screened
- No standing water shall accumulate in or around sanitation facilities — drainage shall be maintained
- Greywater storage containers shall be sealed — open greywater containers are mosquito breeding sites
- Any container of water associated with sanitation systems shall have a tight-fitting cover or shall be emptied and dried when not in active use

**Rodent control:**

**Rodent attraction to sanitation systems:**

- Rodents — rats and mice — are attracted to food odors in waste streams and to the warmth and shelter of latrine superstructures
- Rodents burrowing into pit latrines compromise the pit wall integrity and can create pathways for liquid waste to escape the pit
- Rodents in community spaces spread pathogens from latrines to food storage and preparation areas through their feces, urine, and body contamination

**Rodent exclusion from latrines:**

- Concrete or masonry pit walls prevent burrowing — earthen pit walls are vulnerable to rodent burrowing
- The latrine superstructure shall have no gaps large enough for rodents to enter — a mouse can pass through a 6mm gap (the diameter of a pencil). All openings shall be sealed or screened with hardware cloth (6mm or finer mesh)
- No food shall be stored in or near latrine superstructures — food in the latrine area attracts rodents

**Monitoring for vector activity:**

- Latrines shall be inspected weekly for signs of fly breeding (maggots in or around the pit opening), mosquito breeding (standing water), and rodent activity (droppings, burrow entrances, gnaw marks)
- Evidence of vector activity shall trigger immediate corrective action — the vector control measures above applied to the specific vector identified

**Rationale:** The fly as a disease amplifier framing is included because it reframes the relationship between sanitation systems and fly populations in a way that conventional public health communication often misses. Flies are not simply a nuisance associated with inadequate sanitation — they are active disease transmission agents that convert concentrated pathogen sources (latrines) into dispersed pathogen loads (food, food preparation surfaces, eating areas) throughout the community. A latrine without fly control is producing something closer to the opposite of the intended effect — it is concentrating pathogen sources and providing the warm, moist, protected environment that maximizes fly reproduction, then releasing these flies into the community's food environment. The cover-the-hole standard and the VIP fly trap are not comfort measures — they are pathogen transmission control measures.

The mosquito breeding prevention in septic tanks addresses a hazard that is essentially invisible to septic system operators — the open inspection port or unsealed ventilation pipe that allows mosquitoes to breed in the anaerobic environment of the tank. Septic tanks maintain temperatures and moisture conditions favorable for mosquito breeding year-round in tropical and subtropical climates. An unsealed inspection port over a septic tank is a continuous mosquito production point that bypasses all other vector control measures in the community. The fix — a tight-fitting cover or screen on every opening — is trivial. Its absence in many installed septic systems is a consequence of its invisibility rather than its difficulty.

## 1.11 Community Sanitation Management

**Standard:** Sanitation systems that are not actively managed deteriorate, become unusable, and revert the community to effective open defecation regardless of the quality of their original construction. Sanitation management — the ongoing human activity that keeps systems functional and used — is as important as sanitation infrastructure. This standard establishes the management functions, roles, and accountability systems required for sustained sanitation performance.

**The community sanitation officer:**

Role and responsibilities:

- The community sanitation officer is responsible for: maintaining documentation of all community sanitation systems, conducting regular system inspections, coordinating maintenance activities, monitoring vector activity, managing sanitation emergencies, and educating community members on sanitation standards
- The sanitation officer shall have working knowledge of all the standards in this document — not engineering expertise but operational understanding sufficient to identify problems, implement routine maintenance, and recognize when technical assistance is needed
- A backup sanitation officer shall be designated and trained — sanitation emergencies do not wait for the primary officer to be available

Sanitation system inventory:

- A complete inventory of all sanitation systems in the community shall be maintained — location, type, construction date, capacity, maintenance history, and current condition
- The inventory shall be updated when any system is constructed, modified, or decommissioned
- The inventory enables the sanitation officer to plan maintenance, track system age and condition, and identify systems approaching end of useful life before they fail

**Inspection schedule:**

**Weekly inspections:**

- All community latrines and shared sanitation facilities — cleanliness, cover condition, vector activity, structural integrity
- Greywater systems — drainage function, standing water, odors
- Handwashing stations — water supply, soap or ash availability

**Monthly inspections:**

- Individual household systems — pit fill level, cover condition, structural integrity
- Septic systems — odors, wet spots over drain field, backup signs
- Composting toilets — temperature, moisture, cover material supply

**Annual inspections:**

- Pit latrine fill level assessment — estimate remaining capacity and project when the pit will require replacement or emptying
- Septic tank inspection — assess sludge and scum accumulation, schedule pumping if approaching limits
- System siting review — has community development changed the relationship between sanitation systems and water sources, food production areas, or dwellings? Have new water sources been established within setback distances of existing systems?

**Community behavior — the human factor:**

Sanitation systems fail most often not because of technical problems but because of behavioral ones — facilities that are not used as intended, not maintained, and not kept clean. Behavioral sanitation is as important as technical sanitation.

The key behaviors:

- Using designated facilities rather than open defecation — the foundational behavior
- Handwashing with soap or ash after defecation and before food preparation — the primary disease interruption behavior
- Covering the defecation hole after use — the primary fly control behavior
- Adding cover material (ash, soil, sawdust) after use in pit latrines and composting toilets
- Keeping the facility clean — sweeping, washing the slab
- Reporting problems — broken covers, full pits, structural damage — to the sanitation officer

**Behavior change approaches:**

- Community-Led Total Sanitation (CLTS): a facilitation approach that helps communities collectively recognize the public health consequences of open defecation and motivate community-wide behavior change — effective because it addresses social norms rather than individual behavior
- Social pressure and norms: open defecation is normalized in communities where it is universal and stigmatized in communities where sanitation coverage is high. Building a community norm that associates latrine use with dignity and open defecation with shame is more sustainable than any technical or incentive-based approach
- Child-focused behavior change: children are disproportionately affected by fecal-oral disease and are also effective behavior change agents — they carry messages home from school-based programs and influence adult behavior through what they have learned

**Menstrual hygiene management:**

Menstrual hygiene management (MHM) is a component of sanitation that is frequently neglected in community planning and that has significant implications for women's and girls' dignity, health, and participation in community life.

MHM infrastructure requirements:

- Private facilities where menstrual materials can be changed — the latrine superstructure provides this if it has an adequate door and latch
- Water for washing — both the person and reusable menstrual materials
- A disposal method for used materials — incineration, burial, or sealed disposal container
- In the absence of commercial menstrual products: reusable cloth pads are an appropriate alternative. These require washing, drying, and storage facilities that maintain privacy

MHM and sanitation system design:

- Disposal of menstrual materials into pit latrines is acceptable — they are organic material that decomposes in the pit. Synthetic materials (commercial pads with plastic backings) should not be disposed of in latrines as they do not decompose and accelerate pit filling
- Composting toilet systems require specific guidance on menstrual material disposal — manufacturers' instructions vary. Generally, organic materials are acceptable; synthetic materials are not

**Vulnerable populations:**

**Children:**

- Children's feces carry the highest pathogen loads in communities — they are more likely to be infected with intestinal pathogens and to shed at high concentrations
- Child defecation is frequently not managed through the latrine system — children defecate in the open or near dwellings and caregivers dispose of the feces in the open
- A sanitation system that addresses adult defecation but not child defecation fails to control the highest-concentration pathogen source in the community
- Safe child feces disposal: child feces shall be disposed of in the latrine immediately after defecation. Potty training equipment shall be emptied into the latrine, not in the open environment

**Elderly and disabled community members:**

- Latrine superstructures shall be accessible to elderly and disabled community members — grab rails, adequate door width, stable approach paths
- Where community members cannot reach the latrine independently: provision of portable chamber pots that are regularly emptied into the latrine is an acceptable accommodation
- The 50-meter maximum distance standard is particularly important for elderly and disabled community members who cannot travel farther

**Rationale:** The CLTS approach is mentioned specifically because it represents a fundamental shift in how sanitation programs achieve behavior change — from top-down provision and incentive of facilities to community-driven recognition of the problem and collective action to solve it. CLTS programs in Bangladesh, India, Indonesia, and across Sub-Saharan Africa have achieved community-wide open defecation free status more durably and at lower cost than subsidy-based programs because they address the social norm around open defecation rather than the individual's economic constraint in accessing a facility. A Resolute Society community implementing sanitation standards has an advantage that externally implemented programs lack — the community is already organized around collective purpose and shared standards, creating the social norm environment that makes CLTS effective.

Child feces management is included as a specific standard because it addresses the failure mode most consistently documented in communities with otherwise good sanitation coverage — the 15-30% of total fecal pathogen load produced by children under 5 that is disposed of in the open because "it's just a child." Child feces contains the same pathogens as adult feces — often at higher concentrations because children are more frequently infected. A community with 95% adult latrine use and open disposal of child feces still has significant pathogen contamination of the environment from the child feces. Safe child feces disposal is not a marginal sanitation improvement — it addresses the highest-concentration pathogen source in the community.

## 1.12 Disease Outbreak Response Through Sanitation

**Standard:** When a fecal-oral disease outbreak occurs in the community — cholera, typhoid, dysentery, hepatitis A, or other waterborne or foodborne illness — sanitation system response is the primary public health intervention. Medical treatment of individual cases does not stop an outbreak. Interrupting the transmission pathway through sanitation improvements, handwashing, and water treatment does. This standard establishes the sanitation component of disease outbreak response.

**Recognizing a sanitation-related outbreak:**

Indicators:

- Two or more cases of acute diarrheal illness with onset within 72 hours with plausible common exposure — per the outbreak declaration criteria in Section 2.17
- Cases concentrated in a geographic area sharing a common water source or sanitation facility
- Cases concentrated in households sharing a common food source
- The specific pathogen pattern indicating fecal-oral transmission:
  - Rapid onset (1-24 hours) with predominantly vomiting: S. aureus or B. cereus toxin — food safety response primary
  - 1-3 day onset with watery diarrhea: norovirus or E. coli — both food and water transmission possible
  - 1-5 day onset with watery to bloody diarrhea and fever: Salmonella, Campylobacter, Shigella — fecal-oral transmission confirmed
  - 12-72 hour onset with profuse watery diarrhea: cholera — water transmission most likely, emergency response required

**Cholera — the emergency response:**

Cholera is the fecal-oral disease with the highest potential for rapid community-scale mortality and the clearest transmission pathway — contaminated water. It requires immediate, specific response.

Cholera response priorities:

- Immediate suspension of all water from the suspected contaminated source
- Immediate boiling or chemical treatment of all drinking water — universal, not selective
- Establishment of oral rehydration therapy (ORT) for all cases — cholera kills through dehydration, not the infection itself. ORT replaces the fluid lost through diarrhea
- Immediate sanitation inspection — identify the contamination source and isolate it

Case management:

- Isolate cases from the general community — not for infection control of the case (cholera is fecal-oral, not airborne) but to manage the additional sanitation load from high-volume diarrhea
- Provide ORT continuously — a cholera patient can lose 1 liter or more of fluid per hour. ORT at this rate prevents death
- Disinfect all fecal material from cases — lime, bleach solution (1% chlorine), or burial of liquid fecal waste

**Sanitation response to any fecal-oral outbreak:**

Immediate actions:

- Identify all sanitation failures in the area where cases are concentrated — open defecation, uncovered latrines, overflowing systems, systems within setback distance of water sources
- Correct identified failures immediately — cover open pits, repair broken covers, suspend water from contaminated sources
- Increase handwashing promotion and provision — establish additional handwashing stations, ensure soap or ash availability at all sanitation facilities

**Fecal waste management from cases:**

- Cases produce highly concentrated pathogen loads — their fecal waste is orders of magnitude more infectious than background community waste
- Case fecal waste shall be disposed of in a dedicated facility or disinfected before disposal — lime powder applied directly to fecal waste before disposal, or chlorine solution (1% minimum) applied to liquid waste
- Bedding and clothing soiled with case fecal waste shall be washed in hot water with soap and dried in sunlight — or disinfected with chlorine solution

**Water source protection:**

- All water sources within the affected area shall be assessed for contamination — per the testing and assessment standards in the water sourcing regulation
- Water sources showing evidence of fecal contamination shall be suspended from use until treatment can be established or the contamination source removed
- Emergency water distribution from verified safe sources shall be established for the affected population

**Fly control intensification:**

- During an outbreak, flies are active vectors carrying high-concentration pathogens from case fecal waste to the food environment
- Intensify fly control measures at all latrines in the affected area — covers, screening, surface treatment
- Food protection from fly contact during the outbreak period — covered food storage, covered cooking, covered serving

**Documentation and learning:**

- Document the outbreak: cases, onset times, locations, food and water consumption, sanitation facility used
- Map the cases — geographic clustering indicates the likely transmission source
- Identify the sanitation failure that enabled the outbreak — the investigation methodology in Section 9.2 applies
- Implement permanent corrections to the identified failures — the outbreak is the evidence of a specific sanitation gap that permanent correction will close

**Rationale:** The cholera-specific response is included with specific detail because cholera is the single most dangerous sanitation-related disease in terms of outbreak speed and mortality potential. A community without any prior cholera experience may not recognize its distinctive presentation — profuse rice-water diarrhea without blood, rapid dehydration, and the characteristic "washerwoman's hands" from severe dehydration — as distinct from other diarrheal illnesses. The 1 liter per hour fluid loss rate is specific and shocking — a person without ORT in a cholera outbreak can die within hours of symptom onset. This urgency requires pre-established response capacity: ORT formula known and ingredients available, water treatment capacity immediately deployable, and sanitation officers with authority to suspend water sources and require immediate sanitation improvements. The response to cholera cannot be designed in the middle of an outbreak — it must be pre-planned.

The fecal waste disinfection standard for case management addresses the most dangerous sanitation period in any fecal-oral outbreak — the period when the community is managing sick people whose fecal waste is orders of magnitude more infectious than the community baseline. A community that handles case fecal waste without disinfection, or that disposes of it in ways that create environmental contamination, is amplifying the outbreak through its care response. The specific disinfection agents — lime and chlorine — are included because they are achievable in degraded infrastructure contexts: lime is available wherever masonry is used, and chlorine bleach is widely available. The disinfection step in case management is not medical treatment — it is sanitation practice that prevents the caregiver and the surrounding community from becoming the next cases.
