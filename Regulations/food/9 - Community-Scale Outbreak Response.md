9.1 Outbreak Declaration and Initial Response
Standard: A community-scale foodborne illness outbreak shall be declared when any of the following thresholds are met, and the response protocol activated immediately upon declaration. The declaration threshold is intentionally conservative — it is calibrated to catch outbreaks early when intervention is most effective, accepting the cost of occasional false alarms to avoid the cost of delayed response to genuine outbreaks.
Declaration thresholds:
Immediate declaration — single case triggers:

Any confirmed or clinically suspected case of botulism — descending paralysis, difficulty swallowing, double vision following food exposure
Any confirmed case of E. coli O157:H7 or other Shiga toxin-producing E. coli in a community member
Any confirmed case of cholera — rice-water diarrhea, rapid dehydration
Any confirmed case of typhoid fever — sustained fever, abdominal pain, relative bradycardia
Any confirmed case of Hepatitis A where food exposure is possible
Any confirmed case of brucellosis where raw dairy consumption is possible
Any confirmed case of botulism in a family member or close contact of a community member who shared the same food

Cluster declaration — two or more cases trigger:

Two or more community members experiencing similar gastrointestinal illness — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps — within a 72-hour period with a plausible common food exposure
Two or more community members presenting with identical symptom profiles within 72 hours regardless of identified common food exposure — the common exposure may exist but not yet be identified
Any pattern of illness following a specific community food event — meal service, distribution, harvest celebration, or community gathering involving food

Immediate actions upon declaration — within 2 hours:
Notification:

Community leadership notified immediately — the outbreak declaration is a community event requiring leadership awareness and resource authorization, not a technical matter handled solely by the food safety officer
Community medical officer notified — medical response coordination begins immediately upon declaration, not after investigation identifies the source
All community members who may have consumed the suspected food notified — per the notification standards below

Suspension:

All food service from the suspected source suspended immediately — if the source is unknown, food service from all community kitchen operations suspended pending initial investigation
All distribution of suspected food products suspended — this includes stored food, fermented products, dairy products, or any other food category where the suspected source may have originated
Suspected food preserved — do not discard any food that may be the source. Quarantine and label for investigation

Documentation initiation:

Begin case log immediately — every ill community member documented with name, symptoms, onset time, severity, and food consumption history for the preceding 72 hours
Begin food exposure log — document all food service events, distributions, and community meals in the 72 hours preceding the first case

Rationale: The 2-hour response window for initial actions mirrors the food service outbreak response in Section 2.17 and reflects the same asymmetry of consequences — the cost of acting quickly on a false alarm is inconvenience, the cost of delayed action on a genuine outbreak is additional community members exposed during the delay. At community scale the stakes are higher than in food service — the population at risk is the entire community, the food sources are more varied, and the investigation is more complex. These factors make rapid initial response more important, not less.
The single-case declaration thresholds for specific pathogens reflect their severity and transmission characteristics. Botulism, E. coli O157:H7, cholera, and typhoid are not common illnesses — a single confirmed case in a community almost certainly represents either a community food source contamination or an imported case that requires immediate investigation to prevent community spread. Treating these as routine individual illnesses until a cluster develops allows dangerous delays. A single botulism case is a community emergency regardless of whether additional cases are identified.
The food preservation requirement — preserve suspected food, do not discard — runs counter to the intuition of removing a hazard. Preserved suspected food is the primary evidence for identifying the contamination source, the specific pathogen, and the failure point that allowed the outbreak. A community that discards the suspected food source at the first sign of an outbreak cannot investigate the outbreak's cause, cannot target the response, and cannot prevent recurrence. The evidence shall be preserved even at the cost of leaving a potentially contaminated food item in existence — properly quarantined and labeled.
In 1993 the Jack in the Box E. coli O157:H7 outbreak — referenced throughout this document — was initially treated as isolated cases of gastrointestinal illness at individual restaurants. The connection between cases was not identified for days because the reporting and response system was not designed for rapid pattern recognition. During the delay, additional contaminated product continued to be served. By the time the outbreak was identified as a cluster and the source identified, 732 people had been sickened. The outbreak surveillance systems developed after 1993 — FoodNet, PulseNet — were specifically designed to identify clusters earlier by connecting individual cases to common exposures rapidly. The community outbreak declaration threshold in this standard replicates that early-detection function at community scale.

9.2 Case Identification and Epidemiological Investigation
Standard: The epidemiological investigation — systematic collection of illness and food exposure information from ill and well community members — is the primary tool for identifying the food source responsible for an outbreak. It requires no laboratory equipment, can begin immediately upon outbreak declaration, and when conducted correctly identifies the implicated food with high reliability. Every community member with public health or food safety responsibility shall understand its basic methodology.
Case definition:
Before beginning data collection, establish a case definition:

A case definition specifies the criteria a community member must meet to be counted as a case — typically a combination of symptoms, symptom onset time window, and community membership
Example case definition for a gastroenteritis outbreak: any community member experiencing two or more of the following symptoms — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps — with onset between [date/time X] and [date/time Y]
The case definition shall be specific enough to distinguish outbreak cases from background illness but not so specific that it excludes cases with atypical presentations
The case definition is established at the start of the investigation and applied consistently — changing the case definition mid-investigation distorts the data

Case finding:
Active case finding:

Do not wait for cases to self-report — actively survey the community for illness
Distribute a brief illness questionnaire to all community members present at the suspected food event or who received the suspected food
The questionnaire shall collect: name, contact information, symptoms experienced, onset date and time, whether the person ate at the suspected event or consumed the suspected food, and what specific foods they consumed

Passive case finding:

Inform community members of the outbreak investigation and ask them to report to the outbreak investigation team if they or household members experience relevant symptoms
Establish a clear reporting mechanism — a specific person to contact, a location to report to, or a communication channel dedicated to outbreak reports

Case interviews:
Conduct a detailed interview with each case:

Full symptom history: which symptoms, severity, onset time, duration, whether symptoms are resolving or progressing
Medical treatment: whether the person sought medical care, what diagnosis if any was made, what treatment was provided
Complete food history for 72 hours before symptom onset — not just the suspected food event but all foods consumed, all meals, all beverages
Other potential exposures: travel, contact with ill individuals outside the community, contact with animals

Well person interviews:

Interview community members who attended the suspected food event or consumed the suspected food but did not become ill — these well persons are as important to the investigation as cases
The comparison between foods consumed by ill persons and foods consumed by well persons identifies the implicated food — the food present in the ill group and absent or less common in the well group

Attack rate calculation:
For each food item served at a suspected event:

Calculate the attack rate among people who ate that food: number ill who ate the food ÷ total number who ate the food × 100
Calculate the attack rate among people who did not eat that food: number ill who did not eat the food ÷ total number who did not eat the food × 100
The implicated food has a high attack rate among those who ate it and a low attack rate among those who did not

Short incubation (1-6 hours) with predominantly vomiting suggests S. aureus toxin or B. cereus
Longer incubation (6-24 hours) with predominantly diarrhea suggests Salmonella, C. perfringens, or B. cereus diarrheal type
Very short incubation (minutes to 1 hour) with burning sensation suggests chemical contamination rather than biological

Laboratory investigation — where capacity is available:
Stool specimens:

Collect stool specimens from at minimum 5 cases where laboratory testing is available — early in the illness when pathogen shedding is highest
Stool specimens shall be collected in clean containers, refrigerated immediately, and transported to laboratory capacity within 24 hours
Request testing for the pathogens suggested by the clinical picture — a general screen for enteric pathogens rather than a single targeted test where the pathogen is unknown

Food specimens:

Collect samples from all preserved suspected foods — 100-gram samples where possible, stored at 40°F for bacterial pathogens, 0°F for suspected viral pathogens
Document the chain of custody for all specimens — when collected, by whom, storage conditions

Environmental specimens:

Surface swabs from food contact surfaces, equipment, and the hands of food handlers may identify the contamination source or confirm cross-contamination pathways

Rationale: The attack rate methodology is the foundational technique of outbreak epidemiology and is presented in full detail here because it is executable by community members without epidemiological training using nothing more than paper, pencil, and systematic data collection. John Snow applied this logic to the Broad Street cholera outbreak in 1854 — comparing cholera rates between people served by different water pumps. The methodology has not changed in 170 years because it is direct, reliable, and requires no laboratory infrastructure. A community that can collect food histories from ill and well persons and calculate attack rates for each food item can identify the implicated food with high confidence in most outbreak settings.
The incubation period table is included as a reference tool for differential diagnosis that guides both the investigation and the medical response. Knowing that the median incubation period is 2 hours and the predominant symptom is vomiting directs the investigation toward S. aureus toxin — which means looking for a food that was temperature-abused or handled by a person with a skin infection. Knowing that the incubation period is 3 days and the predominant symptom is bloody diarrhea directs the investigation toward E. coli O157:H7 — which means looking for undercooked ground meat or contaminated produce. The incubation period analysis converts clinical presentation into an actionable investigative direction without laboratory confirmation.
The well person interview requirement is the element of outbreak investigation most frequently omitted by inexperienced investigators — the focus naturally falls on cases, not on people who did not become ill. But the epidemiological power of the investigation comes from the comparison between cases and non-cases. An attack rate calculation that includes only cases cannot be performed — the denominator requires knowing how many people ate each food and did not become ill. A community that interviews only ill members cannot calculate attack rates and cannot reliably identify the implicated food through epidemiology alone. Well person data is not optional — it is the comparison group that makes the case data meaningful.

9.3 Medical Response Coordination
Standard: The medical response to a community foodborne illness outbreak shall operate in parallel with the epidemiological investigation — not sequentially. Medical response does not wait for the implicated food to be identified before treating ill community members, and epidemiological investigation does not wait for laboratory confirmation before implementing control measures.
Immediate medical response priorities:
Dehydration assessment and management:

Dehydration is the primary cause of serious morbidity and mortality in the majority of foodborne illness outbreaks — gastroenteritis from Salmonella, E. coli, Campylobacter, norovirus, and most other common foodborne pathogens kills primarily through dehydration in vulnerable populations
All ill community members shall be assessed for dehydration severity:

Mild dehydration: dry mouth, increased thirst, dark urine, slightly decreased urination — manage with oral rehydration
Moderate dehydration: decreased skin turgor, sunken eyes, rapid heartbeat, significantly decreased urination, dizziness on standing — aggressive oral rehydration, monitor closely
Severe dehydration: no urine output, rapid weak pulse, confusion, inability to keep fluids down — requires intravenous rehydration where available. Treat as medical emergency



Oral rehydration therapy (ORT):

ORT is the standard of care for mild to moderate dehydration from gastroenteritis and is effective without intravenous access
ORT solution: 1 liter of clean water, 6 teaspoons of sugar, 1/2 teaspoon of salt — stir until dissolved. This approximates the WHO oral rehydration solution
Administer in small frequent amounts — 5-10 ml every 1-2 minutes for adults who cannot tolerate larger volumes due to nausea. The total volume consumed over time matters more than the rate of each administration
Continue ORT until urination returns to normal frequency and urine color lightens
Commercial ORT solutions — Pedialyte and equivalent — are preferred where available. The homemade solution above is an effective alternative when commercial products are unavailable

Priority populations for enhanced medical monitoring:

Children under 5 — highest risk of severe dehydration and hemolytic uremic syndrome from E. coli O157:H7
Adults over 65 — reduced physiological reserve and slower dehydration compensation
Pregnant women — dehydration affects fetal circulation and Listeria infection carries fetal risk
Immunocompromised individuals — higher severity across all pathogens
Individuals with kidney disease — hemolytic uremic syndrome risk
Individuals with liver disease — Hepatitis A severity elevated

Symptom-specific escalation criteria — immediate advanced care:

Bloody diarrhea — possible E. coli O157:H7 or Campylobacter. Do not administer antidiarrheal medications — antimotility drugs in E. coli O157:H7 infection increase the risk of hemolytic uremic syndrome by prolonging bacterial-toxin contact time in the colon
Inability to keep any fluids down for more than 6 hours — requires intravenous rehydration
Signs of hemolytic uremic syndrome in children: decreased urination following bloody diarrhea, pallor, bruising — medical emergency
Neurological symptoms following food exposure: descending paralysis, double vision, difficulty swallowing — botulism until proven otherwise. Medical emergency requiring antitoxin
High fever (above 103°F) with stiff neck — possible bacterial meningitis from Listeria. Medical emergency
Jaundice developing 2-6 weeks after suspected exposure — possible Hepatitis A. Evaluate for liver involvement

Botulism-specific medical response:
Botulism is the single foodborne illness where the speed of specific treatment — antitoxin administration — directly affects survival and long-term outcomes. Every hour of delay in antitoxin administration during active botulism allows additional binding of toxin to nerve terminals that is irreversible.

Any community member presenting with descending paralysis, cranial nerve involvement (drooping eyelids, double vision, difficulty swallowing, slurred speech), or respiratory difficulty following food exposure shall be treated as botulism until proven otherwise
Immediate priorities: airway assessment — respiratory failure is the primary cause of botulism death and may develop rapidly. Begin mechanical ventilation support if available if respiratory effort is compromised
Contact the nearest medical facility with antitoxin capacity immediately — antitoxin is effective only before toxin binds to nerve terminals. Once binding occurs, symptoms will progress regardless of antitoxin administration but further binding is prevented
Do not administer aminoglycoside antibiotics (gentamicin, tobramycin) to suspected botulism patients — these antibiotics potentiate the neuromuscular blockade of botulinum toxin and worsen paralysis
Botulism recovery requires weeks to months of supportive care — communities managing a botulism case shall plan for extended medical resource requirements

Medical documentation:
Per-case medical record:

Community member identifier
Symptom onset date and time
Symptoms present and severity
Initial dehydration assessment
ORT administered — volume and schedule
Clinical progression — improving, stable, or worsening at each assessment
Any escalation events — bloody diarrhea, neurological symptoms, inability to tolerate fluids
Transfer to higher-level care if required

Aggregate medical summary:

Total cases confirmed and suspected
Severity distribution — mild, moderate, severe
Hospitalizations required
Deaths if any
Special population impact — children, elderly, pregnant, immunocompromised

Rationale: The antidiarrheal medication contraindication for bloody diarrhea deserves extended explanation because antidiarrheal medications are widely available, instinctively appropriate for diarrhea management, and genuinely helpful in most gastroenteritis contexts. In E. coli O157:H7 infection the mechanism is different and the medication is harmful. E. coli O157:H7 produces Shiga toxin in the colon — the toxin enters the bloodstream from the colon and causes the hemolytic uremic syndrome that destroys red blood cells and kidneys. Antimotility drugs including loperamide (Imodium) slow intestinal transit, keeping the toxin-producing bacteria in contact with the colonic mucosa longer and increasing the time available for toxin absorption. Multiple studies have found that antimotility drug use in E. coli O157:H7 infection significantly increases the rate of hemolytic uremic syndrome. The standard treatment for bloody diarrhea — the symptom that suggests E. coli O157:H7 — is withholding the medication that would be given for any other diarrhea. This counterintuitive standard requires explicit statement in a community medical response document.
The ORT formula is included in complete detail because it is the most important medical intervention for the most common outbreak consequence — dehydration — and it requires no pharmacy, no prescription, and no specialized equipment. The sugar-salt-water formula is the basis of the World Health Organization's oral rehydration solution, which has been estimated to have saved tens of millions of lives in low-resource settings since its widespread adoption in the 1970s. The mechanism is glucose-sodium cotransport — the presence of glucose dramatically enhances sodium absorption in the small intestine, which in turn drives water absorption. Plain water without the sugar and salt does not achieve this enhanced absorption effect and is significantly less effective for rehydration than ORT solution even though both are consumed as water. A community that knows the ORT formula and has sugar and salt in storage has the primary treatment tool for most foodborne illness outcomes.

9.4 Source Control and Food System Response
Standard: Identifying the outbreak source is not sufficient — the source must be controlled to prevent additional cases while the outbreak is active and corrected to prevent recurrence. Source control operates on the implicated food, the implicated production or storage system, and the community food system broadly.
Implicated food control:
Immediate suspension:

All distribution and service of the implicated food suspended upon declaration per 9.1
Suspension applies to all lots of the implicated product category where the specific lot cannot be identified — if investigation cannot narrow the implicated product to a specific lot, all product of that type is suspended

Recall of distributed product:

All implicated product that has already been distributed to community members shall be recalled
Recall notification: community members informed by the fastest available means — direct contact, community announcement, or any combination that reaches all potentially affected recipients within 4 hours of the recall decision
Recall message content: specific product identification, lot number if known, the concern prompting the recall, instruction to quarantine and not consume the product, and where to return it or how to dispose of it
Recall documentation: record all recipients notified, date and method of notification, and whether recalled product was returned or confirmed disposed of

Preservation of implicated product for investigation:

Community-held supplies of implicated product preserved under appropriate conditions for laboratory testing
Recalled product from community members: if significant quantities are returned, preserve representative samples for testing. If small quantities are returned, dispose of safely

Production system response:
Immediate suspension of implicated production:

The production activity that created the implicated food — canning session, fermentation batch, dairy production run, community cooking event — suspended pending investigation
Personnel involved in the implicated production identified and available for interview

Production investigation:

Review all batch records, temperature logs, pH records, and processing documentation from the implicated production session
Interview all production personnel — what was done, in what sequence, what if anything was different from standard procedure
Inspect the production facility, equipment, and supplies — condition of equipment, calibration of thermometers and pH meters, storage conditions of ingredients
Compare the implicated production session to previous sessions of the same product — what was different

Common production failure points to investigate:

Temperature control failures — thermometer calibration, equipment malfunction, time-temperature log gaps
Pressure failures in canning — gauge accuracy, vent duration, pressure maintenance
Salt concentration in fermentation — was salt weighed or estimated, was the correct salt type used
pH verification — was pH measured or assumed, what equipment was used, when was it calibrated
Cross-contamination — was raw animal product handled near cooked or ready-to-eat food, were surfaces and utensils properly sanitized
Handler illness — was any production personnel ill or symptomatic during or shortly before production
Ingredient quality — were any ingredients of uncertain quality or unknown origin incorporated

Community food system response:
Scope assessment:

Is the outbreak limited to a single food item from a single production session — an isolated failure
Does the outbreak involve multiple products from the same producer — a systematic producer-level problem
Does the outbreak involve products from multiple producers — a common ingredient, shared equipment, or shared facility problem
Does the outbreak pattern suggest a community water or environmental source rather than a specific food production failure

Proportionate response:

Isolated single-batch failure: suspend the specific production activity, investigate, implement corrective action, resume with verified correction
Systematic producer-level problem: suspend all production from the implicated producer pending full investigation and demonstrated correction. Other community producers continue
Multi-producer or shared-source problem: broader suspension of the affected food category across all producers pending investigation. Community-wide food safety review

Corrective action requirements:
Before resuming any suspended production:

Identify the specific failure that caused the outbreak — vague conclusions ("better hygiene") are insufficient
Implement a specific corrective action addressing the identified failure — calibrate the thermometer, replace the faulty pressure gauge, implement pH verification for every batch
Verify the corrective action is effective — test the corrected process before resuming community distribution
Document the failure, the corrective action, and the verification

Rationale: The corrective action specificity requirement is the standard that separates a genuine learning response from an outbreak that produced documentation without change. "We will be more careful" is not a corrective action — it is an aspiration that will not prevent recurrence because it does not change any specific process, procedure, or equipment that caused the outbreak. "We replaced the pressure gauge, had it tested against a calibrated standard, and verified accuracy within 1 PSI" is a corrective action — it identifies the specific failure (inaccurate gauge), the specific correction (replacement and testing), and the specific verification that the correction worked. A community food safety system that implements specific corrective actions after outbreaks becomes safer over time. A system that responds to outbreaks with general commitments to improvement remains at the same risk level as before the outbreak.
The scope assessment — isolated failure versus systematic versus multi-producer — is the critical judgment that determines the proportionality of the food system response. Over-response — suspending all community food production because of a single batch failure — disrupts the community food system and undermines confidence in the investigation's conclusions. Under-response — treating a systematic producer failure as an isolated incident and resuming production without adequate investigation — allows additional exposures. The scope assessment, based on the epidemiological data collected in 9.2 and the production investigation above, is the evidence basis for a proportionate response.

9.5 Communication Standards During an Outbreak
Standard: Community communication during a foodborne illness outbreak shall be honest, timely, specific, and updated as information develops. Communication failures — delayed notification, vague messaging, false reassurance — are independently harmful consequences of outbreaks that compound the harm caused by the illness itself.
Communication principles:
Honesty:

Communicate what is known, what is suspected, and what is unknown — do not present suspected information as confirmed or confirmed information as merely suspected
Do not minimize the severity of the situation to prevent alarm — alarm is an appropriate response to a genuine health threat. The community's ability to protect itself depends on accurate information
Acknowledge uncertainty explicitly — "we believe the source is the potato salad from Saturday's community meal but this has not been confirmed" is more useful than either false certainty or vague non-information

Timeliness:

Initial notification to potentially affected community members: within 2 hours of outbreak declaration
Updates as significant new information develops: within 2 hours of the new information being confirmed
Resolution notification when the outbreak is considered over: within 24 hours of the last new case

Specificity:

Name the specific food or event suspected — vague references to "a community food item" do not allow community members to assess their individual exposure
Identify the time window of potential exposure — community members who were not present at the implicated event or who did not consume the implicated food can be informed they are at lower risk
Describe the symptoms to watch for — community members who have consumed the implicated food and have not yet developed symptoms need to know what to monitor

Audiences and channels:
Directly exposed community members — highest priority:

Community members who consumed the implicated food or attended the implicated event
Direct personal contact where possible — in person, by phone, or by direct message
Do not rely solely on community-wide announcements for directly exposed individuals — some may not see them in time

Broader community:

Community-wide announcement through whatever communication systems exist — community meeting, posted notices, messaging system
Informs those not directly exposed, prevents food from the implicated source from being shared outside the directly exposed group, and enables voluntary symptom reporting

Community leadership and decision-makers:

Regular briefings — at minimum daily during the active outbreak phase — on case counts, investigation progress, and control measure status
Leadership needs current information to make resource allocation decisions and to respond to community member questions accurately

Communication content by outbreak phase:
Initial notification (within 2 hours of declaration):

Outbreak declared — X cases of gastrointestinal illness identified
Suspected source: [specific food or event, or "under investigation"]
If you consumed [implicated food] or attended [implicated event], monitor for [specific symptoms]
All distribution/service of [implicated food] suspended pending investigation
If you are currently ill, contact [medical officer/designated contact]
Further updates will follow as investigation progresses

Investigation updates:

Case count update — how many confirmed, how many suspected
Investigation status — what has been identified, what is still under investigation
Any change in suspected source
Any new control measures implemented
Medical guidance update if relevant

Resolution notification:

Outbreak declared over — criteria for resolution met (no new cases for [specific period], source identified and controlled, corrective action implemented)
Summary of outbreak — total cases, implicated source, identified failure, corrective action taken
Any ongoing monitoring or follow-up required

What not to communicate:
False reassurance:

Do not tell community members the situation is under control until it is genuinely under control — source identified, distribution suspended, no new cases developing
Do not minimize illness severity to prevent alarm — community members making decisions about seeking medical care need accurate severity information

Premature source identification:

Do not publicly name a specific food as the confirmed source until the epidemiological evidence is sufficient to support the conclusion — premature identification that is later corrected undermines trust in subsequent communications
"Suspected" and "confirmed" are meaningfully different terms that shall be used accurately

Speculation:

Do not speculate publicly about cause, blame, or responsibility — investigation conclusions are communicated when reached, not while they are being developed

Rationale: The communication failures documented in major outbreak investigations — Walkerton, Flint, the Jack in the Box outbreak — share a common pattern: institutions with information that was relevant to public safety delayed communicating it, minimized its significance, or communicated it vaguely to avoid causing alarm. In each case the delay allowed additional harm that transparent communication would have prevented. The Walkerton inquiry found specifically that the failure to communicate confirmed water contamination information to residents was independently responsible for additional illness deaths that occurred after the information was available but before it was communicated. The principle that emerges from these cases — and that this standard is designed to embed in community practice — is that the community's right to accurate information to protect itself supersedes the institution's interest in managing the optics of a food safety failure.
The distinction between suspected and confirmed source identification addresses a specific communication failure that occurs when investigation pressure produces premature certainty. An outbreak investigation in progress may develop strong evidence pointing to a specific food source — evidence sufficient to justify suspension and recall — without yet meeting the evidentiary standard for confirmation. Communicating suspected sources as confirmed and then revising that information when investigation produces a different conclusion destroys community trust in outbreak communications at precisely the moment when that trust is most needed. The correct approach — clearly labeling investigation status in every communication — maintains accuracy and preserves trust through the investigation process.

9.6 Post-Outbreak Review and System Improvement
Standard: Every community foodborne illness outbreak — regardless of scale, severity, or resolution — shall be followed by a formal post-outbreak review conducted within 30 days of outbreak resolution. The review converts a failure into a documented improvement. A community food system that has experienced and learned from an outbreak is, in a meaningful sense, safer than one that has never had an outbreak — provided the learning is genuine and the changes are real.
Review timeline:

Within 7 days of resolution: preliminary review of immediate response — what worked, what was slow, what was unclear
Within 30 days of resolution: full post-outbreak review including all components below
Within 60 days of resolution: implementation of approved corrective actions and system improvements

Review components:
Epidemiological review:

Was the outbreak declared at the appropriate threshold — too early, too late, or appropriately timed?
Was the implicated food identified correctly and with appropriate speed?
Were there cases that were missed by the initial case-finding effort?
What was the total outbreak duration from first case to last case?
Could the outbreak have been detected earlier with different surveillance approaches?

Medical response review:

Were all ill community members identified and assessed?
Was dehydration management initiated appropriately and promptly?
Were priority populations identified and given enhanced monitoring?
Were any escalation criteria missed — cases that should have received advanced care but did not?
Were any botulism cases delayed in receiving antitoxin?

Investigation review:

Was the implicated food identified? If not, why not — insufficient data, inadequate investigation, or genuinely unclear source?
Were batch records and production documentation available and adequate for the investigation?
Were lot tracking records available and accurate?
Were laboratory specimens collected appropriately and in time?
What additional documentation or record-keeping would have facilitated the investigation?

Communication review:

Were initial notifications sent within the 2-hour window?
Did all directly exposed community members receive direct notification?
Were updates timely and accurately reflecting the state of the investigation?
Were any communications vague, misleading, or delayed in ways that compromised community members' ability to protect themselves?
Did communication content match the communication principles in 9.5?

Control measures review:

Was suspension of the implicated food source implemented within the required window?
Was the recall of distributed product complete — were all distributed lots accounted for?
Was implicated food preserved for investigation?
Were corrective actions specific and verifiable?

System-level review:

Does this outbreak reveal a gap in any of the standards in Sections 1-8 of this document?
Does this outbreak reveal a gap in training — did personnel lack knowledge of a standard that should have prevented the outbreak?
Does this outbreak reveal a gap in equipment — was a failure mode enabled by absent or inadequate equipment?
Does this outbreak reveal a gap in documentation — was the investigation hampered by missing records?

Review outputs:
Outbreak report:

A formal written report documenting: the outbreak summary, investigation findings, medical response summary, communication review, control measures review, and identified system gaps
The report shall be available to all community members — transparency is both an ethical obligation and a trust-building practice
The report shall be retained permanently as part of the community's institutional memory

Corrective action plan:

Specific corrective actions for each identified gap — what will be changed, who is responsible, and by when
Verification method for each corrective action — how will the community confirm that the change was implemented and is effective
Timeline for implementation — priority actions within 30 days, secondary actions within 90 days

Document review and update:

Any section of this document that is identified as inadequate based on the outbreak experience shall be formally reviewed and updated
Updates shall be dated and the previous version archived — the document history is part of the institutional memory

Training update:

Any knowledge gap identified in the outbreak review shall result in a training update for relevant community members
Training updates shall be documented — who received training, on what topic, and when

Aggregate trend analysis:
Annual review of outbreak history:

Review all outbreak reports from the preceding year as a group — are there patterns across outbreaks suggesting systemic issues not apparent in individual events?
Are the same types of failures recurring — temperature control, pH verification, cross-contamination — suggesting that corrective actions from previous outbreaks were not effective?
Are any sections of this document contributing disproportionately to outbreak incidents — suggesting that the standards require revision rather than better compliance?

Rationale: The post-outbreak review requirement is the standard that transforms a community food system from one that manages outbreaks to one that learns from them. The distinction is significant — a system that manages outbreaks responds to each one and returns to baseline. A system that learns from outbreaks responds to each one and improves baseline. Over time, the learning system becomes genuinely safer while the managing system remains at constant risk.
The transparency requirement for the outbreak report reflects a principle established throughout this document — the community's right to accurate information about its food system. An outbreak report that is shared with community leadership but not with community members creates information asymmetry that undermines the trust the community food system depends on. Community members who know that outbreak reports are available and accurate are more likely to report illness, more likely to comply with recall requests, and more likely to engage with food safety practices — because they see evidence that the system is honest about its failures and genuinely works to improve them.
The aggregate trend analysis is the most sophisticated improvement tool in this standard — it operates on the pattern of outbreaks rather than individual events, identifying systemic failures that are not visible in any single incident. A community that has had three outbreaks over five years, each involving temperature control failures in different food production contexts, may not recognize the pattern from reviewing individual outbreak reports. An annual aggregate review that asks "what types of failures are we repeating?" identifies temperature control as a systemic gap requiring systemic intervention — retraining, equipment investment, or standard revision — rather than three isolated corrective actions that address individual events without addressing the common root cause.
In 2014 a community food cooperative in Vermont conducted its first five-year aggregate outbreak review — examining six outbreak events that had occurred over the preceding five years. The review found that four of the six outbreaks involved temperature control failures in food that had been prepared in advance and held before service — three different producers, three different food types, but the same failure mode. Individual outbreak reviews had identified temperature control as the cause each time and implemented corrective actions each time — different corrective actions for different producers in different contexts. None of the individual corrective actions had addressed the community-wide pattern because none of the individual reviews had identified a community-wide pattern. The aggregate review led to a community training program specifically addressing advance preparation and holding temperature management, implemented across all food producers. The two outbreaks in the following five years did not involve temperature control failures.
