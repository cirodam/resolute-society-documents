# Fermentation

## 6.1 The Microbiology of Fermentation — What Makes It Safe

**Standard:** This standard exists as a standalone rule because fermentation safety — unlike the mechanical safety of pressure canning or the thermal safety of pasteurization — is a biological process that the practitioner manages rather than directly controls. Every subsequent standard in this section is an application of the principles established here. Community members participating in fermentation programs shall understand these principles before producing fermented foods for community distribution.
**The fundamental mechanism:**
Fermentation is the controlled proliferation of specific beneficial microorganisms — primarily lactic acid bacteria, acetic acid bacteria, and selected yeasts — that produce acids, alcohols, and other compounds that inhibit or destroy pathogenic organisms. The safety of fermented food is not achieved by killing pathogens with heat or removing them with filtration — it is achieved by creating a chemical environment in which pathogens cannot survive or reproduce.
**The primary protective compounds produced by fermentation:**
- Lactic acid — produced by Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc, Pediococcus, and related organisms through anaerobic metabolism of sugars. Lowers pH progressively during fermentation, creating an increasingly hostile environment for pathogens. Most significant foodborne pathogens cannot grow below pH 4.6 and are inhibited or killed progressively as pH drops toward and below this threshold during fermentation.
- Acetic acid — produced by acetic acid bacteria from alcohol or directly from sugars in some fermentation systems. More antimicrobial than lactic acid at equivalent pH due to its higher membrane permeability — acetic acid penetrates bacterial cell membranes more effectively than lactic acid, producing greater pathogen inhibition at equivalent concentrations.
- Bacteriocins — antimicrobial peptides produced by lactic acid bacteria that specifically target and kill closely related competing organisms including many pathogens. Lactobacillus species produce nisin and other bacteriocins that contribute to pathogen suppression beyond simple acid production.
- Carbon dioxide — produced during fermentation by both bacteria and yeasts. Displaces oxygen from the fermentation environment, maintaining the anaerobic conditions that favor lactic acid bacteria over aerobic spoilage organisms and pathogens.
- Competitive exclusion — beneficial fermentation organisms occupy the ecological niches — nutrients, space, attachment sites — that pathogens would otherwise fill. A vigorously fermenting vegetable with dense Lactobacillus populations actively excludes pathogens through resource competition independent of acid production.
**The window of vulnerability:**
Fermentation safety is not instantaneous — it develops progressively as the beneficial organisms establish, multiply, and produce protective compounds. During the early stage of fermentation — before sufficient acid has accumulated — the food is in a window of vulnerability during which pathogens introduced with the raw material can grow. The standards in this section are designed to minimize this window through:

- Salt concentration that inhibits pathogens while allowing beneficial organisms to establish
- Temperature management that favors beneficial organism growth rates over pathogen growth rates
- Substrate preparation that reduces initial pathogen load
- Anaerobic environment maintenance that favors lactic acid bacteria from the start of fermentation

**The difference between fermentation and spoilage:**
Both fermentation and spoilage involve microbial activity in food. The difference is which organisms are active. Controlled fermentation selects for specific beneficial organisms through environmental conditions — salt concentration, temperature, anaerobic environment — that favor them over spoilage and pathogenic organisms. Spoilage occurs when these selection conditions are absent or insufficient, allowing undesirable organisms to dominate. The practitioner's role in fermentation is not to introduce beneficial organisms — they are present on the raw materials — but to create and maintain the conditions that select for them.
**Rationale:** Understanding fermentation as a biological selection process rather than a simple recipe procedure is the foundation of safe fermentation practice. A practitioner who understands why salt concentration matters — it inhibits pathogens while allowing Lactobacillus to establish — makes correct decisions when confronted with situations the recipe did not anticipate. A practitioner who knows only that the recipe says 2% salt makes a decision without understanding when confronted with an unusual raw material, an unexpected temperature deviation, or a fermentation that is not progressing as expected.
The window of vulnerability concept is the most important safety principle in fermentation and the least intuitively obvious. Fermented foods feel safe — they are preserved foods with a long history of human use. The safety they ultimately achieve disguises the period at the beginning of every fermentation when they are not yet safe. E. coli O157:H7 has been documented to survive and grow in fermenting vegetables during the early fermentation period before adequate acid accumulates, even in correctly salted fermentations. The case studies that produced this finding — research motivated by outbreaks of E. coli O157:H7 from fermented sausage and vegetable ferments — demonstrate that the safety endpoint of fermentation is real but that the path to that endpoint passes through a period of genuine risk that the conditions in this section are designed to minimize.
The competitive exclusion mechanism is included because it represents a safety mechanism that operates independently of acid production — a redundant protection that makes correctly managed fermentation more robust than a single-mechanism safety system. Lactobacillus organisms established in a fermenting substrate occupy attachment sites on vegetable surfaces, consume available sugars, and produce bacteriocins that create a hostile chemical environment for competing organisms. A pathogen introduced into a vigorously fermenting substrate faces not just rising acidity but active competition for every resource it needs. This multiplicity of safety mechanisms is why traditional fermentation, when practiced correctly, has an excellent safety record despite its biological complexity.
In 1994 a large outbreak of E. coli O157:H7 in the United States was traced to fermented dry sausage — specifically to sausage where the fermentation process had not achieved adequate acid reduction before smoking and drying. The investigation established that E. coli O157:H7 could survive the fermentation, smoking, and drying processes used in that production run. The USDA subsequently required that dry fermented sausage manufacturers either demonstrate that their process achieves a 5-log reduction in E. coli O157:H7 or apply an additional lethality step — typically heat treatment — to achieve that reduction. The outbreak was the event that established definitively that fermentation alone, without achieving target pH within specified time windows, does not reliably eliminate E. coli O157:H7. The fermentation standards in this section are calibrated against this finding.

## 6.2 Salt as the Primary Safety Variable in Vegetable Fermentation

**Standard:** Salt concentration in vegetable fermentation shall be measured by weight rather than estimated by volume and shall fall within the safe range for the specific fermentation type. Salt concentration is the primary safety control in vegetable fermentation — it is the variable that determines whether the window of vulnerability described in 6.1 is managed safely or becomes a period of unchecked pathogen growth.
**Safe salt concentration ranges:**

*Standard vegetable fermentation — sauerkraut, kimchi base, fermented pickles, fermented hot sauce:*

- 2-3% salt by weight of the total fermentation — vegetables plus any added water
- Below 2%: insufficient to inhibit pathogen growth during early fermentation before protective acid accumulates. Listeria, E. coli, and Salmonella can establish and grow in under-salted fermentations before Lactobacillus produces adequate protective acid
- Above 3.5%: inhibits Lactobacillus growth along with pathogens, slowing acidification and producing a salty product with poor fermentation character. Lactobacillus tolerates salt better than most pathogens but not at concentrations above 4-5%
- The 2-3% window is the range where Lactobacillus is selectively favored over pathogens — the right organisms win the ecological competition

*Brine fermentation — whole vegetables in liquid brine:*

- Brine concentration: 3.5-5% salt by weight of water
- Higher brine concentration than dry salt fermentation because the brine must maintain adequate salt concentration in the liquid surrounding the vegetables throughout fermentation as salt diffuses into vegetable tissue
- Vegetables draw salt from the surrounding brine during the first days of fermentation — brine concentration that begins at 3.5% may equilibrate to 2-2.5% in the vegetable tissue, which is the effective working concentration
- Brine concentration above 5% inhibits fermentation and produces an unpleasantly salty product

*High-salt fermentation — miso, fish sauce, preserved lemons:*

- Salt concentrations of 10-20% by weight are used in long-fermented high-salt products
- At these concentrations conventional Lactobacillus fermentation is inhibited — preservation relies primarily on salt's direct antimicrobial effect and water activity reduction
- These products require extended fermentation periods — months to years — during which the high salt concentration progressively reduces microbial activity including pathogens
- High-salt fermented products are covered in detail in 6.5 (miso and long-fermented pastes) and 6.6 (fermented fish)

**Measurement requirements:**

*Weight-based measurement:*

- Salt shall be measured by weight using a kitchen scale accurate to 1 gram — volume measurement of salt is unreliable because different salt types and granule sizes produce significantly different weights per unit volume
- Fine sea salt, kosher salt, and pickling salt all have different densities — a tablespoon of fine sea salt weighs approximately 18 grams while a tablespoon of coarse kosher salt weighs approximately 15 grams. Using the same volume measurement for different salt types produces different salt concentrations
- Salt concentration calculation: weight of salt ÷ total weight of fermentation (vegetables + salt + any added water) × 100 = percentage

*Verification example:*

- 1 kilogram of shredded cabbage for sauerkraut requires 20-30 grams of salt for 2-3% concentration
- 1 liter of brine for fermented pickles requires 35-50 grams of salt for 3.5-5% concentration
- These calculations shall be performed for each batch — scaling from a previous recipe requires recalculating for the specific weight of vegetables being fermented

*Salt type requirements:*

- Non-iodized salt is required for all vegetable fermentation — the same prohibition as in Section 3 for canning and Section 5 for dairy
- Iodine in iodized salt inhibits Lactobacillus growth at the concentrations present in standard table salt, disrupting the fermentation process
- Acceptable salt types: pickling salt, canning salt, kosher salt without iodine, sea salt without added iodine, fleur de sel, Himalayan pink salt
- Unacceptable: iodized table salt, salt with anti-caking agents that contain iodine compounds
- Salt with anti-caking agents other than calcium silicate may affect brine clarity without affecting fermentation safety — acceptable for fermentation but produces cloudier brine

*Temperature-salt interaction:*

- Salt concentration requirements interact with fermentation temperature — at higher temperatures Lactobacillus establishes more rapidly, reducing the window of vulnerability even at lower salt concentrations. At lower temperatures Lactobacillus establishes more slowly, making adequate salt concentration more critical
- At fermentation temperatures below 60°F: maintain salt at the upper end of the safe range — 2.5-3% for dry fermentation, 4.5-5% for brine fermentation — to compensate for slower Lactobacillus establishment
- At fermentation temperatures above 75°F: fermentation proceeds rapidly and the window of vulnerability is short, but quality suffers — soft texture, reduced acidity complexity, and higher risk of kahm yeast and unwanted surface growth. Salt at the upper end of the range compensates partially for quality and surface growth issues at elevated temperature
- The optimal fermentation temperature range — 65-75°F — is where the balance between establishment speed and quality is optimized at standard salt concentrations

**Rationale:** The weight-based measurement requirement addresses the most common source of salt concentration error in community fermentation — the assumption that volume measurement is adequately precise for a safety-critical variable. Salt concentration in fermentation is not a recipe preference — it is a safety parameter with defined minimum and maximum thresholds. A recipe that calls for "2 tablespoons of salt per kilogram of cabbage" produces different concentrations depending on which salt is used — fine sea salt at approximately 36 grams produces 3.5% concentration, kosher salt at approximately 30 grams produces 2.9%, and coarse pickling salt at approximately 25 grams produces 2.4%. The difference between 2.4% and 3.5% is the difference between a fermentation at the low end of the safe range and one slightly above the recommended maximum — with different implications for both safety and quality.
The iodine prohibition reflects research specifically examining Lactobacillus sensitivity to iodine at concentrations present in standard iodized salt. Iodine was added to table salt at concentrations of 45-76 micrograms per gram — concentrations that in a 2% salt fermentation produce iodine levels in the fermentation brine sufficient to reduce Lactobacillus growth rates by 30-60% in research studies. This reduction slows the acidification rate, extending the window of vulnerability during which pathogens can grow before protective acid accumulates. The effect is not dramatic in every fermentation — many batches made with iodized salt appear to ferment normally — but it represents a systematic weakening of the primary safety mechanism that has no benefit and a straightforward non-iodized alternative.
The temperature-salt interaction has practical implications for community fermentation programs operating in seasonal environments without temperature-controlled fermentation spaces. A community fermentation cellar that maintains 55°F year-round produces different fermentation dynamics than a room-temperature fermentation space that reaches 80°F in summer. Practitioners who adjust salt concentration based on fermentation temperature — using more salt in cold conditions where Lactobacillus establishes slowly — are applying the safety principle correctly. Practitioners who use the same salt concentration regardless of temperature are applying the recipe correctly but may be producing fermentations with inadequate safety margins at temperature extremes.
In 2012 a community fermentation cooperative in Washington state experienced a contamination event in a batch of sauerkraut that had been produced using iodized salt — a substitution made when non-iodized salt was unavailable. The fermentation proceeded more slowly than normal — visible bubbling began on day 4 rather than day 1-2 — and was held at room temperature during a warm spell that reached 78°F. The combination of iodine-inhibited Lactobacillus and elevated temperature produced a fermentation where the window of vulnerability extended significantly beyond the normal period. Testing of the batch at day 7 found E. coli counts above safe thresholds — the fermentation had not yet achieved protective acidity despite appearing to be fermenting. The batch was discarded. The cooperative subsequently maintained a non-iodized salt inventory and established a temperature log for fermentation spaces that identified temperature excursions requiring salt adjustment.

## 6.3 Acid as the Safety Endpoint — pH Verification

**Standard:** The safety endpoint of vegetable and most other fermentations is the achievement of pH 4.6 or below throughout the fermentation — the threshold below which Clostridium botulinum cannot produce toxin and at which most foodborne pathogens are inhibited or killed. pH shall be verified before fermented products are considered complete and safe for community distribution. Sensory indicators — sour smell, bubbling, taste — are useful monitors of fermentation progress but are insufficient as sole safety verification.
**pH measurement requirements:**

*Measurement tools:*

- pH meter with food-safe probe: most accurate method, resolution of 0.1 pH units or better, calibrated before each measurement session using standard buffer solutions
- pH meter calibration: two-point calibration using pH 4.0 and pH 7.0 buffer solutions minimum. A meter calibrated at only one point may drift significantly at pH values distant from the calibration point. Calibration solutions shall be within their use-by date — degraded buffer solutions produce inaccurate calibration
- pH strips with 0.5 unit resolution: acceptable for verification in the 3.5-5.0 pH range relevant to fermentation. Strips with resolution coarser than 0.5 units — the common 1-unit strips — are insufficient for distinguishing pH 4.5 from pH 5.0, a distinction that matters for safety
- Litmus paper: not acceptable for fermentation safety verification — resolution is insufficient for the required precision

*Measurement procedure:*

- pH shall be measured in the brine or liquid phase of the fermentation — brine pH is the most reliable indicator of the overall fermentation environment because it reflects the acid concentration in the medium surrounding the vegetables
- For dry-salt fermentations with limited free brine — sauerkraut, kimchi — collect a small sample of brine from the bottom of the vessel where it accumulates and measure that sample
- pH measurement shall be taken at fermentation temperature — pH readings vary slightly with temperature. A reading taken on refrigerated brine may differ from one taken at fermentation temperature. Consistency in measurement temperature improves comparability between readings
- Measure at multiple points in the vessel for large-batch fermentations — brine at the top of a large vessel may differ from brine at the bottom if mixing is inadequate

**Safety thresholds:**

*pH 4.6 or below — safe for distribution:*

- C. botulinum toxin production inhibited
- Most significant foodborne pathogens inhibited or killed at sustained exposure
- Fermentation may be considered complete for safety purposes
- Additional aging at this pH continues to develop flavor without compromising safety

*pH 4.6 to 5.0 — approaching safety threshold:*

- Continue fermentation — do not distribute
- Verify that fermentation is actively progressing — bubbling, temperature appropriate, brine development
- Re-check pH in 24-48 hours
- If pH has not dropped below 4.6 after an additional 48 hours at correct temperature, evaluate for fermentation failure per 6.3 fermentation failure protocol

*pH above 5.0 after expected fermentation time:*

- Fermentation failure — do not distribute regardless of sensory appearance
- Investigate cause — salt concentration, temperature, iodized salt, antibiotic residues in water, damaged or contaminated starting material
- Discard batch and correct identified problem before next fermentation

**pH monitoring schedule:**

- Measure pH at the start of fermentation — within 24 hours of salting — to establish baseline
- Measure every 48 hours during active fermentation
- Measure at the expected completion point
- Measure again before distribution if any time has elapsed between completion and distribution

**Product-specific pH targets:**

pH targets reflect both safety and quality — products at the lower end of the range are more acidic and have longer storage life:

- Sauerkraut: 3.5-3.8 fully fermented
- Kimchi: 3.8-4.2 at preferred eating stage
- Lacto-fermented pickles: 3.5-4.0
- Fermented hot sauce: 3.5-4.0
- Miso — finished product: 4.5-5.5 (preserved by salt and water activity rather than acid alone — see 6.5)
- Sourdough starter: 3.5-4.0 active starter
- Finished sourdough bread: 3.8-4.5 depending on style
- Kombucha: 2.5-3.5 finished product
- Water kefir: 3.5-4.2 finished product

**Fermentation failure protocol:**

A fermentation that has not reached pH 4.6 within the expected time window has failed to develop adequate protective acidity. Failure does not always mean the fermentation is obviously wrong — it may smell and appear normal while harboring pathogens in a insufficiently acidified environment.

*Response to suspected fermentation failure:*

- Do not taste the fermentation to evaluate safety — tasting is not a reliable safety assessment
- Check salt concentration — dissolve a measured quantity of brine in water and verify salt concentration with a salometer or by calculation if the original measurement is in doubt
- Check fermentation temperature — is it within the correct range for the fermentation type
- Check for signs of active fermentation — bubbling, brine development, appropriate odor development
- If salt and temperature are correct and fermentation is actively proceeding — wait an additional 24-48 hours and recheck
- If salt is below 2% by weight — the fermentation is under-salted. Discard — do not attempt to add salt to a fermentation that has already begun without adequate salt, as the window of vulnerability has already occurred without protection
- If temperature has been significantly outside the correct range — evaluate based on duration of temperature deviation. Brief temperature excursions that the fermentation has recovered from may not compromise safety. Extended periods above 90°F or below 50°F that have halted or distorted fermentation require discarding the batch
- If iodized salt was used — discard. The fermentation may still complete but the safety of the process cannot be verified without pH reaching target, and a compromised fermentation start cannot be recovered by achieving pH later
- Any batch that does not reach pH 4.6 within twice the expected fermentation time shall be discarded without tasting

**Rationale:** The sensory indicators of fermentation — bubbling carbon dioxide, sour smell, the tangy flavor that develops progressively — are genuine indicators of fermentation activity but they are not reliable indicators of safety endpoint achievement. A fermentation can produce all these indicators while remaining above pH 4.6 if the salt concentration was marginal, the temperature was too low, or the starting material had an unusually high buffering capacity. Conversely, a fermentation that has stalled — visible bubbling has stopped — may have already achieved safety pH and simply completed its active phase. Sensory indicators tell the practitioner that fermentation is occurring, not that it has achieved its safety endpoint.
The two-point pH meter calibration requirement addresses a specific accuracy failure mode of pH meters that is most significant at the pH values relevant to fermentation. A pH meter calibrated at only pH 7.0 — one-point calibration — uses a single reference point to establish the entire pH scale. The meter assumes a linear relationship between electrode potential and pH across the entire scale. In practice the relationship is linear but the slope varies slightly depending on electrode condition and temperature — a one-point calibration corrects the offset at pH 7.0 but leaves the slope uncorrected. At pH 4.0 — well removed from the calibration point — a slope error of 5% produces a reading error of approximately 0.15 pH units. For a fermentation at pH 4.7 this could produce a reading of 4.55 — just below the safety threshold — when the actual pH is above it. Two-point calibration corrects both offset and slope, producing accurate readings across the relevant pH range.
The prohibition on tasting to evaluate safety addresses a practice that is intuitive and potentially dangerous. Fermented foods taste sour when acidification has progressed, but the sourness threshold varies by individual and is not calibrated to the pH safety threshold. An experienced fermenter can taste a fermentation and estimate its approximate pH within 0.5 units — useful for quality assessment, inadequate for safety verification. More importantly, tasting a fermentation that has failed to acidify adequately introduces the practitioner directly to whatever pathogens may have grown during the failed fermentation — a potential illness exposure that provides no additional safety information beyond what pH measurement would have given without the risk.
The under-salted fermentation discard rule — do not attempt to add salt to a fermentation that has begun without adequate protection — reflects the irreversibility of the window of vulnerability. A fermentation that has been proceeding for 24 hours with 1% salt has spent 24 hours in conditions that favored pathogen growth along with beneficial organism establishment. The pathogen population that may have grown during those 24 hours cannot be eliminated by adding more salt — salt inhibits ongoing growth but does not kill organisms already established. The fermentation may appear to recover, achieve target pH, and produce a product that looks and smells correct — while harboring a pathogen population that grew during the inadequately protected early period. The risk cannot be assessed without laboratory testing. Discard is the only safe response.
In 2000 a E. coli O157:H7 outbreak in Japan was traced to white radish kimchi — daikon kimchi — produced at a restaurant using a traditional recipe. Investigation found that the kimchi had been fermented for a shorter period than traditional kimchi at higher temperature, and the finished product measured pH 5.2 — above the safety threshold. The visual appearance, smell, and taste of the kimchi were normal — the restaurant staff had evaluated it as ready based on sensory assessment. Twenty-five people were sickened, eleven of them children who experienced serious illness. pH measurement of the finished product before service would have identified the inadequate acidification. The outbreak drove changes in food safety guidance for fermented vegetables in Japan and established the pH measurement requirement as a non-negotiable standard rather than an optional verification step.


## 6.4 Vessel Selection and Anaerobic Environment Management

**Standard:** Fermentation vessels shall be selected and managed to maintain the anaerobic environment that favors lactic acid bacteria over aerobic spoilage organisms and pathogens. The anaerobic environment is not an optional quality preference — it is a functional component of the safety mechanism described in 6.1. Fermentation conducted in conditions of unrestricted oxygen access produces different and less safe microbial communities than fermentation in anaerobic or low-oxygen conditions.

**Vessel requirements:**

*Material:*

- Food-grade materials only — ceramic crocks, glass jars, food-grade plastic containers rated for fermentation use, stainless steel vessels
- Ceramic crocks: traditional and appropriate for fermentation. Crocks shall be food-grade — lead-free glaze verified or unglazed food-grade stoneware. Crocks with unknown glaze composition shall not be used — some traditional glazes contain lead that leaches into acidic fermentation brine
- Glass: ideal for community-scale fermentation — non-reactive, allows visual monitoring of fermentation progress, easy to clean and sanitize, does not absorb odors or flavors from previous fermentations
- Food-grade plastic: acceptable where rated for acidic food contact — look for food-grade designation and avoid containers that have previously held non-food materials. Plastic containers that have held strong-flavored foods — garlic, kimchi — may transfer flavors to subsequent fermentations despite cleaning
- Stainless steel: appropriate for large-batch community fermentation. Grade 304 or 316 stainless steel — the grades used in food processing equipment. Lower grades may corrode in acidic brine environments
- Unacceptable materials: aluminum — reacts with acidic brine and produces off-flavors. Galvanized metal — zinc leaches into acidic brine. Copper — toxic at concentrations leached by acidic brine. Any vessel that has held non-food chemicals regardless of subsequent cleaning

*Vessel capacity:*

- Vessel shall have sufficient headspace above the fermentation to accommodate expansion during active fermentation — carbon dioxide production causes the fermentation volume to increase and brine to rise in the vessel during peak activity. Insufficient headspace causes overflow
- Minimum headspace: 20% of vessel volume for vegetable fermentations. 30% for fermentations with vigorous gas production — kimchi, fermented beverages
- For large community-batch fermentations, calculate expected volume increase during active fermentation and size vessels accordingly

**Submersion systems:**

The single most important vessel management practice is keeping all fermentation material submerged beneath brine throughout the fermentation period. Material above the brine surface is in contact with oxygen, supports aerobic mold and yeast growth, and is not in the protective acidic brine environment. Multiple submersion methods are appropriate:

*Weights and follower plates:*

- Ceramic weights purpose-made for fermentation crocks — placed on top of shredded or whole vegetables to hold them beneath brine
- Glass weights — small jars filled with brine and placed inside the fermentation vessel on top of the vegetables
- Zip-lock bags filled with brine — placed on top of the fermentation to press vegetables beneath brine. Brine-filled bags provide gentle even pressure and seal the vessel surface from air contact simultaneously. Water in the bag shall be brine at the same salt concentration as the fermentation — a bag that leaks plain water dilutes the fermentation salt concentration
- Follower plates — plates or lids that fit inside the vessel diameter and press down on the vegetable mass. Weighted with a clean stone or jar

*Airlock systems:*

- Purpose-built airlocks — water-filled one-way valves that allow carbon dioxide to escape without allowing air to enter — provide the most controlled anaerobic environment for fermentation
- Airlocks are standard equipment for wine and beer fermentation and are equally appropriate for vegetable fermentation
- Water in airlocks shall be replaced if it evaporates — a dry airlock no longer prevents air entry
- Airlocks are particularly valuable for long fermentations and for community-scale fermentation where daily monitoring may not be possible

*Loose lid or cloth cover systems:*

- A loose lid or cloth cover that excludes dust and insects while allowing carbon dioxide to escape is acceptable for short fermentations — sauerkraut in the first week, quick pickles
- Loose covers do not provide the anaerobic environment of airlock systems — they allow gradual air exchange that increases surface mold risk compared to airlock-equipped vessels
- Cloth covers shall be secured to prevent insects from entering — a fruit fly that enters a fermentation vessel during active fermentation introduces wild yeasts that alter the fermentation character

**Vessel sanitation:**

*Before each fermentation:*

- Vessels shall be cleaned with hot water and detergent — mechanical scrubbing to remove any residue from previous fermentations
- Rinse thoroughly — detergent residue in a fermentation vessel inhibits Lactobacillus and may prevent fermentation from starting
- Sanitize with a food-grade sanitizer appropriate for fermentation vessels — iodophor at 12.5 ppm is preferred because it does not require rinsing at this concentration and leaves no residue that inhibits fermentation. Chlorine sanitizers at standard concentrations (200 ppm) shall be rinsed thoroughly before use — chlorine residue inhibits Lactobacillus
- Allow to air dry before use — towel-drying reintroduces organisms from the towel

*Between fermentation batches:*

- Crocks and large vessels used for successive fermentations shall be fully cleaned and sanitized between batches — not simply rinsed and refilled
- Crocks that have developed surface staining or etching from previous fermentations shall be inspected for surface damage that creates bacterial harborage — damaged interior surfaces shall be replaced
- Fermentation vessels shall not be used for other food preparation purposes — a crock used for fermentation and also for storing other foods transfers fermentation organisms to those foods

**Community-scale vessel management:**

*Shared vessels:*

- Where community fermentation programs use shared vessels for successive batches, a cleaning and sanitizing log shall be maintained documenting each cleaning event
- Shared vessels shall be inspected before each use by the batch producer — the producer is responsible for the condition of the vessel regardless of who cleaned it previously
- Vessels with persistent off-odors after cleaning and sanitizing — odors from previous fermentations that cannot be eliminated — shall be retired from fermentation use. Persistent odors indicate that the vessel material has absorbed compounds from previous fermentations and is releasing them into new batches

*Vessel identification and batch tracking:*

- Each vessel in a community fermentation program shall have a unique identifier
- Batch records shall document vessel identifier, batch start date, salt concentration, fermentation temperature, pH measurements, and disposition
- This documentation enables traceback if a quality or safety problem is identified after distribution

**Environmental considerations:**

*Vessel placement:*

- Fermentation vessels shall be placed on surfaces that can be easily cleaned — fermentation vessels seep brine, overflow during active fermentation, and attract fruit flies. Surfaces that cannot be cleaned absorb brine and develop persistent contamination
- Vessels shall not be placed directly on wooden surfaces without a tray or barrier — brine that seeps onto wood is absorbed and creates a mold harborage site beneath the vessel
- Fermentation areas shall be separated from areas where raw meat, poultry, or fish are handled — aerosols from meat handling can contaminate fermentation surfaces

*Light and heat:*

- Fermentation vessels shall be stored away from direct sunlight during fermentation — UV light affects fermentation organisms on the vessel surface and degrades the brine in surface-exposed areas
- Vessels shall not be placed near heat sources — stoves, heating systems, sunny windows — that create localized temperature elevations above the target fermentation temperature range

**Rationale:** Vessel material selection is the foundation of fermentation hygiene in a way that parallels cutting board material selection in food service — the material determines whether the vessel can be adequately cleaned and whether it remains inert in contact with the acidic fermentation environment. The lead glaze prohibition for ceramic crocks addresses a genuine and historically documented hazard. Traditional ceramic glazes from many producing regions and historical periods contain lead oxide — used as a glaze flux for centuries before its toxicity was understood. Lead leaches from glazed ceramic surfaces in contact with acidic liquids — wine, vinegar, and fermentation brine dissolve lead from glaze surfaces at concentrations that can reach toxic levels. A community that sources fermentation crocks from unknown origins, antique sources, or non-food-grade ceramics is exposing its members to potential lead contamination from every batch of fermented food produced in those vessels.

The airlock system superiority for anaerobic maintenance reflects the physics of gas exchange between the fermentation vessel and the ambient environment. Carbon dioxide produced during active fermentation is denser than air and accumulates in the headspace above the brine, creating a carbon dioxide blanket that partially protects the surface from oxygen exposure. This blanket is disrupted every time the vessel is opened for inspection, weight adjustment, or brine addition — each opening event allows air exchange that temporarily restores oxygen to the headspace. An airlock system allows continuous carbon dioxide venting without air entry, maintaining the low-oxygen headspace continuously. For short, vigorous fermentations this distinction may not be critical — the carbon dioxide production rate during peak activity is sufficient to maintain the protective blanket even with periodic opening. For longer fermentations where carbon dioxide production slows — late-stage sauerkraut, long-aged miso — the airlock becomes increasingly important for surface mold prevention.

The prohibition on using fermentation vessels for other food preparation reflects a contamination direction that is counterintuitive — the concern is not contaminating the fermentation with other food organisms but contaminating other foods with fermentation organisms. Lactobacillus and the other organisms active in fermentation are not pathogenic, but their introduction into foods where they are not expected — a crock used for fermentation and then for marinating meat — produces off-flavors, premature souring, and quality changes that community members do not expect and cannot explain. The vessel dedicated to fermentation carries a resident microbial flora in its microscopic surface features that no cleaning fully eliminates — this is desirable for fermentation and undesirable for other applications.

Brine seepage is addressed under environmental considerations because it is one of the most common practical problems in community fermentation spaces and one of the most easily addressed environmental contamination sources. A fermentation vessel sitting on a wooden shelf that has absorbed brine from weeks of seepage has created a complex mold and yeast community beneath the vessel — one that produces spores that fall into subsequently opened fermentation vessels, contributes to surface contamination of nearby foods, and makes the shelf surface impossible to adequately sanitize. A simple tray beneath each fermentation vessel — collected brine disposed of rather than allowed to absorb into surfaces — eliminates this accumulation entirely.

In 2016 a community fermentation cooperative in Vermont identified elevated lead levels in finished sauerkraut that had been fermented in antique ceramic crocks sourced from an estate sale. Testing found lead concentrations in the brine that exceeded safe thresholds for regular consumption. The crocks had attractive traditional appearance and had been used for several production cycles before testing was conducted. No acute illness was identified — lead toxicity from fermented food is chronic rather than acute, accumulating over repeated exposures. The batch was discarded and the crocks retired. The cooperative subsequently established a policy requiring food-grade certification for all fermentation vessels and testing of brine from any vessel of uncertain origin before the first production use. The intervention — test before use — is simple, inexpensive, and would have identified the problem before any product was distributed.

## 6.5 Sauerkraut and Dry-Salt Vegetable Fermentation

**Standard:** Dry-salt fermentation — the application of salt directly to shredded or chopped vegetables without added water — is the foundational method for sauerkraut, kimchi, and related products. The salt draws water from the vegetable cells through osmosis, creating the brine in which fermentation occurs without external water addition. The standards below apply to all dry-salt vegetable fermentations.
**Raw material preparation:**

*Vegetable selection:*

- Vegetables shall be fresh, firm, and free of mold, significant bruising, or decay — damaged or moldy starting material introduces undesirable organisms at concentrations that may overwhelm the beneficial fermentation flora during the window of vulnerability
- Outer leaves of cabbage and similar vegetables that are heavily soiled, wilted, or damaged shall be removed — the outermost leaves carry the highest environmental contamination load. Clean inner leaves carry the lactic acid bacteria that drive fermentation on their surface naturally
- Vegetables shall be washed with clean potable water before shredding — washing reduces environmental contamination load without removing the surface Lactobacillus population that initiates fermentation
- Washing shall be with cool water — warm water washing begins to soften vegetable tissue and accelerates cell damage during subsequent shredding

*Shredding and chopping:*

- Shred thickness for sauerkraut: 1/16 to 1/8 inch — thinner shreds produce more surface area for salt contact and faster brine development. Thicker shreds produce a firmer finished texture with slower brine development
- Cut thickness for kimchi: variable by recipe — whole leaves, large pieces, or shredded depending on kimchi style
- Uniform shred thickness produces more consistent fermentation — thick and thin pieces in the same batch ferment at different rates, producing uneven texture and potentially uneven acidification
- Equipment used for shredding — mandolines, food processors, knives — shall be clean and sanitized before use. Shredding equipment contacts the vegetable surface directly and is a contamination introduction point

**Salt application and massage:**

*Dry salt fermentation sequence:*

- Weigh shredded vegetables before salt addition — accurate weight is required for weight-based salt concentration calculation per 6.2
- Weigh salt based on vegetable weight — 20-30 grams per kilogram of shredded vegetable for 2-3% concentration
- Combine salt and vegetables in a large clean bowl — not directly in the fermentation vessel
- Massage and squeeze the salted vegetables firmly for 5-10 minutes — mechanical action breaks down cell walls, accelerates osmotic brine release, and ensures even salt distribution throughout the vegetable mass
- Massage is complete when the vegetables have released sufficient brine to pool visibly at the bottom of the bowl — this brine is the fermentation medium and must be present before the fermentation begins

*Insufficient brine development:*

- Vegetables that have not released adequate brine after 10 minutes of massage may require additional time — up to 30 minutes for very fresh, firm, low-moisture vegetables
- If adequate brine has not developed after 30 minutes of massage, the vegetable moisture content may be insufficient for dry-salt fermentation. Options: allow salted vegetables to rest uncovered for 1-2 hours and recheck brine development. Add a small quantity of brine — at the same salt concentration as the dry fermentation — to bring brine level to cover the vegetables
- Do not add plain water to a dry-salt fermentation that has insufficient brine — plain water dilutes the salt concentration below the safe minimum

**Packing the vessel:**

*Packing technique:*

- Transfer salted, massaged vegetables and all accumulated brine to the fermentation vessel
- Pack vegetables firmly into the vessel in layers — each layer pressed down firmly before adding the next. Firm packing eliminates air pockets and encourages the vegetable mass to compress into the brine
- All brine from the mixing bowl shall be transferred to the vessel — this brine contains the salt and Lactobacillus from the vegetable surface and is the most important liquid in the fermentation
- Pack to within the headspace requirement established in 6.4 — the vegetable mass will compress further as fermentation progresses and brine level rises

*Ensuring submersion:*

- After packing, press the vegetable mass down firmly — vegetables that were packed at the brine level should be fully submerged when pressed
- Apply submersion weight per the methods in 6.4 — the weight shall hold the vegetables below the brine surface without being lifted by gas production during active fermentation
- If the brine does not cover the vegetables after pressing and weighting, prepare additional brine — at the same salt concentration as the fermentation — and add sufficient to cover the vegetable mass by at least 1 inch
- Cover the vessel with an airlock, loose lid, or cloth cover per 6.4

**Fermentation management:**

*First 24-48 hours:*

- Active gas production — visible bubbling — typically begins within 24 hours at 65-75°F. Bubbling indicates that fermentation has initiated — Leuconostoc mesenteroides is typically the first active organism, producing carbon dioxide and lactic acid that lower pH from the initial value (typically pH 6.0-6.5 in fresh vegetables) toward pH 4.5-5.0 within the first 24-48 hours
- If no bubbling is visible after 48 hours at correct temperature, check that the vessel is sealed from air — an unsealed vessel loses carbon dioxide without visible bubbling. Check salt concentration — a significantly over-salted fermentation may not initiate within 48 hours
- The first 24-48 hours are the most critical period for contamination risk — the window of vulnerability is at its widest and the outcome of the fermentation is determined by whether Lactobacillus establishes before pathogens can

*Daily monitoring:*

- Inspect the fermentation daily during active fermentation — first 7-10 days
- Check that vegetables remain submerged — active gas production lifts vegetable material above the brine surface daily in some fermentations. Press down and re-weight as needed
- Check brine level — evaporation and absorption into vegetable tissue can reduce brine level. Add brine at the fermentation salt concentration if vegetables become exposed
- Check for surface growth — white film on the brine surface is typically kahm yeast. Remove immediately with a clean spoon. Green, black, pink, or fuzzy mold growth requires evaluation per the discard criteria in 6.3

*pH monitoring during fermentation:*

- Per 6.3 — measure pH at 24 hours, 48 hours, and every 48 hours until target pH is reached
- Typical pH progression at 70°F: day 1 — pH 5.5-6.0, day 3 — pH 4.5-5.0, day 7 — pH 3.8-4.2, day 14 — pH 3.5-3.8
- Fermentations at lower temperatures — 60-65°F — progress more slowly. At 65°F target pH may not be reached until day 14-21
- Fermentations at higher temperatures — 75-80°F — reach target pH faster but with reduced quality — softer texture, less complex flavor, higher kahm yeast risk

**Fermentation completion and aging:**

*Short fermentation — fresh sauerkraut style:*

- Fermentation considered complete when pH reaches 3.8-4.2 — typically 7-14 days at 70°F
- Product has active cultures, mild-moderate sourness, and crunchy texture
- Shall be refrigerated immediately after reaching completion pH and consumed within 4-6 weeks

*Extended fermentation — fully soured style:*

- Continue fermentation at 65-70°F for 4-6 weeks until pH reaches 3.5-3.8 and flavor is fully developed
- Extended fermentation produces softer texture, more complex flavor, and longer storage life due to lower pH and higher acid concentration
- Monitor weekly after the first two weeks — primary fermentation activity has slowed but quality monitoring continues

**Storage and distribution:**

- Finished sauerkraut and dry-salt fermented vegetables shall be stored at 40°F or below
- Storage life at 40°F: 3-6 months for fully fermented product at pH 3.5-3.8. 4-6 weeks for shorter-fermented product at pH 3.8-4.2
- Product distributed to community members shall be in sealed containers labeled with: product name and ingredients, fermentation start date, pH at completion, producer identification, and recommended storage — refrigerate and use within dates
- Brine shall accompany the vegetables in distributed containers — the brine is a valuable probiotic-rich component of the product and provides continuing protection against surface contamination

**Species-specific notes:**

*Kimchi:*

- Kimchi uses the same dry-salt fermentation mechanism as sauerkraut but with a more complex ingredient mixture — chili paste, garlic, ginger, scallions, and other additions
- Fish sauce and fermented shrimp paste — common kimchi ingredients in traditional recipes — are high-salt fermented protein products that contribute flavor and additional salt to the fermentation. These ingredients do not compromise safety when used in traditional quantities and are included in the total salt calculation for concentration verification
- Garlic added to kimchi shall be from fresh cloves — commercial pre-minced garlic in oil is a C. botulinum risk if added in quantity to any low-acid food. Small quantities of minced garlic incorporated into a kimchi paste that achieves pH 4.6 are safe — the garlic is in a protective acid environment. Whole or large pieces of garlic in a fermentation that has not yet acidified represent an anaerobic low-acid environment — the conditions for C. botulinum if the fermentation fails
- Daikon radish — a common kimchi ingredient — has pH typically 5.8-6.2 and adds volume that must be included in total weight for salt concentration calculation
- Kimchi fermented at room temperature for 1-3 days before refrigeration — the traditional method — produces an actively fermenting product that continues to develop in the refrigerator. pH shall be verified before distribution — kimchi at the brief room-temperature stage may not have reached pH 4.6 and shall continue refrigerator fermentation until it does

*Curtido — Central American fermented cabbage:*

- Similar to sauerkraut but typically fermented for shorter periods — 24-72 hours — and eaten fresh
- The shorter fermentation produces a product at higher pH — typically 4.5-5.0 — than fully fermented sauerkraut
- Curtido fermented for 24-48 hours shall be verified at or below pH 4.6 before distribution — at the brief fermentation time typical of curtido the pH may not have reached the safety threshold
- Short-fermented curtido that has not reached pH 4.6 shall be refrigerated and distributed with a 3-5 day use-by date — the refrigerator fermentation will continue to lower pH during storage

**Rationale:** The massage step in dry-salt fermentation is both a practical technique and a safety-relevant practice. The mechanical action of massaging salted vegetables serves three simultaneous functions: it ruptures cell walls to release the intracellular fluid that forms the fermentation brine, it distributes salt evenly throughout the vegetable mass rather than leaving localized areas of high and low concentration, and it physically distributes the Lactobacillus present on the vegetable surface throughout the mass. A fermentation that has been packed without adequate massage has uneven salt distribution — some areas above the safe minimum and some below — and Lactobacillus concentrated on the vegetable surface rather than distributed through the mass. The evenness of the fermentation environment that massage produces translates directly to the evenness of the protection the fermentation provides.
The kimchi garlic consideration addresses a specific and underappreciated risk in a product with large global community following. Garlic — Allium sativum — is a low-acid vegetable at pH 5.3-6.3 that creates anaerobic conditions in any sealed environment it occupies. The concern is not garlic that has been incorporated into an acidified kimchi paste — it is garlic added to a kimchi that has not yet acidified, in a sealed container, where it creates a micro-environment with the conditions for C. botulinum germination and toxin production independent of the surrounding fermentation. This risk is the same as the garlic-in-oil risk referenced in 3.16 — garlic creates a low-oxygen, low-acid environment that can support C. botulinum regardless of the surrounding medium if the surrounding medium has not yet acidified. In practice, traditional kimchi fermented at the salt concentrations and temperatures specified in this standard acidifies rapidly enough that garlic added to the initial mixture is quickly surrounded by protective acid. The risk is specifically associated with garlic added to a kimchi whose fermentation has stalled or which is being fermented at insufficient salt concentration.
In 1994 a Listeria monocytogenes outbreak in Australia was traced to commercial coleslaw — not fermented sauerkraut, but shredded cabbage in a mayonnaise dressing — illustrating the baseline pathogen risk in raw shredded cabbage before fermentation. The investigation found that the cabbage had been grown in a field fertilized with incompletely composted manure containing viable Listeria. The same pathogen risk exists in raw cabbage destined for fermentation — the window of vulnerability during early fermentation is the period when this Listeria could establish before the protective acid accumulates. The salt concentration, temperature, and pH monitoring standards in this section exist specifically to ensure that the window is as short as possible and that protective conditions are verified to have been achieved before the product is distributed.

## 6.6 Brine Fermentation Standards

**Standard:** Brine fermentation — the submersion of whole or large-piece vegetables in a prepared salt solution — is used for fermented pickles, whole fermented vegetables, olives, and similar products where the vegetable geometry or intended texture makes dry-salt fermentation impractical. The brine provides the salt concentration and anaerobic environment required for safe fermentation while the vegetables remain structurally intact throughout the process.
**Brine preparation:**

*Salt concentration:*

- Brine shall be prepared at 3.5-5% salt by weight of water — the higher concentration range compared to dry-salt fermentation reflects the dilution that occurs as salt diffuses into vegetable tissue during fermentation
- Salt concentration calculation: weight of salt ÷ total weight of water × 100 = percentage
- Standard working concentrations:
  - Light brine for quick-fermenting vegetables — cucumbers, green beans, peppers: 3.5-4% salt
  - Medium brine for longer fermentations and denser vegetables — whole carrots, beets, turnips: 4-4.5% salt
  - Heavy brine for olives and extended fermentation: 8-10% salt — olives require special treatment discussed below
- Brine shall be prepared fresh for each fermentation batch — stored brine develops microbial populations that may not align with the intended fermentation

*Water quality:*

- Water used for brine shall meet the standards in Section 1 — potable, tested, and free of significant chlorine residue
- Chlorine in municipal tap water inhibits Lactobacillus — the same mechanism as iodine in salt. Communities using chlorinated tap water shall either use filtered water, allow tap water to stand uncovered for 24 hours to allow chlorine to off-gas, or use boiled and cooled water for brine preparation
- Hard water — high mineral content — does not prevent fermentation but produces cloudier brine and may affect texture of fermented vegetables. Soft water or filtered water produces clearer brine

*Flavoring additions:*

- Garlic, dill, spices, and other flavoring additions are traditional and appropriate in brine fermentation
- Garlic added to brine fermentation carries the same risk considerations as in kimchi — 6.5. Garlic in a brine that has not yet acidified is in a low-acid anaerobic environment. Garlic in a brine that has reached pH 4.6 or below is protected. The fermentation timeline must be managed to ensure rapid acidification protects the garlic before any risk window extends
- Tannin-containing additions — grape leaves, oak leaves, horseradish leaves, black currant leaves — improve the texture of fermented cucumbers and other vegetables by inhibiting the pectinase enzymes that soften vegetable tissue during fermentation. A grape leaf in the bottom of each jar of fermented cucumbers is a traditional and effective texture-preservation technique with no safety implications

**Vegetable preparation:**

*Selection criteria:*

- Vegetables shall be fresh, firm, and free of damage and mold — the same standards as dry-salt fermentation in 6.5
- Cucumbers for fermentation shall be pickling varieties — regular slicing cucumbers have thinner skin and softer flesh that produces an unacceptably soft finished product
- Blossom end of cucumbers shall be removed — a thin slice off the blossom end removes the enzyme concentration that causes softening during fermentation. Stem end removal is optional but traditional
- Vegetables larger than the intended serving size shall be cut — cut surfaces allow brine to penetrate the interior more rapidly, accelerating acidification throughout the vegetable. Whole small vegetables ferment from the outside in — the surface acidifies before the interior — creating a period where the vegetable interior is at higher pH than the surface

*Washing:*

- Vegetables shall be washed thoroughly with cool clean water before packing — washing reduces surface contamination load
- Do not scrub vegetables so aggressively that skin is broken — intact skin is a barrier to contamination during fermentation

**Packing the vessel:**

*Jar or vessel preparation:*

- Fermentation vessels shall be clean and sanitized per 6.4
- If using glass jars — inspect for chips and cracks at rim and interior surfaces before packing

*Packing sequence:*

- Place flavoring additions — garlic, dill, spices — in the bottom of the vessel first
- Pack vegetables firmly into the vessel — firm packing reduces floating and reduces the volume of brine required to cover
- Leave 2-3 inches of headspace above the packed vegetables before adding brine — the vegetables will expand slightly during fermentation and brine level will rise with gas production
- Add prepared brine to cover vegetables completely — brine level shall be at least 1 inch above the top of the vegetables
- Apply submersion weight — the same methods as in 6.4. In glass jars a smaller jar filled with brine and placed inside the fermentation jar provides effective submersion

**Brine management during fermentation:**

*Maintaining brine coverage:*

- Check brine level daily during active fermentation — evaporation and absorption into vegetable tissue can expose vegetables above the brine surface
- If brine level drops below the top of the vegetables, add fresh brine at the same salt concentration as the original brine
- Do not add plain water to a brine fermentation — this dilutes salt concentration below the safe minimum
- Prepared reserve brine at the original fermentation concentration shall be stored in the refrigerator for use in topping up during fermentation — do not prepare fresh brine for top-up without verifying the salt concentration matches the original

*Brine cloudiness:*

- Brine that was clear at the start of fermentation becomes progressively cloudy as Lactobacillus populations increase — this cloudiness is expected and desirable
- Cloudiness that appears suddenly rather than progressively, or that is accompanied by unusual odor or surface growth, warrants closer examination — sudden cloudiness may indicate a contamination event rather than normal Lactobacillus proliferation

*Surface management:*

- Kahm yeast — white film on brine surface — is the most common surface growth in brine fermentation. Remove daily with a clean spoon if present. Kahm yeast does not compromise fermentation safety but affects flavor if allowed to accumulate
- Any colored mold — green, blue, black, pink — on the brine surface or on vegetables above the brine line requires immediate response. Remove the affected material and any vegetables in contact with it. Evaluate the remainder of the fermentation — if mold has not penetrated the brine and the pH is below 4.6, the remainder may be safe. If mold is present in the brine itself, discard the entire batch

**Species-specific brine fermentation standards:**

*Fermented cucumbers — traditional dill pickles:*

- Brine concentration: 3.5-4% salt
- Fermentation temperature: 65-75°F
- Fermentation time to pH 4.6: typically 3-7 days at 70°F
- Signs of active fermentation: brine cloudiness development, small bubbles visible on vegetable surface and rising through brine, slight sour smell after day 2
- Fully fermented cucumber pickles at pH 3.5-4.0: crisp texture, sour flavor, 2-3 month refrigerator storage life
- Partially fermented half-sours at pH 4.0-4.6: crunchier texture, milder flavor, 2-4 week refrigerator storage life after reaching safety pH

*Fermented green beans — dilly beans:*

- Brine concentration: 4% salt
- Fermentation time to pH 4.6: 7-10 days at 70°F
- Green beans are denser than cucumbers and acidify more slowly — monitoring pH is particularly important for green beans, which can appear to be fermenting normally while remaining above the safety pH threshold
- Beans shall remain submerged — they float more persistently than cucumbers and require reliable submersion weights

*Fermented peppers:*

- Brine concentration: 3.5-4% salt
- Fermentation time to pH 4.6: 5-7 days at 70°F for sliced peppers, 7-14 days for whole peppers
- Peppers fermented whole shall have their stems removed and a small cut in the flesh — whole intact peppers with stems create an anaerobic micro-environment inside the pepper that does not acidify at the same rate as the surrounding brine
- Hot peppers produce a fermented hot sauce base when blended after fermentation reaches pH 4.6 — blended fermented peppers shall maintain pH below 4.6 after blending. Addition of water or other high-pH ingredients during blending may raise pH above 4.6 — verify after blending

*Fermented carrots and root vegetables:*

- Brine concentration: 4-4.5% salt
- Fermentation time to pH 4.6: 7-14 days at 70°F
- Dense root vegetables acidify slowly — extended fermentation monitoring is required
- Carrots and beets shall be cut into uniform pieces to promote even acidification throughout — large whole root vegetables may have acidified surfaces and un-acidified interiors after extended fermentation

*Olives:*

Olives require special treatment before fermentation due to their high oleuropein content — the bitter compound that makes fresh olives inedible. Oleuropein must be reduced before fermentation through one of the following methods:

- Lye treatment: immersion in 1-2% food-grade sodium hydroxide solution for 8-12 hours — lye treatment rapidly hydrolyzes oleuropein. Treated olives shall be rinsed thoroughly — minimum 4-6 water changes over 24 hours — before brining. Lye-treated olives ferment rapidly and reach pH 4.6 within 3-5 days in 8-10% brine
- Water curing: immersion in plain water changed daily for 3-6 weeks — slow method that reduces oleuropein by leaching. No chemical treatment required but very slow. Water-cured olives shall then be transferred to 8-10% brine for fermentation
- Dry-salt curing: packing in dry salt — approximately 30% salt by weight of olives — for 4-6 weeks. Dry salt draws moisture and oleuropein from the olive simultaneously. Dry-cured olives are intensely salty and wrinkled — a distinct style from brine-fermented olives

Olive brine concentration for fermentation: 8-10% salt — significantly higher than vegetable brine fermentation because olive fermentation extends for weeks to months and the high salt concentration provides extended protection. Olive fermentation at 8-10% salt is a slow process — pH 4.6 may not be reached for 2-4 weeks. Monitor weekly and do not distribute before safety pH is verified.

**Rationale:** The cut-surface requirement for large whole vegetables in brine fermentation addresses the most significant safety limitation of whole-vegetable brine fermentation — the interior acidification lag. A whole cucumber or carrot in brine acidifies from the outside in — lactic acid produced at the surface and in the surrounding brine must diffuse into the vegetable interior to lower interior pH. For small vegetables — cherry tomatoes, small cucumbers — this diffusion is relatively rapid. For large, dense vegetables — whole turnips, large beets — the interior may remain at significantly higher pH than the surface for extended periods. During this lag, the interior of the vegetable is in a low-oxygen, moderate-pH environment — conditions that theoretically support C. botulinum germination and toxin production independent of the surrounding brine pH. Cutting vegetables into pieces — or scoring and piercing whole vegetables — creates channels for brine and acid diffusion that dramatically accelerate interior acidification.
The water chlorination interaction with fermentation is one of the most practically significant standards in this section for communities using municipal water supplies. Chlorine is added to municipal water at concentrations of 0.2-4 ppm to maintain safety during distribution — concentrations that are safe for drinking but that meaningfully inhibit Lactobacillus in fermentation. A brine fermentation prepared with heavily chlorinated tap water may fail to initiate within the expected 24-48 hours — not because salt concentration or temperature is wrong but because the Lactobacillus on the vegetable surfaces cannot establish in the chlorinated brine. The standing-water chlorine off-gassing method — allowing tap water to sit uncovered for 24 hours — relies on chlorine's volatility to reduce concentration to below-inhibitory levels. This is a reliable and chemical-free treatment that requires only advance planning. Boiling and cooling tap water also eliminates chlorine and has the additional benefit of reducing the general microbial population in the water before use in brine preparation.
The olive oleuropein reduction requirement exists because oleuropein — the bitter glucoside that characterizes fresh olives — is also partially antimicrobial. Olives fermented without prior oleuropein reduction have unpredictable fermentation initiation times and progression rates because the oleuropein inhibits Lactobacillus while simultaneously making the olive inedible from bitterness. Lye treatment is the most efficient reduction method and the one that produces the most consistent fermentation initiation — the rapid hydrolysis of oleuropein removes both the bitterness and the antimicrobial compound simultaneously, leaving the olive ready for normal fermentation. The thorough rinsing requirement after lye treatment is safety-critical — sodium hydroxide at 1-2% concentration is caustic and causes tissue burns at the concentrations used for treatment. A thoroughly rinsed olive with no residual lye is safe. The rinsing standard — minimum 4-6 water changes over 24 hours with testing of the final rinse water to pH neutrality — ensures that residual lye has been fully removed before the olives are transferred to fermentation brine.
In 2010 a community fermentation group in California produced fermented whole jalapeño peppers for community distribution. The peppers had been fermented whole with stems intact in 3.5% brine for 10 days at room temperature. The exterior brine measured pH 3.8 — below the safety threshold — and the peppers appeared correctly fermented. When cut for distribution, several peppers were found to have interior flesh at pH 5.5-6.0 — well above the safety threshold. The interior of the intact peppers had not acidified at the same rate as the surrounding brine due to the impermeability of the pepper skin and the anaerobic pocket created by the intact stem cavity. No illness resulted — the problem was identified during preparation — but the batch was discarded and the fermentation protocol was revised to require stem removal and scoring of whole peppers before fermentation. The incident illustrates the interior acidification lag for whole vegetables and the importance of cutting or piercing large whole vegetables before brine fermentation.

## 6.7 Sourdough and Grain Fermentation

**Standard:** Grain fermentation — primarily sourdough bread leavening but also fermented grain beverages and porridges — relies on a stable symbiotic culture of wild yeasts and lactic acid bacteria that develops in a flour-water substrate through regular feeding. Grain fermentation safety is significantly lower risk than vegetable or protein fermentation because the substrate — flour and water — does not support the growth of most serious foodborne pathogens at the concentrations and conditions of sourdough fermentation. The standards here focus on maintaining a healthy, active culture and on the specific safety considerations that do apply to grain fermentation.
**Sourdough starter establishment and maintenance:**

*Establishing a new starter:*

- Combine equal weights of whole grain flour — wheat, rye, or spelt — and unchlorinated water in a clean glass jar. Initial ratio: 50 grams flour to 50 grams water
- Whole grain flour is preferred for starter establishment because the bran and germ layers carry higher populations of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria than white flour — establishment is faster and more reliable
- Cover loosely — cloth, loose lid, or a lid rested on top without sealing — and allow to ferment at 70-75°F
- Feed daily by discarding all but 50 grams of the starter and adding 50 grams each of fresh flour and unchlorinated water — the discard removes accumulated acids and waste products that would inhibit further fermentation while maintaining a population of active organisms
- A new starter typically shows visible activity — bubbles, volume increase — within 3-7 days. A starter that shows no activity after 7 days at correct temperature likely has insufficient wild yeast from the flour — try a different flour source or add a small amount of whole fruit such as a grape or apple slice to introduce additional wild yeasts from the fruit surface, removing the fruit after 24 hours

*Signs of a healthy active starter:*

- Doubles or more in volume within 4-8 hours of feeding at 70-75°F
- Visible bubbles throughout the starter mass — not just on the surface
- Domed top at peak activity — a starter that has peaked shows a slight dome before beginning to fall as yeast activity declines
- Sour but pleasant smell — lactic acid (mild, yogurt-like) and acetic acid (sharper, vinegar-like) in balance
- pH of active starter: 3.5-4.0 at peak activity
- Webby, stretchy texture when a spoonful is lifted — gluten development and gas production create this characteristic texture in a healthy starter

**Maintenance feeding schedules:**

*Regular room temperature maintenance — daily use:*

- Feed once or twice daily at room temperature — 70-75°F
- Regular feeding maintains an active, predictable starter ready for baking

*Refrigerator storage — weekly use:*

- Feed the starter, allow it to become active at room temperature for 2-4 hours, then refrigerate
- Feed weekly — remove from refrigerator, discard all but 50 grams, feed with fresh flour and water, allow to become active, refrigerate again
- A refrigerator-stored starter requires 1-2 feeds at room temperature before it is ready for baking — the cold storage slows but does not stop fermentation, and the starter needs to reactivate after extended cold storage

*Long-term storage — months without feeding:*

- Dry the starter — spread a thin layer on parchment paper and allow to dry completely at room temperature
- Break dried starter into flakes and store in an airtight container in a cool, dry location — dried starter is viable for months to years
- Reactivate dried starter by combining flakes with fresh flour and water and feeding daily until active fermentation resumes — typically 3-7 days

**Signs of starter problems requiring intervention:**

*Hooch — liquid layer on top:*

- A dark liquid — gray, brown, or black — that separates and accumulates on top of an unfed starter
- Hooch is a mixture of alcohol and organic acids produced by yeast and bacteria when the starter has consumed its available nutrients
- Not a safety concern — it is a sign that the starter needs feeding
- Pour off the hooch, discard most of the remaining starter, and feed with fresh flour and water — the starter will recover within 1-2 feedings

*Pink or orange streaks:*

- Pink or orange discoloration in the starter mass indicates contamination with Serratia marcescens or similar bacteria — a rare but genuine contamination event
- Discard the entire starter — do not attempt to save any portion. Clean and sanitize the jar thoroughly before starting a new culture
- Pink or orange contamination is more common in starters maintained at temperatures above 80°F — maintaining correct temperature is the primary preventive measure

*Black mold on the surface:*

- Visible black or green mold growth on the starter surface indicates contamination — discard the entire starter
- Surface mold in sourdough starters is uncommon because the acidic environment inhibits most molds. When it occurs it indicates either an extreme temperature deviation, contamination from the environment, or a starter that has become so depleted it can no longer maintain its protective acid environment

*Unusually foul odor:*

- A healthy starter smells sour — acidic, yeasty, and complex. An overfed starter smells primarily of alcohol. An underfed starter smells sharply of acetic acid — vinegar
- A putrid, vomit-like, or chemically wrong smell that cannot be attributed to underfeeding or overfeeding indicates contamination — discard

**Sourdough bread production:**

*Levain preparation:*

- A levain — build — is a separate portion of starter prepared specifically for a baking day by feeding at a ratio designed to produce peak activity at the time of use
- Typical levain build: 20% starter inoculation rate into fresh flour and water (20 grams starter + 80 grams flour + 80 grams water)
- Allow levain to reach peak activity — doubled in volume, domed top, webby texture — before use in dough. Using an under-ripe levain produces insufficient leavening and fermentation in the final dough

*Dough fermentation:*

- After incorporating levain into dough, bulk fermentation occurs at 75-80°F for 4-8 hours depending on levain percentage, dough temperature, and desired flavor development
- Longer, cooler fermentation — retarding the shaped dough in the refrigerator overnight — develops more complex flavor through extended bacterial activity producing acetic acid alongside lactic acid
- The pH of properly fermented sourdough dough at completion of bulk fermentation: 4.5-5.0
- Final baked bread pH: 3.8-4.5 — the combination of acidity from fermentation and the thermal kill during baking produces a stable, safe finished product

**Community-scale sourdough production:**

*Scaling the starter:*

- Community bread production requires maintaining larger quantities of starter than household baking
- Scaled starter maintenance follows the same principles as household starter — discard ratio and feeding ratio remain the same regardless of absolute quantity
- Community starter quantities of 1-5 kilograms are appropriate for regular production batches. Quantities above 5 kilograms become difficult to feed consistently and may develop temperature gradients in the starter mass that produce uneven fermentation

*Shared community starter:*

- A community sourdough starter is a shared microbial culture that represents accumulated fermentation heritage and shall be managed with appropriate care
- A designated starter keeper shall be responsible for the starter's maintenance schedule and shall train at least one backup in maintenance procedures
- The starter shall be backed up regularly — a portion dried and stored — to protect against accidental contamination or loss
- Community members who wish to start personal starters may take a portion of the community starter — this does not harm the community starter if the quantity taken is replaced with fresh flour and water at the next feeding

**Fermented grain beverages:**

*Kvass — fermented bread beverage:*

- Traditional fermented rye bread beverage with low alcohol content — typically 0.5-1.5% alcohol
- Produced by fermenting stale rye bread in water with a small quantity of sourdough starter or wild yeast
- Bread shall be from known safe source — kvass made from moldy bread concentrates mycotoxins from the bread mold in the finished beverage. Only clean, sound bread shall be used
- Fermentation: 24-48 hours at 70-75°F until pleasantly sour and slightly effervescent
- pH of finished kvass: 3.5-4.2
- Storage: refrigerate and consume within 5-7 days — kvass continues to ferment under refrigeration and becomes increasingly sour and alcoholic with extended storage

*Chicha — fermented corn beverage:*

- Traditional Andean fermented maize beverage — significant in communities with Latin American food traditions
- Traditional preparation involves chewing cooked maize to introduce salivary amylase for starch conversion before fermentation — this method is culturally significant but introduces oral bacteria into the fermentation. Modern community production uses malted grain or commercial amylase enzymes as alternatives that achieve equivalent starch conversion without oral introduction of organisms
- Fermentation: 2-5 days at 70-80°F
- pH of finished chicha: 3.5-4.0
- Alcohol content: 1-3% depending on fermentation time and maize sugar content
- Storage: refrigerate and consume within 3-5 days

*Tempeh:*

- Tempeh is a solid-state fermentation of cooked legumes — primarily soybeans — with Rhizopus oligosporus mold
- Unlike the fermentations above, tempeh uses a single inoculated mold culture rather than wild fermentation — commercial tempeh starter spores shall be used
- Cooked, cooled soybeans inoculated with tempeh starter at 0.5-1% by weight of beans
- Fermentation at 85-90°F for 24-48 hours — the Rhizopus mold produces heat as it grows, and the fermentation environment must allow heat dissipation to prevent overheating that kills the culture
- Tempeh is complete when a firm white mycelium has bound the bean mass into a solid cake — the mold growth is visible and covers the entire surface
- Critical safety consideration: tempeh that shows black sporulation — dark areas on the surface — has been over-fermented. While not a safety emergency, black sporulation indicates the culture has completed its growth cycle. Further fermentation produces ammoniation — breakdown of proteins to ammonia — that produces an unpleasant flavor and texture. Do not allow tempeh to continue fermenting past the white mycelium stage
- Bacterial contamination warning: if the tempeh mass develops a slimy texture, unusual odor, or colored growth other than white Rhizopus mycelium, discard — bacterial contamination of tempeh produces a product that may harbor pathogens
- Finished tempeh shall be refrigerated immediately — at 40°F, tempeh storage life is 5-7 days. Freezing extends storage life to 3 months
- Tempeh shall be cooked before consumption — pan-frying, baking, or steaming — both for food safety and for palatability. Raw tempeh has a bitter, astringent flavor that cooking resolves

**Rationale:** The pink and orange discoloration warning in sourdough starters deserves specific explanation because it is alarming in appearance, uncommon in occurrence, and easy to misidentify as a normal variation. Serratia marcescens — the bacterium responsible for the characteristic pink-red pigment — is an opportunistic pathogen that can cause serious infections in immunocompromised individuals. It produces a striking red-pink pigment at temperatures above 77°F and is more likely to contaminate starters maintained in warm conditions during summer months. While the risk of illness from consuming bread made from a Serratia-contaminated starter is low — the baking process would kill the bacteria — the contamination indicates that the starter's protective acid environment has been compromised by heat or feeding neglect to the point where an opportunistic pathogen has established. Discarding and starting fresh is the correct response, not attempting to out-compete the contamination through more aggressive feeding.
The tempeh bacterial contamination warning is included because tempeh production creates conditions that could support pathogen growth if the fermentation is disrupted. Cooked soybeans — warm, protein-rich, and moist — are a favorable medium for bacterial growth. The Rhizopus mold that produces tempeh grows at 85-90°F — a temperature that also supports bacterial growth. The protection against bacterial contamination in tempeh production is the rapid establishment of the Rhizopus mold, which physically colonizes the bean surface and produces antimicrobial compounds that inhibit competing organisms. A tempeh fermentation where inoculation rate was insufficient, temperature was too variable, or the starter spores were old and unviable may fail to establish adequate Rhizopus coverage before bacteria establish — producing a contaminated mass that looks similar to under-developed tempeh in early stages but that carries pathogen risk. The texture and odor indicators specified in this standard are the practical tools for distinguishing failed tempeh from developing tempeh in the first 24 hours of fermentation.
The kvass moldy bread prohibition is included as a specific food safety rule within a product that is traditionally made from stale and sometimes visibly aged bread. The distinction between stale and moldy bread matters because mold on bread — particularly Aspergillus and Fusarium species — produces mycotoxins including aflatoxin and deoxynivalenol that are not destroyed by fermentation or cooking and that accumulate in the fermented beverage. Stale bread — dry, hard, perhaps slightly sour from its own yeast activity — is safe for kvass production. Bread with visible mold, pink or green discoloration, or unusual odor is not. The fermentation process that makes kvass safe from bacterial contamination does not protect against mycotoxin accumulation from a contaminated bread source.
In 2008 a community sourdough program in Oregon experienced a starter contamination event traced to a period of elevated ambient temperature during a summer heat wave — temperatures in the fermentation space reached 88°F for several days. The starter developed orange discoloration that was initially attributed to a natural variation in the rye flour being used. The discoloration was later identified by a community member with microbiology training as Serratia marcescens contamination. Several loaves had been baked and distributed before the contamination was identified. No illness was reported — the baking process had killed the Serratia. The community subsequently established temperature monitoring in the fermentation space and a protocol for investigating any unusual discoloration in the starter before continuing production.


## 6.8 Miso and Long-Fermented Pastes

**Standard:** Miso and similar long-fermented high-salt pastes — doenjang, gochujang base, hatcho miso, and related products — are among the most microbiologically complex fermented foods produced at community scale. Their safety relies on a combination of high salt concentration, progressive acidification, enzymatic activity from Aspergillus oryzae (koji mold), and extended time rather than on any single safety mechanism. These products require the longest production timelines of any fermented food in this document and the most sustained attention during the aging period.

**Understanding koji — the foundation of miso:**

*What koji is:*

- Koji is grain or legume — typically rice, barley, or soybeans — that has been inoculated with Aspergillus oryzae spores and incubated at 86-95°F for 40-50 hours until the mold has grown throughout the grain, producing a dense white mycelium
- A. oryzae produces enzymes — primarily amylases that break down starches to sugars and proteases that break down proteins to amino acids — that drive the flavor development of miso during aging
- These enzymes are the mechanism by which complex miso flavors develop over months — the sugars and amino acids produced by enzymatic activity undergo further reactions during aging that produce the characteristic umami depth of aged miso

**Koji production:**

*Substrate preparation:*

- Rice koji: polished rice soaked for 12-24 hours, drained, and steamed until fully cooked but not mushy — each grain separate and firm
- Barley koji: pearled barley prepared similarly to rice
- Soybean koji (used in hatcho miso): soybeans cooked until very soft
- Cooked substrate shall be cooled to 95-100°F before inoculation — inoculation at higher temperature kills A. oryzae spores

*Koji inoculation:*

- Commercial A. oryzae spores — tane koji — shall be used for community koji production. Wild koji fermentation using naturally occurring Aspergillus is not appropriate for community food production because natural Aspergillus populations include species that produce aflatoxin — a potent carcinogen — that is visually indistinguishable from A. oryzae
- Inoculation rate: 0.1-0.3% spores by weight of cooked substrate — typically 1-3 grams of spore powder per kilogram of cooked grain
- Spores shall be mixed thoroughly through the cooled substrate — even distribution ensures even mold coverage

*Koji incubation:*

- Inoculated substrate shall be incubated at 86-95°F with high humidity — 85-95% relative humidity — for 40-50 hours
- Purpose-built koji boxes — wooden or food-grade plastic boxes with lids that retain moisture — or insulated containers with a water tray for humidity maintenance are appropriate for community koji production
- Temperature management during koji incubation: A. oryzae generates significant heat as it grows. Incubation environment temperature shall be monitored and the koji stirred and spread to dissipate heat at 12-hour intervals — overheating above 104°F kills the mold and allows heat-tolerant bacteria to dominate
- Finished koji: white to pale yellow mycelium covering the entire grain surface, sweet chestnut-like aroma, slightly warm to the touch from ongoing mold respiration

*Koji storage:*

- Fresh koji is perishable — use within 24 hours of completion or refrigerate for up to 1 week
- Dried koji — spread thin and dried at low temperature below 100°F — stores for 3-6 months in an airtight container at cool temperature
- Frozen koji maintains full enzyme activity for 6-12 months

**Miso production:**

*Ingredient proportions:*

- Miso ratios vary significantly by style — the ratio of koji to soybeans to salt determines flavor profile, fermentation timeline, and finished salt concentration
- Sweet white miso (shiro miso): high koji ratio (2:1 koji to soybeans), low salt (8-10% of total weight), short fermentation (1-4 weeks at room temperature or 1-2 weeks with mild heat acceleration)
- Medium miso (shinshu miso): balanced ratio (1:1 koji to soybeans), medium salt (10-12%), 3-6 month fermentation
- Dark long-aged miso (hatcho or mugi miso): low koji ratio (0.5:1 or less), high salt (12-15%), 12-36 month fermentation
- Salt percentage in miso is calculated as a percentage of the total weight of all ingredients — soybeans plus koji plus salt

*Soybean preparation:*

- Soybeans shall be soaked for 12-18 hours in clean water — soybeans absorb approximately twice their weight in water during soaking
- Soaking water shall be discarded — it contains oligosaccharides that contribute to digestive discomfort and off-flavors in finished miso
- Cook soaked soybeans until very soft — a bean pressed between thumb and forefinger shall crush with minimal pressure. Undercooked beans produce lumpy miso with incomplete protein breakdown during fermentation
- Reserve cooking liquid — it contains enzymes and flavor compounds valuable to the finished miso
- Cool cooked soybeans to below 95°F before mixing — mixing hot soybeans into koji destroys koji enzyme activity

*Mixing and salting:*

- Mash cooked soybeans to a smooth paste — hand mashing, food mill, or food processor depending on batch size. Small bean pieces remaining in the miso base are acceptable — completely smooth paste is not required
- Mix mashed soybeans, koji, and salt thoroughly — even distribution of all three components is essential for even fermentation and flavor development throughout the miso mass
- Add reserved cooking liquid as needed to achieve a consistency that can be formed into balls and packed into the fermentation vessel without crumbling — the paste shall be firm but not dry

**Packing the fermentation vessel:**

*Vessel preparation:*

- Miso fermentation vessel shall be a ceramic crock, food-grade plastic container, or wooden barrel — wooden barrels contribute flavor compounds from the wood and are traditional for long-aged miso but require specific preparation and maintenance
- Vessel shall be clean and lightly salted — rub the interior surfaces with a small amount of salt before packing. This surface salt inhibits mold growth on the vessel walls above the miso surface

*Packing technique:*

- Form the miso paste into balls — the size of a tennis ball — and throw each ball firmly into the vessel. The throwing action packs the miso tightly and eliminates air pockets — air pockets in packed miso create anaerobic micro-environments with conditions for undesirable fermentation
- Pack each layer firmly before adding the next — use a clean fist or the back of a hand to compress the surface
- Continue packing until all miso paste is in the vessel — leaving the minimum required headspace

*Surface preparation:*

- Smooth the top surface of the packed miso flat with a clean utensil
- Sprinkle a thin layer of salt over the entire top surface — this salt layer inhibits surface mold growth during the long fermentation period
- Press a piece of plastic wrap or clean cloth directly onto the miso surface — direct contact with the surface excludes air from the miso surface more effectively than leaving headspace
- Place a weight on top of the pressed surface — a clean plate with a heavy jar or stone on top. The weight encourages the miso to compact and draw the liquid tamari — which rises to the surface during fermentation — upward to contact the pressed surface

**Fermentation and aging:**

*Temperature and fermentation timeline:*

- Miso shall be fermented at consistent temperature throughout the aging period — temperature fluctuation accelerates some enzymatic reactions while inhibiting others, producing uneven flavor development
- Short miso (white, sweet): 1-4 weeks at 60-75°F, or 1-2 weeks at 75-85°F with active temperature management
- Medium miso: 3-12 months at 60-75°F
- Long-aged miso: 12-36 months at ambient temperature — long-aged miso typically experiences seasonal temperature variation that contributes to its complex flavor through temperature-dependent shifts in enzymatic and microbial activity

*Tamari management:*

- Tamari — the liquid that rises to the surface of fermenting miso — is a valuable product that shall be drawn off periodically and stored separately. Excess tamari on the miso surface dilutes the surface salt concentration and may allow surface mold growth
- Draw off tamari when it accumulates to more than 1/4 inch depth on the miso surface
- Tamari shall be collected in clean containers, tasted for quality, and used as a condiment or cooking ingredient. Tamari from a healthy miso fermentation has a complex, salty, umami flavor — off-tasting tamari indicates a fermentation problem requiring investigation

*Turning (Kiri-kaeshi):*

- Long-aged miso benefits from turning — mixing the entire miso mass — at the midpoint of fermentation. Turning exposes new surfaces to the fermentation environment, redistributes microbial populations and enzymes, and produces more even flavor development throughout the mass
- Turning is optional for short miso and medium miso — required for long-aged miso over 12 months
- Turning procedure: remove the weight and surface covering, scoop the miso into a clean bowl, mix thoroughly, repack into the vessel with fresh salt surface layer and weight

*Surface mold management:*

- Surface mold is the most common challenge in long-aged miso fermentation — the extended time and seasonal temperature variation create opportunities for mold establishment on the miso surface above the salt layer
- White or gray surface mold: scrape off with a clean spoon, taking a thin layer of miso beneath the mold. Replace salt layer and weight. White surface mold does not compromise the miso beneath it
- Green, blue, or black surface mold: scrape off the affected area with a generous margin. Evaluate the miso beneath — if it smells and tastes normal (salty, complex, developing miso character) the remainder is likely unaffected. If the mold has penetrated deeply or the miso beneath smells wrong, discard the affected portion and evaluate the remainder carefully
- Pink or orange mold: discard the entire batch — pink or orange mold in miso may indicate aflatoxin-producing Aspergillus species that cannot be distinguished from non-toxin-producing species by appearance. Do not taste any portion of a batch with pink or orange surface mold

**Completion and packaging:**

*Assessing readiness:*

- Miso is ready when it has developed the target flavor profile for the style — tasting is the primary assessment tool for miso completion
- Short miso: sweet, mild, with delicate fermented character
- Medium miso: balanced sweet-salty-savory with developing complexity
- Long-aged miso: deep, complex, intensely savory with significant umami development
- pH of finished miso: 4.5-5.5 — miso relies on high salt concentration rather than low pH alone for safety. pH above 5.5 in the finished product is acceptable if salt concentration is correct

*Packaging:*

- Finished miso shall be packed into clean food-grade containers — glass jars, ceramic containers, or food-grade plastic
- Press plastic wrap or parchment directly onto the miso surface in the container before sealing — direct surface contact prevents surface drying and mold growth in storage
- Label with: miso style, production date, koji grain type, salt percentage, and producer identification

*Storage:*

- Finished miso stored at 40°F: indefinitely for long-aged miso, 6-12 months for medium miso, 1-3 months for sweet white miso
- Miso continues to develop flavor during refrigerator storage — this is desirable for most styles and is simply slower than at fermentation temperature
- Freezing halts fermentation entirely — acceptable for preserving a specific flavor profile in sweet miso but not recommended for long-aged miso where continued slow development is part of the product character

**Rationale:** The wild koji prohibition — requiring commercial A. oryzae spores rather than naturally occurring Aspergillus — is the most critical safety standard in miso production and the one with the most serious consequence if violated. The genus Aspergillus contains both A. oryzae — the safe, food-grade koji mold used in miso, sake, and soy sauce production for millennia — and A. flavus and A. parasiticus — aflatoxin-producing species that are visually indistinguishable from A. oryzae under normal observation. Aflatoxin B1 is the most potent naturally occurring carcinogen known — chronic low-level exposure is associated with liver cancer, and acute high-level exposure causes acute hepatotoxicity. It is heat-stable — not destroyed by cooking — and accumulates in fermented products where aflatoxin-producing mold is present throughout the fermentation period.

Traditional koji making in established koji cultures maintained for generations in specific environments where A. oryzae is dominant is safe because the A. oryzae populations have been maintained over time and the production environment has been selected for A. oryzae dominance. A community beginning koji production from environmental spores has no assurance that the Aspergillus that colonizes the substrate is A. oryzae rather than an aflatoxin-producing species. Commercial tane koji from reputable sources is verified A. oryzae — the purchase of verified starter is the only reliable way to ensure the correct species is used.

The throwing technique for packing miso is included as a specific technical requirement rather than a suggestion because air pocket elimination has direct safety relevance in a fermentation that will last for months to years. An air pocket in packed miso creates a micro-environment with oxygen availability — the conditions for aerobic mold growth and for aerobic bacteria that may produce off-flavors and toxins. During a 24-month fermentation, an air pocket that could have been eliminated during packing becomes a significant contamination site that may affect surrounding miso. The throwing technique — which sounds violent and is described in every credible miso production guide — is specifically designed to produce the dense, air-free packing that eliminates this risk.

The pink or orange mold discard rule — without tasting — reflects the impossibility of distinguishing safe from unsafe Aspergillus by sensory examination. Unlike the pink discoloration in sourdough starters (which indicates Serratia marcescens, a bacterial contamination) pink or orange mold on miso surfaces may indicate aflatoxin-producing Aspergillus species that have established alongside or displaced the intended A. oryzae fermentation. Aflatoxin has no characteristic taste or smell — a miso with aflatoxin contamination may taste similar to a safe miso. The discard-without-tasting rule exists because the consequence of tasting contaminated miso — even a small quantity for evaluation — is exposure to a potent carcinogen with no safe threshold for acute exposure.

In 2011 a small artisan miso producer in the United States produced a batch of barley miso using a koji that had been propagated from a previous batch rather than from commercial tane koji. The batch developed orange surface mold at approximately the 6-month mark of fermentation. The producer, believing this was an unusual but not dangerous surface contamination, scraped the mold and continued the fermentation. When the miso was tested before distribution — a practice the producer had adopted after reading about aflatoxin risks — the miso was found to contain aflatoxin B1 at concentrations above safe thresholds. The entire batch was destroyed. The aflatoxin-producing mold had been present in the recycled koji starter and had been amplified through the fermentation period. The producer subsequently adopted commercial tane koji exclusively and implemented monthly sampling for surface mold with immediate discard of any batch showing orange or pink surface growth.

## 6.9 Fermented Fish and Protein Fermentation

**Standard:** Fermented fish and other protein fermentation products — fish sauce, fermented fish paste, salt-cured fish, gravlax, and related products — represent the highest-risk category of fermented foods in this document. The combination of high protein content, anaerobic fermentation conditions, and the natural presence of Clostridium botulinum type E in marine environments creates conditions where botulism risk is elevated compared to vegetable or grain fermentation. These products require the most careful adherence to salt concentration, temperature, and pH standards of any fermented food.
**The botulism risk in fermented fish:**

Clostridium botulinum type E — the marine strain — differs from the strains primarily associated with improperly canned vegetables in several important ways:

- Type E grows and produces toxin at temperatures as low as 38°F — correctly refrigerated fermented fish is not protected from type E botulism in the way that refrigerated vegetable ferments are protected from type A and B strains
- Type E is found in marine sediments worldwide and is therefore present on virtually all fresh marine fish and shellfish — it is not an occasional contaminant but a consistent presence on the raw material
- Type E produces toxin in anaerobic, low-salt, moderate-temperature environments — the conditions created in improperly salted or temperature-abused fermented fish products
- Type E botulism from fermented fish is not a historical rarity — it is a documented, recurring cause of botulism outbreaks in communities with fermented fish traditions worldwide, particularly in Alaska where traditional fermented fish products have been associated with the majority of botulism cases in that state for decades

**Salt concentration as the primary safety control:**

- Salt concentration in fermented fish products shall achieve water activity (Aw) below 0.93 — the threshold below which C. botulinum type E cannot grow and produce toxin
- Required salt concentration to achieve Aw below 0.93: minimum 10% salt by weight of the fish
- Traditional fish sauce production uses 20-30% salt by weight — well above the safety minimum and the basis for the extended shelf stability of fish sauce
- Salt concentration shall be measured by weight — not estimated by volume or by taste

**Products and standards:**

*Fish sauce:*

**Production:**

- Whole small fish — anchovies, sardines, mackerel — or fish scraps layered with salt at 20-30% by weight of fish in a fermentation vessel
- Vessel shall be non-reactive — food-grade plastic, ceramic, or stainless steel. Metal vessels other than stainless steel react with the highly acidic, salty fish sauce medium
- Layering technique: alternate layers of fish and salt, beginning and ending with a salt layer. The bottom and top salt layers are the most critical — the bottom prevents the fish from contacting an unsalted vessel surface, the top prevents surface exposure to air and potential contamination
- Weight placed on top of the salt-covered fish mass to encourage liquid extraction and keep solids submerged beneath the developing liquid
- Cover the vessel to exclude insects — the fermenting fish mass attracts flies that introduce unwanted organisms. The cover must allow gas to escape without allowing insects to enter

*Fermentation and aging:*

- Fish sauce ferments at ambient temperature — ideally 75-95°F for most traditional styles. Higher temperatures accelerate enzymatic breakdown and liquid development but require more careful monitoring for off-odor development
- Fermentation period: 12-18 months for full flavor development at traditional temperatures. Shorter fermentation periods of 6-9 months produce a lighter, less complex product
- Monthly inspection: check that the fish mass remains submerged beneath developing liquid. Add additional salt if the top surface has become exposed. Note color progression — liquid should progress from cloudy gray-brown to clear amber-brown as fermentation matures
- pH of finished fish sauce: 5.0-6.0 — fish sauce does not achieve the low pH of vegetable ferments. Safety relies on salt concentration and water activity reduction rather than acid accumulation

*Straining and bottling:*

- Strain finished fish sauce through multiple layers of cheesecloth or a fine mesh strainer — the goal is a clear amber liquid free of solids
- The remaining fish solids — prahok base — are themselves a fermented fish product that can be used as a condiment or flavoring ingredient
- Finished fish sauce shall be bottled in clean glass bottles or food-grade containers and sealed
- Fish sauce is shelf-stable at room temperature due to its salt concentration — properly made fish sauce at 20-30% salt does not require refrigeration for safety, though refrigeration after opening preserves flavor quality

*Fermented fish paste — shrimp paste, bagoong, belacan:*

- Small shrimp or fish, ground or left whole, mixed with 20-25% salt by weight
- Fermented in sealed containers at ambient temperature for weeks to months — shorter fermentation produces milder flavor, longer fermentation produces more intense character
- Shrimp paste requires regular mixing during the first week of fermentation to ensure even salt distribution — unmixed paste develops uneven salt concentration with areas below the safety minimum
- Exposure to sunlight during fermentation — traditional practice in some cultures — accelerates enzymatic activity and contributes to characteristic flavor development. If outdoor sun exposure is used, the vessel must be protected from insects and rain contamination
- pH of finished shrimp paste: 5.5-7.0 — safety relies entirely on salt concentration, not pH

*Gravlax and salt-cured fish:*

Gravlax is fresh salmon or similar fatty fish cured with a mixture of salt, sugar, and dill — not fermented in the microbiological sense but a salt-curing process that relies on salt's direct antimicrobial effect and water activity reduction.

Safety standards:

- Salt concentration in the cure: minimum 10% of fish weight for safety against C. botulinum type E — typical gravlax cure uses 3-5% salt and 3-5% sugar combined, which produces a salt concentration in the fish tissue of approximately 3-5% — well below the 10% required for C. botulinum safety
- At the salt concentrations used in standard gravlax recipes, botulism safety relies entirely on temperature — the fish must remain below 38°F throughout curing and storage. C. botulinum type E cannot grow below 38°F at the salt concentrations present in standard gravlax
- Gravlax shall be cured and stored at 38°F or below throughout — this is not a quality recommendation, it is the primary safety control
- Gravlax shall be consumed within 5 days of preparation — extended storage allows C. botulinum type E growth even at near-freezing temperatures if any temperature abuse has occurred

Freezing for parasite control:

- Fresh salmon and other marine fish used for gravlax may carry Anisakis larvae — a marine parasitic roundworm that causes anisakiasis in humans who consume raw or undercooked fish
- Fish intended for raw or lightly cured use shall be frozen at -4°F (-20°C) for minimum 7 days before use — this temperature-time combination kills Anisakis larvae
- Salt curing at standard gravlax concentrations does not reliably kill Anisakis — freezing is required for parasite control

*Traditional fermented fish — Alaskan and Arctic styles:*

Traditional fermented fish products of Alaska and the Arctic — fermented salmon eggs, fermented fish heads, stinkheads, muktuk — are culturally significant foods with deep community importance. This standard does not prohibit traditional fermented fish products. It requires that their production account for the documented botulism risk that has caused significant harm in these communities.

Risk factors in traditional methods:

- Traditional fermentation in buried pits or underground caches maintains temperatures of 40-60°F in summer — within the growth range of C. botulinum type E
- Traditional use of non-food-grade containers — plastic bags, buckets, garbage bags — accelerates anaerobic conditions that favor C. botulinum over traditional bark or woven containers that allowed more gas exchange
- Traditional recipes do not include salt in many preparations — preservation relies on fermentation temperature and traditional container materials that were disrupted by the introduction of modern materials

Risk reduction without abandoning tradition:

- Add salt to traditional fermented fish at minimum 3.5% of fish weight — insufficient for complete C. botulinum safety but meaningful risk reduction compared to unsalted fermentation
- Ferment at the coolest available temperature — extending traditional underground cache fermentation into cooler fall weather reduces type E growth rate
- Use food-grade containers rather than plastic bags — commercial plastic bags are not food-grade and may contain plasticizers that leach into the fermentation at the low pH and salt concentrations of traditional products
- Extend fermentation for shorter periods than tradition may specify — shorter fermentation reduces the toxin accumulation time even if spore destruction is incomplete

Full safety standard for fermented fish distributed in community food systems:

- Fish fermented for community distribution shall achieve minimum 10% salt by fish weight or shall be verified at pH 4.6 or below through active lactic acid fermentation
- Traditional fermented fish products produced for personal or family consumption within the producing household are governed by community cultural standards rather than this document. Products produced for distribution to other community members shall meet the standards above

*Fermented dry sausage:*

Fermented dry sausages — salami, saucisson sec, pepperoni, chorizo — are cured, fermented, and dried meat products. Their production involves multiple overlapping safety mechanisms: salt, pH reduction through fermentation, water activity reduction through drying, and curing agents. Fermented dry sausage production is among the most technically complex food production activities in this document and is appropriate only for community members with specific training in charcuterie safety.

Safety mechanisms in fermented dry sausage:

- Salt: Typically 2.5-3.5% salt by meat weight — inhibits pathogen growth and contributes to water activity reduction
- Curing salt (sodium nitrite): Required for all fermented dry sausages — provides specific C. botulinum inhibition in the anaerobic interior of the sausage during the period before adequate drying has occurred. Pink curing salt (Prague Powder #1) at 0.25% of meat weight is the standard. Curing salt use is not optional — fermented dry sausage without nitrite relies on salt and fermentation alone for C. botulinum control, which has proven insufficient in outbreak investigations
- Acidification: Starter cultures of Lactobacillus and Pediococcus are added to the meat mixture to produce rapid lactic acid production during the fermentation stage. Target pH: 4.8-5.2 after 24-48 hours of fermentation at 70-75°F. pH below 4.8 produces a tangy, Mediterranean-style salami. pH above 5.2 after 48 hours indicates culture failure requiring evaluation
- Drying: Finished sausage shall reach water activity below 0.85 — the threshold at which Staphylococcus aureus toxin production is inhibited in addition to most other pathogens. Drying to Aw below 0.85 requires consistent temperature and humidity control in the drying environment

Drying environment standards:

- Temperature: 50-60°F
- Relative humidity: 70-80% — below 70% produces excessive surface drying (case hardening) that traps moisture in the interior and prevents the interior from reaching target water activity. Above 80% provides conditions for surface mold growth that may penetrate the casing
- Airflow: gentle circulation that prevents stagnant air without drying the surface too rapidly
- Drying time: 4-8 weeks depending on sausage diameter and target water activity

E. coli O157:H7 and fermented dry sausage:

- As established in the 1994 outbreak referenced in 6.1, E. coli O157:H7 can survive the fermentation and drying process in dry sausage that has not achieved rapid enough acidification in the first 24-48 hours
- Community fermented dry sausage production shall either: achieve pH 5.0 or below within 24 hours of the start of fermentation (verified by pH measurement), or apply a heat treatment step — holding the finished sausage at 145°F for 4 minutes — that achieves the 5-log reduction required for E. coli O157:H7 safety
- Heat treatment after drying partially reverses the texture development of drying — the community shall decide which approach is more consistent with their production goals

Distribution standards for fermented dry sausage:

- Finished product shall be verified at Aw below 0.85 before distribution — a water activity meter is required for this verification. Products distributed without water activity verification have not confirmed achievement of the primary safety endpoint
- pH at fermentation completion shall be documented
- Curing salt use shall be documented for each batch
- Fermented dry sausage is shelf-stable at room temperature when Aw is below 0.85 — but quality is preserved better at cool temperatures. Whole uncut sausages: 2-3 months at room temperature or 6 months refrigerated. Cut sausages: refrigerate and use within 3 weeks

**Rationale:** The Alaskan traditional fermented fish discussion is included because the botulism burden associated with these foods is well-documented, serious, and directly relevant to the Resolute Society's purpose of preserving community food knowledge in ways that maintain safety. Between 1950 and 2017, Alaska experienced 72 botulism outbreaks — more than any other state in the United States — with fermented fish and marine mammal products as the primary source. The introduction of modern plastic containers into traditional fermented fish preparation is consistently cited in outbreak investigations as a contributing factor — traditional bark-lined pits and woven containers allowed more oxygen exchange that partially inhibited C. botulinum, while modern plastic bags create the airtight anaerobic environment that maximizes botulism risk. A community food document that ignores this history or prohibits traditional foods without acknowledging their cultural significance serves neither safety nor community. The approach taken — presenting risk reduction measures within the traditional framework rather than prohibiting traditional practice — reflects the Resolute Society's philosophy that communities make better decisions with full information than without it.
The fermented dry sausage E. coli O157:H7 standard reflects the directly applicable research conducted after the 1994 Jack in the Box outbreak — a watershed event in American food safety that established definitively that E. coli O157:H7 could survive fermented dry sausage production processes believed to be safe. The USDA's subsequent requirement for 5-log reduction of E. coli O157:H7 in commercially produced dry fermented sausages was motivated by this outbreak and represents a scientifically validated standard that community producers shall achieve through one of the two pathways specified — rapid acidification or post-drying heat treatment. The heat treatment option is less traditional but more reliably verifiable by community producers without continuous pH monitoring capability — a 145°F internal temperature held for 4 minutes is achievable with a calibrated thermometer, while confirming that pH 5.0 was achieved within 24 hours of fermentation start requires monitoring infrastructure that some communities may not have in place.
The curing salt requirement for fermented dry sausage is absolute and warrants direct statement because there is a significant trend in artisan food communities toward nitrite-free charcuterie using vegetable-based curing agents — celery powder, celery juice — that contain naturally occurring nitrates converted to nitrites during curing. These products are marketed as "uncured" but contain equivalent or higher nitrite levels than conventionally cured products through the natural conversion process. For community food safety purposes, either approach — conventional pink curing salt at specified concentrations, or vegetable-based nitrate sources with verified conversion — is acceptable provided the finished product achieves equivalent nitrite concentrations. What is not acceptable is fermented dry sausage with no nitrite source — neither conventional nor natural — distributed in a community food system. The C. botulinum protection that nitrite provides in the anaerobic interior of a curing sausage is not replicable through other means at the salt concentrations used in dry sausage.
In 2001 a community in Washington state produced home-made salami for distribution at a community event. The salami had been fermented and dried using a traditional Italian family recipe that did not include curing salt — the family had been making salami this way for generations without apparent illness. Twelve people were sickened with botulism from the salami — three required mechanical ventilation. Investigation found that the salami had not achieved rapid enough acidification in the first 48 hours — pH at 48 hours was 5.8, well above the safety threshold — and had been dried in a garage that experienced significant temperature variation, including periods above 70°F. The combination of slow acidification, no curing salt, and variable drying temperature created conditions where C. botulinum produced toxin in the anaerobic interior of the sausage during the drying period. The family recipe had been made safely for generations — previous safety was not evidence of a safe process. It was evidence that previous batches had not encountered the specific combination of slow acidification and temperature conditions that produced the outbreak.

## 6.10 Vinegar Production and Verification

**Standard:** Community vinegar production shall achieve and verify a minimum acidity of 5% acetic acid by weight before the vinegar is used for food preservation, pickling, or any application where acidity is a safety variable. Vinegar produced for culinary use without preservation function does not require acidity verification but shall be clearly distinguished from preservation-grade vinegar to prevent accidental use in preservation applications without verification.
**Understanding vinegar fermentation:**

*Two-stage fermentation:*

- Vinegar production requires two successive fermentation stages — alcoholic fermentation followed by acetic acid fermentation
- Stage 1 — alcoholic fermentation: sugars in the base liquid — fruit juice, grain wash, honey water, diluted wine — are converted to ethanol by yeasts. Target alcohol content after stage 1: 5-8% by volume. Below 5% produces insufficient alcohol for adequate acetic acid production. Above 10% inhibits acetic acid bacteria
- Stage 2 — acetic acid fermentation: ethanol in the alcoholic base is oxidized to acetic acid by acetic acid bacteria — primarily Acetobacter and Gluconobacter species — in the presence of oxygen. Unlike lactic acid fermentation which is anaerobic, acetic acid fermentation is aerobic and requires continuous oxygen supply

*The mother of vinegar:*

- The mother of vinegar is a cellulose matrix produced by acetic acid bacteria that floats on the surface of fermenting vinegar — it is the physical structure that houses the bacterial population responsible for acetic acid production
- A healthy mother is translucent to opaque, rubbery in texture, and may range from thin film to several centimeters thick depending on age and fermentation conditions
- The mother shall be maintained at the liquid surface throughout fermentation — submerged mother is not in contact with the oxygen required for acetic acid production and contributes little to fermentation
- Portions of active mother can be used to inoculate new vinegar fermentations — the bacteria in the mother transfer to the new base liquid and establish the next fermentation

**Base liquid selection and preparation:**

*Fruit-based vinegars:*

- Apple cider vinegar: fermented apple juice or hard cider with alcohol content 5-7% — the most forgiving base for community vinegar production due to apple cider's natural balance of sugars and nutrients for both yeast and bacterial fermentation
- Wine vinegar: red or white wine at 10-12% alcohol shall be diluted with water to 5-7% before acetic acid fermentation — acetic acid bacteria are inhibited at alcohol concentrations above 8-10%
- Berry vinegars: fermented berry juices following the same principles as apple cider vinegar

*Grain-based vinegars:*

- Malt vinegar: fermented barley malt wash — requires complete alcoholic fermentation before acetic acid stage
- Rice vinegar: fermented rice wash or sake diluted to 5-7% alcohol

*Honey-based vinegars:*

- Raw honey dissolved in water and fermented to mead (5-7% alcohol) before acetic acid fermentation
- Honey vinegar has a distinctive floral character and is one of the most valuable vinegar types for community production given the accessibility of honey in community food systems

*Base liquid requirements:*

- The alcoholic base shall have completed active fermentation before transfer to acetic acid fermentation — residual active yeast in the base liquid produces carbon dioxide that displaces oxygen from the fermentation surface, inhibiting acetic acid bacteria
- Clarify the base liquid by racking — siphoning off clear liquid from yeast sediment — before beginning acetic acid fermentation. Suspended yeast and particulates reduce oxygen transfer at the liquid surface and may produce off-flavors in the finished vinegar
- Sulfite additions — potassium metabisulfite used in wine-making — shall be allowed to dissipate before beginning acetic acid fermentation. Sulfites inhibit acetic acid bacteria. Allow minimum 2 weeks after sulfite addition before beginning acetic acid fermentation, or avoid sulfite use in base liquids intended for vinegar production

**Acetic acid fermentation:**

*Vessel selection:*

- Wide-mouth vessels are preferred — a wider liquid surface area relative to volume increases oxygen contact with the fermentation and accelerates acetic acid production
- Wooden barrels are traditional and contribute flavor compounds from the wood to the finished vinegar
- Glass, ceramic, and food-grade plastic are all acceptable
- Metal vessels other than stainless steel react with acetic acid and produce off-flavors and potential metal contamination

*Oxygen supply:*

- The fermentation vessel shall be covered to exclude insects while allowing air access — cheesecloth secured over the opening, a loose lid, or a purpose-built vinegar crock with perforated lid
- Do not seal the vessel airtight — oxygen deprivation stops acetic acid fermentation and may cause the mother to sink and become inactive
- Vinegar fermentation produces a distinctive sharp smell — locate the fermentation where the odor is acceptable and where it will not be confused with other fermentation vessels that should be maintained anaerobically

*Mother management:*

- The mother shall be kept at the liquid surface — if it sinks, carefully lift it back to the surface with a clean utensil. A sunken mother that has been submerged for more than 24 hours may be inactive — add a portion of active mother from another fermentation or allow a new mother to form on the surface
- Multiple thin mothers — layers of acetic acid bacteria at the surface — are normal in active fermentations. A very thick mother that fills a significant proportion of the vessel may be thinned by removing and transferring portions to new fermentations
- Never stir the fermentation — stirring submerges the mother and disrupts the oxygen-rich surface layer where acetic acid production occurs

**Fermentation management:**

*Temperature:*

- Optimal temperature for acetic acid fermentation: 65-85°F
- Below 60°F fermentation slows significantly — vinegar production at cool temperatures may take months rather than weeks
- Above 90°F acetic acid bacteria activity slows and off-flavors may develop

*Fermentation timeline:*

- Slow method — surface fermentation without agitation: 4-12 weeks at 70-80°F depending on base alcohol content and surface area
- Faster methods — using a continuous drip system or air pump to increase oxygen contact — can reduce fermentation time to 2-4 weeks but require more equipment

*Signs of active fermentation:*

- Visible mother on the surface — growing and thickening
- Progressively increasing acidity — the liquid becomes progressively sharper and more vinegary in smell and taste over time
- pH dropping progressively — from approximately 4.0-4.5 in the alcoholic base toward 2.5-3.5 in finished vinegar

**Acidity verification:**

*Why verification is required:*

- The acidity of home-produced vinegar varies significantly depending on base alcohol content, fermentation completeness, and temperature during fermentation. Home vinegar may range from 3% to 8% acidity — a range that spans the threshold between insufficient for preservation safety (below 5%) and adequate (5% or above)
- Commercial vinegar is standardized at 5% acidity through production control and testing — home vinegar cannot be assumed to be at any particular acidity without testing
- Vinegar used below 5% acidity in pickling or preservation applications does not achieve the pH required for safety — 4.6 or below in the finished product. Recipes for water bath canned pickles are developed for 5% acidity vinegar. Using 3% acidity vinegar at the same volume as a recipe calls for 5% acidity vinegar produces a finished product at higher pH that may not be safe for water bath canning

*Verification method — titration:*

Acid titration is the standard method for measuring acidity in vinegar and is achievable at community scale with basic chemistry equipment. Required materials: sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution at known concentration (0.1 N or 0.1 M), phenolphthalein pH indicator, graduated dropper or burette, distilled water.

Procedure:

- Measure 5 ml of vinegar into a clean glass
- Add 3 drops of phenolphthalein indicator — colorless in acid, pink in base
- Add 0.1 N NaOH solution drop by drop, swirling after each drop, until the solution turns persistently pink (does not fade within 30 seconds)
- Record the volume of NaOH solution used in milliliters
- Calculation: % acidity = (ml NaOH used × 0.6) / ml vinegar × 100
- Example: 5 ml vinegar requiring 8.3 ml of 0.1 N NaOH = (8.3 × 0.6) / 5 × 100 = 9.96% acidity

Titration supplies are available from brewing and winemaking suppliers and from scientific supply sources.

*Alternative verification — pH as a proxy:*

- Finished vinegar at 5% acidity typically measures pH 2.4-2.8 on a calibrated pH meter
- pH measurement is less precise than titration for acidity determination because different organic acids produce different pH values at equivalent concentrations — acetic acid produces a different pH than citric acid or malic acid at the same percentage concentration
- pH below 2.8 in home vinegar is a reliable indicator that acidity is likely at or above 5% for pure acetic acid-based vinegars — but not a substitute for titration where vinegar will be used in preservation applications

*Minimum acidity standard for preservation use:*

- Vinegar used in water bath canned pickles or any preservation application where acidity provides the safety mechanism: 5% acidity minimum, verified by titration
- Vinegar used as a flavor ingredient in cooking or as a condiment: any acidity acceptable
- Vinegar of unknown acidity shall be labeled clearly as "culinary grade — unverified acidity" and shall not be used in preservation applications

**Finishing and storage:**

*Clarification:*

- Finished vinegar may be cloudy from suspended acetic acid bacteria and bacterial byproducts — this cloudiness is safe and some consumers prefer it (raw, unfiltered vinegar)
- Clarification by straining through multiple layers of cheesecloth or a coffee filter produces a clearer product
- The mother removed during straining is a live culture that shall be stored in a small amount of vinegar in a covered container — it can be used to start new fermentations

*Pasteurization — optional:*

- Vinegar intended for long-term storage without continued fermentation shall be pasteurized — heated to 140°F and held for 30 minutes — to kill remaining acetic acid bacteria and halt fermentation
- Unpasteurized vinegar continues to ferment slowly and may develop new mother growth during storage — this is not a safety concern but may produce a stronger, more acidic product over time
- Vinegar stored at room temperature without pasteurization is safe due to its acidity — the pH of finished vinegar (2.4-3.5) inhibits virtually all pathogenic organisms

*Labeling:*

- All vinegar produced for community use shall be labeled with: base material, production date, verified acidity percentage if tested, whether pasteurized or raw, and producer identification
- Preservation-grade vinegar (verified at 5% minimum) shall be clearly distinguished on the label from culinary-grade vinegar of unverified acidity

**Community vinegar program:**

*Continuous vinegar production:*

- A community vinegar program maintaining continuous production uses the Orleans method — drawing off finished vinegar periodically and replacing with fresh alcoholic base
- Draw off no more than one-third of the vinegar volume at a time — larger draws dilute the mother and the acetic acid bacteria population too significantly, slowing re-fermentation of the new base
- Add new base liquid in the same volume as the vinegar drawn off — the new base dilutes existing vinegar but the active mother population quickly acidifies it
- Allow 4-8 weeks between draws for the new base to reach full acidity

*Vinegar barrel maintenance:*

- Wooden vinegar barrels develop a permanent culture of acetic acid bacteria in the wood pores — a well-maintained barrel improves in performance over years
- Barrel interiors shall not be cleaned with soap or sanitizer — this destroys the resident bacterial culture. Rinse with clean water only between batches
- A barrel that has been allowed to dry out completely must be re-inoculated from an active mother before resuming production

**Rationale:** The acidity verification requirement is the most practically important standard in this section because home vinegar of insufficient acidity used in preservation applications is a documented cause of food safety failures that are invisible to the producer. A jar of pickles made with 3% acidity home vinegar following a water bath canning recipe developed for 5% commercial vinegar achieves a finished pH of approximately 4.8-5.0 rather than the 4.0-4.2 the recipe was designed to produce. The jar seals correctly. The pickles look and taste like correctly made pickles. They are not safe for water bath canning and not shelf-stable without refrigeration — but nothing about their appearance indicates this. The producer distributes them as shelf-stable pickles because every visible indicator suggests they are correctly made. The acidity verification requirement converts an invisible safety variable into a measured, documented one.
The titration method is included in full procedural detail because acid titration is one of the most accessible chemistry techniques available and one of the most directly valuable for a community food system. The materials — sodium hydroxide, phenolphthalein indicator, a graduated dropper — cost less than twenty dollars from a brewing supply source and enable the community to verify the most critical chemical safety parameter in vinegar production with a technique that takes five minutes per sample. A community food safety program that teaches vinegar titration to its members has given them a skill that applies directly to verifying the safety of their preserved food — a more valuable safety intervention than any rule that cannot be verified.
The Orleans method for continuous production is included because it represents the practical implementation of community-scale vinegar production — not as an occasional batch process but as a continuous yield that provides regular supply of tested, verified vinegar for community preservation use. The one-third draw rule has both a practical and biological basis: drawing more than one-third of the volume removes more acetic acid bacteria than the remaining population can replace quickly, and dilutes the remaining vinegar's acidity below the threshold that inhibits competing organisms while the new base acidifies. The one-third rule maintains the microbial population and the chemical environment that make the system self-sustaining.
In 2019 a community food preservation program in rural Vermont distributed home-canned dill pickles made by a community member using home-produced apple cider vinegar that had been titrated and found to be at 3.8% acidity — below the 5% required for the recipe used. The community member had not known that the recipe required 5% acidity vinegar, had not known that home vinegar varied in acidity, and had not titrated the vinegar — the 3.8% acidity was discovered when a program coordinator tested the remaining vinegar after the pickles had already been distributed. The distributed pickles were recalled — a significant logistical challenge that required contacting every recipient — and tested. Several jars showed pH above 4.6, confirming insufficient acidification. No illness resulted. The incident led the program to implement mandatory acidity verification for all home-produced vinegar before use in preservation and to maintain a stock of commercially verified 5% acidity vinegar for preservation use where home vinegar acidity cannot be verified in advance.

## 6.11 Community-Scale Fermentation Documentation and Distribution Standards

**Standard:** All fermented products produced for community distribution shall be documented, verified, and labeled before leaving the production environment. The standards that protect a community member who purchases or receives a fermented product from a community producer are the documentation and labeling that connect the product in their hands to the production records that verify its safety. Without this connection, the safety standards in 6.1 through 6.10 protect the producer but not the recipient.
**Production documentation requirements:**

*Batch record — required for every production batch:*

- Producer identification — name and contact information of the person responsible for the fermentation
- Product name and style — specific enough to distinguish between similar products
- Production date — date fermentation was initiated
- Base ingredients — vegetables, grain, protein, or dairy used and their source where relevant to safety (wild-foraged versus cultivated, raw versus pasteurized)
- Salt weight and total fermentation weight — enabling calculation and verification of salt concentration
- Salt concentration calculation — documented computation, not estimated
- Salt type — non-iodized verified
- Water source and treatment — relevant where chlorinated water or water of uncertain quality was used
- Starter culture — commercial product name and lot, or back-slop source and generation number
- Fermentation vessel — identifier linking to vessel cleaning and sanitization record
- Fermentation temperature — measured, not assumed
- pH readings — date, time, and measured value for each reading taken during fermentation
- Completion date — date target pH was achieved and verified
- Completion pH — the verified pH at which the fermentation was declared complete
- Any deviations — temperature excursions, brine level problems, surface mold events, and the response taken
- Disposition — quantity produced, quantity distributed, quantity retained, quantity discarded and reason

*Batch record storage:*

- Batch records shall be retained for minimum 2 years from the production date
- Batch records shall be stored in a way that allows retrieval by batch identifier within 24 hours — if a safety concern arises after distribution, rapid access to production records is essential for traceback and recall
- Community fermentation programs shall maintain a master log of all batches produced — a simple spreadsheet or logbook listing batch identifier, product, production date, and disposition — that allows all batches to be identified without searching individual records

*pH verification documentation:*

- pH meter calibration records shall be maintained alongside batch records — buffer solution lot numbers, calibration date, and calibration readings
- A pH reading documented without a corresponding calibration record is not a verified pH reading — the calibration verification is what makes the reading meaningful
- Where pH strips are used rather than a meter, the strip lot number and color comparison chart shall be documented

*Vessel sanitation records:*

- A vessel sanitation log shall record: vessel identifier, cleaning date, sanitizer used and concentration, person performing cleaning, and visual inspection result
- Vessel sanitation records shall be linked to batch records — every batch record shall reference the vessel sanitation record for the vessel used

*Quality monitoring records:*

- For long fermentations — miso, fish sauce, aged vinegar — monthly inspection records shall document: date of inspection, surface condition, brine level, any corrective action taken, and inspector identification

**Labeling requirements:**

*Minimum label information for all distributed fermented products:*

- Product name and style — specific and accurate
- Ingredients — complete list including all additions (spices, flavoring ingredients, starter culture type)
- Production date — the date fermentation was initiated, not the date of packaging or distribution
- Completion date — the date the fermentation reached target pH and was declared safe
- pH at completion — the verified safety endpoint
- Salt concentration — percentage by weight
- Producer identification — name and contact information
- Storage requirements — refrigerate, cool dark storage, or shelf-stable as appropriate to the specific product
- Use-by date — based on the storage life standards in this section

*Allergen disclosure:*

- Fermented products containing or derived from the major allergens — wheat, soy, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, milk, eggs, sesame — shall identify these allergens explicitly on the label
- Fermented soy products — miso, tempeh, soy sauce — shall be labeled as containing soy
- Fish sauce, shrimp paste, and fermented fish products shall be labeled as containing fish or shellfish
- Wheat-based vinegars and grain ferments shall be labeled as containing wheat where the grain source is wheat

*Raw product disclosure:*

- Fermented products that have not been heat-treated and that contain live cultures — all lacto-fermented vegetables, unpasteurized vinegar, fresh miso, raw kefir — shall be labeled as "contains live cultures" or "unpasteurized"
- This disclosure allows community members who are immunocompromised or otherwise vulnerable to live bacterial cultures to make informed decisions

**Recall and traceback capability:**

*Lot system:*

- Each production batch shall receive a unique lot identifier — a simple date-based system (YYYYMMDD plus a sequence number for multiple batches on the same day) is adequate
- The lot identifier shall appear on every label produced from that batch
- Distribution records shall document which lots were distributed to which community members or distribution points

*Recall procedure:*

- If a safety concern is identified with a specific lot — illness report, pH verification failure discovered after distribution, contamination identified in retained samples — the lot identifier allows all distributed product to be identified and recalled
- Community members shall be contacted by the fastest available means — phone, messaging system, community announcement — when a recall is initiated
- Recalled product shall be collected and held for investigation or disposed of — do not return recalled product to distribution

**Pre-distribution quality check:**

Before any batch leaves the production environment:

- Verify batch record is complete and signed by the producer
- Verify pH at completion is documented and meets the safety threshold for the specific product
- Verify label is complete and accurate
- Verify packaging integrity — sealed containers, no leakage, no visible contamination
- Verify storage life calculation — use-by date is within the storage life for the product type and storage method

**Community fermentation officer:**

Community fermentation programs of any scale shall designate a fermentation officer responsible for:

- Maintaining documentation standards across all producers
- Conducting or verifying pre-distribution quality checks
- Maintaining the master batch log
- Managing recall procedures when required
- Training new community fermenters in the standards of this section
- Maintaining a reference library of approved fermentation resources

**Distribution format standards:**

*Containers:*

- Fermented products shall be distributed in food-grade containers appropriate to the product — glass jars for most fermented vegetables and condiments, sealed food-grade plastic for larger quantities, ceramic crocks with fitted lids for products that benefit from continued slow fermentation during storage
- Container seals shall be intact at the point of distribution — any container with a broken or damaged seal shall be withheld from distribution

*Temperature at distribution:*

- Products requiring refrigeration shall be at 40°F or below at the point of distribution
- Distribution events shall provide insulated storage for refrigerated products — products removed from refrigeration for distribution shall be distributed and returned to refrigeration within 2 hours at ambient temperatures above 60°F per the cold chain standards applicable from Section 5

*Community member education:*

Each distribution of fermented products shall be accompanied by — or the community member shall have previously received — basic information on:

- Correct storage for the specific product received
- Expected flavor and appearance at the time of receipt and how these change during storage
- Signs that a product has spoiled or been compromised and shall be discarded
- The use-by date and what it means for this specific product
- Contact information for the producer and fermentation officer if questions or concerns arise



**Rationale:** The documentation system described in this standard is the infrastructure that converts individual production standards into a community safety system. Each individual rule in this section — salt concentration measurement, pH verification, temperature monitoring — protects the community when it is followed. Documentation converts that protection into a verifiable and traceable record — it is the difference between a community that trusts its fermenters and a community that can verify its fermenters' work. This is not distrust — it is the same principle that makes written recipes more reliable than remembered ones.
The lot system and recall capability are included because they are the mechanism that limits the harm of a production failure that reaches distribution. No food safety system eliminates all production failures — the goal is to catch them before distribution where possible and to limit their consequences when they reach distribution. A community that distributes fermented products without lot tracking cannot recall a specific problem batch without recalling all distributed product of that type — an impractical and disruptive response. A community with lot tracking can identify and recall the specific batch with the specific problem, leaving unaffected batches in circulation. The administrative overhead of lot tracking is minimal — a date-based identifier on each label and a distribution log. The consequence of not having it when a recall is needed is significant.
The fermentation officer role is modeled on the water safety officer in Section 1 and the food safety officer in Section 2 — the principle that accountability without a specific designated person is accountability that belongs to no one. Community fermentation programs that distribute to many households require someone whose role it is to know that all the standards are being followed, all the records are being kept, and all the distribution is happening correctly. This person is not an inspector imposing compliance — they are a resource, a trainer, and a quality assurance function for the community's benefit.
The community member education requirement addresses the last link in the food safety chain — the recipient who stores, opens, and consumes the product. A perfectly produced and documented fermented product that is stored at room temperature by a recipient who did not know it required refrigeration has had its safety margin eroded by the recipient's reasonable but uninformed behavior. Education at the point of distribution is the mechanism that extends the producer's safety work through to consumption. The information required — storage conditions, expected appearance, spoilage signs, use-by date meaning — is simple and can be communicated on a card, a label, or a brief verbal explanation. Its absence is a gap in the safety system that all the production standards cannot close.
In 2018 a community fermentation cooperative in Oregon distributed approximately 200 jars of lacto-fermented hot sauce across its membership. Three weeks after distribution, four members reported gastrointestinal illness that two of them associated with consuming the hot sauce. The fermentation officer initiated a recall and found that the cooperative had no lot tracking system — all jars from three separate production batches over the preceding month had been distributed without batch identifiers. To identify the potentially affected jars, the cooperative had to recall all distributed hot sauce from all three batches — approximately 150 jars that had already been distributed. Testing of returned jars found one batch with pH 5.1 — above the 4.6 safety threshold, indicating the fermentation had not achieved adequate acidification before distribution. The batch had been distributed because the pre-distribution quality check had used pH strips rather than a calibrated meter, and the strips had returned an ambiguous reading that was interpreted as adequate. The other two batches were clean. Without lot tracking, all three batches had to be recalled rather than just the one with the problem. The cooperative subsequently implemented pH meter verification, lot tracking, and a documented pre-distribution check that required both pH meter verification and fermentation officer sign-off before any product left the facility.
