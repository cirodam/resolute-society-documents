# Water Sourcing and Treatment

---

## 1.1 Source Hierarchy

**Standard:** Community water sourcing shall follow this priority order: developed spring → drilled well → dug well → collected rainwater → surface water (rivers, lakes, ponds). No source lower in the hierarchy shall be used when a higher source is available and sufficient.

**Rationale:** Not all water sources carry equal risk. The hierarchy reflects the degree of natural filtration and isolation from surface contamination each source receives. Groundwater from a properly drilled well has passed through hundreds of feet of soil and rock — a remarkably effective biological and particulate filter developed over decades or centuries of percolation. Surface water, by contrast, receives direct input from everything upstream: agricultural runoff, animal waste, human activity, industrial discharge, and whatever died in the water three miles north of you.

The 1854 Broad Street cholera outbreak in London — the event that effectively founded modern epidemiology — was traced by John Snow to a single contaminated public well. The pump handle was removed and the outbreak stopped. Five hundred people died in ten days in a ten-block radius. The hierarchy exists because the cost of using a lower-quality source when a better one is available is never worth it.

---

## 1.2 Setback Distances

**Standard:** Wells and developed springs shall maintain the following minimum distances from contamination sources:

- Septic tanks: 50 feet
- Septic drain fields: 100 feet
- Animal pens and pastures: 100 feet
- Fuel storage tanks: 100 feet
- Roads treated with salt or chemicals: 50 feet
- Industrial activity of any kind: 300 feet minimum, assess individually

These are minimums. Increase distance wherever soil is sandy, fractured, or slopes toward the water source.

**Rationale:** Soil filters contaminants, but not infinitely and not equally. The setback distances above represent the minimum horizontal travel distance required for soil to biologically neutralize fecal pathogens under average conditions. The key word is average — sandy or fractured soils can transmit contaminants far faster and farther than clay-heavy soils, which is why slope and soil type modify the baseline numbers.

The consequences of inadequate setback are well documented. In 2000, Walkerton, Ontario, heavy rainfall washed cattle manure into a shallow municipal well inadequately separated from agricultural land. The water system was contaminated with E. coli O157:H7 and Campylobacter. Seven people died and 2,300 were sickened in a town of 5,000 — nearly half the population. The well had been flagged as vulnerable years earlier. The distance rules existed. They were not enforced.

Setback distances are one of the few regulations where the cost of compliance is almost zero — you choose where to dig — and the cost of non-compliance can be catastrophic and irreversible.

## 1.3 Surface Water as Presumptively Contaminated

**Standard:** All surface water — rivers, streams, lakes, ponds, irrigation ditches, and floodwater — shall be treated as contaminated regardless of appearance, taste, source reputation, or upstream visibility. No surface water shall be consumed or used in food preparation without treatment meeting the standards in 1.6 through 1.9.

**Rationale:** Clear water is not clean water. The most dangerous waterborne pathogens — Cryptosporidium, Giardia, E. coli O157:H7, Campylobacter, Vibrio cholerae — are invisible, odorless, and tasteless at infectious doses. A mountain stream running over clean rock looks identical to one draining a pasture three miles upstream. There is no organoleptic test — no smell, taste, or visual inspection — that reliably distinguishes safe surface water from contaminated surface water.

The presumptive contamination standard exists because the alternative — evaluating each source individually without laboratory testing — requires expertise and equipment most communities won't have consistently available. A rule that says "treat surface water unless it looks clean" will kill people because the judgment call will sometimes be wrong and the consequences are severe.

The 1993 Milwaukee Cryptosporidium outbreak is the largest documented waterborne disease outbreak in United States history. The water came from Lake Michigan, was treated at a municipal facility, passed visual and routine testing, and still sickened 403,000 people and killed over 100 immunocompromised individuals. The treatment process had a gap. The water looked fine.

Surface water is presumptively contaminated because that presumption is almost always correct and the cost of being wrong is too high.

## 1.4 Rainwater Collection Considerations

**Standard:** Rainwater collection systems shall meet the following minimum requirements:

- Collection surfaces must be clean, non-toxic, and free of lead-based paint, treated wood, and asphalt coatings
- First-flush diverters are required on all roof collection systems, discarding a minimum of one gallon per 100 square feet of collection surface
- Storage tanks must be covered, opaque, and sealed against insects and debris
- Collected rainwater shall be treated per standards 1.6 through 1.9 before consumption or food preparation
- Collection is prohibited during and immediately following events that deposit ash, chemical fallout, or industrial contamination

**Rationale:** Rainwater begins clean but picks up contaminants the moment it contacts a surface. Roof collection — the most practical large-scale method — passes water over materials that may leach zinc from galvanized metal, petrochemicals from asphalt shingles, or biological material from bird and rodent activity. The first flush of rain across any roof carries the highest concentration of accumulated contaminants — dust, animal waste, atmospheric deposition, decomposed organic matter. First-flush diverters discard this initial volume automatically, dramatically reducing contamination load before storage.

Opaque covered storage matters because algae requires light to grow and insects — particularly mosquitoes — require access. An uncovered rainwater tank in warm weather becomes a mosquito breeding site and a biological contamination source simultaneously.

In 2014, residents of a rural Australian community using roof-collected rainwater experienced a cluster of Campylobacter infections traced to bird fecal contamination on collection surfaces combined with absent first-flush diversion. The outbreak was entirely preventable with equipment costing less than fifty dollars.

Rainwater collection is one of the most valuable community water resilience tools available. These standards exist not to discourage it but to ensure it delivers what it promises.

## 1.5 Baseline Testing Before Use

**Standard:** Any water source shall be tested before initial community use and after any event that may affect source integrity. Minimum baseline testing shall include:

- Total coliform and E. coli (biological contamination indicator)
- Nitrates (agricultural and septic contamination indicator)
- pH (corrosivity and treatment effectiveness indicator)
- Turbidity (sediment and biological load indicator)
- Heavy metals panel where industrial activity is present or suspected

Testing shall be performed by a designated community water safety role using field test kits at minimum, and laboratory analysis where available. Results shall be documented and retained permanently.

**Rationale:** Testing before use exists because contamination is invisible and the consequences of discovering it after the fact are severe. A baseline test establishes that a source is safe at the point of first use and creates a reference point against which future tests can be compared. A source that tests clean for three years and then shows elevated nitrates is telling you something changed upstream — that information is only useful if you have the baseline to compare against.

Coliform bacteria are used as the primary indicator not because they are themselves always dangerous but because they are present in fecal material and are far easier to detect than the pathogens that accompany them. A positive coliform test means fecal contamination has reached your source. The specific pathogen may be harmless or it may be E. coli O157:H7 — you test for the indicator because testing for every possible pathogen individually is impractical.

Nitrates warrant particular attention in agricultural communities because they indicate that fertilizer or septic effluent is reaching the water source — and because nitrate contamination causes methemoglobinemia in infants, a condition that prevents blood from carrying oxygen. It is historically called blue baby syndrome. It is fast, it is serious, and it is invisible in the water.

In Hagerstown, Maryland in 1943, sixteen infants developed methemoglobinemia from a well later found to have nitrate levels far exceeding safe thresholds. The well looked clean. It had never been tested.

A source that has never been tested is a source whose safety is unknown. Unknown is not the same as safe.

## 1.6 Boiling Standards

**Standard:** Water shall be brought to a rolling boil and maintained for the following minimum durations before consumption or food preparation use:

- Elevations below 6,500 feet: 1 minute
- Elevations above 6,500 feet: 3 minutes

Boiled water shall be stored in clean covered containers and used within 24 hours without further treatment. Containers used for boiled water storage shall not be used interchangeably with untreated water containers.

**Rationale:** Boiling is the oldest, most reliable, and most universally accessible water treatment method available. It requires no chemicals, no specialized equipment, and no technical knowledge beyond the ability to maintain a fire. Its effectiveness against biological contamination is essentially absolute — every waterborne pathogen of consequence to human health, including Cryptosporidium and Giardia which resist chlorination at standard doses, is destroyed at boiling temperature.

The one-minute standard reflects the fact that pathogens are killed well before water reaches a rolling boil at standard elevations — the boil itself is the confirmation that sufficient temperature has been reached throughout the water column, not the mechanism of kill. At high elevations water boils at lower temperatures due to reduced atmospheric pressure, requiring extended time to achieve equivalent pathogen destruction. At 6,500 feet water boils at approximately 199°F rather than 212°F — sufficient for most pathogens but requiring longer contact time for reliable Giardia cyst destruction.

The container separation requirement addresses a failure mode that consistently appears in outbreak investigations: treated water recontaminated through contact with vessels that previously held untreated water. In emergency situations following the 2010 Haiti earthquake, boiling compliance was high but recontamination during storage and transport negated much of the benefit. Studies found that point-of-use treatment combined with safe storage in dedicated containers reduced diarrheal disease significantly more than treatment alone.

Boiling does not remove chemical contamination, heavy metals, or dissolved solids. It is a biological treatment only. Where chemical contamination is suspected, boiling is insufficient as a sole treatment method.

## 1.7 Chlorination Ratios and Contact Time

**Standard:** Where boiling is impractical at scale, water may be treated with unscented liquid chlorine bleach (sodium hypochlorite) or calcium hypochlorite granules according to the following ratios:

Liquid bleach (6–8.25% sodium hypochlorite):

- Clear water: 8 drops per gallon, 30-minute contact time
- Cloudy water: 16 drops per gallon, 30-minute contact time

Calcium hypochlorite granules (68–78% concentration):

- Prepare a stock solution of 1 teaspoon per gallon of water
- Add stock solution at 1 part per 100 parts water to be treated
- 30-minute contact time minimum

Treated water shall have a faint but detectable chlorine smell after contact time. No detectable smell indicates insufficient treatment and requires re-treatment. Chlorine concentration shall be verified with test strips where available.

Bleach used for treatment shall be verified as unscented and containing no additional surfactants or cleaning agents. Bleach older than six months shall be assumed degraded and doses increased by 50% or replaced.

**Rationale:** Chlorination is the treatment method that made large-scale municipal water systems possible and is responsible for one of the largest reductions in infectious disease mortality in human history. Before municipal chlorination became standard in the early twentieth century, typhoid fever killed tens of thousands of Americans annually. By 1936 typhoid mortality had fallen by over 99% in chlorinated cities while remaining elevated in unchlorinated ones. The intervention was chlorine. The mechanism is straightforward — hypochlorous acid penetrates microbial cell walls and disrupts cellular function, killing bacteria and inactivating most viruses at low concentrations.

The contact time requirement is as important as the dose. Chlorine kills pathogens through sustained chemical exposure — dumping chlorine into water and immediately drinking it provides little protection. Thirty minutes at the specified concentration is the minimum for reliable pathogen inactivation under normal conditions.

Turbidity — cloudiness from suspended particles — matters because organic particles consume chlorine and shield pathogens from contact. Cloudy water requires double the dose not because the water is more contaminated but because the chlorine is being consumed by material other than pathogens before it can do its work. Where turbidity is high, filtration before chlorination dramatically improves effectiveness and reduces chemical requirement.

Chlorination does not reliably inactivate Cryptosporidium at practical doses. Where Cryptosporidium is a known or suspected risk, chlorination must be combined with filtration or boiling.

## 1.8 Filtration Hierarchy

**Standard:** Community filtration systems shall be implemented in the following sequence where multiple filter types are available. No stage shall be skipped in favor of a later stage:

- **Stage 1 — Sediment filtration:** Removes suspended particles, sand, silt, and debris. Minimum 5-micron rating for pre-filtration, 1 micron for final sediment stage.
- **Stage 2 — Activated carbon filtration:** Removes chemical contaminants, chlorine byproducts, pesticides, heavy metals including lead, and taste and odor compounds.
- **Stage 3 — Biological filtration:** Ceramic filters (0.2 micron or finer) or biosand filters remove bacteria and protozoa including Cryptosporidium and Giardia. This stage does not reliably remove viruses.
- **Stage 4 — Disinfection:** Chlorination per 1.7 or UV treatment per 1.9 following filtration.

Filter maintenance schedules shall be established and documented at installation. Filters operated beyond their rated capacity or service life shall be treated as absent. A filter that appears to be working is not necessarily working — flow rate reduction and visible fouling are late indicators of failure, not early ones.

**Rationale:** Filtration and disinfection address different categories of contamination and are not interchangeable. Filtration removes particles, including biological ones, through physical exclusion — nothing larger than the filter pore size passes through. Disinfection kills or inactivates biological contaminants chemically or with radiation but does nothing to remove particles, heavy metals, or chemical contamination. Each stage addresses what the previous stage cannot.

The sequence matters because each stage protects the next. Sediment filtration extends the life of carbon and ceramic filters by removing the bulk particulate load that would otherwise foul them rapidly. Carbon filtration removes chlorine before it reaches ceramic filters, preventing degradation of filter media. Biological filtration removes the protozoa that disinfection alone cannot reliably kill. Skipping a stage doesn't just remove that stage's protection — it degrades every subsequent stage.

The biosand filter deserves particular attention as a community-buildable technology requiring no manufactured components beyond sand and gravel. Developed and refined through decades of deployment in low-infrastructure environments, a properly constructed biosand filter develops a biological layer — the schmutzdecke — in its top few centimeters that provides highly effective pathogen removal through biological predation and physical exclusion combined. A 2009 review of field studies across multiple countries found biosand filters achieving 90–99% reduction in fecal coliform and significant Cryptosporidium reduction. They can be built from local materials, maintained by non-specialists, and scaled to community use. Their limitation is flow rate — they cannot be rushed without destroying the biological layer that makes them effective.

Filter failure is insidious because it is often invisible. A cracked ceramic filter element passes water normally while providing no biological protection. A carbon filter past its service life removes nothing while appearing functional. Maintenance schedules and replacement protocols are not optional additions to a filtration system — they are the system.

## 1.9 UV Treatment Conditions and Limitations

**Standard:** Ultraviolet treatment systems shall meet the following requirements to be considered effective:

- UV dose shall meet or exceed 40 mJ/cm² — the standard for reliable inactivation of bacteria, viruses, and protozoa including Cryptosporidium
- Water shall pass through sediment and carbon filtration per 1.8 stages 1 and 2 before UV treatment
- Turbidity of water entering the UV chamber shall not exceed 1 NTU (nephelometric turbidity unit). Water exceeding this threshold shall not be considered treated by UV alone
- UV lamp intensity shall be verified with a UV sensor where available. Absent a sensor, lamps shall be replaced on the manufacturer's schedule without exception
- UV systems require reliable electrical power. Communities shall not rely on UV as a sole treatment method without documented backup power or an alternative treatment protocol

UV treatment shall be considered a disinfection stage only. It does not remove chemical contamination, heavy metals, particulates, or dissolved solids.

**Rationale:** Ultraviolet treatment works by damaging the DNA and RNA of microorganisms, preventing replication. At sufficient dose it is effective against bacteria, viruses, and protozoa — including Cryptosporidium, which resists chlorination. Unlike chemical disinfection it leaves no residual in the water and produces no disinfection byproducts, which are a long-term health concern with chlorination at scale.

Its critical limitation is turbidity. UV light is absorbed and scattered by suspended particles — the same particles that make water appear cloudy. A pathogen shielded behind a particle of sediment receives a fraction of the intended UV dose and may pass through the chamber viable. The 1 NTU turbidity threshold is not conservative — laboratory studies have demonstrated meaningful pathogen survival at turbidities above this level even at standard UV doses. This is why pre-filtration is not optional for UV systems but foundational to them.

The lamp degradation issue kills people quietly. UV lamps lose intensity gradually and continuously from the moment they are first used. A lamp operating at 50% of rated intensity may still produce visible light while delivering half the required dose — water appears treated and is not. In 2001 a UV disinfection system serving a resort in Canada experienced lamp failure that went undetected for an extended period. Guests contracted Cryptosporidium from water the system indicated was being treated. Fourteen people were hospitalized.

UV systems are among the most effective treatment technologies available when properly maintained and properly preceded by filtration. They are among the most dangerous when either condition is absent, because they provide the appearance of treatment without its substance.

## 1.10 Community-Scale Distribution and Container Sanitation

**Standard:** Water distribution systems and storage vessels shall meet the following requirements:

- All containers used for treated water storage and distribution shall be food-grade, opaque, and fitted with covers that seal against contamination
- Containers shall be sanitized before first use and after any suspected contamination event with a chlorine solution of 1 tablespoon bleach per gallon of water, contact time 30 seconds minimum, followed by thorough rinsing
- Separate dedicated containers shall be maintained for treated and untreated water and shall never be used interchangeably. Containers shall be permanently labeled and color-coded where possible
- Distribution points shall be designed to prevent contact between the dispensing mechanism and personal containers, hands, or other contamination sources. Gravity-fed spigots are preferred over open-dip access
- Piped distribution systems shall maintain a minimum chlorine residual of 0.2 mg/L at the furthest point from treatment to indicate system integrity
- Dead-end pipe segments shall be flushed regularly on a documented schedule
- Any break in a distribution pipe shall trigger immediate system testing before resuming distribution

**Rationale:** Treatment at the source is necessary but not sufficient. The pathway between treatment and consumption is where a significant proportion of waterborne illness originates — water that is clean when it leaves the treatment point and contaminated when it reaches the mouth. This recontamination pathway is well documented and consistently underestimated.

Container cross-contamination is the most common recontamination mechanism at household and community scale. The practice of using the same vessel for treated and untreated water — which happens wherever containers are scarce, unlabeled, or managed by multiple people — directly negates treatment. A study following the 2010 Haiti earthquake found that households practicing point-of-use treatment but storing water in open or multi-use containers had diarrheal disease rates approaching those of untreated households. The treatment was real. The storage undid it.

Open-dip access — reaching a cup or hand into a shared water vessel — introduces fecal-oral contamination directly into the treated supply. A single person with contaminated hands accessing a communal water container can seed an entire community's water supply. Spigot-based dispensing eliminates this pathway entirely with negligible additional cost or complexity.

Dead-end pipe segments — sections of distribution pipe that do not circulate — are a persistent problem in community water systems because water in them stagnates, chlorine residual depletes, and biofilm develops on pipe walls. When pressure fluctuates, that biofilm and the organisms in it can be released into the distribution system. The 2001 Walkerton inquiry identified stagnant distribution segments as a contributing factor in pathogen persistence after the initial contamination event. Regular flushing is the only remedy short of system redesign.

Pipe breaks warrant particular attention because they create two simultaneous problems: pressure loss that can draw groundwater and soil contamination into the pipe through the break, and the assumption among users that the system is still functioning normally. A pipe break that goes undetected and untested before resuming distribution has introduced an unknown contamination event into a system people trust.

## 1.11 Designated Water Safety Role

**Standard:** Every community water system shall have a designated Water Safety Officer responsible for the following functions:

- Maintaining a written record of all source tests, treatment activities, maintenance performed, and distribution system inspections
- Executing and documenting testing schedules per 1.5 and 1.12
- Maintaining treatment chemical inventory and equipment in operational condition
- Establishing and enforcing filter replacement schedules per 1.8
- Verifying UV lamp function and replacement per 1.9
- Training a minimum of two additional community members in all Water Safety Officer functions at all times
- Issuing community notification when water safety is compromised or uncertain
- Maintaining a written emergency protocol per 1.13

The Water Safety Officer role shall not be combined with sanitation, waste management, or medical roles in communities where population and workload permit separation. Records maintained by the Water Safety Officer are community property and shall be accessible to any community member on request.

**Rationale:** Water safety fails systemically when it is everyone's responsibility and therefore no one's. The history of waterborne disease outbreaks in otherwise capable communities is substantially a history of diffused accountability — systems where multiple people assumed someone else was monitoring, testing, or maintaining, and where that assumption went unchallenged until people got sick.

The Walkerton outbreak, referenced in 1.2, is again instructive. The town had a water system, treatment equipment, and testing protocols. What it lacked was consistent, accountable execution of those protocols by a person whose primary responsibility was water safety. The operators responsible had inadequate training and divided attention across multiple municipal functions. Testing results showing contamination were not acted upon. The system existed on paper and failed in practice.

The two-backup requirement addresses a failure mode specific to small and mid-sized communities: the single point of knowledge failure. A Water Safety Officer who is the sole person capable of operating the treatment system creates a catastrophic vulnerability — illness, injury, departure, or death of that individual leaves the community without water safety capacity at precisely the moment stress on all systems is likely elevated. Knowledge redundancy is not a luxury — it is a core system requirement.

Public record access matters because water safety is a community interest, not an institutional one. A community that cannot inspect its own water safety records cannot verify its own safety. Opacity in water system management has historically preceded the most serious institutional failures — Flint, Michigan being the most prominent recent example, where residents were denied accurate information about lead contamination for over a year while harm accumulated. Transparency is not administrative courtesy. It is a safety mechanism.

## 1.12 Testing Frequency and Trigger Events

**Standard:** Water sources and distribution systems shall be tested according to the following minimum schedule.

Routine testing:

- Total coliform and E. coli: monthly at source, monthly at furthest distribution point
- Nitrates: quarterly
- Turbidity: weekly at treatment intake, daily during high precipitation periods
- Chlorine residual in distribution system: weekly at multiple points
- pH: monthly

Trigger testing — immediate re-testing required following any of the following events:

- Flood or significant precipitation event affecting source watershed
- Any death or significant die-off of animals upstream or near source
- Any new agricultural, industrial, or construction activity within setback distances
- Any distribution pipe break or repair
- Any breach of storage vessel integrity
- Two or more community members presenting with gastrointestinal illness of unknown origin within a 72-hour period
- Any visible change in source water appearance, odor, or taste
- Extended power outage affecting treatment systems
- Any community member reporting unusual taste or odor from treated water

Following a trigger event the water source shall be treated as compromised until testing confirms safety. Alternative water sources or emergency treatment protocols per 1.13 shall be activated immediately and not held pending test results.

**Rationale:** Routine testing establishes the baseline and catches slow-developing contamination before it reaches harmful levels. Trigger testing exists because contamination events are rarely gradual — they are typically discrete events with identifiable causes that produce rapid changes in water quality. A testing schedule that only operates on a calendar misses the events most likely to cause acute harm.

The 72-hour gastrointestinal illness cluster trigger is particularly important and consistently underutilized in community water management. When two or more people develop gastrointestinal symptoms of unknown origin within a short window the most likely common exposure is water or food. Waiting for a third or fourth case before investigating the water supply allows a potentially growing contamination event to continue while the community attributes illness to coincidence. The 1993 Milwaukee Cryptosporidium outbreak — referenced in 1.3 — was preceded by a detectable increase in gastrointestinal illness reports in the days before the outbreak was formally recognized. Earlier trigger response would not have prevented the initial contamination but could have shortened the exposure window significantly.

The presumption of compromise following a trigger event — rather than waiting for test results before acting — reflects an asymmetry of consequences. If the water is safe and you treat it as compromised, you have caused inconvenience. If the water is compromised and you treat it as safe pending testing, you have exposed your community to a pathogen during the testing window, which may be 24 to 48 hours or longer depending on available laboratory capacity. The precautionary direction is unambiguous.

Visible changes in appearance, odor, or taste are listed as triggers despite being unreliable indicators of biological contamination — because while clear water can be contaminated, visibly abnormal water is almost always telling you something has changed in the source. Any change from baseline is information and warrants investigation.

## 1.13 Emergency Water Protocols

**Standard:** Every community water system shall maintain a written emergency water protocol that is reviewed annually, stored in at least two physical locations, and known to the Water Safety Officer and all trained backups. The protocol shall address the following minimum requirements.

Minimum safe water quantity:

- Drinking and food preparation: 1 gallon per person per day minimum, 2 gallons preferred
- Basic sanitation: 1 additional gallon per person per day minimum
- Medical care and wound treatment: additional reserve to be determined by community medical capacity

Emergency treatment hierarchy when primary system is unavailable:

1. Stored treated water from sealed containers
2. Boiling per 1.6 using any available heat source
3. Chlorination per 1.7 of best available source
4. Combined filtration and chlorination of surface water per 1.8 and 1.7
5. Solar disinfection (SODIS) as last resort — clear plastic bottles filled with filtered water exposed to direct sunlight for minimum 6 hours, 2 days if cloudy

Emergency supply requirements:

- Community shall maintain a minimum 72-hour treated water reserve in sealed food-grade containers at all times
- Treatment chemical reserves — chlorine bleach or calcium hypochlorite — shall be maintained at sufficient quantity to treat the community's full daily water requirement for a minimum of 30 days
- At least one gravity-fed filtration system capable of community-scale output shall be maintained in operational condition independent of electrical power
- Emergency protocol documentation shall include the location of all secondary and tertiary water sources within reasonable distance of the community

Community notification standards:

- Any water safety compromise shall be communicated to all community members within 2 hours of identification through whatever communication systems are available
- Notification shall specify: nature of the compromise, affected sources or distribution points, recommended action, and estimated resolution timeline
- Notification shall not wait for confirmed test results when a trigger event per 1.12 has occurred

**Rationale:** Emergency protocols exist because water system failures do not announce themselves in advance and do not wait for convenient timing. A community that develops its emergency water response after the primary system fails is a community making critical decisions under stress, with degraded information, and with people already consuming potentially compromised water. The protocol must exist, be known, and be practiced before it is needed.

The 72-hour reserve requirement reflects the minimum time required to identify a contamination event, implement emergency treatment, and restore or replace primary supply under favorable conditions. In practice, serious water system failures often take longer to resolve — the reserve is a floor, not a target. Communities with greater storage capacity should use it.

Solar disinfection — SODIS — is listed as a last resort because it is genuinely a last resort: effective under the right conditions, unreliable under others, and dependent on sunlight that may be absent precisely when other systems have failed due to severe weather. A 2001 study published in the Journal of Water and Health confirmed SODIS effectiveness against E. coli, Salmonella, and Vibrio cholerae under optimal conditions. It does not reliably inactivate Cryptosporidium and its effectiveness drops sharply with turbid water or cloud cover. It is included because knowing an imperfect method is better than knowing nothing when no other option exists.

The two-hour community notification standard reflects the ethical foundation of community water management: the people consuming the water have an immediate right to know when its safety is uncertain. Delayed notification — whether from uncertainty about test results, concern about causing panic, or institutional inertia — consistently appears in post-outbreak investigations as an aggravating factor that extended community exposure. In Walkerton, residents continued drinking tap water for days after officials had reason to suspect contamination. In Flint, residents were told their water was safe for over a year after internal testing showed lead levels requiring action. The notification standard is not a bureaucratic requirement. It is the community's most basic protection against the failure of those managing its water on its behalf.
