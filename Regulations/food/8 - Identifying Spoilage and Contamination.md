8.1 The Limits of Sensory Inspection
Standard: This standard exists as a foundational rule because the most dangerous failure mode in food safety is confidence in sensory inspection that the inspection cannot justify. Community members who understand what sensory inspection can and cannot detect make safer decisions than those who trust their senses without understanding their limits.
What sensory inspection reliably detects:
Spoilage from aerobic organisms:

Mold growth — visible on surfaces, identifiable by fuzzy or powdery texture and characteristic colors
Yeast activity — visible as surface growth, detectable by characteristic fermented or alcoholic odor, sometimes visible as bubbling
Aerobic bacterial spoilage — detectable by off-odors (putrid, sour, rancid, ammonia-like), surface sliminess, discoloration, and texture changes
These spoilage indicators are reliable because aerobic organisms produce metabolic byproducts — gases, acids, enzymes — that produce visible, olfactory, and tactile changes in the food

Oxidative rancidity:

Detectable by characteristic crayon-like, paint-like, or sharp metallic odor and taste
Visible in some oils as cloudiness or discoloration
Reliable indicator in fats, oils, and fat-containing foods

Physical deterioration:

Moisture damage — sogginess, clumping, caking
Pest damage — gnaw marks, frass (insect feces), webbing, visible insects
Physical contamination — foreign objects, unusual particles

What sensory inspection cannot reliably detect:
Botulinum toxin:

As established in Section 3.12 — the most critical limitation of sensory inspection. Food containing lethal concentrations of botulinum toxin may have normal appearance, normal odor, and normal taste. No sensory test distinguishes safe from botulinum-contaminated low-acid preserved food
This limitation applies to home-canned low-acid foods, improperly fermented low-acid vegetables, and traditional fermented fish products

Salmonella, E. coli O157:H7, Campylobacter, and Listeria at infectious doses:

These pathogens produce no detectable sensory changes in contaminated food at the concentrations required to cause illness
A piece of chicken containing 10⁶ Salmonella cells per gram — well above the infectious dose — is indistinguishable from safe chicken by appearance, odor, or texture
This limitation applies to all raw animal products and to any food that may have been cross-contaminated with raw animal products

Viral pathogens — norovirus, Hepatitis A:

Viruses produce no metabolic byproducts in food — no off-odors, no texture changes, no visible growth
A food item contaminated by a food handler with norovirus is completely indistinguishable from an uncontaminated item by any sensory means

Chemical contamination:

Most chemical contaminants — pesticide residues, heavy metals, cleaning chemical residues — are odorless and tasteless at the concentrations that cause harm
The exceptions are acute contamination with strong concentrations — bleach, ammonia, obvious chemical odor — that are detectable but rare

Mycotoxins:

Aflatoxin and other mold-produced toxins are present in food at concentrations far below sensory detection thresholds. Mold-contaminated food that has been cleaned of visible mold may still contain mycotoxins in the underlying food tissue

The fundamental principle:
Sensory inspection is a useful tool for detecting spoilage that has progressed to the point of producing detectable changes. It is not a tool for confirming safety — a food that passes sensory inspection is not confirmed safe, it is confirmed free of detectable spoilage. These are different things. The safety of food is established by correct production, handling, and storage — not by sensory inspection at the point of use.
The when-in-doubt-discard principle:

When any doubt exists about the safety of a food item — uncertain storage history, suspected temperature abuse, proximity to a known contaminated item, unusual sensory characteristics even if not definitively spoilage — the correct response is discard
The asymmetry of consequences justifies this standard: discarding a safe food wastes that food. Consuming an unsafe food may cause illness or death. These outcomes are not equivalent and the decision rule should reflect their asymmetry
"When in doubt, discard" is not a counsel of excessive waste — it is a recognition that the consequence of being wrong about safety is categorically more serious than the consequence of being wrong about spoilage

Rationale: The when-in-doubt-discard principle requires specific rationale because it conflicts with the food conservation instinct that is appropriate and valuable in a community food system — reducing waste, using available resources fully, not discarding food carelessly. The principle does not conflict with food conservation in general — it establishes a specific exception for the case where safety is uncertain. A community that discards every food item with any imperfection wastes food carelessly. A community that consumes every food item regardless of safety concerns exposes itself to serious harm. The when-in-doubt-discard principle defines the specific threshold — genuine safety uncertainty, not cosmetic imperfection — at which discarding is the correct choice despite the food conservation value of preservation.
The botulinum toxin invisibility point is repeated here as a standalone principle because it is the most important safety limitation in the entire document and the one most frequently forgotten or minimized by community members who have never encountered botulism. Every rule in Section 3 and Section 6 that addresses botulism risk is rendered irrelevant if a community member opens a jar of questionable home-canned green beans, finds it looks and smells normal, and consumes it. The sensory inspection limitation means that correct process — not sensory inspection — is the only protection against botulism in preserved foods.

8.2 Fresh Meat and Poultry
Standard: Fresh meat and poultry shall be evaluated using the indicators below before preparation. Any product failing one or more indicators shall be discarded without tasting.
Normal characteristics of fresh meat:

Beef: bright cherry-red on cut surfaces exposed to oxygen, dark purplish-red in vacuum-packed or oxygen-excluded cuts (the dark color is normal myoglobin in reduced state — it brightens to cherry-red within 30 minutes of air exposure). Firm texture, no surface sliminess, mild meaty odor
Pork: pale pink to grayish-pink, firm texture, mild odor
Lamb: pinkish-red, firm texture, mild characteristic odor slightly stronger than beef
Poultry: pink to pale pink skin, firm texture, mild odor. Vacuum-packed poultry may have slightly pink-tinged liquid — normal. Strong odor even after rinsing is abnormal

Discard indicators:
Odor:

Any sour, ammonia-like, sulfurous, or putrid odor — these odors indicate bacterial breakdown of proteins producing volatile compounds including hydrogen sulfide, trimethylamine, and putrescine
Odor is the most reliable sensory indicator for meat spoilage — it develops before visible changes in most meat types
Evaluate odor immediately upon opening packaging — the first smell before other odors in the environment compete is the most reliable assessment

Color:

Gray or brown color throughout the meat (not just at the surface) — indicates advanced oxidation or bacterial activity
Green discoloration — indicates significant bacterial or mold activity, always discard
Dark spots or patches inconsistent with the normal cut — evaluate carefully, may indicate bruising (safe to trim and use) or bacterial activity (discard)
Exception: vacuum-packed beef that is dark purple-red is normal. Allow 30 minutes of air exposure before evaluating color

Texture:

Surface sliminess — a thin slippery layer on the meat surface indicates significant bacterial growth. Sliminess that persists after rinsing is discard criteria. Sliminess that appears only in vacuum-packed product liquid and is absent from the meat surface after rinsing may be normal
Stickiness that pulls when touched — similar significance to sliminess

Storage time exceeded:

Meat at or past its use-by date shall be evaluated carefully — it is not automatically unsafe but requires passing all sensory indicators above before use
Meat significantly past use-by date — more than 2 days beyond at 38-40°F storage — shall be discarded regardless of sensory indicators. Bacterial populations may be at dangerous levels before spoilage indicators develop

Poultry-specific indicators:

Off-odor in poultry is more significant than in red meat — Campylobacter and Salmonella contamination produces no odor at infectious doses, but bacterial spoilage producing off-odor in poultry is a reliable indicator of overall hygiene and handling failure
Yellow or greenish skin discoloration beyond normal yellow fat coloring — discard
Any ammonia odor from poultry — discard immediately


8.3 Cured and Fermented Meats
Standard: Cured and fermented meats — salami, saucisson, cured ham, bacon, jerky, and similar products — shall be evaluated using the indicators below. The extended production process of these products creates specific failure modes distinct from fresh meat.
Normal characteristics:

Dry fermented sausage (salami): firm, dry exterior with white mold bloom (intended surface mold), dense firm interior with characteristic salt-acid-fermented aroma, visible fat distribution, deep red meat color from curing
Cured whole muscle (ham, prosciutto, coppa): firm texture, deep red-pink color, salt-forward aroma with characteristic cured notes, white fat that is firm and not sticky
Jerky: dry, firm, bends slightly before breaking or breaks cleanly. Characteristic smoky or seasoned aroma with no off-notes
Bacon: firm fat, pink-red meat, characteristic cured and smoked aroma

Discard indicators:
Fermented dry sausage:

Green, blue, or black mold on cut surfaces — surface white mold on uncut exterior is normal and shall be brushed or wiped off before cutting. Interior mold of any color is a discard indicator
Soft or mushy texture in areas that should be firm — indicates inadequate drying or moisture intrusion during storage
Off-odor beyond the characteristic acidic, fermented, smoky aroma — ammonia, putrid, or rancid notes
Slimy surface beneath the mold bloom — indicates bacterial activity on the casing surface. Wipe and evaluate — persistent sliminess after wiping is a discard indicator

Cured whole muscle:

Green or black discoloration in the meat interior — always discard
Ammonia or putrid odor — discard
Soft spots in areas that should be uniformly firm — indicates moisture intrusion or inadequate curing
Excessive moisture at the cut surface beyond normal — firm cured products should not weep liquid

Jerky:

Any visible mold — discard. Jerky mold indicates moisture content was above safe threshold at storage
Soft, pliable texture when it was dry at production — indicates moisture absorption during storage. Evaluate water activity before consuming — this is a storage failure indicator
Off-odor — rancid, putrid, or unusual notes beyond the characteristic jerky aroma

The critical limitation:

Botulinum toxin risk in improperly cured fermented sausage is invisible — as established in 6.9. A dry fermented sausage that did not achieve rapid enough acidification or that lacked adequate curing salt may contain botulinum toxin with no sensory indicator. The protection against this is correct production — not inspection at the point of consumption


8.4 Fresh and Processed Dairy
Standard: Fresh dairy products — fluid milk, cream, fresh cheeses, yogurt, and butter — and processed dairy shall be evaluated using the indicators below. Dairy spoilage is generally detectable by sensory means but the most dangerous dairy pathogens — Listeria, E. coli O157:H7 — produce no sensory changes at infectious doses.
Fluid milk:
Normal characteristics: White to slightly off-white, homogeneous, mild sweet-lactic aroma, clean taste
Discard indicators:

Sour odor — indicates lactic acid bacterial spoilage. Mildly sour milk that has not yet developed off-flavors may be used in cooking where acidity is acceptable (pancakes, baking) but not for drinking or dairy production
Bitter, unclean, or rancid odor — indicates more advanced spoilage or lipase activity
Stringy or ropy texture — indicates specific bacterial contamination producing exopolysaccharides
Visible curdling without intentional acidification — indicates significant souring. Use immediately for cooking or discard
Unusual color — pink or yellow tint indicates contamination. Discard

The Listeria limitation:

Raw milk and inadequately pasteurized milk may contain Listeria at dangerous concentrations with no sensory indicator. Correct pasteurization — not sensory inspection — is the protection

Fresh cheeses (chèvre, ricotta, cottage cheese, mozzarella):
Discard indicators:

Any visible mold — fresh cheeses shall be discarded entirely on first mold appearance. Unlike hard cheese where surface mold is trimmable, soft cheese mold penetrates throughout the product
Sour or ammoniated odor beyond the expected mild acidity of fresh cheese
Slimy surface texture
Pink, orange, or yellow discoloration
Unusual bitterness

Yogurt:
Discard indicators:

Visible mold of any color — discard entirely
Pink, orange, or unusual discoloration
Off-odor beyond expected sourness — putrid, cheesy, or ammonia notes
Excessive liquid separation beyond normal whey — excessive whey suggests continued bacterial activity producing over-acidification

Butter:
Discard indicators:

Rancid odor — the primary butter spoilage indicator. Sharp, crayon-like, or paint-like smell
Yellow or orange surface discoloration penetrating beyond a thin oxidized surface layer
Mold — surface mold on butter may be scraped if superficial and the underlying butter smells and tastes normal. Mold that has penetrated more than 1/4 inch is a discard indicator


8.5 Aged Cheese
Standard: Aged hard and semi-hard cheeses have different spoilage indicators from fresh cheeses due to their lower moisture content and complex rind ecology. The general principle is that hard cheese surface mold is often trimmable while interior spoilage or mold in soft cheeses is always a discard indicator.
The trimming rule for hard cheese:

Hard cheeses (moisture below 35%) with surface mold: cut away mold and at least 1 inch in all directions from visible mold. The underlying cheese, if it smells and tastes normally for that cheese style, is safe
This rule applies to: cheddar, gouda, parmesan, pecorino, gruyère, manchego, and similar hard cheeses
This rule does not apply to: brie, camembert, fresh mozzarella, ricotta, cottage cheese, or any soft or fresh cheese regardless of age

Normal aging characteristics by style:

White surface bloom (brie, camembert): intended Penicillium candidum mold — white, velvety, mild mushroom-earthy aroma. Normal throughout the aging period
Orange-pink washed rind (limburger, taleggio): intended Brevibacterium linens surface — pungent ammonia-funk aroma that is characteristic, not a spoilage indicator
Blue interior veining (gorgonzola, stilton): intended Penicillium roqueforti — blue-green veins throughout the interior. Normal at any age

Discard indicators for aged cheese:
Whole uncut wheels:

Cracks in the rind with mold penetrating beyond the surface into the crack channel — the crack mold is not trimmable because the penetration depth cannot be assessed without cutting
Pink or orange discoloration in cracks or on rind surfaces where it is not expected from the cheese style — may indicate pathogenic mold
Unusual ammonia odor beyond the characteristic level for the specific cheese style — excessive ammonia indicates protein breakdown from putrefactive bacteria

Cut cheese:

Interior mold that is not the intended mold for that cheese style — blue-green interior mold in a cheddar is not intentional P. roqueforti, it is contamination
Slimy interior surfaces — indicates high-moisture bacteria establishing in the cut surface
Unusual bitter or putrid flavor — normal aged cheese may be sharp, pungent, or acidic. Bitter or putrid notes are spoilage indicators
Discard any cut cheese where the surface mold extends to the depth of the cut surface on both sides of a wedge — the mold has penetrated through the cut piece


8.6 Canned and Home-Preserved Foods
Standard: This section consolidates the inspection standards from Section 3.12 and extends them to commercially canned foods. The critical limitation — botulinum toxin invisibility — applies equally to home-preserved and commercially canned low-acid foods.
Pre-opening inspection — all canned foods:
Commercial cans:

Discard any can with: swelling or bulging at top or bottom, rust penetrating the metal (surface rust that can be wiped off is acceptable, rust that has pitted the metal is not), dents at seams (top or bottom edge dents — body dents not involving seams are generally safe), leaking seals
Swollen commercial cans: the gas producing the swelling is almost certainly from microbial activity inside the can. Swollen commercial cans of low-acid foods present the same botulism risk as swollen home-canned jars — discard without opening

Home-canned jars:

Apply full pre-opening inspection from Section 3.12:

Lid lift test — sealed lids cannot be lifted by finger pressure
Concave lid — sealed lids curve downward at center
No rust, corrosion, or damage to lid
No swelling or gas pressure at seal



Upon opening — all canned foods:

Outward gas hiss upon opening — any gas escaping from the jar or can (versus normal inward rush of air as vacuum releases) indicates internal gas production. Discard without tasting
Unusual odor — evaluate immediately upon opening before other room odors compete
Unusual appearance — unexpected color, texture, or liquid clarity changes

The botulism reminder — consolidation:

Low-acid home-canned foods (pH above 4.6) that pass all sensory inspection may still contain botulinum toxin
The 10-minute boiling treatment for uncertain home-canned low-acid foods — per Section 3.12 — destroys toxin that may be present. This treatment is appropriate for food of uncertain processing history, not as a routine step for correctly processed food
Never taste-test canned food to evaluate safety — tasting is the primary mechanism of botulism exposure from contaminated home-canned food


8.7 Fermented Foods
Standard: Fermented foods have a broader range of normal sensory characteristics than most other foods — sourness, carbonation, strong odors, and unusual textures are expected. The spoilage indicators below distinguish genuine spoilage from normal fermentation character.
Normal fermentation characteristics that are not spoilage:

Sour odor and taste — expected in all lacto-fermented products
Carbonation and bubbling — expected in active fermentations and normal in stored fermented beverages
White film on brine surface (kahm yeast) — aesthetically undesirable but not a safety concern if pH is below 4.6
Cloudiness in brine — normal result of lactic acid bacteria proliferation
Strong pungent odor in fermented fish products, kimchi, and aged miso — characteristic, not spoilage

Discard indicators for fermented vegetables:

Green, blue, black, or pink mold of any kind — not kahm yeast film but actual mold growth
Putrid or fecal odor — beyond the expected sour-fermented character
Soft, mushy vegetables that were firm at production and during normal fermentation — indicates pectinase activity from unwanted organisms
pH above 4.6 at completion — as established in Section 6.3. Food that has not acidified cannot be saved by sensory inspection finding it acceptable

Discard indicators for fermented beverages:

Vinegar-like odor in a product not intended to be vinegary — indicates acetobacter contamination converting alcohol to acetic acid
Unusual colors — pink, orange, brown discoloration beyond expected amber-gold tones
Ropy or slimy texture — bacterial contamination producing exopolysaccharides

Discard indicators for miso and long-fermented pastes:

Pink or orange mold — as per Section 6.8. Do not taste. Discard
Putrid ammonia odor beyond the expected miso character — indicates protein breakdown from unwanted bacteria


8.8 Dry Stored Goods
Standard: Dry stored grains, legumes, and flours are among the most stable foods in community storage but develop specific spoilage indicators when storage conditions have failed. The indicators below identify storage failures that have affected safety or usability.
Normal characteristics of correctly stored dry goods:

Grains and legumes: dry, hard, free-flowing, characteristic mild grain odor. No visible insects or webbing. No surface discoloration or unusual markings
Flours: fine, dry, free-flowing powder. Mild grain odor. No clumping beyond minor caking from humidity
Dried pasta: hard, brittle, breaks cleanly

Discard indicators:
Insect infestation:

Visible insects — weevils, beetle larvae, Indian meal moth larvae — in the grain or flour
Webbing — Indian meal moths produce visible silk webbing in infested grain
Frass — small dark insect droppings visible in the grain mass
A small number of visible dead insects in grain treated with oxygen absorbers is normal — the insects that were present as eggs at packaging have died from oxygen deprivation. This is not active infestation and the grain is safe after sifting
Active infestation (live insects) requires evaluation: freeze at 0°F for 4 days to kill all insects, then sift, inspect, and repackage. If infestation is extensive and damage is significant, assess usability of the remaining grain

Mold:

Visible mold growth on grains or legumes — usually gray or blue-green
Musty odor — the distinctive smell of mold metabolites even before visible growth is apparent
Clumping of grains that were not clumped before — indicates moisture accumulation supporting mold growth
Mold on stored grain is a discard indicator — the mycotoxins produced by grain molds (aflatoxin, deoxynivalenol, fumonisin) are invisible, distributed throughout the affected grain mass, not removable by sifting, and not destroyed by cooking

Moisture damage:

Clumping that cannot be broken apart — indicates moisture absorption above safe water activity
Gray or dark discoloration of grains that were previously uniform in color — may indicate early mold activity
Any evidence of water intrusion — darkening in patterns consistent with liquid flow, wet appearance of grain at the container base

Flour-specific indicators:

Rancid odor — whole wheat flour and other fat-containing flours develop rancidity over time. Mildly rancid flour may be used in strongly flavored baked goods. Significantly rancid flour shall be discarded
Lumps in flour that do not break apart — indicates moisture absorption. Evaluate water activity before using in storage applications


8.9 Fats, Oils, and Rancidity Assessment
Standard: Rancidity is the primary spoilage mechanism in fats and oils. The assessment criteria below enable identification of rancid fats before they are used in food preparation.
Rancidity assessment:
Smell:

Fresh fat or oil: mild characteristic odor appropriate to the source — butter-like for butter and ghee, olive-fruity for olive oil, neutral for refined vegetable oils, coconut-like for coconut oil
Rancid fat or oil: one or more of the following: sharp crayon-like or waxy smell, paint-like or varnish-like smell, sharp metallic smell, musty or cardboard-like smell
The crayon or wax smell is the most reliable early indicator of oxidative rancidity — it is produced by the aldehyde compounds generated in the early stages of fat oxidation

Taste:

Rancid fat produces a sharp, bitter, or acrid aftertaste distinct from the normal flavor of fresh fat
Do not consume significant quantities of rancid fat for evaluation — a small taste to identify rancidity is sufficient

Appearance:

Cloudy vegetable oils — may indicate rancidity but also may be normal cold-temperature cloudiness that resolves at room temperature. Evaluate odor before concluding rancidity from cloudiness alone
Unusual color development — yellowing in normally clear oils, darkening in normally pale oils

Discard standard:

Any fat or oil with rancid odor or taste shall be discarded — rancid fats cannot be remediated and the harmful oxidation products they contain are not eliminated by cooking


8.10 Fresh Produce
Standard: Fresh produce evaluation is the food safety assessment performed most frequently in community settings. The indicators below focus on distinguishing normal surface imperfections from genuine spoilage requiring discard.
The trim versus discard decision:

Surface damage without penetration — bruising, skin breaks, insect surface damage — in firm produce (apples, carrots, potatoes, winter squash): trim the affected area with a margin and use the remainder
Mold on firm produce: trim with a generous margin (1 inch minimum). If mold has penetrated to the trimmed surface, discard entirely
Any soft spot or mushy area: discard — soft tissue indicates cell breakdown that has allowed bacterial or mold penetration throughout the affected area and potentially beyond

Discard indicators by produce type:
Root vegetables (carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips):

Soft spots anywhere on the root — discard the entire root or trim aggressively to firm tissue only
Mold at the crown or cut top surface — trim to below the mold. If mold is extensive, discard
Fermented or alcoholic odor — indicates anaerobic bacteria in damaged tissue. Discard

Potatoes:

Green skin or flesh — solanine development from light exposure. Trim all green areas with generous margin. Potatoes that are more than 25% green shall be discarded — solanine concentration throughout is too high for safe consumption
Soft or hollow areas — discard affected portions. Hollow potatoes (hollow heart) are a physiological condition, not spoilage — the hollow area is safe if the surrounding tissue is firm and normal
Black internal areas — bacterial ring rot or blackheart. Discard

Onions and garlic:

Soft spots in the bulb — discard the entire bulb. Soft spots in alliums indicate bacterial soft rot that has penetrated throughout the bulb layers
Mold on the outer dry skins only — remove affected outer layers. If inner layers are firm and free of mold, use
Blue-green mold between layers — discard

Leafy vegetables:

Slimy leaves — remove and discard slimy layers. If sliminess is limited to outer leaves and inner leaves are crisp and odor-free, use inner leaves promptly
Black or brown rot penetrating from the cut base upward — discard

Winter squash:

Soft spots — discard the entire squash. Soft spots in squash indicate moisture has entered and bacterial rot has begun throughout the flesh
Surface mold on intact squash skin — wipe with a cloth dampened with dilute vinegar solution. If mold has penetrated the skin, discard


8.11 Mold — When to Trim Versus Discard
Standard: Mold on food is among the most common spoilage scenarios in community food storage and one where the trim-versus-discard decision has the most significant food waste implications. This standard consolidates the guidance from throughout the document into a single reference.
The principle behind the trim-versus-discard decision:
Mold grows from spores that land on food surfaces and extend hyphae — root-like structures — into the food. The depth of hyphal penetration depends on the food's moisture content and texture. In hard, dry foods the hyphae cannot penetrate deeply and visible mold represents essentially all the contamination. In soft, moist foods hyphae penetrate deeply and quickly — visible surface mold represents only the tip of the contamination.
Discard without trimming — mold on any of these:

Soft fruits and vegetables — berries, tomatoes, cucumbers, peaches, plums, avocados
All fresh and soft cheeses — ricotta, cottage cheese, fresh chèvre, fresh mozzarella, cream cheese
Bread, cakes, and baked goods
Yogurt, sour cream, and other cultured dairy products
Fluid milk and cream
Deli meats and cooked meats
Jams and jellies — despite high sugar content, mold hyphae penetrate throughout
Nuts and nut butters — aflatoxin risk from mold penetration
All dried fruits with visible mold

Trim with 1-inch margin, use remainder — mold on these only:

Hard cheeses (moisture below 35%) — cheddar, gouda, parmesan, gruyère, manchego
Hard vegetables — carrots, turnips, parsnips, beets, cabbage (remove outer leaves)
Hard fruits — apples, pears (trim to firm flesh with no discoloration at trim surface)
Hard cured meats — firm dry-cured salami (surface mold only, not interior)

Discard all regardless of firmness:

Any food with pink, orange, or black mold — these colors may indicate toxin-producing species
Any food where mold has been recurring in the same location — indicates contamination source that surface removal does not address
Any food where the trimmed surface reveals discoloration, unusual odor, or soft tissue at the cut


8.12 Pest Contamination Indicators
Standard: Pest contamination of stored food — from rodents, insects, or other animals — requires specific evaluation because pest damage creates both direct contamination (feces, urine, hair, body parts) and secondary contamination pathways (packaging breaches that allow moisture, oxygen, and additional pest access).
Rodent contamination indicators:

Droppings — small dark pellets in or near stored food. Rodent droppings indicate that rodents have had access to the storage area and potentially to food containers
Gnaw marks — rodents chew through packaging and container walls. Any container with gnaw marks shall be considered contaminated
Urine staining — may be visible under UV light as fluorescent trails. Rodent urine carries Leptospira bacteria and hantavirus in some regions
Hair — rodent hair in food is visible and indicates direct contact
Nesting material — shredded packaging, paper, or fabric in or near food containers indicates nesting activity in the storage facility
Unusual food odors — rodent urine has a characteristic ammonia-like odor that transfers to contaminated food

Response to rodent contamination:

Any food with confirmed rodent contact — droppings in the container, gnaw marks on the container, visible rodent hair in the food — shall be discarded
The surrounding facility shall be inspected for the extent of infestation before addressing individual contaminated items — a facility with active rodent infestation will continue to contaminate food until the infestation is resolved
Rodent droppings on sealed containers without breached packaging do not necessarily indicate food contamination — clean the exterior and inspect the packaging integrity before evaluating the contents

Insect contamination indicators:

Visible live or dead insects in food — evaluate whether active infestation or dead insects from oxygen absorber treatment (dead insects in oxygen-treated sealed grain are normal)
Webbing — Indian meal moth webbing in grain is distinctive and indicates active or recent infestation
Frass — insect feces in grain or flour, visible as small dark particles
Clumped grain — grain that has been webbed together by insect activity

Response to insect contamination:

Active infestation (live insects): freeze at 0°F for 4 days to kill all life stages. Sift thoroughly to remove insects and frass. Inspect carefully — grain that has been significantly damaged by insect feeding may not be worth keeping. Repackage with oxygen absorbers
Dead insects without active infestation (oxygen absorber treated): sift to remove dead insects and frass. The grain is safe — the insects were killed by oxygen deprivation before they could damage the grain significantly
Heavily infested grain where insect activity has degraded the grain quality significantly: discard


8.13 Community Response to Suspected Contamination
Standard: When a community member identifies suspected spoilage or contamination in a distributed food product, the response shall be systematic — addressing the immediate product, the potential batch, and the facility or production source. The response protocols below are scaled to the nature and severity of the suspected contamination.
Individual product response:
Spoilage identified in a single item:

Discard the affected item
If the item came from community storage, notify the storage manager with lot number if available
If the item came from a community production program (fermentation, canning, dairy), notify the fermentation officer or food safety officer with batch identification
No further action required unless the item came from a lot that has been distributed to multiple community members

Batch response — potential distribution of affected lot:
Triggers for batch response:

Spoilage identified in an item from a known lot
Multiple community members reporting similar problems with items from the same production batch
Discovery of storage facility conditions that may have affected an entire section of inventory (moisture intrusion, temperature failure, pest activity)

Batch response protocol:

Identify all community members who received items from the affected lot using distribution records
Notify affected recipients by the fastest available communication means — describe the concern, identify the specific product and lot, and instruct recipients to quarantine the product pending further evaluation
Retrieve or evaluate retained samples from the affected lot — compare to recipient reports
Evaluate the scope of the problem — is it limited to one lot, one production session, or a systematic production failure?
Issue disposition instructions to affected recipients — discard, return, or hold pending further evaluation

Illness outbreak response:
If one or more community members report illness potentially associated with a community food product:

Activate the outbreak response protocol from Section 2.17, adapted for the specific product category
Suspend distribution of the suspected product immediately
Preserve all remaining samples from the affected lot
Notify the community medical officer
Document all ill community members, their symptoms, onset times, and the products consumed
Coordinate with the outbreak investigation process in 2.17

Facility contamination response:
If contamination is identified in the storage facility itself (mold, moisture, pest activity):

Assess the extent of facility contamination before evaluating individual items
Any item in the directly affected area shall be individually inspected before use or distribution
Address the facility contamination source — moisture intrusion, pest entry point, temperature failure — before returning items to the facility
Document the contamination event, affected area, and corrective actions taken

Rationale: The systematic response protocol — individual item, then batch, then facility — reflects the investigative logic of food safety response. A single spoiled item may be an isolated failure. Multiple reports from the same batch indicate a production or storage failure affecting many community members simultaneously. A facility contamination that was not identified until a consumer report may have been affecting multiple batches over an extended period. The investigation must proceed from the specific to the general — confirming the scope of the problem before the response can be appropriately scaled.
The distribution record requirement — knowing which lots went to which community members — is the operational infrastructure that makes batch response possible. A community that distributes food without lot tracking cannot notify affected recipients specifically — it must notify all recipients of all similar products, generating unnecessary alarm and waste beyond the actually affected lot. The lot tracking system described in Section 6.11 for fermented products and Section 7.8 for stored goods is the enabling infrastructure for the targeted response this protocol describes.
