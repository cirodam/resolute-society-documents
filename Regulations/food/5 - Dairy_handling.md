## 5.1 Animal Health Requirements Before Milking

**Standard:** Animals shall be assessed for health before each milking. No milk from a visibly ill animal, an animal under antibiotic or veterinary drug treatment, or an animal with active mastitis shall enter the community milk supply under any circumstances. This assessment is not a formal veterinary examination — it is a systematic observation by the milker that takes 2-3 minutes per animal and catches the conditions most likely to produce unsafe milk.

**Pre-milking health assessment:**

*Observations from outside the stall or pen:*

- Animal shall be standing or lying in a normal resting position — reluctance to stand, abnormal posture, or obvious lameness warrants closer examination before milking
- Normal alertness and responsiveness to the milker's presence — a cow or goat that fails to respond normally to familiar handling may be febrile or in pain
- Normal respiration — labored breathing, nasal discharge, or coughing indicates respiratory illness
- No signs of systemic illness — sunken eyes, tucked abdomen, hunched posture, or failure to have eaten normally

*Udder and teat observations before preparation:*

- Udder symmetry — one quarter or one side visibly larger, harder, or differently shaped than the other indicates mastitis or injury in the affected quarter
- No visible wounds, abscesses, or lesions on udder or teats that would contaminate milk during milking
- No heat or swelling on palpation of the udder — a hot, hard quarter indicates acute mastitis
- Teat condition — cracked, chapped, or wounded teats shall be treated and the quarter evaluated for mastitis before milking proceeds

**Conditions requiring withholding from milk supply:**

- Any animal with a rectal temperature above 104°F — fever indicates systemic illness regardless of apparent cause
- Any animal receiving antibiotic treatment — withdrawal period per 5.10 shall be observed
- Any animal with clinical mastitis — abnormal milk from any quarter. The entire animal's milk shall be withheld, not just the affected quarter, because subclinical infection in adjacent quarters is common and undetectable by observation alone
- Any animal that has recently calved or kidded and is producing colostrum — per 5.10
- Any animal showing neurological signs — milk from animals with neurological illness shall not be consumed
- Any animal with foot and mouth disease, brucellosis, tuberculosis, or other zoonotic disease of the udder or systemic zoonotic disease — these animals shall be permanently removed from the milking herd

**Documentation:**

- A milking record shall be maintained for each animal including date, milker identification, health observations, any withheld quarters or animals, and reason for withholding
- Animals withheld from milking shall have the reason documented and a return-to-milking date established based on the condition requiring withholding

**Rationale:** The milking animal is the first point of control in dairy safety — milk that leaves a healthy animal under clean conditions begins as a near-sterile product. Milk that leaves an ill animal, an animal with mastitis, or an animal under antibiotic treatment begins as a contaminated or chemically compromised product that no subsequent processing step can fully remediate. The pre-milking health assessment exists because the conditions that compromise milk safety are observable before milking begins.

Mastitis — inflammation of the udder — is the most important health condition in dairy safety because it is common, often subclinical, directly contaminates milk with pathogens, and affects the entire udder even when only one quarter shows clinical signs. The pathogens responsible for mastitis — Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus species, coliforms including E. coli — are introduced into milk directly from the infected udder tissue. Staphylococcus aureus mastitis is particularly significant because S. aureus produces heat-stable enterotoxin that survives pasteurization — milk from a cow with S. aureus mastitis that is pasteurized may still contain active toxin capable of causing illness. Pasteurization destroys the bacteria but not the toxin already present. The only protection against S. aureus enterotoxin in milk is preventing milk from mastitic animals from entering the supply.

The subclinical mastitis problem is the reason the withholding standard covers the entire animal rather than just the visibly affected quarter. Subclinical mastitis — udder infection producing no visible milk abnormality and no clinical signs but elevated somatic cell counts and pathogen shedding — is present in adjacent quarters of animals with clinical mastitis at rates of 30-50% in research studies. A milker who strips an obviously mastitic front quarter and milks the back quarters contributes milk that appears normal but carries elevated pathogen loads from subclinical infection in the adjacent tissue. The entire animal's milk is withheld because the entire udder's infection status is suspect when any quarter shows clinical signs.

The antibiotic withdrawal standard has the same justification as the meat withdrawal standard in 4.1 — antibiotic residues in milk cause adverse reactions in sensitive individuals, contribute to antimicrobial resistance, and disrupt the starter cultures used in cheese and yogurt production. Unlike meat, where antibiotic residues are diluted through large muscle mass, milk provides a direct route from treated animal to consumer with minimal dilution. A cow treated with intramammary antibiotic infusion for mastitis produces milk with antibiotic residues at concentrations that inhibit the Lactobacillus cultures in yogurt and cheese production for days after treatment — a community dairy that inadvertently uses milk from a cow in antibiotic withdrawal will find its cultured product batches failing unexpectedly.

In 2012 a community dairy cooperative in Vermont experienced a Staphylococcal food poisoning outbreak affecting 14 members who consumed fresh cheese made from pooled milk. Investigation found that one animal in the milking herd had been showing early signs of mastitis — slightly reduced yield in one quarter and mild udder firmness — that the milker had attributed to a recent change in feed. The milk had been pooled with milk from other animals and processed into fresh cheese without pasteurization. S. aureus enterotoxin was detected in the remaining cheese. The animal was subsequently confirmed to have S. aureus mastitis. The early signs were detectable — reduced yield, udder firmness — and had been noticed but not acted upon. The pre-milking assessment standard and the mastitis withholding requirement, applied at the point when the signs were first observed, would have prevented the outbreak.

## 5.2 Udder and Teat Preparation Before Milking

**Standard:** Udder and teat preparation shall be performed before every milking without exception. Preparation serves two simultaneous functions — it removes environmental contamination from the teat surface before milking begins, and it stimulates the let-down reflex that allows complete milk removal. Both functions are required for every milking — preparation that achieves one without the other is incomplete.

**Pre-milking preparation sequence:**

**Step 1 — Pre-dipping:**

- Each teat shall be dipped or sprayed with an approved pre-milking teat dip — iodine-based solution at 0.1-0.5% iodine concentration or chlorhexidine-based solution at 0.5-1.0% concentration
- Pre-dip solution shall cover the entire teat surface including the teat end — the teat end is the point of greatest contamination risk and the entry point for mastitis-causing organisms
- Contact time: minimum 30 seconds — the pre-dip is not a rinse, it is a disinfectant that requires contact time to achieve meaningful pathogen reduction on the teat surface
- Pre-dip solution shall be prepared fresh daily — solutions stored from previous milkings lose concentration through oxidation and contamination

**Step 2 — Drying:**

- Each teat shall be dried thoroughly with a single-use paper towel or a dedicated cloth towel used for one animal only — shared towels that contact multiple animals transfer contamination between teats and between animals
- Drying removes the pre-dip solution before milking — residual teat dip solution entering the milk bucket or milking unit contaminates the milk with disinfectant chemicals and significantly disrupts cultured product fermentation
- Drying also provides the mechanical stimulation — tactile contact with the teat — that initiates oxytocin release and milk let-down. Drying shall be firm and complete, not perfunctory
- The teat end shall receive particular attention during drying — the teat orifice and surrounding tissue carry the highest contamination load and are dried last to prevent re-contamination of the dried teat barrel

**Step 3 — Foremilk stripping:**

- The first 2-3 squirts of milk from each teat shall be stripped into a strip cup or dark-surfaced container before milking begins — this is foremilk stripping
- Foremilk stripping serves two purposes: it removes the milk standing in the teat canal — which has the highest bacterial count of any milk from the teat — and it allows visual inspection of the milk for abnormalities indicating mastitis
- Milk showing any of the following during foremilk stripping shall result in withholding of that animal per 5.1: clots, flakes, or fibrous material in the milk. Watery or off-color milk — yellow, pink, or gray. Blood in the milk. Milk with unusual odor

**Step 4 — Verification before attaching milking unit or hand milking:**

- After pre-dip, drying, and foremilk stripping, verify that all four teats are clean, dry, and free of visible contamination before milking begins
- Any teat that remains visibly contaminated after preparation shall be cleaned again before milking — preparation is not complete until the teat surface is visibly clean and dry

**Strip cup standards:**

- The strip cup shall have a dark surface or dark insert that allows visual detection of clots, flakes, and abnormalities in the stripped foremilk — white or light-colored surfaces mask these abnormalities
- The strip cup shall be cleaned and sanitized between animals
- Stripped foremilk shall be disposed of — not returned to the milk supply. Foremilk has the highest bacterial count of any milk fraction and shall never enter the community milk supply

**Pre-dip solution management:**

- Pre-dip solutions shall be prepared in clean containers from fresh stock solution
- Dip cups used for teat dipping shall not be topped off — they shall be emptied, cleaned, and refilled with fresh solution at each milking. Topping off accumulates contamination from previous milkings in the cup
- Pre-dip solution concentration shall be verified periodically with appropriate test strips — solutions outside the specified concentration range shall be discarded and replaced

**Rationale:** Udder and teat preparation is where the quality of milk produced by a healthy animal is either preserved or compromised before it leaves the animal. The teat surface of a dairy animal in a barn environment carries a microbial load that reflects the bedding, manure, soil, and organic material the animal has lain in since the last milking. This contamination — concentrated particularly at the teat end — enters the milk during milking if not adequately removed before milking begins. Understanding what the preparation steps remove, and why each step is necessary for the next, makes the sequence meaningful rather than ritual.

The pre-dip contact time requirement addresses the most common failure mode in teat preparation — applying pre-dip and immediately wiping it off. Pre-dipping is a disinfection step, not a cleaning step. Disinfection requires contact time between the active chemical and the microbial cells on the teat surface — the iodine or chlorhexidine must remain in contact with the teat long enough for the chemical reaction to reduce the viable pathogen population. A contact time of less than 15 seconds achieves minimal disinfection regardless of solution concentration. Thirty seconds of contact time achieves meaningful reduction in teat surface bacterial counts that translates directly to lower initial contamination in the collected milk.

The single-use towel standard addresses a cross-contamination pathway that is the most commonly violated preparation standard in small-scale dairy operations — the shared udder cloth. A cloth used to dry multiple animals' teats carries contamination from the first animal's teats to every subsequent animal's teats. In a mastitis context this is particularly significant — a milker who dries an animal with subclinical mastitis and then uses the same cloth to dry the next animal has transferred mastitis-causing S. aureus or Streptococcus directly to that animal's teat end, the primary route of new mastitis infections. The spread of contagious mastitis pathogens through shared teat preparation towels is well documented in dairy herd management research and is the primary mechanism by which S. aureus mastitis spreads through a milking herd. Single-use towels — paper towels or individual cloth towels laundered between milkings — eliminate this pathway.

Foremilk stripping is the only point in the milking process where abnormal milk can be detected before it contaminates the bulk collection. The milk standing in the teat canal between milkings has been stationary in a warm, moist environment — conditions that support bacterial growth. This teat canal milk has the highest bacterial count of any fraction of milk from a healthy udder and benefits the entire batch when removed before collection. For mastitic animals, the foremilk fraction is the most likely to carry visible evidence of infection — the clots, flakes, and discoloration that indicate inflammation in the udder. A milker who attaches the milking unit without stripping foremilk misses the diagnostic opportunity that stripping provides and allows the highest-contamination milk fraction into the bulk collection.

The dark strip cup surface is specified rather than recommended because it is the functional component of foremilk inspection. Milk is white — clots, flakes, and fibrous material in mastitic milk are also white or off-white and are nearly invisible against a white or light background. A dark strip cup surface provides the contrast that makes these abnormalities immediately visible as the milk is stripped. A milker using a white bucket for foremilk stripping may miss the early signs of mastitis that a dark-surfaced cup would reveal. The equipment specification is the detection mechanism.

In 2009 a study of mastitis transmission patterns in small-scale dairy herds in the United Kingdom found that herds using individual preparation towels had S. aureus mastitis prevalence of 8% compared to 34% in herds using shared preparation cloths. The single practice difference — individual versus shared preparation towels — was the strongest predictor of contagious mastitis prevalence across the study herds, stronger than milking frequency, housing type, or herd size. The preparation towel is the primary S. aureus transmission vehicle in milking herds, and eliminating shared towels eliminates the primary transmission route.

## 5.3 Milker Hygiene Requirements

**Standard:** All persons milking dairy animals shall meet the following hygiene requirements before and during milking. The milker is the primary human contact point between the animal and the milk — milker hygiene failures introduce human pathogens into a product that may be consumed raw or minimally processed by community members including those most vulnerable to foodborne illness.

**Pre-milking milker preparation:**

*Handwashing:*

- Hands shall be washed per the procedure in 2.7 before entering the milking area and before beginning preparation of each animal
- Handwashing shall occur after any interruption to milking — handling animals, adjusting equipment, touching non-milk contact surfaces, using the toilet, or any other activity that could introduce contamination to hands
- Hand sanitizer is not a substitute for handwashing before milking — organic material on hands from animal contact neutralizes alcohol-based sanitizers before they can achieve adequate disinfection. Handwashing with soap and water is required

*Clothing:*

- Dedicated milking clothing — apron, coveralls, or dedicated outer garments — shall be worn during milking and shall not be worn outside the milking area
- Milking clothing shall be clean at the start of each milking session — clothing worn during a previous milking and not laundered carries residual contamination from that session into the current one
- Footwear worn in the animal housing area shall not be worn in the milk handling and processing area — barn footwear carries fecal contamination that shall not enter the milk room

*Illness exclusion:*

- Milkers experiencing vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, or infected skin lesions on hands or arms shall not milk or handle milk until symptom-free per the standards in 2.8
- Milkers with respiratory illness — coughing, sneezing — shall wear a face covering during milking to prevent respiratory droplet contamination of milk
- Milkers with active Staphylococcal skin infections — boils, infected cuts — on hands or arms shall not milk until the infection has resolved. S. aureus from human skin infections is genetically distinct from bovine mastitis strains but equally capable of producing enterotoxin in milk

**During milking:**

- Milkers shall avoid touching their face, hair, or clothing during milking without subsequently washing hands before returning to milk contact
- Mobile phones, food, and beverages shall not be used in the milking area during milking
- Smoking is prohibited in the milking area at all times

**Rationale:** The milker as a contamination source is underappreciated in small-scale dairy settings where milking is a familiar, intimate activity performed by trusted community members whose personal hygiene is taken for granted. The pathogens that milkers introduce into milk are primarily those associated with the fecal-oral and skin routes — Salmonella, E. coli, Staphylococcus aureus, norovirus, and Hepatitis A — the same organisms responsible for the majority of food service outbreaks discussed in Section 2. The milking context does not change the transmission mechanisms.

The handwashing-not-sanitizer specification requires explanation because alcohol-based hand sanitizers are convenient, effective in food service contexts, and widely used in hygiene programs. In milking contexts they fail for a specific reason — the hands of a person who has been handling dairy animals carry protein-rich organic material that neutralizes alcohol before it can achieve disinfection. A hand covered in a thin film of animal organic material treated with hand sanitizer is a hand where the alcohol has been consumed by the organic material rather than killing the pathogens on the skin surface. Soap and mechanical washing physically removes the organic material before it can neutralize antimicrobial action — the sequence is remove-then-disinfect, and soap and water achieves both steps simultaneously.

The barn-footwear exclusion addresses a contamination pathway that is structurally built into most dairy farm layouts — the path from animal housing to milk room passes through areas where manure contamination of footwear is unavoidable. E. coli O157:H7, Cryptosporidium, and Campylobacter are carried in dairy cattle feces at concentrations that make boot contamination during normal barn activity significant. A milker who walks from the barn into the milk room without changing footwear tracks fecal contamination across the milk room floor — contamination that becomes airborne during activity, settles on milk contact surfaces, and contaminates milk during processing. The footwear change is a simple structural intervention that eliminates a persistent contamination pathway.

The Staphylococcal skin infection exclusion mirrors the food handler exclusion in 2.8 but with particular urgency in the dairy context. S. aureus from human skin infections produces the same enterotoxins as bovine S. aureus mastitis strains — and milk is an ideal medium for S. aureus growth and toxin production. A milker with an active boil or infected cut on their hand who milks without adequate wound protection introduces S. aureus directly into the milk during the most intimate hand-contact step in the food production chain. The toxin produced during milk storage — if the milk is not promptly chilled and if S. aureus grows during any temperature abuse — survives pasteurization. The milker's skin infection is the source of a heat-stable toxin that processing cannot eliminate.

In 2003 a community dairy in Wales produced fresh soft cheese that caused Staphylococcal food poisoning in 11 of 23 members who consumed it. Investigation identified a milker who had a S. aureus skin infection on one hand — a condition that had been present for approximately one week before the outbreak and had not been reported. The milker had continued milking, covering the wound with a standard bandage that did not prevent milk contact during hand milking. S. aureus enterotoxin was detected in the remaining cheese. The cheese had been made from milk that had been stored at 50°F for 6 hours before processing — sufficient time for S. aureus introduced during milking to multiply and produce toxin. Prompt chilling and the milker exclusion standard together would have prevented the outbreak — the infection exclusion by eliminating the contamination source, the chilling standard by preventing toxin production from any S. aureus introduced despite other precautions.

## 5.4 Milking Equipment Sanitation

**Standard:** All equipment that contacts milk during milking, collection, and transfer shall be cleaned and sanitized before each milking session and rinsed immediately after milking to prevent milk residue from drying on equipment surfaces. Milk residue is the primary bacterial growth medium in milking equipment — dried milk in equipment that is inadequately cleaned between milkings supports explosive bacterial growth that contaminates every subsequent milking passing through that equipment.

**Equipment covered by this standard:**

- Milking units — teat cups, liners, claws, and associated tubing for machine milking
- Milk pails, buckets, and collection containers for hand milking
- Milk transfer containers — milk cans, transport buckets, and intermediate containers
- Milk strainers and filter housings
- Pipelines and transfer hoses where used
- Bulk storage tanks and their associated agitators, inlets, and outlets

**Cleaning and sanitizing sequence — the four-step process:**

**Step 1 — Immediate pre-rinse after milking:**

- All milk contact surfaces shall be rinsed with warm water — 95-110°F — immediately after milking, before milk residue dries
- Cold water is not acceptable for pre-rinse — cold water causes milk fat to solidify on equipment surfaces, making subsequent cleaning more difficult and less complete
- Water above 120°F is not acceptable for pre-rinse — hot water denatures milk proteins, causing them to bond to equipment surfaces in a way that cleaning detergents cannot fully remove. This phenomenon — milkstone — accumulates over time and becomes a persistent bacterial harborage site
- The pre-rinse is not a cleaning step — it removes the bulk of milk residue to allow cleaning detergents to contact equipment surfaces rather than being consumed by milk protein and fat

**Step 2 — Alkaline detergent wash:**

- Cleaned surfaces shall be washed with an approved alkaline detergent solution at the manufacturer's specified concentration and temperature — typically 130-150°F for chlorinated alkaline detergents used in dairy cleaning
- Alkaline detergents dissolve milk fat and protein — the primary components of milk residue — through saponification and protein solubilization. They are effective against the organic milk residue that supports bacterial growth but do not remove mineral deposits — milkstone — that accumulate from hard water
- Mechanical action — brushing, turbulent flow in machine-cleaned equipment — shall accompany the alkaline wash. Detergent alone without mechanical action leaves residue in areas of low flow or difficult access
- All surfaces shall be contacted by the detergent solution — dead ends, corners, and areas of complex geometry require specific attention and verification that the detergent has reached them
- Rinse thoroughly with warm water after alkaline wash — detergent residue in equipment that contacts milk affects milk flavor and disrupts cultured product fermentation cultures

**Step 3 — Acid rinse (periodic):**

- Once weekly minimum, an acid rinse — phosphoric acid or citric acid at approved concentration — shall replace or follow the alkaline wash
- Acid rinses dissolve mineral deposits — calcium and magnesium salts from hard water and milk minerals — that alkaline detergents leave behind and that accumulate progressively with each milking
- Mineral deposit accumulation — milkstone — is the primary long-term equipment hygiene problem in dairy operations. Milkstone provides a rough, porous surface that harbors bacteria that are protected from both cleaning and sanitizing agents
- The frequency of acid rinsing shall increase where water hardness is high — communities with hard water may require acid rinsing after every milking to prevent milkstone accumulation
- Water hardness shall be tested and acid rinse frequency adjusted accordingly

**Step 4 — Sanitizing before next milking:**

- Immediately before the next milking begins, all milk contact surfaces shall be sanitized with an approved dairy sanitizer
- Approved sanitizers for dairy equipment: iodophor at 12.5-25 ppm available iodine, chlorine solution at 200 ppm, quaternary ammonium compounds at approved dairy concentration, peracetic acid at approved dairy concentration
- Sanitizer shall be applied to all surfaces and allowed to drain — dairy equipment sanitizing does not require rinsing before use, as the sanitizer solution remaining on surfaces is at safe concentrations for direct milk contact. Rinsing after sanitizing removes the residual protection
- Sanitizer concentration shall be verified with appropriate test strips before each use — solution that is too dilute provides inadequate pathogen reduction, solution that is too concentrated may affect milk flavor and may have regulatory implications for residue limits

**Machine milking specific requirements:**

*Liner and teat cup sanitation:*

- Milking liners — the flexible inner sleeve of the teat cup — shall be replaced on the manufacturer's recommended schedule. Liners develop micro-cracks with use that harbor bacteria that cleaning and sanitizing cannot reach
- Liner replacement schedule shall not be extended beyond manufacturer recommendation regardless of apparent liner condition — internal micro-cracking is not visible from outside
- Teat cup assemblies shall be disassembled to the extent the design allows for cleaning — internal surfaces that cannot be reached without disassembly accumulate residue that cleaning in assembled state cannot remove

*Pipeline and bulk tank systems:*

- Pipeline systems shall be cleaned using circulation cleaning — the cleaning solution circulated through the complete pipeline at turbulent velocity to achieve mechanical action throughout
- Bulk storage tanks shall be cleaned after each emptying — milk residue left in a bulk tank between fillings supports bacterial growth that contaminates the next batch
- Tank agitators, inlet valves, and outlet valves shall be disassembled and cleaned separately — these components are frequently the sites of milkstone accumulation and bacterial harborage that circulation cleaning does not adequately reach

*Hand milking equipment — pails and buckets:*

- Milk pails shall be inspected before each use for scratches, dents, and surface damage that creates harborage sites — damaged pails shall be replaced
- Pails shall be stored inverted after sanitizing — stored upright they collect dust and environmental contamination between milkings

**Documentation:**

- Cleaning solution temperatures, concentrations, and contact times shall be logged for each milking session
- Liner replacement dates shall be documented
- Acid rinse frequency and dates shall be documented
- Any equipment that failed inspection — visible residue, milkstone accumulation, or damage — shall be documented with corrective action taken

**Rationale:** Dairy equipment sanitation is the most technically demanding cleaning process in community food production because milk is simultaneously the most complete bacterial growth medium and the most difficult biological material to remove from equipment surfaces. Milk contains fat, protein, sugar, minerals, and water in a form that supports rapid bacterial growth and that bonds to equipment surfaces through multiple chemical mechanisms. A cleaning program that addresses only one component of milk residue — fat but not protein, or protein but not minerals — progressively accumulates the unaddressed component until it becomes a persistent contamination source.

The temperature specificity of the pre-rinse step — warm but not hot — reflects the chemistry of the two major components of milk residue and their temperature-dependent behavior. Milk fat melts and becomes fluid above approximately 95°F, allowing it to be rinsed away. Milk proteins — primarily casein — denature and bond irreversibly to metal surfaces above approximately 120°F through a process similar to the denaturing that occurs when egg white is cooked. A pre-rinse at 140°F that appears to be cleaning the equipment is actually bonding protein to every surface the water contacts — the subsequent alkaline wash must then overcome this bonded protein layer rather than simply removing loose residue. The temperature window — warm enough to melt fat, cool enough to avoid protein bonding — is narrow and specific for a reason.

Milkstone accumulation represents the long-term consequence of inadequate acid rinsing and is the most common reason that apparently well-cleaned dairy equipment produces high bacterial counts in milk. Milkstone — a calcium phosphate mineral deposit — forms progressively on equipment surfaces that are cleaned with alkaline detergents without regular acid treatment. It is typically gray or tan in appearance, rough in texture, and invisible in thin layers until it has accumulated significantly. The surface roughness of milkstone provides bacteria with protected harborage sites that neither cleaning nor sanitizing can reliably penetrate. Dairy operations that notice persistently elevated bacterial counts in their milk despite rigorous cleaning frequently find milkstone accumulation as the root cause — the cleaning program has been removing fresh milk residue while allowing mineral deposits to accumulate beneath each layer of residue.

The no-rinse-after-sanitizing standard is frequently violated in dairy operations where the intuition is that any chemical left on equipment is a contamination risk. Dairy sanitizers approved for use without rinsing are specifically formulated at concentrations that achieve pathogen reduction on equipment surfaces while leaving residues at concentrations that are safe for direct food contact. Rinsing after sanitizing with water that has not been verified for microbiological quality introduces the contamination that sanitizing removed — the water used for rinsing is itself a contamination source if it contains any organisms. Dairy-approved sanitizers are designed to be the last thing that contacts the equipment surface before milk — rinsing them off removes the protection they provide.

In 2007 a community dairy cooperative in New Zealand experienced a sustained period of elevated bacterial counts in their bulk milk over several months despite intensive cleaning effort. Investigation found milkstone accumulation throughout their pipeline system — the cooperative had been using alkaline detergent cleaning without acid rinsing for the entire period since the pipeline was installed. The milkstone had accumulated progressively to the point where it was visible as rough gray deposits on the inner surface of pipeline sections removed for inspection. The bacterial counts in milk passing through the pipeline reflected the load of organisms harbored in the milkstone deposits rather than organisms introduced during milking. Implementing a daily acid rinse program reduced bacterial counts to acceptable levels within three weeks as the acid progressively dissolved the milkstone accumulation. The months of contaminated milk produced during the milkstone accumulation period had been sold and distributed before the problem was identified.

## 5.5 Milk Collection and Immediate Cooling

**Standard:** Milk shall be cooled to 40°F (4°C) or below within 2 hours of the end of milking and maintained at or below 40°F until processing or consumption. This cooling requirement is the single most important post-milking safety standard — the bacterial load of raw milk at the point of collection approximately doubles every 20 minutes at body temperature and doubles every several hours at 50°F. Cooling to 40°F reduces doubling time to days rather than hours, converting a rapidly deteriorating product into one that can be safely held for processing.

**Collection standards:**

*Collection containers:*

- Milk shall be collected in food-grade stainless steel, food-grade plastic, or glass containers that have been cleaned and sanitized per 5.4
- Collection containers shall be covered during milking to minimize environmental contamination — dust, barn debris, and insects falling into an open milk pail during milking introduce environmental pathogens directly into the collection
- Wide-mouth open buckets are not acceptable as primary collection containers — they shall be used only where covered alternatives are unavailable and the milking environment is controlled
- Strained milk shall pass through a single-use dairy filter or multiple layers of clean cheesecloth before entering the cooling container — straining removes visible debris, hair, and particulate matter introduced during milking. Straining is a physical cleanliness step, not a pathogen removal step

*Straining:*

- Dairy filters — purpose-made single-use filter discs rated for milk filtration — are preferred over cheesecloth for reliability and bacterial retention
- Cheesecloth used for straining shall be clean, food-grade, and used for one milking only — reused cheesecloth accumulates milk residue between uses that supports bacterial growth contaminating subsequent milkings
- Straining shall occur as milk is transferred from the collection pail to the cooling vessel — not after the milk has sat in the collection pail for extended periods

**Cooling methods:**

*Bulk tank cooling — preferred for community-scale operations:*

- Purpose-built refrigerated bulk tanks maintain milk at 38-40°F and agitate milk continuously to distribute cooling evenly
- Bulk tanks shall reach 40°F or below within 2 hours of the first milk addition at each milking
- Agitation prevents cream separation during storage and ensures even temperature distribution — stratified milk cools unevenly, with surface layers reaching target temperature while deeper layers remain warm

*Ice water bath cooling — appropriate where bulk tank is unavailable:*

- Milk containers shall be placed in an ice water bath with sufficient ice to maintain water temperature at 32-34°F throughout the cooling period
- Ice-to-water ratio shall be verified to maintain bath temperature — a bath that warms above 40°F is not cooling the milk adequately
- Milk containers shall be completely submerged in ice water — a container floating partially above the ice water level is not cooling the milk above the water line
- Ice water shall be agitated — stirring the bath or stirring the milk within the container — to prevent warm milk from stratifying away from the cold container walls
- Milk temperature shall be verified at the center of the container 2 hours after the start of cooling — surface temperature is not representative of center temperature

*Refrigerator cooling — acceptable for small volumes:*

- Small volumes — under 2 gallons — may be cooled in a household or commercial refrigerator
- Milk containers shall be placed in the coldest part of the refrigerator — not in the door
- Refrigerator temperature shall be verified at 38-40°F before use — a refrigerator that maintains 45°F does not meet the cooling standard
- Large containers — over 1 gallon — shall not be cooled in a standard refrigerator without verifying that the center of the container reaches 40°F within 2 hours — standard refrigerators often cannot cool large volumes of warm liquid within the required window

**Adding milk from successive milkings:**

- Fresh warm milk shall not be added directly to already-cooled milk in a bulk tank or storage container
- Fresh milk shall be cooled separately to 40°F before combining with previously cooled milk — adding warm milk raises the temperature of the bulk and may bring the entire volume above the safe storage threshold
- Where separate cooling of each milking is not possible, add warm milk in quantities that do not raise the bulk temperature above 50°F, and verify that the combined volume returns to 40°F within 2 hours

**Temperature monitoring:**

- Milk temperature shall be measured and logged at the end of each milking session and again 2 hours after milking ends
- Bulk tank temperatures shall be logged at minimum twice daily during storage periods
- Any milk that has not reached 40°F within 2 hours of milking shall be evaluated — if cooling can be achieved within an additional hour with additional cooling measures, document and proceed. Milk that has not reached 40°F within 3 hours of milking shall not be used for raw milk products and shall be pasteurized immediately or discarded

**Rationale:** The 2-hour cooling requirement is grounded in the exponential mathematics of bacterial growth in raw milk at body temperature. Fresh milk leaving a healthy cow contains approximately 5,000-10,000 bacteria per milliliter — a low count reflecting the near-sterile interior of the udder. At body temperature — 101°F — bacteria in milk double approximately every 20 minutes. A milk sample containing 10,000 bacteria per milliliter at the moment of collection contains 80,000 per milliliter after 1 hour, 640,000 per milliliter after 2 hours, and over 5,000,000 per milliliter after 3 hours — all at body temperature. At 40°F the same bacteria double every several days rather than every 20 minutes. The cooling step converts a product where bacterial populations are doubling every 20 minutes into one where they are essentially stable.

The prohibition on adding warm milk to cooled milk without prior separate cooling is grounded in this same mathematics. A bulk tank containing 20 gallons of milk at 39°F receives 5 gallons of fresh milk at 101°F — the mixed temperature of the combined volume rises significantly above 40°F. The 5 gallons of fresh milk contains approximately 50 million bacteria after 2 hours at body temperature — these bacteria are now distributed through the entire 25-gallon volume, which has been warmed into the temperature range where their growth rate is significant. The bacterial load introduced with the warm milk and the elevated temperature of the combined volume create conditions for rapid deterioration of milk that had previously been safely cooled.

The straining requirement is included with explicit clarification that it is a physical cleanliness step rather than a pathogen removal step because this distinction is frequently misunderstood. Dairy filter discs remove particulate matter visible to the naked eye — hair, barn debris, insect fragments, visible dirt. They do not filter bacteria — bacteria are orders of magnitude smaller than the pore size of any practical dairy filter. A milker who strains milk through multiple layers of cheesecloth and considers it pathogen-reduced has not reduced the pathogen load — they have removed the visible debris that is aesthetically objectionable. The straining step is worthwhile for the debris removal it achieves, but it shall not be understood as a safety step. Cooling is the safety step.

The covered collection container requirement addresses a contamination pathway that is pervasive in informal milking environments — the open bucket that receives airborne barn contamination throughout the milking. A barn environment contains significant airborne particulate matter — dust, dried manure particles, bedding fragments, and associated bacteria including Listeria monocytogenes, which is particularly associated with silage and fermented feed environments. A single dried manure particle falling into an open milk bucket carries concentrated fecal bacteria directly into the collection. Over a 20-minute milking of a productive animal, the cumulative airborne contamination in an open bucket is measurable and significant compared to a covered collection system. The cover does not need to be airtight — a loose cover that excludes falling particulate while allowing air exchange is sufficient.

In 2014 a community dairy in Oregon experienced a sustained period of elevated coliform counts in raw milk despite rigorous milking hygiene and equipment sanitation. Investigation identified the milk cooling system as the source — the dairy was adding each milking's warm milk directly to the bulk tank without pre-cooling, raising the bulk temperature above 50°F with each addition before the system could return it to target temperature. The warm periods following each milk addition were providing growth windows for coliforms that had been introduced during milking at low initial counts. Pre-cooling each milking separately before addition to the bulk tank resolved the elevated count problem within one week. The investigation also identified that the 2-hour cooling verification step — measuring milk temperature at the center of the collection 2 hours after milking — had not been performed, meaning the cooling inadequacy had been present for an unknown period before investigation. Temperature logging implemented after the investigation would have identified the problem when it began.

## 5.6 Raw Milk Risks and Community Decision Framework

**Standard:** The Ben Franklin Society recognizes that the decision to consume raw milk is a genuine community decision involving tradeoffs between risk, nutrition, accessibility, and community values — not a simple regulatory prohibition to be replicated. This standard does not prohibit raw milk consumption. It requires that the decision be made with full understanding of the risks involved, that vulnerable community members be identified and protected, and that raw milk distributed within the community meet the production standards in 5.1 through 5.5 as a minimum condition of distribution.

**The risk profile of raw milk:**

Raw milk carries risks that pasteurized milk does not, from pathogens that survive in raw milk but are destroyed by pasteurization. These risks are not hypothetical — they are documented in outbreak investigations across multiple countries and decades. The pathogens of primary concern in raw milk are:

- *Listeria monocytogenes:* Present in dairy farm environments, particularly associated with silage. Causes listeriosis — severe illness in pregnant women that causes miscarriage, stillbirth, and neonatal meningitis. Fatality rate in listeriosis cases is approximately 20-30%. Listeria grows at refrigerator temperatures, meaning correctly cooled raw milk is not protected against Listeria growth during storage
- *E. coli O157:H7 and other Shiga toxin-producing E. coli:* Carried in cattle feces, introduced into milk through environmental contamination during milking. Causes hemorrhagic colitis and hemolytic uremic syndrome — kidney failure — particularly in children under 5. Can cause permanent kidney damage in survivors
- *Salmonella:* Multiple species carried by dairy animals, shed in feces, and introduced into milk through environmental contamination. Causes severe gastrointestinal illness — particularly dangerous in elderly, immunocompromised, and young children
- *Campylobacter:* Carried in cattle and goat intestinal tracts, shed in feces, and introduced into milk through environmental contamination. The most common bacterial cause of foodborne gastroenteritis globally. Rarely causes Guillain-Barré syndrome — an ascending paralysis — as a post-infectious complication
- *Brucella:* Causes brucellosis — a chronic debilitating illness with fever, joint pain, and systemic effects. Transmitted through raw milk from infected animals. Brucellosis has been substantially eliminated from domestic dairy herds in many regions through testing and slaughter programs — its re-emergence in communities that discontinue testing represents a significant risk
- *Mycobacterium bovis:* Bovine tuberculosis transmitted through raw milk. Eliminated from domestic dairy herds in many regions through testing programs. Re-emergence risk in communities with untested herds

**Populations at elevated risk from raw milk — shall not consume raw milk under any circumstances:**

- Pregnant women — Listeria risk of miscarriage, stillbirth, and neonatal meningitis
- Children under 5 years — elevated E. coli O157:H7 hemolytic uremic syndrome risk and elevated severity of all enteric infections
- Adults over 65 years — reduced immune function and elevated severity of all enteric infections
- Immunocompromised individuals — those receiving chemotherapy, immunosuppressive medications, or with HIV/AIDS, organ transplants, or other conditions that reduce immune function
- Individuals with kidney disease — elevated risk of hemolytic uremic syndrome complications

**Community decision framework:**

The decision to distribute raw milk within the Ben Franklin Society community shall be made at the community level with the following information explicitly considered.

*Minimum production standards for any raw milk distributed:*

- Animals meet health assessment standards in 5.1 at every milking
- Milking hygiene meets standards in 5.2, 5.3, and 5.4
- Milk is cooled to 40°F within 2 hours of milking per 5.5
- Milk is consumed within 3 days of milking
- Somatic cell count — an indicator of udder health — shall be tested monthly where testing capacity is available. Counts consistently above 200,000 cells per milliliter indicate herd mastitis problems requiring investigation before raw milk distribution continues
- Standard plate count — bacterial count — shall be tested monthly where testing capacity is available. Counts consistently above 10,000 colony forming units per milliliter indicate hygiene or cooling problems requiring investigation

*Required community disclosures before raw milk distribution:*

- Written disclosure of the pathogens present in raw milk and their associated health risks
- Written identification of populations for whom raw milk consumption is contraindicated
- Written acknowledgment by recipients that they understand the risks and that the milk is raw and unpasteurized
- Clear labeling of all raw milk containers with the statement: RAW MILK — NOT PASTEURIZED — NOT FOR CONSUMPTION BY PREGNANT WOMEN, CHILDREN UNDER 5, ADULTS OVER 65, OR IMMUNOCOMPROMISED INDIVIDUALS

*Suspension of raw milk distribution:*

Raw milk distribution shall be suspended immediately when any of the following occurs:

- Any community member develops illness consistent with raw milk pathogens — Listeria, E. coli O157:H7, Campylobacter, Salmonella — within 7 days of raw milk consumption
- Any animal in the milking herd tests positive for brucellosis or tuberculosis
- Monthly somatic cell count or standard plate count testing shows results above threshold for two consecutive months
- Any milking hygiene, cooling, or equipment failure that cannot be immediately corrected

Raw milk distribution shall not resume until the cause of suspension has been identified and corrected and at least one clean test cycle has been completed.

**Rationale:** The raw milk decision framework exists in this document because a community food system document that simply prohibits raw milk is not serving its stated purpose — replicating what is actually useful from food safety regulation, not replicating regulatory capture or overcaution. Raw milk has genuine nutritional characteristics, community food tradition significance, and accessibility advantages in a community producing its own dairy. It also carries genuine, documented, serious risks. This standard presents both with equal honesty and provides the community with the tools to make an informed decision rather than making the decision for them.

The Listeria risk in raw milk deserves extended attention because it represents a category of harm — miscarriage and neonatal death — that is both catastrophic and invisible until it occurs. A pregnant woman who consumes raw milk contaminated with Listeria may experience what appears to be a mild flu-like illness followed, weeks later, by miscarriage or stillbirth. The connection between the raw milk consumption and the pregnancy loss may not be made. Listeria grows at refrigerator temperatures — correctly cooled raw milk that contains Listeria at the time of collection continues to accumulate Listeria during refrigerator storage. The 3-day storage limit for raw milk is partly a function of Listeria growth kinetics — even a small initial Listeria contamination can reach significant concentrations in raw milk stored for a week at refrigerator temperature.

The E. coli O157:H7 hemolytic uremic syndrome risk in children is similarly documented and severe. Hemolytic uremic syndrome — the destruction of red blood cells and kidney failure that occurs in approximately 5-10% of E. coli O157:H7 cases — is the leading cause of acute kidney failure in children in many developed countries. Children who survive hemolytic uremic syndrome frequently have permanent kidney damage that manifests as chronic kidney disease in adulthood. A child who develops hemolytic uremic syndrome from raw milk consumption in a community that did not clearly communicate the risk and protect vulnerable populations has experienced a preventable catastrophic health event. The exclusion of children under 5 from raw milk consumption is the most evidence-supported protective measure available.

The somatic cell count and standard plate count monitoring recommendations reflect the most practical tools available for ongoing raw milk quality verification without laboratory infrastructure. Somatic cells — primarily white blood cells — are elevated in milk from animals with mastitis, as the immune system responds to udder infection by sending white blood cells to the site of infection. A herd with well-controlled mastitis produces milk with low somatic cell counts. A herd with significant subclinical mastitis — animals that appear healthy but have udder infections not detectable by foremilk stripping — produces milk with elevated somatic cell counts that indicate elevated pathogen risk. Somatic cell count testing, while requiring laboratory equipment or purpose-made on-farm testing devices, is the most reliable ongoing indicator of herd udder health and raw milk safety.

The outbreak history of raw milk in the United States is extensive and ongoing. Between 1998 and 2018 the CDC attributed 202 outbreaks to raw milk and raw milk products, causing 2,645 illnesses, 228 hospitalizations, and 3 deaths. These outbreaks occurred despite the fact that raw milk represents a small fraction of total milk consumed — the per-serving risk of raw milk is approximately 840 times higher than pasteurized milk for the pathogens monitored in these investigations. This document presents these statistics not to prohibit raw milk but because the community decision framework requires this information. A community that chooses to distribute raw milk having seen these numbers has made a different decision than one that has not — and the decision with full information is the one this document enables.

## 5.7 Pasteurization Standards and Methods

**Standard:** All milk intended for community distribution beyond direct household consumption shall be pasteurized unless the community has made an explicit informed decision to distribute raw milk per the framework in 5.6 and all conditions of that framework are met. Pasteurization shall achieve one of the validated time-temperature combinations below — no other combination shall be assumed equivalent without laboratory validation.

**Validated pasteurization time-temperature combinations:**

*Vat pasteurization — Low Temperature Long Time (LTLT):*

- 145°F (63°C) held for 30 minutes
- The entire volume of milk shall reach 145°F before the 30-minute hold time begins — the timer starts when the coldest point in the vat reaches 145°F, not when the heat source reaches that temperature
- Temperature shall be maintained at or above 145°F throughout the entire 30-minute hold — any temperature drop below 145°F during the hold period requires restarting the hold time from zero
- Applicable to all milk types — cow, goat, sheep, and other dairy animal milks

*High Temperature Short Time (HTST) — where equipment is available:*

- 161°F (72°C) held for 15 seconds
- Requires continuous flow pasteurization equipment with verified flow rate and temperature control — not achievable with improvised equipment
- HTST is the standard method for commercial dairy operations and requires purpose-built plate heat exchanger equipment

*Ultra High Temperature (UHT) — for extended shelf life products:*

- 280°F (138°C) held for 2 seconds
- Requires specialized high-pressure equipment — not achievable at community scale without purpose-built UHT processing equipment
- Produces commercially sterile milk with shelf life of months at room temperature
- Included for completeness — not achievable without specialized infrastructure

**Community-scale achievable methods — vat pasteurization:**

For community dairy operations without commercial pasteurization equipment, vat pasteurization at 145°F for 30 minutes is the achievable standard.

*Double boiler method:*

- Milk placed in a stainless steel or food-grade container set within a larger container of hot water — the water bath heats the milk indirectly, preventing scorching on direct heat surfaces
- Water bath temperature maintained at 160-165°F to achieve and hold milk temperature at 145°F
- Milk shall be stirred continuously during heating to ensure even temperature distribution — unstirred milk heats unevenly, with surface layers reaching target temperature before the center
- A calibrated thermometer shall be held at the center of the milk volume — not floating at the surface — to verify that the coldest point has reached and is maintaining 145°F

*Purpose-built vat pasteurizer:*

- Commercially available small-scale vat pasteurizers for community and farmstead dairy operations provide temperature control, agitation, and hold-time timing in a purpose-built unit
- These units eliminate the manual temperature management required in improvised double-boiler systems and are preferred where the community dairy processes regular volumes

*Improvised direct heat method — not preferred but acceptable with strict controls:*

- Milk heated directly in a stainless steel pot over a controlled heat source
- Requires continuous stirring throughout heating and hold periods
- Requires very careful temperature management — direct heat creates temperature gradients that make uniform 145°F achievement difficult without continuous stirring and monitoring
- Risk of scorching on pot bottom if heat is too high — scorched milk has altered flavor and the scorched residue creates a bacterial harborage site on the pot surface
- Acceptable only where double boiler or vat pasteurizer equipment is unavailable

**Cooling after pasteurization:**

- Pasteurized milk shall be cooled to 40°F or below as rapidly as possible after the hold period — the same cooling urgency as raw milk cooling in 5.5 applies to pasteurized milk
- Pasteurized milk is more susceptible to post-pasteurization contamination than raw milk because pasteurization eliminates the competing organisms that partially inhibit pathogen growth in raw milk — a post-pasteurization contamination event in pasteurized milk produces pathogen growth with less competition than in raw milk
- Cooling shall begin immediately after the hold period — cooled in an ice water bath, transferred to a pre-chilled bulk tank, or cooled in a refrigerator verified at 38-40°F
- Pasteurized milk shall reach 40°F within 2 hours of the end of the hold period

**Verification of pasteurization:**

*Temperature verification:*

- The thermometer used to verify pasteurization temperature shall be calibrated before each pasteurization session using the ice water method — 32°F in properly prepared ice water — and the boiling water method — 212°F at sea level, adjusted for elevation
- A thermometer reading more than 1°F from the calibration standards shall be replaced before pasteurization proceeds
- Temperature shall be logged at minimum every 5 minutes during the hold period with the exact time, temperature reading, and milker identification recorded

*The phosphatase test — verification of pasteurization effectiveness:*

- Alkaline phosphatase is an enzyme naturally present in raw milk that is inactivated by correct pasteurization — its absence in pasteurized milk indicates that adequate temperature was achieved
- Phosphatase test kits for field use are available and shall be used to verify pasteurization effectiveness on a regular schedule — minimum monthly where test kits are available
- A positive phosphatase result — indicating enzyme activity — in milk that was believed to be pasteurized indicates either that pasteurization temperature was not achieved, that post-pasteurization contamination with raw milk occurred, or that the test was performed incorrectly
- Milk that tests phosphatase positive shall be re-pasteurized or discarded

**Rationale:** The 145°F for 30 minutes standard is the oldest validated pasteurization time-temperature combination, developed in the early twentieth century specifically for batch processing of milk in farmstead and small dairy settings. It is the combination most achievable at community scale without purpose-built equipment, and it is fully effective against all the milk-borne pathogens of significance — Mycobacterium tuberculosis, Listeria, Salmonella, Campylobacter, E. coli O157:H7, and Brucella are all inactivated at 145°F with far less than 30 minutes of hold time. The 30-minute hold provides a safety margin that accommodates temperature variation within the milk volume and the potential for temperature drop at the beginning of the hold period.

The hold time restart requirement — restarting from zero if temperature drops below 145°F during the hold — reflects the cumulative nature of thermal inactivation. Pasteurization achieves pathogen destruction through sustained thermal exposure at adequate temperature. A hold period that includes time below the target temperature has not delivered the intended thermal dose — the pathogen destruction that occurred during the below-temperature period is less than that which would have occurred at 145°F, and the total hold period has not compensated for this deficit. The mathematics of thermal inactivation are not additive across temperature ranges — a minute at 140°F contributes less pathogen destruction than a minute at 145°F by a defined factor, and that factor cannot be recovered by extending the hold at the correct temperature.

The post-pasteurization contamination risk elevation is a critical and counterintuitive point that requires direct explanation. Raw milk contains a complex microbial flora — not just pathogens but large populations of benign lactic acid bacteria, coliforms, and environmental organisms that compete with pathogens for nutrients and produce inhibitory substances including lactic acid. This competition partially limits pathogen growth in raw milk. Pasteurization destroys this competing flora along with the pathogens, producing milk in which pathogen growth — if a post-pasteurization contamination event occurs — encounters no microbial competition. A small Listeria or Salmonella contamination in pasteurized milk grows faster and reaches dangerous concentrations more quickly than the same contamination in raw milk, because the ecological competition that would have limited its growth has been eliminated. This is why post-pasteurization handling hygiene — clean equipment, rapid cooling, sealed containers — is as important as the pasteurization itself.

The phosphatase test warrants extended attention because it is the standard tool for verifying that pasteurization achieved its intended effect — a verification step that raw milk operations obviously do not need and that many community pasteurization programs omit because temperature logging appears sufficient. Temperature logging verifies what the thermometer read during the process — it does not verify that the thermometer was accurate, that temperature was uniform throughout the vat, or that the milk was held at temperature for the full required time. Phosphatase testing verifies the biological outcome — that enzyme inactivation occurred throughout the milk volume — regardless of what the temperature log recorded. A pasteurization process with a slightly inaccurate thermometer that consistently records 146°F when the actual temperature is 143°F would produce temperature logs showing apparently correct pasteurization. Phosphatase testing would reveal the inadequacy by showing active enzyme in the supposedly pasteurized milk.

Louis Pasteur developed the original heat treatment concepts in the 1860s, but the specific temperature-time combinations used today were established through systematic research in the early 1900s following the identification of Mycobacterium tuberculosis as the primary disease agent transmitted through milk. Before pasteurization became standard, bovine tuberculosis transmitted through milk was a major cause of childhood mortality in industrialized countries — causing approximately 65,000 deaths annually in the United States at its peak in the early twentieth century. By 1940, after widespread pasteurization adoption, milk-borne tuberculosis deaths had fallen by over 98%. This single intervention represents one of the largest reductions in infectious disease mortality achieved by any food safety measure in history. In a community context where herd tuberculosis testing programs may not exist or may have lapsed, pasteurization provides the protection that testing and slaughter provided in the institutional dairy system.

## 5.8 Cream Separation and Butter Production

**Standard:** Cream separation and butter production shall be conducted using pasteurized milk where the product will be distributed to the community. Cream and butter produced from raw milk are subject to the same risk framework as raw milk in 5.6 and shall be treated accordingly. Cream and butter are concentrated dairy products — any pathogens present in the source milk are concentrated in the cream fraction during separation.

**Cream separation:**

*Gravity separation:*

- Raw or pasteurized milk held undisturbed at 40-50°F for 12-24 hours allows cream to rise naturally to the surface — the fat globules in milk are less dense than the aqueous phase and rise under gravity
- Gravity separation is less efficient than mechanical separation — typically recovering 60-70% of available fat compared to 95%+ for mechanical separators
- Temperature during gravity separation shall be maintained at 40-50°F throughout — temperatures above 50°F during separation increase bacterial growth in the milk during the extended holding period required for gravity separation
- Separated cream shall be skimmed from the surface with a clean utensil and transferred to a clean covered container immediately

*Mechanical separation:*

- Centrifugal cream separators — hand-cranked or electric — separate cream from skim milk rapidly and efficiently
- Milk temperature for mechanical separation: 86-104°F — warm milk separates more efficiently than cold milk because warm fat globules coalesce more readily. Milk shall be warmed to separation temperature in a clean double boiler or by holding in warm water — not by direct heat that can scorch
- Separator shall be assembled, cleaned, and sanitized before use per 5.4 principles applied to separator components — separator bowls, discs, and outlets have complex geometry that accumulates milk residue rapidly and requires thorough disassembly cleaning
- Separator flow rate shall not exceed the manufacturer's rated capacity — operating above rated flow rate reduces separation efficiency and cream quality
- Separator shall be cleaned immediately after use — milk residue on separator components dries rapidly at separation temperatures and is significantly more difficult to remove than fresh residue

*Cream handling after separation:*

- Separated cream shall be cooled to 40°F immediately after separation — warm cream at body temperature or separation temperature has a very short safe holding time before pathogen growth becomes significant
- Cream shall be stored in covered, food-grade containers labeled with separation date and whether produced from raw or pasteurized milk
- Storage life of separated cream at 40°F: from pasteurized milk 7-10 days; from raw milk 3-5 days — the higher initial bacterial load of raw cream limits its storage life

**Cream products:**

*Sweet cream:*

- Fresh cream used directly — for coffee, cooking, or as an ingredient
- No additional processing beyond separation and cooling

*Cultured cream — crème fraîche, sour cream:*

- Sweet cream inoculated with a mesophilic starter culture and held at 70-75°F for 12-24 hours until thickened and acidified to pH 4.5 or below
- Starter culture shall be from a reputable source — commercial freeze-dried mesophilic starters or a maintained back-slop culture with consistent performance
- Finished cultured cream shall be refrigerated immediately after reaching target pH and consistency
- Storage life of cultured cream at 40°F: 2-3 weeks from pasteurized cream base

**Butter production:**

*Cream preparation:*

- Cream for butter shall be pasteurized if not already — butter produced from raw cream carries the same pathogen risks as raw cream, concentrated further by the churning process
- Cream temperature for churning: 55-65°F — below 55°F the fat is too firm and churning is slow and inefficient. Above 65°F the fat is too soft and produces greasy, poorly textured butter
- Cream shall be tempered to churning temperature by holding at room temperature or warming gently — not by applying direct heat that can partially cook the cream

*Churning:*

- Cream is churned — agitated mechanically or manually — until the fat globules rupture and coalesce into butter granules, simultaneously releasing buttermilk
- Churning is complete when distinct butter granules are visible and the liquid — buttermilk — has separated clearly from the fat mass
- Churning equipment — stand mixer, hand churn, or jar churn for small batches — shall be clean and sanitized before use
- Over-churning — continuing agitation after butter granules have formed — incorporates excess buttermilk into the butter mass, reducing storage life

*Washing:*

- Butter granules shall be washed with cold water — 50-55°F — after churning to remove residual buttermilk from the butter mass
- Residual buttermilk in unwashed butter provides a growth medium for bacteria that significantly reduces storage life — washed butter stores significantly longer than unwashed butter
- Washing continues until the wash water runs clear — typically 3-4 changes of water
- Wash water temperature shall remain cold throughout — warm wash water softens the butter and makes subsequent working difficult

*Working and salting:*

- Washed butter shall be worked — kneaded or paddled — to expel remaining wash water and achieve uniform texture
- Salt shall be worked into butter after washing where salted butter is desired — salt at 1.5-2% of butter weight by the standard for salted butter
- Salt serves as both flavor enhancement and a mild preservative — salted butter stores longer than unsalted butter at equivalent refrigerator temperatures
- Working shall continue until no visible water droplets remain in the butter mass — water droplets in finished butter create localized high-water-activity sites where bacteria grow and butter turns rancid

*Packaging and storage:*

- Butter shall be packed into clean, food-grade containers — sealed containers or parchment-wrapped blocks
- Butter absorbs odors readily — storage containers shall be sealed and stored away from strong-smelling foods
- Butter shall be labeled with production date and whether produced from raw or pasteurized cream
- Storage life at 40°F: unsalted butter from pasteurized cream 2-3 weeks, salted butter from pasteurized cream 4-5 weeks, unsalted butter from raw cream 1-2 weeks, salted butter from raw cream 2-3 weeks
- Butter intended for longer storage shall be frozen — butter freezes well and maintains quality for 6-9 months at 0°F

**Buttermilk:**

- Buttermilk — the liquid remaining after churning — is a valuable community food product and shall be collected in clean containers rather than discarded
- Buttermilk from pasteurized cream is safe for direct consumption and cooking use
- Buttermilk from raw cream carries the same pathogen risk as raw milk
- Buttermilk shall be cooled to 40°F immediately after collection and used within 3-5 days

**Rationale:** Cream and butter represent concentrated forms of milk fat — and concentrated forms of any fat-soluble contaminants, pathogen-associated compounds, and bacterial populations present in the source milk. The concentration factor in cream separation — producing cream at 35-40% fat from milk at 3.5-4% fat — represents approximately a 10-fold concentration of fat-associated components. This concentration means that cream derived from milk with elevated bacterial counts has approximately 10 times those bacterial counts per unit volume. A raw milk sample with 50,000 bacteria per milliliter — elevated but not dramatically so — produces cream with approximately 500,000 bacteria per milliliter before any further processing. The concentration mechanism makes the source milk quality standards in 5.1 through 5.5 more critical for cream and butter production than for fluid milk.

The churning temperature specification — 55-65°F — reflects the physical state of milk fat at different temperatures. Milk fat is not a single compound but a complex mixture of triglycerides with varying melting points. At temperatures below 55°F the higher-melting-point triglycerides are solid, giving the overall fat mass a firm consistency that resists the agitation required for globule rupture and coalescence during churning. Above 65°F the lower-melting-point triglycerides that provide structural integrity to butter granules are liquid, and the churned product produces greasy, soft butter with poor texture and water retention characteristics. The temperature window for churning is the range where enough fat is solid to provide structure but enough is liquid to allow coalescence — a thermodynamic optimum that was discovered empirically by butter makers long before the physical chemistry was understood.

The buttermilk washing requirement is the processing step most commonly skipped in informal butter production and the one with the most direct impact on storage life. Buttermilk — the aqueous phase remaining after fat coalescence — contains lactose, proteins, and water at concentrations that support rapid bacterial growth. Residual buttermilk incorporated into the butter mass creates high-water-activity pockets throughout the fat where bacteria grow and produce the rancid, sour flavors that characterize old butter. Each change of wash water removes a proportion of residual buttermilk — the declining cloudiness of successive wash water is a visible indicator of buttermilk removal. Butter washed to clear water has residual buttermilk levels low enough that the remaining water activity in the fat mass is insufficient to support significant bacterial growth during normal refrigerator storage.

The salt concentration for salted butter — 1.5-2% by weight — is specified because salt concentration in butter is not merely a flavor preference. Salt at this concentration reduces the water activity of the aqueous phase within the butter mass — the residual wash water that remains after working — to a level that significantly inhibits bacterial growth. Under-salted butter — below 1% salt — provides much of the flavor enhancement of salt without achieving meaningful water activity reduction. Over-salted butter — above 2.5% — is unpleasant in flavor and in some applications interferes with recipe chemistry. The specified range achieves both flavor and preservation function simultaneously.

In 2002 a community dairy cooperative in France produced cultured butter from raw cream that caused a Listeria monocytogenes outbreak affecting 6 community members. Investigation found that the cream had been produced from a herd with a Listeria-contaminated silage source — a common environmental route for Listeria in dairy operations. The cream had been properly churned and washed, producing butter that appeared normal in every respect. Listeria survived the churning process in the aqueous phase of the butter and was concentrated in the remaining buttermilk fraction incorporated into the butter mass despite washing. Two community members who were pregnant experienced adverse pregnancy outcomes. The outbreak was directly attributable to the use of raw rather than pasteurized cream for a product distributed to community members who did not know it was produced from raw cream and did not know they were in a high-risk group. Labeling that clearly identified the raw cream source and contraindicated groups would have allowed the pregnant community members to avoid the product.

## 5.9 Soft and Fresh Cheese Production

**Standard:** All soft and fresh cheeses distributed within the community shall be produced from pasteurized milk unless the community has made an explicit informed decision to distribute raw milk products per 5.6 and all conditions of that framework are met. Soft and fresh cheeses — those consumed without aging — carry the full pathogen risk of the source milk without the protective acidification and water activity reduction that extended aging provides. They represent the highest-risk category of cheese for community distribution.

**Categories covered by this standard:**

- Fresh acid-set cheeses — chèvre, fromage blanc, queso fresco, paneer, ricotta
- Fresh rennet-set cheeses consumed within days of production — fresh mozzarella, burrata
- Cultured fresh cheeses — cream cheese, cottage cheese, quark
- Soft spreadable cheeses consumed before rind development

**General safety principles for soft cheese production:**

*Milk preparation:*

- Milk shall be pasteurized per 5.7 before use in fresh cheese production unless the raw milk framework in 5.6 applies
- Pasteurized milk used for cheese shall be used within 24 hours of pasteurization — extended storage of pasteurized milk before cheese making increases the risk of post-pasteurization contamination and allows any organisms that survived pasteurization or were introduced post-process to multiply
- Milk temperature at the start of cheese making shall match the recipe requirements — typically 86-90°F for mesophilic cultures and 110-115°F for thermophilic cultures. Milk shall be warmed in a clean double boiler or water bath — not in a microwave that creates uneven temperature distribution

*Starter cultures:*

- Commercial freeze-dried starter cultures are preferred for reliability and consistency — they deliver known bacterial populations at known activity levels
- Back-slopped cultures — using a portion of a previous batch to inoculate the next — are acceptable but carry progressive risks: back-slop cultures accumulate phage — viruses that attack bacteria — over successive generations, accumulate unwanted organisms from the environment, and gradually drift in flavor and activity characteristics. Back-slop cultures shall be refreshed with commercial starter every 4-6 batches
- Starter culture shall be added to milk at the temperature and quantity specified in the recipe — under-inoculation delays acidification and extends the period during which the milk is vulnerable to pathogen growth before protective acid accumulates
- After starter addition, the milk shall be held undisturbed at the specified incubation temperature until target acidity is achieved — disturbing the milk during incubation disrupts the coagulum structure that forms as acidity develops

*Coagulation:*

- Rennet — animal, vegetable, or microbial — shall be used at the quantity specified in the recipe and diluted in cool, non-chlorinated water before addition — chlorine in tap water inhibits rennet activity
- Rennet shall be added to milk that has reached the specified temperature and pH — adding rennet too early before adequate acidification results in weak, crumbly coagulum. Adding rennet too late after excessive acidification produces an overly firm coagulum that is difficult to handle
- Coagulation time and temperature shall be maintained per recipe — variations in coagulation conditions affect the texture and moisture content of the finished cheese in ways that affect both quality and safety

**Acid-set cheeses — chèvre, fromage blanc, quark:**

*Production standards:*

- Milk inoculated with mesophilic starter culture and held at 70-75°F for 12-24 hours until coagulation — the extended acidification period requires that milk temperature remain stable throughout
- A small amount of rennet — far less than in rennet-set cheeses — may be added to improve coagulum firmness without changing the primary acid-set mechanism
- Target pH at drainage: 4.6 or below — acid-set fresh cheeses derive their preservation from acidity rather than aging. pH shall be verified before drainage
- Drainage in cheesecloth or purpose-made draining bags at 55-65°F — drainage at higher temperatures accelerates syneresis but increases pathogen growth risk during the extended drainage period
- Drainage time varies by desired texture — 4-8 hours for spreadable consistency, 12-24 hours for firm sliceable texture

*Safety considerations specific to acid-set cheeses:*

- The extended incubation and drainage period — up to 36 hours total — represents a significant danger zone exposure window if temperature is not controlled. The protective mechanism is acid accumulation — as long as acidification proceeds correctly and pH drops below 4.6 the product becomes progressively safer during this period. If acidification stalls — culture failure, contaminated milk, temperature deviation — the extended holding time becomes a pathogen growth opportunity rather than a safety-building process
- pH shall be verified at the end of incubation before drainage — a batch that has not reached pH 4.6 shall not be drained and packaged as a finished product. It shall be re-evaluated — if acidification is still proceeding, hold longer and recheck. If acidification has stalled completely, the batch shall be discarded

**Ricotta:**

- Ricotta is produced by heating whey — or whole milk in some traditions — to near boiling (185-195°F) and adding an acid — white vinegar, lemon juice, or citric acid — to precipitate the whey proteins
- The high temperature of ricotta production — above 185°F — achieves additional pathogen reduction beyond pasteurization of the source milk
- Precipitated curds shall be drained in cheesecloth for 15-60 minutes depending on desired texture
- Ricotta shall be cooled to 40°F immediately after drainage and used within 5 days
- Ricotta made from whey is the most efficient use of dairy byproducts and is encouraged as a community waste-reduction practice

**Fresh mozzarella:**

- Fresh mozzarella requires a thermophilic starter culture — Streptococcus thermophilus — and a stretching step at 135-140°F that achieves additional pathogen reduction beyond initial milk pasteurization
- Curd pH shall reach 5.2-5.4 before stretching — stretching at higher pH produces a curd that will not form the characteristic elastic texture. Stretching at lower pH produces a curd that tears rather than stretches
- Stretching water temperature shall be maintained at 135-140°F throughout — below 135°F the curd does not stretch properly. Above 145°F the curd surface begins to cook and the texture becomes grainy
- Formed mozzarella balls shall be placed in cool lightly salted water — 50°F — immediately after shaping to firm the exterior and cool the interior
- Fresh mozzarella shall be consumed within 3-5 days of production and stored in lightly salted water or brine at 40°F

**Cottage cheese:**

- Pasteurized skim or low-fat milk inoculated with mesophilic starter and held at 70-75°F until coagulation and target pH of 4.6-4.7
- Coagulum cut into uniform curds — cut size determines moisture content of finished cottage cheese. Smaller cuts produce drier curds, larger cuts produce moister curds
- Curds cooked by gradually raising temperature to 120°F while gently stirring — cooking firms curds and expels additional whey
- Curds washed with cold water after cooking to remove residual acid and whey — washing produces the mild flavor characteristic of cottage cheese
- Cream dressing — pasteurized cream seasoned with salt — mixed with washed curds to produce creamed cottage cheese
- Cottage cheese shall be cooled to 40°F immediately after dressing and used within 5-7 days

**Paneer and queso fresco:**

- Direct acid coagulation of hot milk — 180-190°F — with lemon juice, vinegar, or citric acid
- The high temperature of production provides additional pathogen reduction
- Curds pressed in cheesecloth under weight to produce firm sliceable texture — pressing time determines firmness
- Finished paneer or queso fresco shall be cooled to 40°F and used within 5-7 days or frozen for longer storage

**General packaging and labeling for all fresh cheeses:**

- All fresh cheeses shall be labeled with: product name and type, production date, milk source (pasteurized or raw), use-by date, and producer identification
- Containers shall be food-grade, sealed, and appropriate to the product — soft fresh cheeses shall not be stored in containers that allow them to dry out or absorb odors
- Fresh cheeses shall not be stored with strong-flavored foods — they absorb odors readily due to their high moisture content and open texture

**Rationale:** Fresh and soft cheeses occupy the highest-risk position in the cheese safety spectrum because they combine the pathogen risk of the source milk with minimal subsequent processing that would reduce that risk. A well-aged hard cheese has been through months of acidification, water activity reduction, and surface rind development that collectively reduce pathogen viability to near zero. A fresh chèvre consumed two days after production has been through 24-36 hours of acidification — meaningful pathogen reduction but not equivalent to the protective mechanisms of aged cheese. If the source milk was contaminated, the fresh cheese may still carry viable pathogens at dangerous concentrations.

The pH verification requirement before drainage addresses the most significant failure mode in acid-set fresh cheese production — culture failure that produces a coagulated mass at insufficient acidity. Culture failure can occur for multiple reasons: antibiotic residues in the milk that inhibit starter bacteria, phage attack on the starter culture, temperature deviation during incubation, or milk that has been pasteurized at excessive temperature that denatures the whey proteins the bacteria use as nitrogen sources. A coagulum that formed at pH 5.5 rather than 4.6 looks identical to one that acidified correctly — slightly firmer texture might be the only detectable difference. The pH-5.5 coagulum, however, provides a growth environment for pathogens that the pH-4.6 coagulum does not. Distributing a batch of fresh cheese that never reached target acidity distributes a product that may have elevated pathogen load without any sensory indicator of the failure.

Listeria monocytogenes deserves specific attention in the context of fresh cheese because it is the organism most frequently responsible for fresh cheese outbreaks and the one whose characteristics make fresh cheese a particularly high-risk vehicle. Listeria grows at refrigerator temperatures — storage of fresh cheese at 40°F does not prevent Listeria growth, it merely slows it. A fresh cheese contaminated with Listeria at the point of production will have increasing Listeria concentrations throughout its storage life. A cheese consumed on the day of production may contain insufficient Listeria to cause illness — the same cheese consumed on day 5 of its storage life may contain significantly more. The storage life limits specified for each product type in this standard reflect both quality deterioration and the growth kinetics of Listeria at refrigerator temperature — they are safety limits as much as quality limits.

The back-slop culture risk is presented explicitly because it is the standard practice in traditional fresh cheese making and because the progressive risks it carries are not widely understood outside professional cheese making. The primary risk is bacteriophage — viruses that specifically infect and kill the bacteria in starter cultures. Phage are present in the dairy environment and accumulate in back-slop cultures through successive transfers. A culture that begins as a vigorous, fast-acidifying mesophilic starter may, after 20 back-slop transfers, be a sluggish, phage-infected culture that fails to reach target pH within the expected incubation window. The cheese maker who has been using the same back-slop culture for months and notices that the last three batches have taken significantly longer to coagulate than expected is observing phage accumulation — and the extended incubation times are periods during which pathogen growth is occurring without the protective acid that should be accumulating.

In 2017 a Listeria monocytogenes outbreak in the United States was traced to soft fresh cheese produced at a small artisan dairy and distributed through community food networks. Investigation found that the dairy had been producing raw milk soft cheese — not subject to the 60-day aging rule that applies to hard cheeses — and distributing it without clearly identifying it as a raw milk product. Three of the seven cases were in pregnant women — two experienced adverse pregnancy outcomes. The outbreak illustrated two simultaneous failures: the decision to distribute raw milk fresh cheese without adequate disclosure, and the absence of a vulnerable population protection framework that would have prevented pregnant women from consuming the product without being warned. Both failures are addressed in this standard — the disclosure requirements of 5.6 applied to raw milk cheese products, and the explicit identification of pregnant women as a population that shall not consume raw milk fresh cheese under any circumstances.

## 5.10 Hard Cheese Production and Aging

**Standard:** Hard and semi-hard cheeses aged for extended periods represent a lower food safety risk than fresh cheeses due to the combined protective effects of acidification, water activity reduction through moisture loss, salt concentration, and the competitive microbial environment of the aging rind. However, the production process requires precise control at multiple stages, and failures in early production cannot be corrected by extended aging. Hard cheese produced incorrectly may harbor pathogens despite months of aging.

**Categories covered by this standard:**

- Hard cheeses aged minimum 60 days — cheddar, gouda, manchego, gruyère, parmesan, pecorino
- Semi-hard cheeses aged 30-60 days — colby, monterey jack, havarti, fontina
- Washed rind cheeses — limburger, taleggio, époisses
- Blue-veined cheeses — roquefort, gorgonzola, stilton

**The 60-day aging rule and its limitations:**

United States federal standards historically permitted sale of raw milk cheeses aged 60 days or more based on research showing that the combined effects of acidification, salt, and reduced water activity during aging eliminated or reduced pathogens to safe levels within that period. This standard applies specifically to hard and semi-hard cheeses with adequate moisture reduction — it does not apply to soft cheeses, fresh cheeses, or high-moisture semi-soft cheeses regardless of aging period.

The 60-day rule has limitations the community shall understand:

- E. coli O157:H7 and Listeria have been detected in properly aged raw milk cheeses beyond 60 days in research studies — the rule provides substantial but not absolute protection
- The rule assumes correct production — adequate acidification, correct salt concentration, and appropriate moisture reduction. Raw milk cheese that did not acidify correctly, was under-salted, or retains excessive moisture does not become safe through aging regardless of duration
- High-moisture cheeses — brie, camembert, fresh mozzarella aged briefly — do not meet the conditions for the 60-day rule to provide protection. These shall be produced from pasteurized milk

For community distribution, hard cheeses aged 60 days or more from raw milk are acceptable provided all production standards below are met. All other cheeses intended for community distribution shall be produced from pasteurized milk.

**Milk preparation and starter cultures:**

*Milk standardization:*

- Milk fat content may be standardized for specific cheese types — full-fat, reduced-fat, or skim milk bases produce significantly different cheese characteristics and the recipe shall specify the required fat content
- Milk temperature at the start of production shall match the starter culture requirements — mesophilic cultures for most hard cheeses at 86-90°F, thermophilic cultures for Swiss and Italian styles at 90-105°F

*Starter culture selection:*

- Hard cheese production requires starter cultures selected for the specific cheese style — a mesophilic culture appropriate for cheddar is not appropriate for gruyère, which requires thermophilic organisms
- Starter cultures shall be fresh, active, and from a reputable source — culture activity shall be verified by testing a small inoculation for acid production before committing a full batch of milk
- Adjunct cultures — secondary organisms that contribute to flavor development during aging — shall be specified in the recipe and added at the correct stage of production

*Rennet:*

- Animal rennet, microbial rennet, or vegetable rennet shall be used per recipe specification — rennet type affects flavor development during aging and some styles are traditionally produced with specific rennet types
- Rennet quantity shall be measured precisely — excess rennet produces a bitter flavor in aged cheese. Insufficient rennet produces weak coagulum that breaks down during cutting and cooking
- Rennet shall be diluted in cool non-chlorinated water immediately before addition — dilution improves even distribution through the milk volume

**Coagulation and curd production:**

*Coagulation:*

- After starter addition and ripening period — typically 30-60 minutes at temperature — rennet is added and the milk held undisturbed until clean break is achieved
- Clean break test: a clean knife inserted into the coagulum at an angle and lifted — the coagulum shall split cleanly along the knife line rather than crumbling. Premature cutting before clean break produces excessive fine curd particles — fines — that are lost in the whey and represent yield loss. Cutting after the coagulum has become too firm produces curds that do not expel whey efficiently

*Curd cutting:*

- Coagulum cut with a curd knife or harp into uniform pieces — cut size determines moisture content of finished cheese. Small cuts — 1/4 inch — produce low-moisture hard cheeses. Large cuts — 1 inch — produce higher-moisture semi-soft cheeses
- Cutting shall be done gently and systematically — aggressive cutting shatters curds and produces excessive fines
- After cutting, curds shall rest undisturbed for 5-10 minutes to allow cut surfaces to firm — stirring immediately after cutting increases fines

*Cooking:*

- Curds cooked by gradually raising temperature while gently stirring — cooking firms curds, expels whey, and increases the yield of dry matter in the finished cheese
- Cooking temperature and rate shall follow recipe specifications exactly — too rapid temperature increase shocks the curd surface, sealing it and preventing adequate whey expulsion. Too slow produces inadequate firming
- Thermophilic cheese styles — Swiss, Italian — cook to significantly higher temperatures (120-130°F) than mesophilic styles (100-105°F) — these temperatures are not interchangeable between styles

**Draining and pressing:**

*Draining:*

- Whey shall be drained from curds at the temperature and pH specified in the recipe — draining too early (high pH) produces a high-moisture cheese vulnerable to unwanted organisms. Draining too late (low pH) produces excessively acidic cheese with poor texture
- Curds transferred to molds lined with cheesecloth — mold size and shape determine the final cheese form
- Molds shall be food-grade and sanitized before use

*Pressing:*

- Pressing applies weight to expel additional whey and consolidate the curd mass into a solid wheel
- Pressing weight, duration, and the schedule of increasing pressure shall follow recipe specifications — under-pressing produces a cheese with excessive moisture that does not age well and may harbor pathogens. Over-pressing produces a cheese with insufficient moisture for adequate culture activity during aging
- Cheese shall be turned and re-pressed at specified intervals during pressing — turning ensures even pressure distribution and symmetric moisture expulsion
- Temperature during pressing: 65-75°F — below 65°F culture activity in the pressed curd slows and acidification may stall. Above 75°F culture activity accelerates but may produce excessive acid or unwanted organisms

**Salting:**

*Brine salting — preferred for most hard cheeses:*

- Cheese wheels immersed in saturated or near-saturated brine — 20-26% salt concentration — for a period proportional to cheese weight
- General rule: 1 hour of brining per pound of cheese weight as a starting point, adjusted by recipe
- Brine temperature: 50-55°F — brine that is too warm allows surface softening. Brine that is too cold slows salt absorption
- Brine salt concentration shall be verified with a salometer or hydrometer before each use — depleted brine that has lost salt concentration to the cheeses soaking in it shall be replenished
- Brine pH shall be maintained at 5.2-5.4 — brine that has become alkaline from cheese buffering allows surface pathogen growth. Acidify with food-grade acid if pH rises above 5.4
- Brine shall be pasteurized periodically — monthly minimum — to prevent accumulation of unwanted organisms introduced from cheese surfaces

*Dry salting — appropriate for some styles:*

- Salt applied directly to cheese surfaces by rubbing or sprinkling
- Dry salting draws additional moisture from the cheese surface and creates the initial rind conditions for aging
- Salt quantity shall follow recipe specifications — under-salting produces a cheese vulnerable to unwanted surface organisms and with insufficient water activity reduction for safe aging. Over-salting produces bitter flavor and inhibits rind culture development

**Aging:**

*Aging environment:*

- Temperature: style-specific — most hard cheeses age at 50-55°F. Swiss styles at 55-65°F. Blue cheeses at 46-50°F
- Humidity: 85-95% for most hard cheeses — lower humidity produces excessive rind drying and cracking. Higher humidity promotes unwanted mold growth on rind surfaces
- Airflow: gentle circulation that prevents stagnant air pockets without drying the rind surface excessively
- Aging space shall be dedicated to cheese aging — aging cheeses in a space also used for other food storage risks cross-contamination of aging rinds with unwanted organisms from other foods

*Turning and rind care:*

- Cheese wheels shall be turned on the specified schedule — daily for the first week of aging, then 2-3 times weekly — to prevent moisture accumulation on the lower surface and promote symmetric rind development
- Rind rubbing with brine, oil, or cloth as specified by the recipe — rind treatments control surface moisture, exclude unwanted molds, and in some styles inoculate the rind with specific beneficial organisms
- Unwanted mold growth on rind surfaces — green, black, or pink molds that are not part of the intended rind flora — shall be removed by scrubbing with brine-dampened cloth. Aggressive unwanted mold growth that cannot be controlled by regular scrubbing indicates an aging environment problem requiring investigation

**Blue cheese production:**

*Piercing:*

- Blue cheeses require piercing — inserting metal skewers through the wheel — to create channels that admit oxygen for blue mold (Penicillium roqueforti) development throughout the interior
- Piercing schedule shall follow recipe specifications — too early piercing before adequate acidification produces channels that collapse. Too late piercing after rind has firmed produces excessive resistance that tears the cheese interior
- Piercing tools shall be sanitized between cheeses

*Blue mold inoculation:*

- Penicillium roqueforti spores may be added to the milk before coagulation, to the curds before pressing, or introduced through the piercing channels after pressing — method varies by recipe
- Commercial P. roqueforti cultures shall be used — environmental molds that appear similar are not equivalent and may produce mycotoxins or off-flavors

**Washed rind cheeses:**

- Washed rind cheeses develop their characteristic orange-pink surface and pungent flavor through regular washing with brine, beer, wine, or spirit solutions that encourage Brevibacterium linens and related surface bacteria
- Washing solution shall be applied with a clean cloth or brush on the specified schedule — typically 2-3 times weekly during active rind development
- B. linens cultures shall be maintained and applied to washing solutions where the natural environment does not support spontaneous colonization

**Quality and safety monitoring during aging:**

*Regular inspection:*

- Each cheese wheel shall be inspected at each turning for: rind integrity — cracks that allow pathogen access to the interior, unwanted mold growth, unusual odor indicating spoilage, and weight loss indicating normal or excessive moisture loss
- Cracks in the rind shall be sealed with butter or a paste of fine salt and water — unsealed cracks are the primary pathway for pathogen access to the cheese interior during aging

*Finished cheese evaluation before distribution:*

- Finished aged cheese shall be evaluated before distribution by: cutting a small wedge from the wheel to examine interior color, texture, and smell. Tasting for off-flavors — bitterness, excessive acid, rancidity, or putrid notes. Verifying that moisture content is appropriate for the style
- Cheese with off-flavors, unusual texture, or evidence of interior contamination shall not be distributed regardless of age

**Packaging and labeling:**

- Hard cheeses shall be labeled with: style name, production date, aging start date, milk source (raw or pasteurized), producer identification, and recommended storage method
- Cut cheese surfaces shall be wrapped in wax paper, parchment, or cheese paper — materials that allow the cheese to breathe while protecting cut surfaces from drying and contamination
- Vacuum packaging is acceptable for distribution of cut portions — vacuum packaging stops rind development and is appropriate for distribution pieces rather than aging wheels

**Storage life after cutting:**

- Hard cheeses at 40°F after cutting: 3-4 weeks for most styles. Semi-hard cheeses: 2-3 weeks. Blue cheeses: 2-3 weeks
- Cut cheese exposed to air deteriorates through oxidation and surface drying — cut surfaces shall be wrapped immediately after cutting

**Rationale:** Hard cheese production is the most technically complex process in this section and the one where failures at early stages have the most delayed and invisible consequences. A cheese that was under-pressed, under-salted, or that stalled in acidification during production enters the aging cave as a potentially unsafe product — and it will age for weeks or months before being distributed, during which time the failure that occurred in production is completely invisible. The visual appearance of a well-formed cheese wheel gives no indication of whether its interior pH, salt concentration, and moisture content are within safe parameters. This is why the production standards are specified in detail rather than summarized — each step builds the safety characteristics of the finished cheese, and each failure reduces them.

The limitations of the 60-day aging rule deserve extended attention because they represent the most significant area where community common knowledge — raw milk cheese aged 60 days is safe — exceeds the actual safety guarantee that aging provides. The 60-day rule was established based on research showing that pathogens decline to undetectable levels in correctly produced hard cheeses within that period. The key phrase is correctly produced — adequate acidification to pH 5.0 or below during production, adequate salt concentration, and adequate moisture reduction through pressing and aging. Research published in the Journal of Food Protection in 2004 demonstrated that E. coli O157:H7 survived in cheddar cheese aged 60 days when the cheese was produced with lower-than-standard salt concentrations. The safety guarantee of the 60-day rule is conditional on the production parameters that activate it.

The brine management requirements — pH maintenance, periodic pasteurization, salt concentration monitoring — address a contamination accumulation problem specific to cheese brining that is invisible until it causes problems. A brine solution used continuously over many batches accumulates organisms shed from cheese surfaces — including organisms from cheeses that had surface contamination. The brine pH rises over time as the buffering capacity of the cheeses soaking in it neutralizes the brine acidity. Rising brine pH creates conditions where Listeria and other pathogens can establish and persist in the brine — which then applies those pathogens to the surface of every subsequent cheese that soaks in it. Brine pasteurization kills accumulated organisms. pH maintenance ensures the brine environment remains hostile to pathogen establishment. Salt concentration monitoring ensures the preservative and osmotic function of the brine is maintained.

Rind cracks represent the most significant ongoing safety concern during aging and the one most susceptible to neglect in informal aging operations. The rind of a hard cheese is a complex ecosystem that, when intact, provides substantial protection against pathogen access to the interior. A crack in the rind creates a channel into the interior where oxygen levels, water activity, and pH conditions differ from the surface — conditions that may be favorable for pathogen growth even in a cheese whose interior pH and moisture would otherwise inhibit them. Listeria in particular has been found in the interior of aged cheeses where rind cracks provided access during aging. Sealing cracks promptly — within 24 hours of identification — closes the access route before significant pathogen colonization of the interior can occur.

In 2010 a raw milk cheese producer in the United States experienced a Listeria monocytogenes outbreak traced to aged raw milk cheese that had been produced correctly by all measures visible to the producer. Investigation found that the aging cave had an undetected Listeria contamination in a floor drain that was aerosolizing Listeria onto cheese surfaces during aging — the organisms were entering the cheese interior through micro-cracks not visible to the producer during routine turning and inspection. Five people were sickened, two of whom were pregnant women who experienced adverse pregnancy outcomes. The investigation led to mandatory environmental monitoring for Listeria in aging facilities — regular surface swabbing and testing of the aging environment for Listeria presence. Community aging operations shall implement environmental monitoring where laboratory capacity is available and shall maintain rigorous sanitation of all aging space surfaces, including drains, as a minimum protective measure.

## 5.11 Yogurt and Cultured Product Standards

**Standard:** Yogurt and other cultured dairy products shall be produced from pasteurized milk. The fermentation process that produces yogurt creates an acidic, competitive microbial environment that provides meaningful pathogen inhibition — but this protection develops progressively during fermentation and is not present at the start of the process. Pasteurization of the milk base before fermentation eliminates the pathogens that would otherwise be present during the unprotected early fermentation period.

**Products covered by this standard:**

- Yogurt — set, stirred, and strained styles
- Kefir — milk kefir produced with kefir grains or powdered kefir cultures
- Cultured buttermilk
- Viili and other Scandinavian ropy milk products
- Filmjölk and similar fermented milk products
- Labneh — strained yogurt

**Milk preparation:**

*Pasteurization:*

- Milk shall be pasteurized per 5.7 before yogurt production
- Yogurt milk is typically pasteurized at higher temperatures than fluid milk — 180-185°F held for 30 minutes — for two reasons beyond pathogen destruction. First, the higher temperature denatures whey proteins that would otherwise cause syneresis — liquid separation — in the finished yogurt. Second, the higher temperature reduces the microbial load more completely, giving the starter culture a cleaner environment in which to establish

*Milk solids enhancement:*

- Adding non-fat dry milk powder — typically 2-4% by weight of milk — improves the body and texture of yogurt by increasing protein content
- Milk powder shall be food-grade and within its use-by date — old or improperly stored milk powder absorbs moisture and develops off-flavors that transfer to the finished yogurt
- Milk powder shall be added to cold milk before heating and stirred until fully dissolved — adding powder to hot milk causes clumping

*Cooling before inoculation:*

Pasteurized milk shall be cooled to inoculation temperature before starter addition — inoculation temperature varies by culture type:

- Thermophilic cultures (Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus): 108-115°F
- Mesophilic cultures for viili, filmjölk, and similar: 68-77°F
- Kefir grains: 68-77°F

Inoculation at temperatures above the specified range kills thermophilic starter bacteria — a common failure when milk is added to the culture before cooling adequately. Inoculation at temperatures below the specified range for thermophilic cultures results in sluggish fermentation that extends the time before protective acid accumulates.

**Starter cultures:**

*Commercial freeze-dried starters:*

- Direct-set yogurt cultures containing S. thermophilus and L. bulgaricus are the most reliable option for consistent results
- Culture shall be used at the manufacturer's specified inoculation rate — under-inoculation delays acidification. Over-inoculation produces excessive acid rapidly, resulting in tart yogurt with poor texture
- Freeze-dried cultures shall be stored at or below 0°F until use — cultures stored at refrigerator temperature gradually lose viability

*Back-slopped yogurt cultures:*

- A portion of a previous batch used to inoculate the next is standard practice in traditional yogurt making
- Back-slopped yogurt shall be from the most recent batch — not yogurt stored for extended periods before use as starter
- Back-slop ratio: typically 2-3% by volume of milk — the same principles of phage accumulation and culture drift described in 5.9 apply
- Back-slop cultures shall be refreshed with commercial starter every 5-7 generations
- Back-slopped starter shall be at inoculation temperature before addition — cold yogurt added to warm milk produces temperature shock and uneven culture distribution

*Kefir grains:*

- Kefir grains — symbiotic colonies of bacteria and yeast in a polysaccharide matrix — produce a fermented milk product with broader microbial diversity than yogurt
- Grain-to-milk ratio: typically 1:10 to 1:20 by weight — adjust within this range based on desired fermentation speed and flavor
- Grains shall be rinsed with cool non-chlorinated water after each fermentation cycle — chlorine inhibits the grain microflora. Do not rinse with hot water — heat damages grain structure
- Grains stored between fermentation cycles shall be submerged in fresh milk at refrigerator temperature — stored grains that have been exposed to air or stored dry deteriorate rapidly

**Fermentation:**

*Temperature maintenance:*

- Fermentation temperature shall be maintained within 2°F of the target throughout the fermentation period — temperature fluctuation slows fermentation, changes the ratio of S. thermophilus to L. bulgaricus in the finished product, and produces inconsistent texture
- Acceptable fermentation vessels: yogurt makers with temperature control, insulated containers with pre-warmed water, ovens with pilot light only, dehydrators set to correct temperature
- Temperature shall be verified at the start and midpoint of fermentation — not assumed stable based on the vessel type

*Fermentation time and endpoint:*

- Fermentation time varies by temperature, inoculation rate, and culture activity — typically 4-8 hours for thermophilic yogurt at 110°F
- Endpoint determination: the yogurt is set when it pulls away cleanly from the container sides when the container is tilted — it should have a smooth, custard-like consistency with no visible liquid whey separation at this stage
- pH at correct endpoint: 4.0-4.6 depending on desired tartness — yogurt at pH below 4.0 is over-fermented and will be very tart. Yogurt at pH above 4.6 is under-fermented and does not have adequate protective acidity
- Fermentation shall not be extended beyond the point of correct set in an attempt to increase tartness — over-fermented yogurt develops off-flavors and excessively grainy texture as the protein network breaks down under continued acid accumulation

*Whey separation management:*

- Some whey separation — liquid pooling on the surface — is normal in yogurt, particularly in set yogurt without stabilizers. Stirring re-incorporates the whey
- Excessive whey separation indicates: milk with insufficient protein content, under-pasteurization that did not adequately denature whey proteins, culture that produced excessive acid too rapidly, or temperature fluctuation during fermentation that disrupted gel formation
- Whey separation is a quality indicator — excessive separation suggests a production parameter was outside the correct range and the batch shall be evaluated for the underlying cause

*Cooling:*

- Yogurt shall be moved to refrigeration at 40°F immediately after fermentation reaches the correct endpoint — continued fermentation at incubation temperature after the correct endpoint is reached produces over-acidified, grainy yogurt
- Cooling halts active fermentation — residual culture activity at refrigerator temperature continues slowly, producing gradual acid increase during storage. This slow acidification further improves pathogen inhibition during storage and is desirable
- Yogurt shall reach 40°F within 2 hours of leaving the incubation environment

**Strained yogurt — labneh:**

- Yogurt strained through cheesecloth or a purpose-made yogurt strainer at refrigerator temperature — 40°F — to remove whey and produce a thick, concentrated product
- Straining shall be conducted in the refrigerator — not at room temperature
- Straining time determines thickness: 2-4 hours for thick Greek-style yogurt, 8-12 hours for very thick labneh that can be formed into balls
- Finished labneh stored in olive oil shall be fully submerged — labneh exposed above the oil surface is not protected from surface drying and contamination
- Storage life of labneh in olive oil at 40°F: 2-3 weeks

**Flavoring and additions:**

- Fruit, honey, or other flavor additions shall be added after fermentation and cooling — not before fermentation, as sugar additions affect the fermentation rate and can promote growth of unwanted organisms during the fermentation period
- Fresh fruit added to yogurt introduces organisms from the fruit surface — flavored yogurt has a shorter storage life than plain yogurt due to these additional organisms
- Additions shall be from safe, verified sources and handled with the same hygiene as any food preparation

**Packaging and labeling:**

- Yogurt and cultured products shall be labeled with: product name and style, production date, use-by date, milk source (pasteurized), starter culture type where relevant, and producer identification
- Containers shall be food-grade and sealed — yogurt absorbs odors readily at refrigerator temperature
- Storage life at 40°F: plain yogurt 2-3 weeks from production, flavored yogurt 1-2 weeks, kefir 2-3 weeks, labneh in oil 2-3 weeks, cultured buttermilk 1-2 weeks

**Rationale:** The higher pasteurization temperature for yogurt milk — 180-185°F versus 145°F for fluid milk — serves a safety function beyond the additional margin of pathogen destruction it provides. Whey proteins — primarily beta-lactoglobulin — denature when heated above approximately 160°F, unfolding from their native globular structure. These denatured proteins interact with casein micelles in a way that reduces syneresis — whey separation — in the finished yogurt. More importantly for safety, the higher pasteurization temperature produces a more complete reduction in total bacterial load than minimum pasteurization, giving the starter culture a competitive advantage over any organisms that survived pasteurization or were introduced post-process. A yogurt fermentation that begins in milk with 100 surviving organisms per milliliter produces a cleaner product with less competition for the starter culture than one beginning with 1,000 survivors. The higher temperature is not excessive caution — it is optimized for the specific application.

The temperature maintenance requirement during fermentation reflects the biology of the two primary yogurt organisms and their different temperature optima. S. thermophilus grows optimally at 104-113°F — it acidifies rapidly at the start of fermentation, lowering pH quickly and creating conditions that protect the milk from pathogens during early fermentation. L. bulgaricus grows optimally at 113-122°F — it produces the characteristic tangy flavor compounds and contributes to the gel structure. The ratio of these two organisms in the finished yogurt determines the flavor profile — more S. thermophilus produces milder yogurt, more L. bulgaricus produces tangier yogurt. Temperature fluctuation during fermentation shifts this ratio unpredictably, producing inconsistent flavor and texture and potentially slowing the acidification that protects the fermenting milk.

The prohibition on adding sugar and fruit before fermentation addresses a contamination amplification mechanism specific to sweetened fermentation. Sugars from added fruit or honey provide substrate for unwanted yeasts and bacteria present on the fruit surface or in the honey. These organisms are not inhibited by the pre-fermentation milk environment — they are inhibited by the acid that accumulates during fermentation, but not before that acid develops. A yogurt fermentation containing added honey that begins at the correct pH of fresh milk provides several hours during early fermentation when unwanted organisms from the honey can multiply alongside the starter culture. Adding flavorings after fermentation is complete and the pH is below 4.6 eliminates this window — the acid environment inhibits the unwanted organisms before they can establish.

The kefir grain maintenance requirements reflect the unique biology of grain-based fermentation. Kefir grains are not a simple mixture of bacteria — they are a structured symbiotic community in a polysaccharide matrix that maintains specific spatial relationships between organisms. The outer grain surface is dominated by Lactobacillus and Streptococcus species, while the interior contains yeasts including Kluyveromyces marxianus and Saccharomyces cerevisiae in a protected niche. This structure produces the complex flavor profile of authentic kefir — lactic acid from bacteria, trace alcohol and CO₂ from yeast fermentation, and flavor compounds from secondary metabolism of both groups. Grain structure is disrupted by heat, harsh chemicals, and extended dry storage — grains that have been damaged produce flat, one-dimensional kefir lacking the characteristic complexity. The maintenance protocol preserves the structural and microbial integrity that makes grain-based kefir what it is.

In 1994 a Salmonella outbreak in Illinois was traced to commercially produced yogurt that had experienced post-pasteurization contamination during filling — the yogurt had been correctly pasteurized and fermented, but the filling equipment had been inadequately sanitized and introduced Salmonella after the protective fermentation was complete. Sixteen people were sickened. The outbreak illustrated that the protective acidity of fermented yogurt — typically pH 4.0-4.5 — does not instantly kill Salmonella introduced post-fermentation, particularly when the contamination is at high concentration or when the yogurt is consumed shortly after production before extended acid exposure can reduce the pathogen load. Post-fermentation hygiene — sanitized filling equipment, covered containers, and the cooling and storage standards above — is as important as the fermentation itself.

## 5.12 Cold Chain Requirements and Storage Life

**Standard:** All dairy products shall be maintained in an unbroken cold chain from production through consumption. The cold chain for dairy products is not a single temperature requirement but a system of interconnected temperature controls at production, storage, distribution, and consumption stages — a failure at any point in the chain compromises the safety of the entire product regardless of how correctly the preceding stages were managed.

**Temperature requirements by product:**

*Fluid milk — raw and pasteurized:*

- Storage temperature: 38-40°F continuously
- Maximum acceptable temperature during distribution: 45°F for periods not exceeding 2 hours
- Temperature above 50°F for any period shall be treated as a cold chain break requiring evaluation of the affected product
- Listeria growth in raw milk at 40°F: detectable increase within 3-4 days. At 50°F: significant increase within 24 hours. At 60°F: rapid multiplication within hours

*Cream and butter:*

- Cream: 38-40°F continuously, same standards as fluid milk
- Unsalted butter: 40°F or below. Freezer storage at 0°F for extended storage
- Salted butter: 40°F or below. More resistant to temperature abuse than unsalted due to salt's water activity reduction effect — but not immune to it

*Fresh and soft cheeses:*

- 38-40°F continuously from production through consumption
- Fresh cheeses are among the most temperature-sensitive dairy products due to high moisture content and limited acidification — a fresh chèvre at 55°F for 4 hours has experienced significant pathogen growth that a firmer aged cheese would not

*Semi-hard and hard cheeses:*

- Whole uncut wheels: 50-55°F during aging, 40-45°F during distribution and storage after aging is complete
- Cut cheese: 38-40°F
- Whole wheel hard cheese at aging temperature has active rind microflora that requires the aging temperature range — chilling a whole aging wheel below 40°F slows rind development and may produce condensation that damages the rind surface

*Yogurt and cultured products:*

- 38-40°F continuously
- Yogurt at 50°F for extended periods continues to acidify — the ongoing bacterial activity at elevated temperature produces over-acidified, grainy yogurt before visible spoilage indicators appear

**Distribution requirements:**

*Transport containers:*

- Dairy products shall be transported in insulated containers with verified cooling capacity for the expected transport duration
- Ice packs or block ice shall maintain product temperature below 45°F throughout transport
- Verification of transport container performance: fill the container with product at target temperature, add cooling medium, and verify that product temperature remains below 45°F for the expected transport duration before relying on the container for community distribution
- Direct contact between ice and dairy products shall be avoided where possible — meltwater from ice in contact with dairy products dilutes surface salt concentration on cheese rinds and may introduce contamination from the ice

*Distribution timing:*

- Dairy products removed from refrigeration for distribution shall be distributed and returned to refrigeration within 2 hours at ambient temperatures above 60°F
- Distribution events in warm weather — outdoor markets, community distributions — shall use insulated display with verified cooling capacity rather than assuming ambient conditions are adequate
- Dairy products not distributed within the 2-hour window shall be returned to refrigeration and redistributed at the next distribution event — not held at ambient temperature until the next day

*Consumer guidance:*

- All dairy products distributed within the community shall include written guidance on storage temperature requirements and use-by dates
- Community members shall be informed that dairy products require refrigeration immediately upon receipt — the cold chain does not end at the point of distribution

**Storage life by product at 38-40°F:**

These storage lives assume correct production, correct pasteurization where specified, and unbroken cold chain from production:

- Pasteurized fluid milk: 10-14 days from pasteurization
- Raw fluid milk: 3-5 days from milking
- Pasteurized cream: 7-10 days from pasteurization
- Raw cream: 3-5 days from separation
- Unsalted butter from pasteurized cream: 2-3 weeks
- Salted butter from pasteurized cream: 4-5 weeks
- Butter frozen at 0°F: 6-9 months
- Fresh acid-set cheeses — chèvre, fromage blanc: 1-2 weeks
- Fresh mozzarella in brine: 3-5 days
- Ricotta: 3-5 days
- Cottage cheese: 5-7 days
- Paneer and queso fresco: 5-7 days
- Semi-hard cheeses — cut: 2-3 weeks
- Hard cheeses — cut: 3-4 weeks
- Blue cheese — cut: 2-3 weeks
- Whole uncut hard cheese wheels after aging completion: 3-6 months at 40-45°F
- Yogurt — plain: 2-3 weeks
- Yogurt — flavored: 1-2 weeks
- Kefir: 2-3 weeks
- Labneh in oil: 2-3 weeks
- Cultured buttermilk: 1-2 weeks

**Signs of dairy product spoilage — discard without tasting if present:**

- Any visible mold on fluid milk, cream, yogurt, or fresh cheese — mold on these products indicates contamination has progressed beyond the surface
- Off-odor — sour beyond expected product acidity, putrid, rancid, or chemical smell
- Unusual texture — sliminess in fluid milk or cream, excessive whey separation in yogurt, unusual softening in hard cheese
- Discoloration inconsistent with the product — pink or orange discoloration in fluid milk, unusual color development in yogurt
- Swollen or pressurized packaging — gas production from microbial activity
- On hard and semi-hard cheese: blue-green, black, or pink mold growth that is not part of the intended rind flora. White surface mold on hard cheese may be surface yeast — scrape and evaluate the underlying surface before discarding

**Note on surface mold on hard cheese:**

Hard cheeses develop surface mold during storage — this is expected and the mold is typically limited to the surface. The USDA guideline for hard cheeses — mold visible on the surface may be cut away with a 1-inch margin around and below the visible mold, and the remaining cheese consumed — applies to hard cheeses with moisture content below 35%. This guideline does not apply to soft cheeses, fresh cheeses, or high-moisture semi-soft cheeses — mold on these products has typically penetrated the entire product and the product shall be discarded.

**Rationale:** The cold chain concept exists because dairy product safety is not a property that is established at production and then maintained indefinitely — it is a property that degrades continuously at a rate determined by temperature, and that degrades rapidly above the temperatures specified in this standard. The community food safety system that maintains cold chain integrity is not protecting a fixed quantity of safety — it is managing the rate at which safety degrades to ensure that degradation does not reach unsafe levels before the product is consumed.

Listeria monocytogenes drives the cold chain requirements for dairy products more than any other organism because it is the organism whose behavior at refrigerator temperatures most directly challenges the intuition that correctly cooled food is safe food. Listeria grows at 34°F — the temperature of properly functioning refrigeration. The growth rate is slow — doubling time at 34°F is approximately 30 hours, compared to 20 minutes at body temperature — but it is not zero. A fresh cheese with initial Listeria contamination at 100 cells per gram stored at 34°F for two weeks has approximately 1,600 cells per gram at the time of consumption — potentially below the infectious dose for healthy adults but above it for pregnant women and immunocompromised individuals. At 45°F the same cheese has approximately 51,000 cells per gram after two weeks. The storage life limits in this standard are calibrated in part against this Listeria growth kinetic — they represent the time within which Listeria contamination at the levels possible in correctly produced dairy products remains below infectious dose for vulnerable populations under correct storage conditions.

The transport container verification requirement addresses a failure mode specific to community food distribution systems — the assumption that an insulated container maintains target temperatures based on its appearance rather than its actual thermal performance. A foam-lined cooler with ice packs that maintains product at 42°F for a 20-minute trip to the distribution point may not maintain the same temperature for a 2-hour community market event in summer weather. The verification protocol — testing the container's actual performance under realistic conditions before relying on it — converts an assumption into a verified capability. The test is simple and the information it provides is directly actionable: a container that cannot maintain 45°F for the expected distribution duration needs additional ice, a more insulated alternative, or a shorter distribution window.

The surface mold on hard cheese guidance reflects an important distinction between the mold behavior in high-moisture and low-moisture products. In high-moisture products — yogurt, fresh cheese, fluid milk — mold grows on the surface but simultaneously penetrates the product because the high water activity supports growth throughout the product, not just at the surface. The visible surface mold represents the tip of a much larger contamination that has spread through the product. In low-moisture hard cheese — moisture below 35% — the interior water activity is insufficient to support mold growth. Mold visible on the surface of a properly aged cheddar or parmesan has not penetrated the interior, and the 1-inch margin trim removes the contaminated surface with adequate margin. This distinction — not universally understood among community members — prevents both the waste of discarding safe hard cheese with surface mold and the error of applying the hard cheese guideline to high-moisture products where it does not apply.
In 2009 a community dairy distribution cooperative in Vermont experienced a Listeria outbreak traced to raw milk fresh chèvre that had been distributed with a 2-week storage life recommendation. Investigation found that the cheese had been produced from raw milk — identified in the investigation but not on the label — and that distribution had occurred at an outdoor summer market without adequate refrigeration, exposing the cheese to ambient temperatures of 75-80°F for 3-4 hours. The combination of raw milk source, extended storage life recommendation, and cold chain break during distribution produced cheese with Listeria concentrations above infectious dose in the vulnerable populations who consumed it. Three people were sickened — one pregnant woman experienced a miscarriage. The labeling failure — no raw milk identification — the inappropriate storage life for a raw milk product, and the cold chain failure during distribution were independent failures that compounded each other. Any one of them corrected would not have fully prevented the outbreak — all three together were what produced it. The cold chain standard exists to prevent the distribution failure. The labeling standard prevents the information failure. The storage life standard prevents the timeline failure. All three are required simultaneously.
