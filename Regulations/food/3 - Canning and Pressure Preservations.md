3.1 The Botulism Threat — Why This Section Exists
Standard: This standard exists as a standalone rule because the hazard it addresses is categorically different from the other hazards in this document. Every food handler in the community shall understand the following before participating in any canning or preservation activity:
Clostridium botulinum produces the most acutely lethal toxin known to science. The lethal dose in humans is estimated at 1-2 nanograms per kilogram of body weight — a quantity invisible to the eye, undetectable by smell or taste, and present in food that looks, smells, and tastes completely normal. There is no organoleptic test for botulinum toxin. A jar of home-canned green beans containing lethal concentrations of botulinum toxin is indistinguishable from a safe jar by any sensory examination.
C. botulinum spores are present in soil worldwide and therefore on virtually all fresh vegetables, fruits, and meats. The spores themselves are harmless — the danger begins when spores germinate and the bacteria begin producing toxin. Germination occurs under the following conditions simultaneously:

Low oxygen environment — the interior of a sealed jar
Low acid environment — pH above 4.6
Temperature between 40°F and 120°F
Sufficient moisture

A sealed jar of improperly processed low-acid food meets all four conditions. It is, in the absence of correct processing, a botulinum toxin production environment.
Botulism poisoning produces descending paralysis beginning with the face and progressing downward — drooping eyelids, double vision, difficulty swallowing, progressive respiratory failure. Without medical intervention including mechanical ventilation it is fatal. With treatment, recovery takes weeks to months. Antitoxin must be administered before paralysis is advanced to be effective.
The rules in this section are not conservative interpretations of best practice. They are the minimum conditions under which home-preserved food is safe to consume. Deviation from them in the direction of less processing, lower pressure, or shorter time is not a judgment call — it is a decision to accept botulism risk in food that will be distributed to community members who have no way of knowing that risk exists.
Rationale: Most food safety rules in this document address risks that are serious but recoverable — a Salmonella outbreak causes significant illness and potential death but responds to supportive care and resolves. Botulism is different in character. It is neurological, progressive, and in the absence of specific antitoxin and mechanical ventilation, fatal at a rate that makes most other foodborne pathogens look manageable by comparison.
The history of home canning botulism in the United States follows a consistent pattern: an experienced home canner who has canned for years without incident uses a process that feels adequate — it always worked before — and produces food that kills or seriously injures the people who eat it. The previous years of safety were not evidence of a safe process. They were luck. C. botulinum spores are not present in every batch of every vegetable. The process that fails to destroy them is safe until the batch that contains them.
In 1919 a church dinner in Bedford Ohio served home-canned string beans. Thirty-three people ate them. Twenty-four developed botulism. Eleven died. The beans had been canned by an experienced home canner using a process considered adequate at the time — water bath processing of low-acid vegetables, which is now known to be categorically insufficient. The knowledge that destroyed this community's understanding of home canning safety was not available to them. It is available now. This section exists to ensure that the hard-won knowledge of what kills people in preserved food is not lost when the institutions that currently hold it degrade.

3.2 Acid as the Fundamental Safety Variable
Standard: All foods intended for canning and preservation shall be classified by pH before determining the appropriate processing method. No other characteristic — appearance, texture, sugar content, salt content, or previous successful processing — substitutes for pH classification in determining safe processing method.
Classification:

High-acid foods (pH 4.6 or below): Safe for water bath canning. C. botulinum cannot germinate or produce toxin at or below pH 4.6. Includes most fruits, properly acidified tomatoes, pickles with verified acid content, and jams and jellies made with high-acid fruits
Low-acid foods (pH above 4.6): Require pressure canning at minimum 240°F (116°C). Water bath canning of low-acid foods is categorically unsafe regardless of processing time. Includes all vegetables except properly acidified tomatoes, all meats and poultry, all seafood, beans, corn, beets without added acid, and mixed foods containing any low-acid component

The 4.6 threshold is absolute. A food measuring pH 4.7 is a low-acid food requiring pressure canning. A food measuring pH 4.5 is a high-acid food safe for water bath canning. The threshold does not flex based on other food characteristics.
pH verification:

Foods of uncertain or borderline acidity shall be tested with calibrated pH strips or a pH meter before processing method determination
pH strips shall have a resolution of 0.5 pH units or finer for borderline foods
Where pH cannot be verified, the food shall be treated as low-acid and pressure canned or acidified to verified pH 4.6 or below before water bath processing

Acidification of borderline foods:

Foods that are naturally near the pH 4.6 threshold — tomatoes, figs, Asian pears, certain melon varieties — shall be acidified with bottled lemon juice or citric acid per approved quantities before processing
Vinegar may be used for acidification only where its flavor is acceptable to the product and its acidity is verified at 5% acidity minimum — many commercial vinegars meet this standard but it shall be verified on the label
Home-produced vinegar shall not be used for acidification because its acidity cannot be reliably verified without laboratory analysis

Rationale: The pH 4.6 threshold is not a regulatory convention — it is a biological fact. Clostridium botulinum cannot produce toxin in environments at or below pH 4.6 because the acidity denatures the enzymatic processes required for toxin synthesis. This is why fruit preserves made with high-acid fruits have been safely water-bath canned for generations while vegetable preserves processed identically have killed people. The fruit's natural acidity provides a chemical barrier that processing temperature alone does not.
The danger of borderline foods — tomatoes being the most significant example — is that their acidity varies by variety, growing conditions, and ripeness in ways that cannot be assessed by taste or appearance. Modern tomato varieties have been selectively bred for sweetness and lower acidity compared to historical varieties. A tomato that tastes acidic may measure pH 4.4 — safely high-acid. The same variety grown in different soil or harvested at a different ripeness stage may measure pH 4.8 — a low-acid food requiring pressure canning or acidification. The canner who processed tomatoes safely for twenty years using water bath methods was processing tomatoes that happened to be sufficiently acidic. When conditions change — different variety, different growing season, different ripeness at harvest — the same process produces unsafe food.
The prohibition on home-produced vinegar for acidification is absolute because vinegar acidity is the mechanism of safety and home-produced vinegar acidity is variable and unmeasurable without laboratory equipment. Home fermented vinegars typically range from 4% to 8% acidity. At 4% acidity a vinegar used at standard acidification quantities may not achieve the target pH. The canner has no way of knowing whether their home vinegar is at the high or low end of this range. Commercial vinegar labeled at 5% acidity is a reliable, verifiable input. Home vinegar is not.
In 2017 a botulism outbreak in Fairbanks Alaska sickened 8 people following a potluck where home-canned muktuk — fermented whale skin and blubber — was served. The food had been processed using traditional fermentation methods without the acidification or pressure processing required to eliminate botulism risk in animal products. One person required mechanical ventilation. Traditional preservation methods that predate the scientific understanding of C. botulinum are not automatically unsafe — many produce adequate acidity or water activity control through fermentation — but they require evaluation against the biological standards in this section, not acceptance based on historical use alone.

3.3 Understanding Water Activity in Preservation
Standard: All food preservation methods shall account for water activity (Aw) as a primary safety variable alongside pH. Preservation methods that rely on water activity reduction — drying, dehydration, salt curing, sugar preservation — shall achieve and verify the following minimum standards:
Water activity thresholds:

C. botulinum growth: inhibited below Aw 0.93
Most spoilage bacteria: inhibited below Aw 0.91
Staphylococcus aureus: inhibited below Aw 0.86
Most yeasts and molds: inhibited below Aw 0.80
No microbial growth of consequence: below Aw 0.60

Preservation method standards by water activity mechanism:
Dehydration:

Fruits and vegetables intended for long-term storage shall be dried to a final moisture content producing Aw 0.60 or below
Practical field test for fruits — the bend test: properly dried fruit bends without breaking and returns to shape without sticking to itself. Fruit that is brittle is over-dried but safe. Fruit that is sticky or moist has not reached safe water activity
Meat jerky shall reach Aw 0.70 or below — lower than fruit because meat carries higher pathogen load at the start of processing. Jerky that is still pliable and moist has not reached safe water activity regardless of color or surface appearance
Dehydrated foods shall be conditioned after drying — stored loosely in a sealed container for 7-10 days with daily shaking to redistribute moisture — before final packaging. Conditioning identifies pieces with uneven moisture distribution that require further drying

Salt curing:

Dry salt curing shall use non-iodized curing salt at concentrations sufficient to achieve Aw below 0.93 in the finished product
Brine curing shall use verified salt concentrations — brines below 3.5% salt by weight do not reliably inhibit C. botulinum in meat products
Iodized table salt shall not be used as the sole curing agent — iodine interferes with fermentation cultures and curing chemistry. Non-iodized salt or dedicated curing salts shall be used
Curing salt containing sodium nitrite (pink curing salt, Prague Powder #1) shall be used for all cured meats not reaching internal temperatures sufficient for pathogen destruction during the curing process — nitrite provides specific protection against C. botulinum in low-oxygen curing environments

Sugar preservation:

Jams, jellies, and preserves relying on sugar for water activity reduction shall achieve minimum 65% soluble solids content (Brix) in the finished product
The gel or set test — a small amount of product placed on a cold plate firms within 60 seconds — is a practical indicator of sufficient sugar concentration in pectin-set products
Reduced-sugar or no-sugar-added preserves shall not be assumed safe for water bath canning based on standard recipes — reduced sugar changes both water activity and pH in ways that may require pressure canning

Rationale: Water activity is the measure of free water available for microbial activity in a food — not total moisture content but the portion of moisture that microorganisms can use for growth. A food can contain significant total moisture and still have low water activity if that moisture is bound to solutes like sugar or salt. Honey, for example, contains approximately 17% moisture but has a water activity of approximately 0.6 due to its high sugar concentration — low enough to inhibit virtually all microbial growth, which is why honey found in Egyptian tombs thousands of years old has been found unspoiled.
The distinction between total moisture and water activity is not academic — it is the difference between a preservation method that works and one that appears to work while failing. A piece of jerky that looks dry and feels firm may still contain enough free water to support Staphylococcal growth if the surface dried faster than the interior equilibrated. The conditioning step exists precisely to identify and correct this unevenness before packaging seals in the moisture gradient.
Sodium nitrite in curing salts deserves specific explanation because it is both essential for safety in certain applications and potentially dangerous if misused. Nitrite provides protection against C. botulinum in cured meats through a mechanism independent of water activity or pH — it inhibits the iron-containing enzymes that C. botulinum requires for toxin production. In low-oxygen curing environments — whole muscle cures, fermented sausages, smoked meats — where water activity reduction alone may not be sufficient before the product is consumed, nitrite provides a critical additional barrier. The bright pink color of curing salts is an intentional safety feature — it makes them visually distinct from table salt and prevents accidental substitution in both directions. Using too little curing salt in a meat cure removes the botulism barrier. Using too much introduces nitrite toxicity risk. The concentration shall follow established curing recipes precisely.
In 1982 a botulism outbreak in Clovis New Mexico was traced to home-cured beef. The curer had used an insufficient concentration of curing salt based on an imprecise recipe, producing a product with inadequate nitrite concentration to inhibit C. botulinum in the anaerobic interior of the cure. Thirty-four people were affected. The beef had been prepared by an experienced home curer who had made similar products many times. The previous batches had not contained C. botulinum spores at concentrations sufficient to cause illness. This batch did. The curing process that was insufficient had always been insufficient — previous safety was not validation of the method.
The 65% soluble solids standard for sugar preserves connects water activity to a practical measurable threshold. Below 65% Brix, sugar preserves have insufficient solute concentration to reliably suppress yeast and mold growth during storage, and may have insufficient acidity combined with insufficient water activity reduction to be safe for water bath canning without refrigeration. The cold plate gel test is a field-expedient indicator because a proper gel set requires sufficient pectin activation which in turn requires sufficient sugar concentration — the set is a proxy for Brix that requires no equipment. It is less precise than a refractometer measurement but reliable enough for field use when proper set is achieved.

3.4 Pressure Canner Requirements and Testing
Standard: All pressure canning of low-acid foods shall be performed in equipment meeting the following requirements. No substitution of equipment — including pressure cookers, instant pots, steam canners, or any vessel not specifically designed and rated for pressure canning — is acceptable for low-acid food processing.
Equipment requirements:
Pressure canner design:

Minimum capacity of 4-quart jar equivalent — smaller vessels do not allow adequate steam circulation and temperature distribution around jars
Fitted with a pressure gauge or weighted gauge system that has been tested for accuracy within the preceding 12 months
Fitted with a functioning safety vent or petcock that releases pressure if the canner exceeds safe operating pressure
Fitted with a rack that elevates jars off the canner floor to allow steam circulation beneath jars
Lid gasket or sealing surface in good condition — no cracks, deformation, or hardening that would prevent an airtight seal

Gauge requirements and testing:

Dial gauges shall be tested for accuracy annually and after any event that may have affected calibration — dropping, over-pressurization, or extended storage
Dial gauge testing shall be performed against a known accurate standard — a recently calibrated master gauge or by a local cooperative extension service where available. A gauge reading more than 2 PSI high or low shall be replaced before use
Weighted gauges — which regulate pressure by weight rather than measuring it — do not require calibration but shall be inspected for damage, deformation, or debris that could affect their seating and function before each use
A dial gauge that cannot be tested for accuracy shall be treated as unreliable and replaced with a weighted gauge system or a tested dial gauge before low-acid food processing

Pre-use inspection before each canning session:

Vent pipe and petcock clear of debris — insert a thin wire or pipe cleaner through the vent opening to verify it is unobstructed
Gasket pliable and undamaged
Lid locks and handles functioning correctly
No visible cracks, warping, or damage to canner body or lid
Rack present and undamaged

Prohibited equipment:

Pressure cookers under 4-quart jar capacity regardless of pressure rating
Electric multi-cookers and instant pot style appliances — these do not maintain consistent pressure and temperature required for safe low-acid processing and have not been validated for canning by their manufacturers
Pressure canners with dial gauges that have not been tested within 12 months
Any pressure vessel with visible damage to body, lid, or sealing surfaces
Steam canners — they do not achieve the pressure and temperature required for low-acid food processing

Rationale: The pressure canner is the critical instrument in low-acid food preservation and its reliability is the foundation on which all processing standards rest. A processing time of 25 minutes at 10 PSI is safe only if the canner is actually achieving and maintaining 10 PSI — which corresponds to an internal temperature of 240°F, the minimum required to destroy C. botulinum spores. A dial gauge reading 10 PSI while actually delivering 7 PSI means the canner interior is reaching approximately 232°F — insufficient for reliable spore destruction. The food processed under these conditions appears correctly processed and is not.
The prohibition on electric multi-cookers and instant pot style appliances for low-acid canning is absolute and requires direct address because these appliances are increasingly common in community kitchens and their manufacturers' marketing creates the impression that they are suitable for canning. The fundamental problem is pressure and temperature consistency. Standard pressure canning requires maintaining a specific pressure within a defined range for the entire processing time. Electric multi-cookers cycle pressure on and off to maintain an average pressure rather than a consistent minimum pressure. During low-pressure cycles the internal temperature drops below the threshold required for spore destruction. The processing time required for safe low-acid canning was developed and validated for equipment maintaining consistent pressure throughout — it has not been validated for cycling appliances and cannot be assumed equivalent.
The USDA conducted testing of electric multi-cooker appliances specifically for canning safety in 2020 and concluded that they could not be recommended for home canning of low-acid foods due to insufficient and inconsistent pressure and temperature maintenance. This is not a conservative institutional position — it is the result of direct testing of the appliances against canning safety standards.
Dial gauge accuracy deserves extended attention because gauge drift is invisible and consequential. Pressure gauges drift over time through normal use — the Bourdon tube mechanism that converts pressure to dial movement fatigues with repeated pressurization cycles and reads progressively less accurately. A gauge that read accurately when new may read 2-3 PSI high or low after several seasons of use. A gauge reading 3 PSI high tells the canner to stop adding heat when the gauge reads 10 PSI — but the actual pressure is only 7 PSI and the temperature is below the spore destruction threshold. The canner stops heating, processes for the correct time, and produces food that has never reached safe temperature.
In 2015 a botulism outbreak in Illinois was traced to home-canned green beans processed in a pressure canner with an uncalibrated dial gauge subsequently found to be reading significantly above actual pressure. The canner had processed beans at what she believed was 10 PSI for the correct processing time. The actual pressure had been insufficient to reach spore destruction temperature. She had canned beans using the same equipment for years. The gauge had drifted gradually. Previous batches had been safe — not because the process was adequate but because those batches had not contained viable C. botulinum spores. This batch did.

3.5 Water Bath Canner Requirements and Limitations
Standard: Water bath canning is approved for high-acid foods only — those verified at pH 4.6 or below per 3.2. Water bath canning of any low-acid food is categorically prohibited regardless of processing time, jar size, or previous practice.
Equipment requirements:
Water bath canner design:

A large pot with a fitted lid, sufficient depth to submerge jars under a minimum of 1 inch of actively boiling water throughout the entire processing time
Fitted with a rack that elevates jars off the canner floor and allows water circulation beneath and between jars
Rack shall allow jars to be lowered into and raised from boiling water without tipping or contact between jars
Any large pot meeting these requirements is acceptable — dedicated water bath canners, stockpots, and large enamelware pots are all suitable provided they meet the depth requirement with the specific jar size being processed

Water depth verification:

Before beginning processing, verify that jars submerged on the rack are covered by at least 1 inch of water with additional water available to maintain this level through the processing period
Water level shall be checked and maintained throughout processing — evaporation during long processing times can reduce water level below the minimum coverage requirement
If water level drops below jar coverage during processing the batch is compromised and processing time shall restart from zero once water level is restored, or the batch shall be pressure canned or refrigerated and used immediately

Temperature requirements:

Water shall be at a full rolling boil — large bubbles breaking the surface continuously — before processing time begins
Processing time begins when a full rolling boil resumes after jars are lowered into the canner — not when jars enter the water
A simmer or gentle boil does not constitute processing temperature. At standard elevations water bath canning operates at 212°F. A simmer operates at 180-200°F — insufficient for the thermal destruction the processing times assume

Altitude adjustment:

Water bath processing times are developed for elevations at or below 1,000 feet
At higher elevations water boils at lower temperatures and processing times must be increased:

1,001 to 3,000 feet: add 5 minutes to processing time
3,001 to 6,000 feet: add 10 minutes to processing time
6,001 to 8,000 feet: add 15 minutes to processing time
Above 8,000 feet: add 20 minutes to processing time


Communities at elevation shall post altitude adjustment tables in food preservation areas and apply them without exception

Limitations — water bath canning cannot:

Achieve temperatures above 212°F at standard elevation — it is physically incapable of reaching the 240°F required for C. botulinum spore destruction in low-acid foods
Compensate for insufficient acidity through extended processing time — longer processing of low-acid foods in a water bath canner does not make them safe, it makes them mushy and unsafe
Substitute for pressure canning in any low-acid food application regardless of the processing time used

Rationale: The water bath canner's fundamental limitation is thermodynamic — boiling water at standard elevation cannot exceed 212°F regardless of how vigorously it boils or how long food remains in it. This temperature is sufficient to destroy vegetative bacterial cells including Salmonella, E. coli, and Listeria and to inactivate most yeasts and molds. It is insufficient to destroy C. botulinum spores, which require 240°F for reliable destruction. In high-acid foods this limitation is irrelevant because C. botulinum cannot germinate below pH 4.6 — the spores survive processing but cannot produce toxin in the acidic environment of the jar. In low-acid foods the spores survive processing, find ideal germination conditions in the sealed jar, and produce toxin during storage.
The history of botulism from home-canned vegetables in the United States is substantially a history of water bath canning of low-acid foods — a practice that was standard before the science of C. botulinum was understood and that persists in communities where that knowledge has not penetrated. Recipes passed down through families, community cookbooks from the early twentieth century, and well-intentioned preservation guides predating modern food safety science frequently include water bath processing instructions for green beans, corn, beets, and other low-acid vegetables. These recipes are not safe. They represent the preservation knowledge of an era before the mechanism of botulism was understood. Following them because they come from a trusted source is following a trusted source into error.
The rolling boil requirement addresses a failure mode that is particularly common in community-scale canning where multiple batches are processed sequentially and the pressure to maintain pace through a large volume of product creates shortcuts. A pot of water that was at a rolling boil when the first batch was lowered in may have dropped to a simmer by the time the second batch is processed — the cold jars lower the water temperature and if heat input is insufficient to restore the boil quickly, processing time that appears to be running is not actually processing at safe temperature. Processing time begins when the boil resumes, not when the clock starts.
Altitude adjustment is included because it is among the most consistently overlooked variables in water bath canning and because its effect is directly proportional to elevation in ways that matter at community scale. At 5,000 feet elevation water boils at approximately 203°F — 9°F below sea level boiling point. Processing times developed at sea level assume 212°F throughout. At 203°F thermal destruction rates are lower and the standard processing time is insufficient. Communities in mountain or high plateau environments who apply sea level processing times to water bath canning are systematically under-processing every batch.
In 1978 a botulism outbreak in New Mexico sickened 34 people from home-canned chili peppers processed using water bath methods. The peppers — a low-acid food — had been processed using a recipe that had circulated in the community for years without causing illness. Investigation found that previous batches had been stored for shorter periods before consumption — botulinum toxin production in improperly processed food is time-dependent, and jars consumed quickly after processing may not yet contain dangerous toxin concentrations even if the processing was inadequate. Jars stored longer gave the toxin time to accumulate to dangerous levels. The recipe had appeared safe because previous use patterns had not allowed toxin to accumulate. Changed storage practices revealed the underlying process failure.

3.6 Jar, Lid, and Seal Standards
Standard: All containers used for heat-processed canning shall meet the following requirements. The container is the final barrier between the sterile processed food and the environment — container failure is process failure regardless of how correctly every other step was executed.
Jar requirements:
Approved jars:

Jars manufactured specifically for home canning — Mason, Ball, Kerr, and equivalent purpose-made canning jars
Jars shall be free of cracks, chips, nicks, or scratches particularly at the sealing surface — the rim where the lid contacts the jar
Jars shall be inspected by running a finger around the entire rim before each use — surface damage invisible to the eye is detectable by touch
Jars shall be of uniform thickness — purpose-made canning jars are designed to withstand the thermal stress of canning. Commercial food jars — mayonnaise jars, pickle jars, pasta sauce jars — are not designed for repeated heat processing and shall not be used

Prohibited containers:

Commercial food jars not manufactured for home canning
Jars with wire bail and rubber gasket closures for heat processing of low-acid foods — these do not create a reliable vacuum seal verifiable by the methods in 3.9
Zinc lid canning jars — an older design with zinc lids and rubber rings, no longer manufactured, that cannot achieve reliable seals with modern equipment
Any jar with visible damage to body or sealing surface
Jars that have previously failed to seal

Lid requirements:
Two-piece metal lids:

Flat lid with sealing compound and separate screw band are the standard for modern home canning
Flat lids shall be used only once for heat processing — the sealing compound deforms during processing and cannot reliably re-seal in a subsequent use. Used lids may be reused for dry storage or refrigerator storage but not for heat processing
Screw bands may be reused if free of rust, dents, or deformation
Lids shall be inspected before use for damage to the sealing compound — gaps, cracks, or uneven compound application disqualify the lid
Lids shall be kept in clean, dry storage until use — lids stored in damp conditions may have compromised sealing compound

Reusable canning lids:

Certain reusable lid systems — Tattler and equivalent — are acceptable for home canning when used strictly per manufacturer instructions
Reusable lids require different handling than standard two-piece lids — specifically different tightening technique during processing — and shall not be used interchangeably with standard lid protocols
Reusable lids shall be inspected before each use for cracks, deformation, or damage to sealing surfaces and replaced when any damage is present

Band tightening standard:

Screw bands shall be tightened to fingertip tight — firm hand pressure without tools, not cranked down with full hand strength
Over-tightened bands prevent air from venting during processing, trapping air in the jar and preventing proper vacuum seal formation
Under-tightened bands allow the lid to shift during processing, breaking the seal
Fingertip tight is the correct torque regardless of jar size, lid type, or food being processed

Rationale: The integrity of the canning jar and lid system is the physical foundation of preservation safety. A correctly processed jar of food that develops a seal failure during storage has become an unsealed container of food that was heat-processed to eliminate competing organisms, providing ideal growth conditions for any contaminant that subsequently enters. In low-acid foods this means C. botulinum spores that survived processing — which is expected, since the spores survive 212°F and some survive even 240°F at short exposure — find an environment with no competing organisms, adequate moisture, and no acid barrier. A seal failure in a low-acid jar is not a quality problem. It is a safety emergency.
The prohibition on commercial food jars for heat processing is grounded in glass manufacturing standards. Canning jars are manufactured from glass formulated and tempered to withstand repeated thermal cycling — rapid temperature changes from cold to hot and back — without fracturing. Commercial food jars are manufactured to be filled once at the factory under controlled conditions and are not designed for home canning thermal stress. They may withstand the first processing cycle, the second, and many more — and then fracture without warning during a subsequent processing cycle, either in the canner or during storage. The risk is not only the loss of the batch — a jar fracturing in a pressure canner at 240°F and 10 PSI is a safety hazard to everyone in the vicinity.
The fingertip tight standard is among the most precisely specified and most frequently misunderstood requirements in home canning. The intuition that a tighter lid creates a better seal is wrong in a specific and important way. During heat processing, air and steam inside the jar expand and must vent past the lid to prevent over-pressurization of the jar. This venting occurs past the lid sealing compound when the band is at fingertip tight — the compound is slightly compressed but not locked down, allowing controlled venting. When the jar cools, the contraction of contents creates the vacuum that pulls the lid down and creates the final seal. An over-tightened band prevents venting, trapping air in the jar and preventing vacuum formation. The jar appears sealed — the lid may even appear depressed — but the internal pressure equilibrium has been disrupted and the seal is unreliable.
Single-use lid requirements exist because the sealing compound — a plastisol or rubber-based material applied to the underside of the flat lid — is designed to flow slightly under heat and pressure during processing to conform to the jar rim and create an airtight seal. This flow is a one-time process. After processing the compound has conformed to the specific rim of the specific jar it sealed and partially deformed in doing so. Attempting to re-seal the same lid on any jar — including the same jar — cannot reliably reproduce the original seal because the compound no longer has sufficient undeformed material to flow into and conform to a rim.
In 2004 a family in Texas experienced botulism from home-canned carrots processed in commercial pasta sauce jars that had been reused for several canning seasons. Investigation found that the jars had developed micro-fractures in the sealing surface rim — invisible to visual inspection but sufficient to prevent reliable vacuum seal formation. Jars that appeared sealed had not maintained seal integrity through the storage period. The family had reused the jars successfully for several seasons before the cumulative rim damage reached the threshold of seal failure. The previous seasons of apparent success were not evidence of safety — they were evidence that rim damage had not yet progressed to consistent seal failure.

3.7 Approved Recipe Adherence — Why Improvisation Kills
Standard: All heat-processed canned foods shall be processed using recipes and processing times from approved sources only. Modification of approved recipes in ways that affect food density, pH, water activity, or jar fill ratio is prohibited. The following constitute approved sources for canning recipes and processing times:
Approved sources:

USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning — current edition
National Center for Home Food Preservation publications
Ball Blue Book Guide to Preserving — current edition
University cooperative extension service publications from land-grant universities
Any recipe developed and validated through laboratory testing with documented safety validation methodology

Prohibited recipe modifications:

Increasing jar size beyond what the recipe specifies — a recipe validated for pint jars has not been validated for quart jars. Heat penetration to the center of a quart jar takes significantly longer than to the center of a pint jar. Processing time is not interchangeable between jar sizes
Increasing the density of solid ingredients beyond recipe specifications — additional vegetables, meat, or other solids in a recipe slow heat penetration to the jar center
Reducing acid additions below recipe specifications — lemon juice, citric acid, and vinegar quantities in approved recipes are calculated to achieve target pH, not to add flavor
Substituting ingredients that affect pH or density — replacing one variety of tomato with another is acceptable, replacing tomatoes with a lower-acid vegetable is not
Reducing processing time for any reason including preference for firmer texture, concerns about over-cooking, or belief that the food was already adequately heated during preparation
Thickening sauces or soups beyond recipe specifications with flour, cornstarch, or other thickeners — thickeners reduce heat penetration rate and invalidate processing times developed for unthickened products

Acceptable modifications:

Reducing or eliminating salt in recipes where salt is present for flavor only — salt in canning recipes is almost never a safety ingredient, it is a flavor ingredient. Exceptions are recipes where salt contributes to water activity reduction, which approved recipes will specify
Substituting equivalent spices and herbs that do not affect pH or density
Reducing sugar in high-acid fruit products where the recipe specifies this is safe — some approved recipes include reduced-sugar variations that have been separately validated

New recipe development:

Recipes not from approved sources and not previously validated shall not be used for heat-processed canning of low-acid foods under any circumstances
New high-acid recipes may be evaluated by verifying pH of the finished product at or below 4.6 and selecting a processing time from approved sources for the most similar validated product
Any community member wishing to develop new canning recipes for community use shall submit them for laboratory validation before community distribution — pH testing, water activity measurement, and inoculated pack studies are the standard validation methodology

Rationale: Approved canning recipes are not culinary guidelines — they are the output of laboratory safety validation processes that test the specific combination of ingredients, densities, jar sizes, and processing parameters required to achieve safe heat penetration to the coldest point in the jar. The coldest point — the geometric center of the most densely packed jar — is the point that takes longest to reach safe temperature. Processing time is calculated to ensure that this coldest point reaches the temperature required for pathogen destruction, not the temperature at the jar surface or the average temperature throughout the jar.
When a cook modifies a canning recipe — adding extra vegetables because the garden is producing abundantly, increasing jar size to reduce the number of jars needed, thickening a soup because the family prefers it that way — they are changing the heat penetration characteristics of the product in ways that invalidate the processing time without knowing by how much. The center of a densely packed quart jar of vegetable soup with added cornstarch may take twice as long to reach safe temperature as the pint jar of unthickened soup the recipe was validated for. The processing time printed in the recipe does not account for this change. The food is processed for the validated time and appears correctly processed. The center of the jar has not reached safe temperature.
The inoculated pack study — the gold standard for canning recipe validation — involves deliberately introducing C. botulinum spores into the product at concentrations far exceeding those found in naturally contaminated food, processing at the proposed time and temperature, and then incubating the processed jars to determine whether any spores survived and germinated. This process requires laboratory facilities, controlled conditions, and access to live C. botulinum cultures. It is not feasible at community scale without specialized infrastructure. This is why approved sources — which have conducted this validation — are the only acceptable basis for processing parameters.
The salt modification exception requires explanation because it contradicts the intuition that any recipe modification is dangerous. In the vast majority of canning recipes salt is present at concentrations far too low to affect water activity or pH — it is a flavoring ingredient. Reducing or eliminating it changes the taste of the product and nothing else. The approved source publications explicitly acknowledge this because the salt modification is so commonly desired and so genuinely safe that prohibiting it would undermine confidence in the other, genuinely important restrictions.
In 1974 a botulism outbreak in Westchester County New York sickened 14 people from home-canned mushrooms. The canner had modified a standard recipe by increasing the quantity of mushrooms packed per jar — a natural impulse when the goal is to maximize yield from a productive harvest. The denser pack slowed heat penetration to the jar center. The processing time that was adequate for the standard recipe was inadequate for the modified pack density. The jars sealed correctly, stored correctly, and appeared normal. The mushrooms at the center of the densely packed jars had never reached safe temperature. Fourteen people ate them. The modification that caused the outbreak seemed trivial — more mushrooms per jar. The consequence was not trivial.
The prohibition on using unvalidated recipes for low-acid foods is absolute because the consequence of being wrong is not a spoiled batch — it is a jar of food containing botulinum toxin that is indistinguishable from a safe jar by any means available to the community without laboratory analysis. A community food preservation program that distributes home-canned low-acid foods processed using unvalidated recipes is distributing an unknown quantity of potential botulism risk across its membership with no mechanism for identifying which jars are safe and which are not.

3.8 Low-Acid Food Pressure Canning Requirements
Standard: All low-acid foods — those with pH above 4.6 per 3.2 — shall be processed in a pressure canner meeting the requirements of 3.4 at the pressures and times specified in approved sources per 3.7. The following minimum standards apply to all low-acid pressure canning:
Pressure requirements by elevation:

At or below 2,000 feet: 10 PSI with weighted gauge, 11 PSI with dial gauge
2,001 to 4,000 feet: 10 PSI with weighted gauge, 12 PSI with dial gauge
4,001 to 6,000 feet: 10 PSI with weighted gauge, 13 PSI with dial gauge
6,001 to 8,000 feet: 10 PSI with weighted gauge, 14 PSI with dial gauge
Above 8,000 feet: 15 PSI with both gauge types

Processing sequence — mandatory steps in order:
Preparation:

Jars filled, headspace verified per 3.10, lids applied fingertip tight per 3.6
Minimum 2 inches of hot water added to canner before jars are loaded
Rack in place before jars are loaded
Jars loaded without tipping or contact between jars

Venting:

Lid locked in place with all locks engaged
Heat applied on high until a steady stream of steam — not wisps, a continuous column — vents from the vent pipe for a minimum of 10 minutes before the weight is placed or the petcock is closed
The 10-minute vent period is not adjustable. It exists to purge air from the canner. Air pockets inside a pressure canner create temperature gradients — areas at the correct pressure but below the correct temperature because air does not conduct heat as efficiently as steam. A canner that has not been fully vented may read correct pressure while containing air pockets where jars are not reaching processing temperature

Pressurization:

Weight placed or petcock closed after 10-minute vent
Pressure allowed to rise to processing level without adjustment — do not attempt to slow pressurization
Processing time begins when pressure reaches the required level and stabilizes — not when the weight begins to rock or the gauge begins to rise

Pressure maintenance:

Heat adjusted to maintain steady pressure throughout processing — weighted gauges should rock gently 2-3 times per minute, dial gauges should hold within 1 PSI of target
If pressure drops below processing level at any point during processing, bring pressure back to processing level and restart the processing time from zero — partial processing time at insufficient pressure cannot be added to subsequent processing time at correct pressure
Do not attempt to accelerate processing by exceeding specified pressure — higher pressure does not meaningfully reduce required processing time for most foods and risks jar breakage and equipment damage

Depressurization:

Remove canner from heat when processing time is complete
Allow pressure to drop to zero naturally — do not run under cold water, do not open vent, do not attempt to accelerate depressurization
Wait an additional 10 minutes after gauge reads zero before opening
Open lid away from face and body — residual steam exits when lid is opened
Remove jars without tilting, using a jar lifter, and place on a towel with minimum 1-inch spacing for air circulation
Do not press lid centers, adjust bands, or disturb jars during the 12-24 hour cooling and sealing period

Rationale: The pressure canning sequence is not a collection of independent best practices — it is an integrated process where each step depends on the previous step having been correctly executed. Understanding why each step exists allows community members to recognize when something has gone wrong and respond correctly rather than proceeding with a compromised batch.
The venting requirement is the step most frequently shortened or skipped by experienced canners who have processed many batches without apparent problems. The consequences of inadequate venting are invisible — a canner with air pockets reads the correct pressure, the weighted gauge rocks correctly, the processing time runs correctly, and the jars seal correctly. The food inside jars located in air pockets has not reached processing temperature. There is no indicator of this failure during or after processing. The only protection against it is completing the full 10-minute vent period every time, without exception.
The physics of air and steam mixtures in pressure canners was established by research conducted by the USDA in the 1940s and 1950s specifically to investigate botulism outbreaks in home-canned foods where processing appeared to have been correct. Researchers found that canners not fully vented before pressurization contained air-steam mixtures that reached correct pressure at temperatures significantly below the steam-only temperature corresponding to that pressure. At 10 PSI in a steam-only environment the temperature is 240°F. At 10 PSI in a 50% air-50% steam mixture the temperature is approximately 228°F — above boiling but below the spore destruction threshold. The jars in that canner were processed at insufficient temperature for the entire processing period while the gauge read correctly throughout.
Natural depressurization is required because rapid pressure reduction — running the canner under cold water or opening the vent — creates a sudden pressure differential between the interior of sealed jars and the canner environment. This differential can cause liquid to boil out of jars, disturb the forming seal, or in extreme cases cause jar fracture. Beyond the immediate safety and quality consequences, liquid loss from jars during depressurization changes the headspace and density of the jar contents in ways that affect both seal integrity and the safety margin of the processing time.
The restart requirement for pressure drops during processing addresses an error that creates significant pressure to cut corners — a pressure drop midway through a 90-minute processing run means restarting the clock and adding 90 more minutes to a session that is already long. The temptation to estimate that the brief pressure drop didn't matter much, or that the partial processing time already completed provides some safety margin, is understandable and wrong. Processing time calculations assume continuous exposure to processing temperature. A period at insufficient temperature partway through the processing cycle does not provide partial pathogen destruction that can be added to a subsequent correct processing period — bacterial and spore kill at insufficient temperature is significantly less than proportional, and the total exposure required for safety cannot be disaggregated across multiple pressure cycles.
In 1996 a botulism outbreak in Texas was traced to home-canned venison processed in a pressure canner that had experienced pressure fluctuations during processing. The canner had maintained what appeared to be adequate average pressure but had dropped below processing pressure for several periods during the run. The processor had not restarted the processing time after pressure drops, reasoning that the total time at pressure was sufficient. Investigation found that the cold spots in the venison — the center of dense meat pieces — had not reached spore destruction temperature during any continuous period of correct pressure, even though the cumulative time at correct pressure appeared adequate. Four people were sickened. One required mechanical ventilation.

3.9 Seal Verification
Standard: All processed jars shall be verified for seal integrity using the following protocol before storage. A jar that has not achieved a verified seal is not a sealed jar regardless of how correctly it was processed and shall be treated accordingly.
Verification protocol — three independent methods, all three shall be applied:
Method 1 — The sound test:

After jars have cooled completely — minimum 12 hours, 24 hours preferred — press the center of each lid firmly with one finger
A properly sealed lid is concave — curved downward — and produces no movement or sound when pressed
An unsealed lid flexes downward under finger pressure and produces a distinct popping sound when released
Any lid that flexes or pops is unsealed regardless of whether it appeared sealed immediately after processing

Method 2 — The visual test:

Examine each lid from the side at eye level
A properly sealed lid has a slight downward curve at its center — the vacuum created during cooling pulls the lid down
A flat or upward-curved lid center indicates absent or insufficient vacuum — the jar is not sealed
Remove the screw band and attempt to lift the lid by its edges with fingers only — a properly sealed lid cannot be lifted without a tool. A lid that can be lifted by finger pressure is not sealed

Method 3 — The tap test:

Tap the center of the lid with a metal spoon
A properly sealed lid produces a clear, high-pitched ringing tone
An unsealed lid produces a dull, hollow thud
Note: the tap test is least reliable of the three methods — a jar containing food that contacts the lid from below will produce a dull sound regardless of seal status. The tap test shall be used in conjunction with methods 1 and 2, not as a standalone verification

Response to failed seals:

Jars that fail seal verification within 24 hours of processing may be reprocessed within 24 hours using a new lid, or refrigerated and used within 48 hours, or frozen
Reprocessed jars shall be treated as having been processed once for quality purposes — texture and nutritional content will be further degraded by second processing
Low-acid foods in failed jars shall not be reprocessed and used as shelf-stable products — the combination of two heat processing cycles without achieving a reliable seal indicates either a jar or lid defect that reprocessing will not reliably correct. Refrigerate and use within 48 hours or freeze
Jars discovered to have failed seals after the initial 24-hour window — during storage — shall be discarded without tasting per 3.12
Screw bands shall be removed from all sealed jars before storage — bands left on sealed jars can rust in place, making them difficult to remove, and can trap moisture that corrodes the lid and compromises the seal over time

Documentation:

The seal verification result for each batch shall be logged including number of jars processed, number achieving seal, number failing seal, and disposition of failed jars
A batch with seal failure rate above 10% indicates a systematic problem — jar rim damage, lid defects, improper band tightening, or processing error — that shall be investigated before the next canning session

Rationale: Seal verification is the final quality control step in the canning process and the last opportunity to identify processing failures before food enters storage and distribution. A jar that failed to seal is a jar that failed the entire purpose of canning — creating a hermetically sealed, shelf-stable product — regardless of how correctly every preceding step was executed. The three-method verification protocol exists because no single verification method is perfectly reliable and the consequences of a missed seal failure in low-acid canned food are severe enough to justify redundant checking.
The physics of seal formation explains why the verification window matters. During heat processing the contents of the jar expand and some liquid and steam vents past the lid. As the jar cools, the contents contract and the steam in the headspace condenses, creating the partial vacuum that pulls the lid down against the jar rim and completes the seal. This process takes time — a lid that appears sealed immediately after processing may not have completed vacuum formation, and a lid that appeared correctly positioned during processing may fail to seal as the jar cools if the sealing compound did not make full contact with the rim. The 12-24 hour waiting period allows this process to complete before verification is attempted.
The screw band removal requirement after verification runs contrary to the intuition that leaving bands on provides additional security for stored jars. The reality is the opposite — bands left on sealed jars create conditions that mask seal failure during storage. A jar whose seal fails after banding removal is immediately obvious — the lid is loose and the failure is detected before the jar is opened and consumed. A jar whose seal fails while the band remains in place may hold the lid in position despite the failed vacuum, concealing the seal failure until the jar is opened. In low-acid foods this means a jar that failed its seal weeks or months into storage — and has been producing botulinum toxin since the seal failed — may present with a lid that appears correctly positioned until the band is removed and the lid lifts freely. Removing bands before storage ensures that seal failures during storage are immediately detectable.
The 10% failure rate threshold for investigation reflects the normal expected seal failure rate for correctly executed canning — occasional seal failures are expected and acceptable in any batch. A rate significantly above this threshold indicates a systematic problem rather than random variation. Common causes of elevated failure rates include jar rim damage from a batch of chipped jars, a defective lot of lids, consistent over or under-tightening of bands, insufficient headspace preventing proper venting, or processing temperature problems that prevented adequate jar heating. Each of these causes produces a characteristic pattern of failures that investigation can identify and correct.
In 2009 a community food preservation group in Vermont distributed home-canned low-acid vegetables to members without performing seal verification before distribution. Several jars with failed seals — identifiable by the flexible lid and popping sound of the unsealed state — were included in the distribution. Three members consumed food from unsealed jars. Two developed gastrointestinal illness from spoilage organisms that had entered through the failed seal. The third jar contained a low-acid vegetable that had been producing botulinum toxin since the seal failed — the member who received it noticed the lid was loose before opening and discarded the jar. Seal verification before distribution would have identified all failed jars before they left the preservation facility.

3.10 Headspace Requirements
Standard: All canned foods shall be filled to the headspace specified in the approved recipe for that product. Headspace is measured from the top surface of the food or liquid to the underside of the lid when the lid is in place. The following general headspace guidelines apply where a specific recipe does not specify otherwise:
Standard headspace by product type:

Juices, fruits, pickles, and tomato products: ¼ inch
Jams, jellies, and marmalades: ¼ inch
Vegetables and low-acid foods in liquid: 1 inch
Meats and poultry: 1 inch
Dry packed foods: 1 inch
Beans and legumes: 1 inch

Headspace measurement:

Headspace shall be measured with a headspace tool, ruler, or marked utensil — visual estimation is insufficiently precise for safety-critical headspace management
After filling, air bubbles trapped in the product shall be released by running a thin non-metallic utensil — a wooden skewer, plastic knife, or dedicated bubble remover — around the inside of the jar between the food and jar wall. Headspace shall be re-checked and adjusted after bubble removal as bubble release changes the effective headspace
Liquid level shall cover solid food components in liquid-pack products — exposed solid food above the liquid line is not adequately processed

Response to incorrect headspace:

Insufficient headspace — food or liquid too close to the lid: remove food or liquid to achieve correct headspace before applying lid. Do not process jars with insufficient headspace
Excessive headspace — food or liquid too far below correct level: add additional liquid to achieve correct headspace where the recipe permits, or process with noted excess headspace and reduce expected storage life — excess headspace increases the air volume in the jar, requiring more complete oxygen removal for proper sealing and increasing oxidation during storage

Rationale: Headspace serves three distinct and equally important functions in the canning process, and the specified measurement for each product type represents the optimum balance between these three requirements. Understanding what headspace does makes the requirement meaningful rather than arbitrary.
Function one — thermal expansion accommodation. Food expands when heated. A jar filled too close to the lid will force food against the lid during processing, potentially preventing proper venting, displacing the sealing compound, and preventing seal formation. The headspace provides room for this expansion without creating pressure against the lid sealing surface.
Function two — vacuum formation. The vacuum that creates and maintains the seal forms as the headspace steam condenses during cooling. A jar with too little headspace has insufficient steam volume in the headspace to create adequate vacuum when it condenses. A jar with too much headspace has excess air that was not fully purged during processing — this residual air reduces the strength of the vacuum formed during cooling and increases the oxygen available for oxidative spoilage during storage. The specified headspace for each product type represents the volume that, after thermal expansion and steam generation during processing and condensation during cooling, produces the optimal vacuum.
Function three — heat penetration. In liquid-pack products the liquid is the heat transfer medium — it heats faster than solid food and transfers heat to the center of solid pieces. Solid food components above the liquid line are not immersed in this heat transfer medium and receive heat only through slower conduction from adjacent food. Processing times for liquid-pack products assume that solid components are submerged. Solid food protruding above the liquid line may not reach processing temperature within the specified processing time.
The bubble removal step addresses a failure mode that is particularly common in dense or starchy products — beans, corn, thick sauces — where air bubbles are trapped throughout the food mass during filling. Trapped air bubbles serve as insulating pockets that slow heat penetration to surrounding food, effectively creating air pockets within the jar analogous to the air pockets within an insufficiently vented pressure canner discussed in 3.8. Beyond the heat penetration effect, bubbles that remain in the jar during processing expand as temperature rises, potentially forcing food upward and reducing effective headspace below the minimum.
The specific headspace differences between product types reflect their processing requirements. The ¼ inch headspace for high-acid liquid products reflects their lower thermal expansion rate and the smaller steam volume required for adequate vacuum formation in water bath processing. The 1 inch headspace for low-acid foods reflects their greater thermal expansion during pressure canning and the larger steam volume required for adequate vacuum formation in the higher-temperature pressure canning process.
In 1985 a community canning cooperative in Oregon experienced a systematic seal failure rate of approximately 30% across a production run of pressure-canned beans. Investigation identified that volunteers filling jars were estimating headspace visually rather than measuring, consistently filling to approximately ½ inch rather than the specified 1 inch. Beans expanded during pressure processing, forcing liquid against lids and preventing adequate venting and seal formation. No botulism resulted from this incident because the failed seals were identified during verification and the unsealed jars were refrigerated rather than distributed. The investigation noted that had the failures been less systematic — had some jars appeared to seal despite inadequate headspace — the failure mode would have been far more difficult to identify and some compromised jars might have been distributed.


3.11 Labeling and Dating Requirements

Standard: All processed and preserved foods shall be labeled before storage. No jar, container, or package of preserved food shall enter community storage or distribution without a label meeting the following minimum requirements:

Required label information:

    Product name — specific enough to distinguish between similar products. "Tomatoes" is insufficient where both whole tomatoes and crushed tomatoes are produced. "Green beans" is insufficient where both dill-pickled and plain pressure-canned green beans are stored
    Processing date — month and year at minimum, full date preferred
    Processing method — water bath or pressure canned, and at what pressure for pressure-canned products
    Lot or batch identifier where multiple batches of the same product are produced on the same date — enables traceback to a specific processing session in the event of a quality or safety concern
    Producer identifier — the individual or team responsible for processing, for traceback purposes
    Any added ingredients that affect allergen status or dietary restrictions — added salt, sugar, vinegar, specific spices

Recommended additional information:

    Jar size
    Approved recipe source and any permitted modifications
    Recommended use-by date based on storage life guidelines in 3.13

Label requirements:

    Labels shall be written in permanent ink or produced by a method that survives storage conditions including humidity and temperature variation
    Labels shall be applied to the lid or jar body — not to the screw band, which is removed after seal verification per 3.9
    Labels shall remain legible throughout the expected storage period — faded or illegible labels shall be treated as unlabeled jars

Unlabeled jars:

    Any jar in community storage without a legible label meeting minimum requirements shall be treated as identity unknown and processed as follows: if seal is intact and contents are identifiable by appearance, label with best available information and note as estimated. If identity cannot be determined, discard without opening
    Unlabeled jars of unknown processing date shall not be distributed or consumed regardless of apparent condition

Rationale: Labeling is the information system that makes a canned food inventory manageable, safe, and traceable. Without adequate labeling a community food storage system becomes a collection of jars whose contents, processing history, and age are unknown — and unknown is not the same as safe.

The product specificity requirement addresses a failure mode that compounds over time in active community storage. A storage room containing jars labeled only "tomatoes" from multiple processing sessions over multiple years contains no actionable information about which jars are oldest and should be used first, which were water bath processed versus pressure canned, which contain added acid and which do not, or which came from a processing session that subsequently produced quality concerns. The label is the only connection between the jar in storage and the knowledge of how it was made.

The producer identifier requirement is not about accountability in a punitive sense — it is about traceback capability in a safety sense. If a member of the community develops botulism symptoms and investigation identifies a jar of pressure-canned corn as the likely source, the producer identifier on that jar is the thread that leads to every other jar from that processing session — the same canner, the same day, the same equipment, the same pressure readings. Without that identifier, every jar of pressure-canned corn in community storage becomes a potential risk that cannot be narrowed by investigation. With it, the investigation can identify and quarantine the specific batch in minutes.

The processing method and pressure recording requirement exists because visual inspection of a sealed jar of food cannot distinguish between water bath and pressure canned product. A jar of water bath processed green beans and a jar of pressure canned green beans look identical in storage. If the label is lost or illegible, the only safe treatment for a low-acid food of unknown processing method is discard — there is no way to determine which processing method was used and therefore no way to determine whether the contents are safe.

Dating requirements connect directly to the storage life standards in 3.13. First-in-first-out rotation — the practice of using oldest stock first to maintain quality and prevent indefinite storage of aging product — is only possible when processing dates are known. A storage system without dates accumulates old product behind new product, progressively aging without being used, and eventually produces jars whose storage life has been exceeded with no way of knowing it.

The treatment of unlabeled jars as identity unknown rather than attempting to identify contents by appearance reflects the safety asymmetry between the cost of discarding a jar of food and the cost of consuming food of unknown processing history. The contents of most sealed jars are visually identifiable to an experienced eye — green beans look like green beans, corn looks like corn. The processing method, date, and batch identity are not visually identifiable. For low-acid foods an unlabeled jar is a jar of unknown processing method — it may be correctly pressure-canned food or it may be water-bath processed food containing botulinum toxin. The contents may look and smell identical. Discarding the jar costs one jar of food. Consuming it costs potentially everything.

In 2011 a community food bank in Montana received a donation of home-canned goods from an estate — several dozen jars of various preserved foods, none labeled with processing dates or methods. The food bank's policy was to accept labeled home-canned goods with processing information and reject unlabeled goods. All unlabeled jars were discarded. A subsequent investigation of the estate found that the deceased had canned extensively using methods that predated modern safety standards, including water bath processing of low-acid vegetables. Had the unlabeled jars been distributed without processing information, recipients would have had no way of knowing the processing method used and no basis for evaluating safety. The food bank's labeling policy, applied consistently, prevented distribution of an unknown quantity of potentially unsafe food. The cost was the discarded jars. The benefit was unquantifiable.

3.12 Pre-Opening Inspection and the Botulism Invisibility Problem
Standard: Every jar of home-processed food shall be inspected before opening using the following protocol. This protocol applies to all home-canned foods regardless of source, age, or confidence in the processing method used.
Pre-opening inspection sequence:
Step 1 — Visual inspection of lid and seal:

Examine the lid for bulging, rust, or corrosion
Examine the seal — the lid should be concave, curving downward at center
Any lid that is flat, convex — bulging upward — or shows any upward curvature shall be treated as a failed or compromised seal
Any lid with rust, corrosion, or visible damage shall be treated as potentially compromised

Step 2 — Band examination:

If the screw band was left on during storage — contrary to the recommendation in 3.9 — remove it carefully before proceeding
Examine the area under the band for rust, corrosion, or moisture accumulation that may have affected the seal

Step 3 — Lid lift test:

With band removed, attempt to lift the lid by its edges using fingers only
A properly sealed jar cannot be opened this way — the vacuum holds the lid firmly against the jar rim
Any lid that lifts freely or with minimal resistance indicates a failed seal — the jar is not sealed and has not been sealed for an unknown period

Step 4 — Opening:

Open the jar in a well-ventilated area away from face and eyes
Note any hiss of escaping gas as the lid is removed — a hiss of inward air as vacuum releases is normal. A hiss of outward gas — gas escaping from the jar — is abnormal and indicates internal gas production by microorganisms
Note any unusual odor immediately upon opening — putrid, fermented, or simply "off" odors that are inconsistent with the expected product

Step 5 — Visual inspection of contents:

Examine contents for unusual color, texture, or appearance inconsistent with correctly processed product
Note any visible mold, unusual cloudiness in liquid, or floating material inconsistent with the product
Note any bubbling or fizzing in the contents — gas production by microorganisms

Discard criteria — discard without tasting if any of the following are present:

Bulging or convex lid before opening
Lid that lifted freely before opening
Outward gas hiss upon opening
Unusual or off odor upon opening
Visible mold in contents
Unusual bubbling, fizzing, or foaming in contents
Any combination of visual or sensory indicators inconsistent with correctly processed product
Any jar of unknown processing date or method
Any jar of low-acid food processed by water bath method
Any jar that was dropped, frozen, or subjected to temperature extremes during storage

The botulism invisibility problem — the most critical standard in this section:
Botulinum toxin produces no reliable sensory indicators in contaminated food. A jar of home-canned green beans containing lethal concentrations of botulinum toxin may present with:

A correctly concave, properly sealed lid
Normal inward hiss upon opening
Normal appearance — correct color, texture, and consistency
Normal odor
Normal taste

The only reliable protection against consuming botulinum toxin in home-canned low-acid food is correct processing — not sensory inspection of the finished product.
The discard criteria above identify jars where something has clearly gone wrong. They do not identify all jars where something may have gone wrong without producing sensory indicators. A jar of low-acid food that passes every sensory inspection may still contain botulinum toxin if processing was inadequate.
This is why the standards in 3.4 through 3.8 are non-negotiable. Sensory inspection at opening is a last line of defense against jars with obvious failure indicators — it is not a substitute for correct processing and cannot be relied upon to catch botulinum toxin contamination.
Heat treatment before consuming home-canned low-acid foods:

Home-canned low-acid foods from any source where processing method cannot be fully verified shall be boiled for 10 minutes before consumption — botulinum toxin is heat-labile and is destroyed by boiling for 10 minutes even when the spores that produced it are not
This boiling treatment is a precautionary measure for food of uncertain processing history — it is not a substitute for correct processing and shall not be used to make incorrectly processed food safe for distribution

Rationale: The botulism invisibility problem is the defining safety characteristic of home-canned low-acid foods and the reason why this section exists as a discrete rule rather than a note within another standard. Every other foodborne hazard discussed in this document produces some sensory indicator of its presence — Salmonella and E. coli in spoiled meat produce off-odors, mold is visible, fermentation produces fizzing and sour odors, Staphylococcal toxin in advanced spoilage produces rancid smells. Botulinum toxin in home-canned low-acid food produces nothing detectable by human senses at concentrations sufficient to kill.
This invisibility is a consequence of the mechanism of C. botulinum toxin production in sealed jars. C. botulinum is an anaerobic organism — it grows in the absence of oxygen. In a properly sealed jar it encounters ideal conditions: no oxygen, no competing organisms that survived heat processing, adequate moisture, and a temperature in the germination range. Toxin production occurs without the visible spoilage, gas production, or odor that would indicate the presence of other organisms. Some strains of C. botulinum produce gas as a byproduct — which can cause lid bulging — but others do not. The proteolytic strains that break down protein — causing softening, odor, and gas — are detectable by sensory inspection. The non-proteolytic strains that produce toxin without visible spoilage are not.
The historical cases that most directly illustrate the botulism invisibility problem are the ones where experienced, careful food handlers tasted food before deciding whether to serve it — and died. In 1971 a woman in New York tasted a small amount of home-canned vichyssoise from a jar that appeared normal in every respect. She developed botulism and died. Her husband ate a larger amount from the same jar and also died. The jar had a normal lid, normal appearance, normal odor, and normal taste. The only abnormality was the botulinum toxin it contained at concentrations invisible to every human sense.
The 10-minute boiling recommendation for low-acid foods of uncertain processing history is grounded in the heat sensitivity of botulinum toxin — the toxin itself, as distinct from the spores that produce it, is destroyed by boiling. A jar of home-canned green beans that may have been improperly processed, boiled for 10 minutes before serving, has had its toxin destroyed even if the spores survived processing and produced toxin during storage. This treatment does not make the jar safe in the sense of destroying spores or preventing future toxin production — it makes the current contents safe to consume. The jar should not be resealed and stored after boiling treatment.
The boiling treatment is not presented as a routine step for all home-canned low-acid foods because the appropriate routine step is correct processing — and normalizing boiling treatment as standard practice creates the false impression that it compensates for processing uncertainty in a way that makes distribution of questionably processed food acceptable. It does not. The boiling treatment is a safety net for food whose processing history is uncertain and cannot be recovered — food from unknown sources, unlabeled jars, inherited stock, or community donations of unknown origin. It is not a license to process food incorrectly and boil it before serving.
In 1978 a family in Colorado shared home-canned peppers at a family gathering. The peppers came from a jar that had been processed by a family member who had canned for decades and was considered highly experienced. The lid was correctly sealed, the contents appeared normal, the odor was normal. Eight family members consumed the peppers. Five developed botulism. The processing had been done by water bath — the standard of the era in which the canner had learned to preserve food, and inadequate for a low-acid vegetable regardless of the canner's experience and intention. Experience does not make an incorrect process correct. The invisibility of the toxin meant that no sensory inspection could have protected the family. Only correct processing — pressure canning at adequate pressure and time — would have done so.

3.13 Storage Conditions and Storage Life
Standard: All home-processed and commercially preserved foods in community storage shall be stored under conditions that maintain seal integrity, prevent chemical degradation, and allow systematic inventory rotation. Storage conditions that accelerate quality loss also accelerate safety margin loss — storage standards are not quality preferences.
Physical storage requirements:
Temperature:

Home-canned foods shall be stored at temperatures between 50°F and 70°F (10°C and 21°C)
Storage temperatures above 95°F (35°C) accelerate chemical reactions that degrade food quality and reduce the safety margin of processed foods — elevated storage temperature increases the rate of seal compound degradation and accelerates any residual microbial activity in marginally processed food
Storage temperatures below freezing damage jar seals through expansion of contents and thermal stress on glass — frozen home-canned food shall be treated as seal-compromised and inspected per 3.12 before consumption
Temperature stability matters as much as average temperature — repeated cycling between warm and cool temperatures stresses seal integrity more than consistent storage at a slightly elevated temperature

Light:

Home-canned foods shall be stored in darkness or low light
Prolonged light exposure — particularly direct sunlight — degrades color, flavor, and nutritional content through photochemical reactions and accelerates oxidation in jars with residual oxygen
Light degradation is a quality issue that also masks early signs of seal failure — discoloration from light damage can obscure the color changes that indicate spoilage

Humidity:

Storage areas shall maintain moderate humidity — high humidity accelerates lid and band corrosion, which can compromise seal integrity over time
Storage areas with persistent moisture — basement walls, areas near water pipes, areas subject to flooding — shall not be used for long-term preserved food storage without moisture mitigation measures
Corrosion of the lid metal in high humidity environments can progress from the outer surface inward to the sealing compound, compromising the seal without producing visible bulging or the other indicators described in 3.12

Physical arrangement:

Jars shall be stored in a single layer where possible — stacking jars increases pressure on lower jar lids and increases the risk of jars falling and breaking
Where stacking is necessary for space reasons, stable shelving with lip barriers shall be used and stacking height limited to two layers maximum
Jars shall not be stored directly on concrete floors — concrete wicks moisture and produces temperature cycling at the floor surface that stresses jars and accelerates corrosion

Storage life guidelines:
These guidelines represent the period during which correctly processed, correctly stored food maintains both safety margin and acceptable quality. Food stored beyond these periods is not automatically unsafe — but its safety margin has been reduced and quality has degraded to the point where the product no longer delivers the nutritional and caloric value for which it was preserved:

High-acid water bath processed foods — jams, jellies, pickles, fruit, tomatoes: 12-18 months optimal, 24 months acceptable with quality degradation
Low-acid pressure canned foods — vegetables, meats, beans: 12-24 months optimal, 36 months acceptable with quality degradation
Fermented and pickled foods under refrigeration: 3-6 months depending on product
Dehydrated fruits and vegetables at Aw below 0.60: 12-24 months in sealed packaging
Dehydrated meats and jerky at Aw below 0.70: 1-2 months at room temperature, 6 months refrigerated, 12 months frozen
Dry goods in sealed pest-resistant storage — grains, legumes, dried corn: 2-5 years depending on product and storage conditions

Inventory rotation:

Community food storage shall implement first-in-first-out rotation — newest product placed behind oldest product, oldest product used first
Rotation shall be actively managed, not assumed — jars placed in front of existing stock without moving existing stock to the front defeats the rotation system
Monthly inventory checks shall verify that rotation is being maintained and identify products approaching or past optimal storage life
Products past optimal storage life shall be flagged, inspected per 3.12, and used promptly or discarded — they shall not be returned to general rotation

Rationale: Storage conditions are where the investment of time, resources, and processing expertise in food preservation is either protected or squandered. A correctly processed jar of food stored in a hot, humid, bright environment loses quality and safety margin at a rate that correctly processed food stored in cool, dark, dry conditions does not. The processing standards in 3.4 through 3.11 create a product with a defined safety margin — storage conditions determine how long that margin is maintained.
The temperature ceiling of 95°F warrants specific attention for community storage in warm climates and uninsulated storage facilities. Summer temperatures in storage areas without climate control frequently exceed this threshold — attics, outbuildings, and poorly ventilated storage rooms in warm regions routinely reach 100-110°F during summer months. At these temperatures chemical reactions that degrade the sealing compound, denature remaining enzymes in the food, and accelerate oxidation proceed at significantly elevated rates. Food stored at 110°F for one summer experiences chemical aging equivalent to several years of storage at 70°F. The storage life guidelines above assume storage within the specified temperature range — food stored outside this range has reduced storage life that cannot be precisely calculated without knowing the actual temperature exposure history.
Freezing damage to sealed jars addresses a failure mode that is particularly relevant for communities in cold climates storing food in unheated spaces during winter. Water expands approximately 9% when it freeze — the liquid contents of a sealed jar that freezes exert significant outward pressure on the jar and particularly on the lid seal. The seal is the weakest point in the sealed jar system and is most vulnerable to this pressure. Jars that have frozen and thawed may appear normal — correct lid position, normal appearance — while having experienced microscopic seal disruption that compromises their hermetic integrity. The treatment of frozen home-canned food as seal-compromised pending inspection is conservative but reflects the impossibility of visually detecting microscopic seal damage.
First-in-first-out rotation is the inventory management principle that prevents community food storage from accumulating aging product while newer product is consumed. Without active rotation management a storage room fills from the front — new product is placed in accessible locations while old product retreats to the back, is forgotten, and eventually exceeds its storage life unnoticed. The failure mode is not dramatic — no single decision causes it — but the cumulative result is a storage inventory of unknown age where the oldest and most degraded food is least accessible and least likely to be used before it should be discarded.
The monthly inventory check is specified rather than quarterly or annual because monthly frequency allows detection of problems — seal failures, pest activity, moisture intrusion, temperature spikes — while they are still limited in scope. A seal failure detected in monthly inventory represents one compromised jar. A seal failure detected in annual inventory may represent months of undetected spoilage in adjacent jars that shared storage space with the failed jar, absorbed its odors, and potentially cross-contaminated through pest activity attracted to the leaking contents.
In 2008 a preparedness community in Idaho that had built a substantial home-canned food reserve discovered during an inventory review that a significant portion of their oldest stock — jars stored in a poorly ventilated outbuilding that had experienced summer temperatures exceeding 100°F for multiple seasons — had experienced lid corrosion and seal degradation. Many jars appeared intact but failed the lid lift test in 3.12, indicating seal failure. The community had not implemented rotation or regular inspection. The oldest jars — those the storage system was theoretically protecting as emergency reserve — were the most compromised. The food was discarded. The labor and resources invested in processing it were lost entirely. Active storage management would have identified the storage condition problem and the aging inventory before either became a total loss.

3.14 Special Considerations — Tomatoes
Standard: Tomatoes shall be treated as a borderline-acid food requiring mandatory acidification before water bath canning regardless of variety, ripeness, or previous successful processing without acidification. No exception to the acidification requirement is permitted for water bath processed tomatoes.
Acidification requirements:

Whole, crushed, or juiced tomatoes processed in a water bath canner shall receive one of the following acid additions per jar before processing:

Bottled lemon juice: 2 tablespoons per quart jar, 1 tablespoon per pint jar
Citric acid powder: ½ teaspoon per quart jar, ¼ teaspoon per pint jar
Vinegar at 5% acidity: 4 tablespoons per quart jar, 2 tablespoons per pint jar — note that vinegar affects flavor more significantly than lemon juice or citric acid and is less preferred for most tomato products


Acid additions shall be added directly to each jar individually — adding acid to the bulk product before filling does not ensure consistent acid distribution across all jars
Acidification quantities are not adjustable based on taste preference — the quantities above are the minimum required to reliably achieve pH 4.6 or below across the natural pH variation of tomatoes

Tomato products that require pressure canning regardless of acidification:

Tomato products containing low-acid additions that cannot be compensated by acidification:

Tomato sauce or salsa with added onions, peppers, or other vegetables where the ratio of low-acid ingredients exceeds the validated recipe specifications
Tomato soup or sauce with added meat, poultry, or seafood
Tomato products with added starch thickeners
Tomato products with added oil in quantities exceeding recipe specifications


Any tomato product where the ratio of tomato to low-acid additions has not been validated in an approved recipe shall be pressure canned

Variety and ripeness considerations:

Overripe tomatoes shall not be used for water bath canning — overripeness reduces acidity and increases pH above the range that acidification at standard quantities reliably corrects
Damaged, cracked, or partially decomposed tomatoes shall not be used — surface mold produces alkaline byproducts that raise pH locally and may not be corrected by standard acidification
Tomato varieties specifically bred for low acidity — labeled as "low acid" or "sweet" varieties — shall be pressure canned or tested for pH before water bath processing even with standard acidification

pH verification for tomato products:

Community-scale tomato processing operations shall verify pH of finished product in randomly selected jars from each batch using calibrated pH strips or meter
Verified pH shall be recorded with batch documentation
Any jar testing above pH 4.6 after acidification indicates either insufficient acid addition, overripe or damaged fruit, or low-acid variety — the entire batch shall be pressure canned or discarded

Rationale: Tomatoes occupy a unique and dangerous position in home canning history because they were historically classified as a high-acid food safe for water bath canning without acidification — a classification that was accurate for the tomato varieties of previous generations and is increasingly inaccurate for modern varieties. Tomato pH has been rising for decades as commercial and home varieties have been selectively bred for sweetness, lower acidity, and reduced bitterness. A tomato variety that measured pH 4.2 in 1950 may have a modern equivalent measuring pH 4.5 — still technically within the safe range, but with a margin so thin that overripeness, growing conditions, or varietal drift can push it above 4.6.
The practical consequence is that families and communities that have water bath canned tomatoes without acidification for generations — and done so safely — cannot conclude that their process is safe. They can only conclude that their tomatoes have happened to be sufficiently acidic in every batch they processed. The batches they did not process — the ones where the tomatoes were more ripe, grown in different soil conditions, or of a slightly different variety — might have measured above 4.6. They were never tested. The generational experience of safe processing without acidification is not evidence that acidification is unnecessary — it is evidence that the specific tomatoes processed happened to be acidic enough, in a food category where that is not guaranteed.
The mandatory acidification standard is not unique to this document. The USDA updated its tomato canning recommendations to require acidification in 1994 following analysis of tomato pH data across modern varieties and growing conditions. The update was controversial precisely because it contradicted decades of community practice that had appeared safe. The controversy illustrates the challenge of updating safety standards in communities with strong food preservation traditions — the tradition feels like evidence of safety, and the update feels like institutional overcaution. In this case the science was correct and the tradition was operating on a safety margin that had been quietly eroding as tomato varieties changed.
The prohibition on using overripe or damaged tomatoes for water bath canning addresses the acidity reduction that accompanies overripeness and decomposition. As tomatoes ripen past their peak, enzymatic and microbial activity continues to metabolize the organic acids that provide acidity — pH rises as ripeness progresses. A tomato at pH 4.3 when perfectly ripe may measure pH 4.7 when overripe. Mold growth on damaged or cracked tomatoes produces alkaline metabolic byproducts that raise pH locally in affected tissue. These pH changes are invisible — an overripe tomato looks like a ripe tomato with some surface softening, not like a fundamentally different food safety risk. The visual inspection that identifies overripe tomatoes as lower quality for eating identifies them as a safety concern for canning.
The additive composition requirements for tomato-based products reflect a common and dangerous assumption — that because tomatoes are a high-acid food, tomato-based products are high-acid foods. A tomato sauce containing a large proportion of onions, peppers, garlic, and herbs is not a tomato product in the safety sense — it is a mixture of tomato and low-acid vegetables whose combined pH depends on the ratio of acidic to low-acid ingredients. Approved salsa recipes specify ingredient ratios precisely because they have been tested at those ratios to verify that the finished product achieves the target pH. A salsa made with twice the specified quantity of peppers and onions may have a pH above 4.6 despite containing substantial tomato. It requires pressure canning.
In 2015 two people in Ohio developed botulism from home-canned salsa. Investigation found that the canner had used a tested and approved salsa recipe as a starting point but had significantly increased the proportion of low-acid vegetables — adding extra peppers and onions because the family preferred a chunkier, more vegetable-forward product. The modification pushed the finished product pH above 4.6. The salsa had been water bath canned at the processing time specified for the original recipe. The original recipe was safe. The modified recipe was not. The modification seemed culinary rather than safety-relevant — more vegetables, same processing. The safety consequence of the modification was invisible until two people were in the hospital.

3.15 Special Considerations — Meats and Poultry
Standard: All meats, poultry, and seafood intended for shelf-stable preservation shall be pressure canned at the pressures and times specified in approved sources per 3.7. No alternative preservation method — water bath canning, steam canning, oven canning, or any method that does not achieve 240°F throughout the product — is acceptable for shelf-stable meat preservation. Meats and poultry are among the highest-risk foods for C. botulinum contamination and require the most rigorous processing standards in this section.
Pre-processing requirements:
Meat quality and source:

Only fresh or properly frozen and thawed meat shall be used for canning — meat that has begun to spoil before canning will continue to deteriorate inside the jar and produces unacceptable quality and safety outcomes regardless of processing correctness
Meat shall be chilled to 40°F or below at all times during preparation until it enters the canner — warm meat during preparation accelerates bacterial growth that processing must then overcome
Wild game shall be field dressed promptly and chilled to 40°F within 4 hours of harvest — game that has not been properly field dressed and chilled is not safe for canning regardless of subsequent processing

Fat removal:

Excess fat shall be trimmed from meat before canning
Fat inhibits heat penetration to the center of meat pieces — heavily fatty cuts require significantly longer processing times than lean cuts to achieve equivalent center temperatures
Fat also degrades in quality during pressure canning and produces rancid flavors during storage — fat removal improves both safety and quality
Rendered fat and grease shall not be canned — fat without sufficient water activity reduction through other means creates ideal C. botulinum germination conditions

Processing standards:
Hot pack versus raw pack:

Hot pack — meat pre-cooked before jarring — is preferred for most meat products because pre-cooking shrinks meat, allowing more product per jar and improving heat penetration by reducing the density of the packed jar
Raw pack — uncooked meat packed directly into jars — is acceptable for approved recipes specifying raw pack but requires longer processing times than hot pack for equivalent products — raw pack and hot pack processing times are not interchangeable
The packing method used shall match the approved recipe exactly — using hot pack times with raw packed meat or vice versa invalidates the processing time

Liquid requirements:

Meat shall be packed with sufficient boiling liquid — water, broth, or cooking juices — to cover solid pieces, leaving 1 inch headspace per 3.10
Dry packed meat — meat canned without added liquid — is acceptable only for specific approved recipes. Dry pack significantly slows heat penetration compared to liquid pack and requires validated processing times developed specifically for dry pack products
Liquid level shall be verified before lid application — meat that rises above the liquid line during filling shall be submerged or removed to maintain correct liquid coverage

Processing times by product — minimum standards from approved sources:
General guidance — specific processing times shall come from approved recipe sources per 3.7:

Chicken or rabbit pieces, hot pack, quart jars: 75 minutes at 10 PSI
Chicken or rabbit pieces, raw pack, quart jars: 90 minutes at 10 PSI
Ground or chopped meat, hot pack, quart jars: 90 minutes at 10 PSI
Strips, cubes, or chunks of meat, hot pack, quart jars: 90 minutes at 10 PSI
Strips, cubes, or chunks of meat, raw pack, quart jars: 90 minutes at 10 PSI
All times adjusted for elevation per 3.8

Processing times for meat are among the longest in home canning — 75 to 90 minutes at pressure is standard for quart jars. Processing time shall not be reduced for any reason including preference for less-cooked texture, time constraints, or belief that pre-cooking during hot pack preparation has already achieved sufficient pathogen destruction. Pre-cooking during hot pack preparation is a preparation step — it does not substitute for or reduce required pressure processing time.
Specific meat types requiring additional attention:
Poultry:

Poultry bones may be left in or removed — bone-in poultry requires the same processing time as boneless but produces a product of different quality and texture
Poultry skin shall be removed before canning — skin inhibits heat penetration and produces significant fat release during processing that degrades quality and interferes with seal formation
Poultry canned with skin has a higher rate of processing failure and quality defect than skinless poultry

Wild game:

Wild game shall be treated with the same processing standards as domestic meat with the additional requirement of verified field handling per pre-processing requirements above
Game birds shall be processed using poultry processing times
Large game — deer, elk, bear, boar — shall be processed using beef and large game processing times from approved sources
Bear meat requires special attention — bear is a known reservoir for Trichinella and shall be pre-cooked to 160°F internal temperature before canning in addition to standard pressure processing

Seafood:

Seafood is among the highest-risk foods for C. botulinum — marine sediments are a primary environmental reservoir for C. botulinum type E, which grows at lower temperatures than other strains and is particularly associated with seafood
All seafood for shelf-stable preservation shall be pressure canned using seafood-specific processing times from approved sources — seafood processing times are not interchangeable with meat processing times
Clams, oysters, and other bivalves require specific processing protocols — approved sources shall be consulted for each specific seafood type

Post-processing quality indicators:

Correctly processed canned meat will appear fully cooked, with liquid that may be slightly gelatinous from collagen extraction during processing
Some darkening of meat color during processing is normal and does not indicate a safety problem
Significant fat separation — a visible fat layer floating above the meat — indicates insufficient fat removal before processing and reduced storage life due to fat rancidity, but does not indicate a safety problem in correctly pressure-processed product
Liquid cloudiness from protein extraction during processing is normal

Rationale: Meat and poultry present the most demanding canning challenges in community food preservation for two related reasons: they are among the most common natural reservoirs for C. botulinum spores, and they present the most significant heat penetration challenges due to their density, variable fat content, and protein structure. The combination of high initial spore load and slow heat penetration means that the margin for error in meat canning is smaller than in vegetable canning — a processing time or pressure shortfall that might leave a small residual spore population in a vegetable product may leave a significantly larger residual population in a dense meat product.
The prohibition on canning rendered fat addresses a specific and underappreciated risk. Rendered lard, tallow, and other animal fats are low-acid, low-water-activity products that appear to be shelf-stable at room temperature based on historical practice — and are, under most conditions. The problem arises when rendered fat is placed in a sealed jar, creating an anaerobic environment, with any water present from imperfect rendering or condensation. C. botulinum spores introduced during rendering — from the raw tissue being rendered — find in the sealed jar an environment of low oxygen, low acid, and sufficient moisture to germinate and produce toxin. The historical practice of storing rendered fat in open crocks at cool temperatures was relatively safe because the aerobic surface environment inhibited C. botulinum germination. Sealed jars remove this protection.
The hot pack versus raw pack distinction requires particular attention because the intuition that pre-cooked meat has already been made safe by cooking — and therefore requires less processing — is wrong in an important way. Hot pack processing reduces the processing time required in some products by reducing the thermal mass that must be heated during canning — pre-cooked, already-hot meat reaches processing temperature faster than cold raw meat. But the processing time for hot pack products still assumes that the center of the densely packed jar must reach 240°F for the required duration for C. botulinum spore destruction. Pre-cooking does not destroy C. botulinum spores — it destroys vegetative cells while spores survive. The pressure processing step is what destroys the spores, and it must be carried out at full specified time regardless of pre-cooking.
Bear meat warrants specific attention because Trichinella spiralis — the parasitic roundworm responsible for trichinosis — is endemic in bear populations in North America and produces cysts in muscle tissue that survive at temperatures below 160°F. Standard pressure canning temperatures destroy Trichinella cysts reliably, but the pre-cooking requirement provides an additional safety margin and ensures that cysts in the deepest muscle tissue are exposed to lethal temperature before the density of the packed jar slows heat penetration during canning. Pork from non-commercial sources — feral hogs, traditional farm-raised pigs — also carries Trichinella risk in regions where the parasite is present and shall be treated with the same pre-cooking requirement.
The seafood botulism risk reflects genuine epidemiological history. C. botulinum type E — the marine strain found in seafood — differs from the strains most commonly associated with vegetable and meat products in its ability to grow and produce toxin at refrigerator temperatures, as low as 38°F. This means that seafood products with marginal processing that leaves residual spores can produce toxin even under refrigeration — a failure mode that does not apply to the type A and B strains primarily associated with land-based foods. Correctly pressure-processed seafood destroys type E spores at the same temperatures required for other strains, but the consequence of under-processing is more severe because refrigeration — the standard backup safety barrier for marginally processed food — does not inhibit type E growth.
In 2007 a botulism outbreak in Alaska sickened eight people from home-canned salmon. Investigation found that the salmon had been processed in a pressure canner at insufficient pressure due to a malfunctioning gauge — the same failure mode discussed in 3.4. The gauge had not been tested for accuracy. The salmon appeared correctly processed — correct seal, normal appearance, normal odor. Seven of eight affected individuals required hospitalization. Two required mechanical ventilation. Salmon — a high-fat, dense fish with high natural C. botulinum type E spore load — is among the most demanding products for home canning and among the most dangerous when processing is inadequate. The processing was inadequate because the gauge was wrong and no one knew.

3.16 Special Considerations — Fermented and Pickled Foods
Standard: Fermented and pickled foods shall be classified by their preservation mechanism and processed accordingly. The preservation safety of fermented and pickled foods depends on achieving and verifying the acid levels required to inhibit pathogen growth — these levels shall be verified rather than assumed based on recipe completion or sensory indicators alone.
Classification by preservation mechanism:
Vinegar-pickled products — quick pickles and shelf-stable pickles:

Preservation depends on sufficient acetic acid concentration in the finished product
Vinegar used for pickling shall be verified at minimum 5% acidity — labeled on commercial vinegar bottles. Home-produced vinegar shall not be used per 3.2
The ratio of vinegar to water in brine shall follow approved recipe specifications exactly — diluting vinegar below recipe specifications reduces acetic acid concentration below safe levels
Sugar, spices, and other additions do not affect the safety acid concentration and may be adjusted within recipe guidelines
Vinegar-pickled products intended for shelf-stable storage shall be water bath processed using approved processing times — vinegar concentration alone without heat processing does not produce a shelf-stable product safe for room temperature storage

Fermented products — lacto-fermented vegetables, kimchi, sauerkraut, fermented pickles:

Preservation depends on lactic acid produced by naturally occurring Lactobacillus bacteria during fermentation
Fermentation safety depends on salt concentration sufficient to suppress competing pathogens while allowing Lactobacillus growth — salt concentration shall follow approved recipes precisely
Salt concentration for vegetable fermentation: 2-3% by weight of finished product — approximately 1 tablespoon non-iodized salt per 2 pounds of vegetables as a general guideline, verified by weight for community-scale production
Iodized salt shall not be used for fermentation — iodine inhibits Lactobacillus and suppresses the fermentation that creates the protective acid environment
Fermentation shall proceed at temperatures between 65°F and 75°F — above 75°F fermentation proceeds too rapidly producing off-flavors and potentially allowing pathogen growth before adequate acid accumulates. Below 65°F fermentation proceeds too slowly, extending the window during which pathogens can grow before protective acid levels are achieved
pH shall be verified at the completion of fermentation — a finished fermented vegetable product shall measure pH 4.6 or below before being considered safe for storage

Brine management:

Vegetables shall remain submerged beneath brine throughout fermentation — exposure to air above the brine allows mold and yeast growth that can consume the protective acid and create pathways for pathogen growth
Weights, follower plates, or purpose-made fermentation weights shall keep vegetables submerged
Brine lost through fermentation activity — bubbling that pushes brine out of the vessel — shall be replaced with fresh brine at the same salt concentration as the original brine
Vessels shall be covered to exclude dust and insects while allowing gas produced during fermentation to escape — airtight sealing of active fermentation vessels creates pressure buildup that can cause vessel failure

Surface mold management:

Surface mold on fermented vegetables — kahm yeast, white mold at the brine surface — is common and does not automatically indicate a failed fermentation
White or cream-colored surface growth that is flat and film-like is typically kahm yeast — undesirable for flavor but not a safety concern if the fermentation has achieved target pH and the underlying vegetables are not affected
Green, black, blue, or pink mold growth indicates contamination that has penetrated the brine — discard the entire batch without tasting
Fuzzy mold growth of any color at or below the brine surface — not just at the air-brine interface — indicates a failed fermentation — discard without tasting
Any off-odor beyond the expected sour, tangy, or pungent character of the specific fermented product — putrid, fecal, or chemical odors — indicates fermentation failure — discard without tasting

Shelf-stable fermented products:

Fermented products intended for shelf-stable storage without refrigeration shall be water bath processed after fermentation is complete and pH has been verified
Processing halts fermentation and produces a shelf-stable product — the processing also changes the character of the product, killing the live cultures that give fresh fermented foods their probiotic properties
Unprocessed fermented products shall be refrigerated and consumed within the storage periods specified in 3.13

Kombuchas, water kefirs, and other fermented beverages:

Fermented beverages present unique safety considerations due to variable carbonation, pH, and alcohol content
Fermented beverages shall achieve pH 3.5 or below for safety — lower than the 4.6 threshold for solid fermented foods because beverage pH is more uniform throughout the product and more reliably assessed
Bottled fermented beverages that have continued secondary fermentation after bottling present explosion risk from carbonation pressure — bottles shall be vented daily during secondary fermentation and stored in containers that contain potential breakage
Fermented beverages shall not be sealed in glass bottles without venting provisions during active fermentation

Rationale: Fermented and pickled foods occupy a unique position in community food preservation because their preservation mechanism — acid produced biologically or added chemically — is the same mechanism that makes them safe from C. botulinum. Unlike pressure canning where the safety mechanism is temperature and time applied by the canner, fermentation safety is a biological process that the canner manages but does not directly control. The variables — salt concentration, temperature, wild bacterial populations, vegetable sugar content — interact in ways that produce the target pH reliably when conditions are correct and fail to produce it when conditions are wrong. Verification of the outcome — pH testing — is therefore more important in fermentation than in pressure canning, where the safety parameters are directly controlled.
The salt concentration requirement is the most critical variable in lacto-fermentation and the one most commonly estimated rather than measured. Salt in fermentation serves two simultaneous functions: it draws water from vegetable cells through osmosis, creating the brine in which fermentation occurs, and it selectively suppresses the growth of spoilage bacteria and pathogens while allowing salt-tolerant Lactobacillus to grow and produce lactic acid. Too little salt allows pathogen growth before protective acid accumulates. Too much salt inhibits Lactobacillus and prevents the fermentation that creates the protective environment. The 2-3% by weight range is the window within which Lactobacillus reliably outcompetes pathogens and produces adequate acid within a timeframe that limits pathogen exposure.
The iodized salt prohibition requires direct address because iodized salt is the default salt in most community kitchens and the prohibition on its use for fermentation is frequently unknown. Iodine was added to table salt beginning in the 1920s as a public health measure to prevent iodine deficiency — it is a genuine and significant public health intervention. In fermentation, however, iodine acts as an antimicrobial agent that is not selective — it inhibits Lactobacillus as effectively as it inhibits pathogens, suppressing the fermentation that creates the protective acid environment. A fermentation started with iodized salt may fail to reach target pH, leaving vegetables in a salt solution that is insufficiently acidic to inhibit pathogens and insufficiently preserved to be safe without refrigeration.
The submerged vegetable requirement connects to the oxygen requirements of the key organisms involved. Lactobacillus is a facultative anaerobe — it functions in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions but produces lactic acid most efficiently under anaerobic conditions beneath the brine. Molds and many spoilage yeasts are aerobic — they require oxygen and grow at the air-brine interface. Vegetables exposed above the brine are in an aerobic environment where molds can grow and where the protective lactic acid environment established below the brine does not extend. A fermentation vessel that appears to be fermenting correctly below the brine may have surface mold actively consuming lactic acid in the exposed upper layer, raising pH in the upper portion of the vessel toward pathogen-permissive levels.
The fermented beverage carbonation explosion risk is included because it is a genuine physical hazard that has caused serious injuries in community food production settings. Secondary fermentation in sealed bottles produces carbon dioxide continuously as residual sugars are consumed. In properly managed secondary fermentation this produces the desired carbonation. In over-fermented or incorrectly managed beverages it produces pressures exceeding the structural limits of glass bottles — particularly swing-top bottles and recycled commercial glass not designed for pressure — with results ranging from slow seepage to violent explosive failure. The daily venting requirement during active secondary fermentation is not cautious overcorrection — it is the difference between controlled carbonation and a glass fragmentation event in a food storage area.
The C. botulinum safety relationship with fermented foods warrants specific explanation because it differs from the relationship in canned foods. C. botulinum cannot produce toxin below pH 4.6 — the same threshold that governs canning safety. A correctly fermented vegetable product that has reached pH 4.6 or below is safe from C. botulinum toxin production for the same reason that high-acid canned foods are safe. The difference from canning is that the acid in fermented foods is produced progressively during fermentation rather than being present from the start — there is a window at the beginning of fermentation when pH is above 4.6 and C. botulinum could theoretically produce toxin. The salt concentration requirement is what protects this window — salt at correct concentration inhibits C. botulinum growth during the period before protective acid accumulates.
In 1987 a botulism outbreak in New York was traced to garlic-in-oil — a product that is neither properly fermented nor acidified but that is sometimes confused with pickled garlic. Garlic submerged in oil creates an anaerobic, low-acid environment — ideal for C. botulinum — without any of the protective acid that vinegar pickling or lacto-fermentation would provide. Three people were seriously sickened. Garlic-in-oil without acidification or other preservation measures is one of the most reliably dangerous home food preservation products and shall not be produced for community distribution under any circumstances. Garlic intended for oil infusion shall be acidified to pH 4.6 or below before submersion in oil, verified by pH testing, and refrigerated with a storage life not exceeding one week.
In 2012 a community fermentation cooperative in Oregon experienced a fermentation failure across an entire production batch of sauerkraut due to use of iodized salt — a mistake made by a new volunteer unfamiliar with the prohibition. The fermentation produced insufficient acid, failing to reach target pH after the standard fermentation period. The batch had the appearance of correctly fermented sauerkraut — appropriate color, some bubbling during fermentation, brine development — but measured pH 5.2 at completion. The batch was discarded after pH verification caught the failure. Had pH verification not been performed the batch would have been distributed as shelf-stable fermented food that was not shelf-stable and was not safe for room temperature storage.

3.17 Special Considerations — Jams, Jellies, and High-Sugar Preserves
Standard: Jams, jellies, marmalades, and other high-sugar fruit preserves shall be produced and processed according to the following standards. These products occupy a specific safety niche — they are high-acid, high-sugar products that rely on the combination of pH and water activity reduction for preservation — and both parameters must be achieved for the product to be shelf-stable and safe.
Ingredient requirements:
Fruit selection:

Only fully ripe, undamaged fruit shall be used — overripe or damaged fruit has reduced pectin content and reduced acidity, both of which compromise gel formation and preservation safety
A proportion of slightly underripe fruit — approximately 25% of total fruit by weight — improves pectin content and gel formation in recipes not using commercial pectin
Fruit showing mold, significant bruising, or decay shall be discarded entirely rather than trimmed — mold penetrates fruit tissue beyond the visible surface and produces mycotoxins that are not destroyed by the cooking process

Sugar:

Sugar quantity shall follow approved recipes exactly — sugar in high-sugar preserves is not solely a flavoring ingredient. It is the primary water activity reduction agent and a necessary component of pectin gel formation
Reducing sugar below recipe specifications reduces water activity, potentially above the threshold required for mold and yeast inhibition, and inhibits gel formation
Sugar substitutes — artificial sweeteners, honey substitutes — shall only be used in recipes specifically developed and validated for those substitutes. Standard jam and jelly recipes are not safely modifiable by substituting alternative sweeteners

Pectin:

Commercial pectin — liquid or powdered — shall be used per manufacturer instructions exactly, including the specific sugar quantities specified for that pectin product
Different pectin products — regular, low-sugar, liquid, powder — are not interchangeable in recipes. Processing times, sugar ratios, and gel activation requirements differ between pectin types
Recipes using commercial pectin shall use the pectin within its labeled use-by date — pectin degrades over time and old pectin may not achieve adequate gel formation regardless of correct technique

Processing standards:
Cooking:

Fruit and sugar shall be cooked to the gel point before jarring — the gel point is the temperature and concentration at which the pectin network forms and the product will set to the correct consistency upon cooling
Gel point verification methods:

Temperature method: 220°F at sea level, reduced by 2°F for every 1,000 feet of elevation
Plate test: a small amount of product placed on a chilled plate and returned to the refrigerator for 1 minute — product at gel point will wrinkle when pushed with a finger and hold its shape
Sheet test: product dropped from a cold metal spoon — product at gel point falls in a sheet rather than individual drops


Undercooking below gel point produces a product with insufficient sugar concentration for water activity reduction and insufficient set for quality — it is a preservation failure as well as a quality failure
Overcooking beyond gel point darkens the product, degrades pectin structure, and can produce a product that sets too firmly — it is a quality issue but not a safety issue in correctly acidified products

Headspace and processing:

Jams and jellies shall be filled hot — above 180°F — into hot sterilized jars immediately after reaching gel point
Headspace: ¼ inch for all jam and jelly products per 3.10
Water bath processing times for jams and jellies:

Half-pint and pint jars: 10 minutes at sea level, adjusted for elevation per 3.5
Processing time begins when water returns to a full rolling boil after jars are loaded



The open kettle method — prohibited:

The open kettle method — filling hot jars with hot product, applying lids, and allowing jars to seal from the heat of the product without water bath processing — is prohibited for community food preservation
Open kettle processed jars may achieve apparent seals — the hot product creates a partial vacuum as it cools — but these seals are not equivalent to water bath processed seals and do not provide the same pathogen destruction
Open kettle processing was standard practice in previous generations and produces food that is often safe — but produces food that is sometimes unsafe in ways that are not detectable without laboratory analysis. The apparent seal is indistinguishable from a water bath processed seal by sensory inspection

Paraffin wax sealing — prohibited:

Sealing jelly glasses with melted paraffin wax instead of lid and water bath processing is prohibited for community food preservation
Paraffin seals are unreliable — they shrink from jar walls as they cool, creating microscopic channels that allow mold access to the product surface
Paraffin-sealed products have significantly higher mold contamination rates than water bath processed products and must be refrigerated after opening immediately upon detecting any surface mold
The historical prevalence of paraffin sealing in older cookbooks and family recipes reflects the standards of an era before water bath processing of jams and jellies was understood to be necessary — it does not reflect a safe preservation method

Reduced-sugar and no-sugar-added preserves:

Standard jam and jelly recipes shall not be modified to reduce sugar beyond the specified quantity without using a recipe specifically validated for reduced sugar
Low-sugar and no-sugar-added pectin products with validated recipes are acceptable — these use different pectin chemistry that gels without the sugar concentration required by standard pectin
Reduced-sugar products have higher water activity than standard sugar-concentration products and may require refrigeration rather than room temperature storage — the specific product recipe shall specify storage requirements
Reduced-sugar products water bath processed using standard jam processing times are acceptable where the approved recipe specifies water bath processing

pH verification for non-standard fruit:

Standard jam and jelly recipes are developed using fruits with known acidity ranges — strawberries, raspberries, blueberries, apples, and other commonly used fruits with well-characterized pH
Less common fruits — figs, Asian pears, papayas, bananas, melons — have pH at or above 4.6 and require acidification before use in water bath processed preserves
Figs in particular — pH typically 4.9 to 5.5 — shall be acidified with bottled lemon juice at 2 tablespoons per quart of product before processing
Any fruit of uncertain or borderline acidity shall be pH tested in the finished product before water bath processing

Rationale: Jams, jellies, and high-sugar preserves are among the most accessible entry points for community food preservation — they are familiar, the ingredients are simple, the process is relatively straightforward, and the products have broad appeal and long storage life when correctly processed. They are also the category where the gap between traditional practice and current safety standards is most visible — the open kettle method and paraffin sealing are embedded in generations of community food preservation culture and produce food that appears identical to correctly processed food.
The open kettle prohibition requires extended explanation because it will be the standard in tension with the most established community practices. The argument for open kettle processing — that hot food in a hot jar creates adequate seal and that generations of practitioners have used it without apparent harm — contains two embedded errors. The first is that the apparent seal created by open kettle processing is thermally equivalent to a water bath processed seal — it is not. Water bath processing heats the entire contents of the jar to boiling temperature for a specified time, destroying mold spores, yeasts, and bacteria present in the fruit, on jar surfaces, and introduced during filling. Open kettle processing heats the product before filling but allows recontamination during the filling process and does not destroy organisms introduced after cooking. The second error is the inference from apparent safety to actual safety — generations of practitioners who did not experience illness from open kettle processed preserves were not protected by the process. They were protected by the combination of high sugar concentration, high acid, and low water activity that makes correctly made jam and jelly inhospitable to the most dangerous pathogens. The process added nothing to the safety provided by the product composition itself — and failed to add the protection against mold and quality degradation that water bath processing provides.
The paraffin wax prohibition follows the same logic applied to the specific failure mode of paraffin sealing. Paraffin contracts as it cools from liquid to solid, and the contraction is not uniform — paraffin pulls away from jar walls in microscopic channels that are invisible without magnification and that allow mold spores, which are ubiquitous in air, to reach the product surface. Studies comparing mold incidence in paraffin-sealed versus water bath processed jelly found significantly higher surface mold rates in paraffin-sealed products — rates high enough that paraffin-sealed jelly should be considered a refrigerator product with short storage life rather than a shelf-stable product, regardless of how it has historically been stored.
The fig acidification requirement addresses the same borderline-acid problem discussed for tomatoes in 3.14 but is less widely known because figs are less universally grown than tomatoes. Figs are genuinely low-acid fruit — their natural pH is well above the 4.6 threshold even when fully ripe — and fig preserves made without acidification and water bath processed are not safe for shelf-stable storage. This fact is absent from many older fig preserve recipes and from community canning traditions in regions where fig cultivation is common. The historical practice of water bath canning fig preserves without acidification has produced illness — a 1989 outbreak of botulism from home-canned figs in California sickened three members of a family who had canned figs using a traditional family recipe without acidification for many years without previous incident.
The gel point requirement connects preservation safety to process completion in a practical way. Undercooking jam or jelly below gel point produces a product that is runny — obviously not set — and also a product that has not achieved the sugar concentration required for water activity reduction. A jam cooked to gel point at 220°F has concentrated its sugars through water evaporation to the point where water activity is below the mold and yeast inhibition threshold. A jam removed from heat before reaching gel point retains more water, has higher water activity, and is not safely shelf-stable even if it achieves an apparent seal through water bath processing. The gel point is simultaneously a quality indicator and a safety indicator — the set is evidence that concentration has been achieved.
In 2003 a community food cooperative in Vermont distributed home-preserved strawberry jam produced using the open kettle method by a well-regarded community member who had made jam the same way for forty years. Within three months of distribution, multiple recipients reported surface mold on unopened jars — the jars had apparent seals that had not prevented mold access through microscopic gaps in the open kettle seals. The cooperative recalled all jars from that production batch. No illness resulted — the mold was surface mold on a high-acid product rather than a pathogen — but the recall required identifying and contacting every recipient of the batch, a logistical exercise that illustrated the importance of the producer identification and lot documentation requirements in 3.11. The cooperative adopted water bath processing as its standard after the incident. The producer, confronted with the evidence of seal failure across the batch, acknowledged that previous years of apparent success had not been evidence of a safe process — it had been evidence of lucky outcomes from an unreliable one.
