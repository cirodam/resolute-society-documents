4.1 Animal Health Assessment Before Slaughter
Standard: Every animal intended for slaughter shall be observed and assessed for health before slaughter. No animal showing signs of illness, injury beyond minor wounds, or abnormal behavior shall be slaughtered for community food use without veterinary evaluation where available, or community meat inspection officer evaluation where veterinary services are unavailable.
Ante-mortem observation period:

Animals shall be observed in a low-stress environment for a minimum of 12 hours before slaughter where practical — 24 hours preferred for animals transported from other locations
Observation period allows identification of illness signs that may not be apparent in a brief examination and allows animals to recover from transport stress before assessment
Animals that arrive showing signs of transport stress — extreme agitation, difficulty breathing, inability to stand — shall rest a minimum of 24 hours before slaughter assessment

Conditions requiring immediate condemnation — do not slaughter:

Animal unable to stand or walk unassisted — downer animals
Visible systemic illness — fever, labored breathing, nasal or ocular discharge, jaundice
Neurological signs — circling, head pressing, seizures, loss of coordination
Severe emaciation
Generalized swelling or edema
Obvious systemic infection — large abscesses, widespread skin lesions
Unknown cause of death — animals found dead shall never be processed for food regardless of apparent freshness
Any animal suspected of rabies exposure or showing neurological signs consistent with rabies

Conditions requiring evaluation before slaughter:

Localized wounds or abscesses that may be removed during processing if the infection has not spread systemically
Lameness of uncertain cause
Pregnancy — late-term pregnant animals may be slaughtered but require additional inspection of reproductive tract
Animals recently treated with veterinary medications — withdrawal periods for antibiotics, antiparasitic drugs, and other veterinary medications shall be observed before slaughter. Meat from animals slaughtered during medication withdrawal periods shall not enter the community food supply

Documentation:

Health assessment findings shall be documented for each animal before slaughter
Animals passing ante-mortem assessment shall be identified and this identification maintained through processing to enable traceback if post-mortem inspection reveals problems
Any animal condemned at ante-mortem assessment shall have the condemnation reason documented

Rationale: Ante-mortem — before death — assessment is the first of two inspection points that together constitute the community meat inspection system. Its purpose is to identify animals that are systemically ill before slaughter, preventing both the processing of unsafe meat and the contamination of the slaughter environment with pathogens from a diseased animal.
The importance of ante-mortem assessment is grounded in the behavior of systemic illness in food animals. An animal with a systemic bacterial infection — septicemia — has pathogens circulating throughout its bloodstream and distributed through muscle tissue, organs, and lymph nodes. Processing this animal distributes those pathogens across the slaughter environment, contaminating equipment, surfaces, and adjacent carcasses. The meat itself carries a pathogen load that normal slaughter hygiene cannot eliminate because the contamination is internal rather than surface-introduced. Post-mortem inspection can identify many systemic infections — but not all, and not with the reliability that rejecting visibly ill animals before slaughter provides.
Downer animals — those unable to stand — require specific explanation because the instinct to slaughter a downer animal quickly before it dies and is wasted is understandable and dangerous. Downer status in cattle is the primary risk indicator for bovine spongiform encephalopathy — BSE, or mad cow disease — a prion disease that is invariably fatal in humans who consume contaminated neural tissue and for which there is no treatment. BSE produces no reliable ante-mortem test in living animals and no visible changes in meat. A downer cow may have gone down for reasons entirely unrelated to BSE — a broken leg, milk fever, a metabolic disorder — but the inability to distinguish downer animals with neurological causes from those with mechanical causes makes the categorical prohibition on processing downer animals the only safe standard. The 2003 United States BSE case — the first confirmed domestic case — was a downer dairy cow in Washington state. The subsequent investigation and recall affected 10,000 pounds of beef and generated international trade consequences. The animal should never have entered the slaughter process.
Medication withdrawal periods exist because veterinary drugs administered to food animals distribute through muscle tissue, organs, and fat at concentrations that decline over time as the animal metabolizes and excretes them. Slaughtering an animal before the withdrawal period is complete means slaughtering an animal whose meat contains drug residues at concentrations that may cause adverse reactions in consumers — antibiotic residues that contribute to antimicrobial resistance, antiparasitic residues with direct toxicity, hormone residues with endocrine effects. Withdrawal periods are established by the pharmacokinetics of each specific drug and represent the time required for residues to fall below safe consumption thresholds. They are not conservative estimates with built-in margins — they are the calculated minimum time required for safety.
The prohibition on processing found-dead animals is absolute and requires direct address because food scarcity situations create pressure to utilize apparently fresh animals that have died from unknown causes. An animal found dead may have died from disease, toxin ingestion, lightning strike, or injury — causes whose safety implications differ dramatically. Without knowing the cause of death there is no basis for evaluating the safety of the meat, and the stress, temperature exposure, and circulatory shutdown that accompany death accelerate pathogen distribution through the carcass in ways that render even a recently dead animal significantly more contaminated than a properly slaughtered one. The rule is not that found-dead animals are always unsafe — some causes of death produce perfectly safe meat. The rule is that without knowing the cause, the risk cannot be evaluated, and in a community food system where the consequences of serving unsafe meat fall on many people, unknown risk is not acceptable.

4.2 Pre-Slaughter Handling and Stress Minimization
Standard: Animals shall be handled in the period before slaughter in ways that minimize fear, pain, and physiological stress. Stress minimization is simultaneously an animal welfare requirement and a food safety requirement — the physiological consequences of pre-slaughter stress directly affect meat safety and quality in ways that cannot be corrected after slaughter.
Handling requirements:
Movement and restraint:

Animals shall be moved using low-stress handling techniques — calm, quiet movement that uses the animal's natural flight zone and point of balance to guide direction rather than force, pain, or fear
Electric prods shall not be used as routine handling tools — their use shall be limited to situations where an animal is endangering itself or handlers and no other means of movement is effective. Electric prods shall never be used on the face, anal region, or genitals of any animal
Hitting, kicking, dragging, or lifting animals by legs, ears, tails, or fleece is prohibited
Animals shall not be moved faster than a walk in the immediate pre-slaughter period
Ramps, chutes, and handling facilities shall be designed to prevent falls, provide secure footing, and avoid sharp turns that cause animals to balk — an animal that falls in a handling facility experiences both physical injury and significant fear response

Social considerations:

Prey animals — cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, poultry — are social species that experience significant stress when isolated from conspecifics
Where possible animals shall be moved to slaughter in small groups rather than individually
Animals shall not be held in sight or smell range of active slaughter operations — exposure to blood, the sounds of slaughter, and the stress signals of other animals in distress causes measurable physiological stress responses in waiting animals
Companion animals that have been housed together shall be slaughtered together where possible rather than separating them and slaughtering individually

Waiting area requirements:

Pre-slaughter holding areas shall provide adequate space for animals to stand, turn, and lie down without being forced into contact with other animals
Water shall be available in holding areas at all times
Shade and weather protection shall be provided — temperature extremes in holding areas cause physiological stress independent of handling
Holding areas shall be free of protruding metal, broken fencing, and other hazards that cause injury

Timing:

The period between arrival at the slaughter facility and slaughter shall be minimized — extended holding in an unfamiliar environment is itself a stressor
Where holding is necessary animals shall be given adequate rest before slaughter — a minimum of 2 hours rest after any stressful handling or transport event before slaughter proceeds
Slaughter shall not be performed on visibly agitated animals that have not calmed — agitation at the moment of slaughter indicates elevated stress hormones that affect meat quality

Rationale: The relationship between pre-slaughter stress and meat quality is well-established in food science and represents one of the clearest examples in this document of animal welfare and food safety interests being identical rather than competing. Understanding this relationship makes the handling standards meaningful rather than sentimental.
When an animal experiences fear or pain, the sympathetic nervous system initiates the fight-or-flight response — adrenaline and cortisol are released, heart rate increases, and glycogen stored in muscle tissue is mobilized and metabolized to provide energy for emergency response. This glycogen depletion has direct food safety and quality consequences. In a rested, calm animal at slaughter, muscle glycogen is metabolized after death to lactic acid through anaerobic glycolysis — this post-mortem acidification lowers muscle pH from approximately 7.0 to a final pH of 5.4 to 5.8, creating an environment that inhibits pathogen growth and produces the characteristic texture, color, and water-holding capacity of high-quality meat. In a stressed animal whose glycogen has been depleted before slaughter, this acidification cannot occur — the muscle reaches a final pH of 6.0 or above, producing what is called dark, firm, and dry — DFD — meat in beef and dark cutting meat in poultry.
DFD meat presents food safety concerns beyond its obvious quality problems. The elevated pH of DFD meat — closer to neutral than normal meat — is significantly more hospitable to pathogen growth than correctly acidified meat. Listeria monocytogenes, Salmonella, and other meat-borne pathogens grow significantly faster in high-pH meat and survive longer under refrigeration. The inhibitory effect of post-mortem acidification that makes normal meat relatively resistant to rapid pathogen growth is absent. A piece of DFD beef or dark-cutting poultry has a shorter safe storage life, a higher pathogen growth rate under equivalent storage conditions, and a smaller safety margin across the entire cold chain than normally acidified meat from a calm animal.
Pigs present an additional stress-related meat quality concern — pale, soft, and exudative — PSE — pork, which occurs when pigs experience extreme acute stress immediately before slaughter, typically from rough handling, fighting with unfamiliar pigs, or extreme heat. PSE pork has the opposite pH problem from DFD — pH drops too rapidly in the first hour after slaughter before body temperature has declined, producing a meat texture that is pale, soft, and unable to hold moisture. PSE pork is a quality failure rather than a direct safety failure, but it indicates a handling failure that also increases pathogen load through the stress mechanisms described above.
The prohibition on electric prod routine use requires direct address because electric prods are standard equipment in conventional livestock operations and their routine use is normalized in conventional meat production culture. The normalization exists because prods are effective at moving animals quickly — they are not effective at moving animals calmly, and speed of movement through handling facilities is the priority in high-throughput commercial operations where throughput is the primary metric. In a community food system where the quality and safety of the meat produced is the primary metric, calm movement that takes longer is categorically preferable to rapid movement that causes stress. Temple Grandin's decades of research on livestock handling have documented that animals moved through well-designed low-stress handling systems using behavioral principles move as quickly as those moved with prods while showing dramatically lower stress indicators — there is no throughput tradeoff in a well-designed system.
In 2004 an investigation of a large commercial poultry processor in the United States found that chronic rough handling of birds during loading and transport was producing DFD meat in a significant proportion of the flock, with corresponding elevated pathogen loads that required additional processing interventions to manage. The handling practices had been standard in the industry for decades. The connection between the handling practices and the elevated pathogen loads had not been made because the conventional response to elevated pathogen loads — additional chemical interventions at processing — was cheaper than redesigning handling systems. In a community food system without the option of chemical pathogen intervention at scale, the handling practices are the pathogen management system.

4.3 Fasting Requirements Before Slaughter
Standard: Animals shall be fasted for specified periods before slaughter to reduce gut fill and minimize the risk of gastrointestinal tract contamination during evisceration. Fasting requirements vary by species and shall be applied without exception. Water shall remain available throughout the fasting period — water withdrawal is prohibited.
Fasting periods by species:
Cattle and large ruminants — beef cattle, dairy cattle, bison:

12-24 hours feed withdrawal before slaughter
Rumen contents at slaughter are significantly reduced after 12 hours — a full rumen is more difficult to remove without rupture during evisceration and contains larger quantities of potential contamination if rupture occurs
24-hour maximum — extended fasting beyond 24 hours in ruminants causes rumen pH changes that increase E. coli O157:H7 shedding, increasing rather than decreasing contamination risk

Small ruminants — sheep, goats, lambs:

12-18 hours feed withdrawal before slaughter
Rumen and intestinal fill reduce more quickly in small ruminants than large — 12 hours is generally adequate for significant gut fill reduction

Swine:

12-18 hours feed withdrawal before slaughter
Swine gastric emptying is slower than ruminants — 12 hours minimum ensures significant reduction in stomach contents
Extended fasting in swine beyond 24 hours increases fighting behavior and stress in group-housed animals and shall be avoided
Swine fasted appropriately produce less gut contamination during evisceration and have lower rates of Salmonella shedding at slaughter — fasting is a direct pathogen reduction intervention in swine

Poultry:

8-12 hours feed withdrawal before slaughter
Poultry intestinal transit time is rapid — 8 hours is generally sufficient to significantly clear intestinal contents
Fasting period longer than 12 hours in poultry increases Salmonella and Campylobacter cecal shedding — the same counter-productive effect seen in ruminants fasted beyond recommended periods

Rabbits:

4-6 hours feed withdrawal before slaughter
Rabbit cecum is large relative to body size and contains significant fermentation activity — extended fasting disrupts cecal flora and is not recommended beyond 6 hours

Water requirements during fasting:

Fresh clean water shall be available continuously throughout the fasting period for all species
Water withdrawal during fasting is prohibited — dehydration causes physiological stress that affects meat quality through the mechanisms described in 4.2 and increases the concentration of pathogens in the gastrointestinal tract
Animals arriving dehydrated from transport shall be rehydrated before fasting begins — fasting time begins after adequate hydration is confirmed

Feed restriction versus complete fasting:

The fasting periods above refer to complete feed withdrawal — no hay, grain, pasture access, or supplemental feed
For ruminants on pasture, removal from pasture constitutes feed withdrawal — animals shall be moved to a dry lot or pen without vegetation access for the fasting period
Bedding in fasting pens shall not be edible — straw bedding that animals consume partially defeats the purpose of fasting

Documentation:

Feed withdrawal time shall be documented for each animal or group — start time of fasting and time of slaughter shall be recorded
Where fasting period cannot be verified — animals of unknown recent feed history — a conservative estimate shall be used and noted in records

Rationale: Fasting before slaughter is one of the most direct pathogen reduction interventions available in community meat production, and its mechanism is straightforward — a gastrointestinal tract with less content is less likely to rupture during evisceration, and if it does rupture the quantity of contamination released is substantially smaller. The gastrointestinal tract of a food animal at slaughter contains the highest concentration of pathogens in the entire animal — E. coli O157:H7, Salmonella, Campylobacter, Clostridium, and others are present in the gut contents at concentrations many orders of magnitude higher than anywhere else in the carcass. A ruptured gut during evisceration is the single most significant contamination event in the slaughter process, and fasting reduces both the probability and the consequence of rupture.
The counter-intuitive finding that extended fasting increases pathogen shedding in both ruminants and poultry requires explanation because it contradicts the intuition that emptier is always better. In cattle, the rumen maintains a pH of approximately 5.5 to 6.5 during normal feeding — an environment that is moderately hostile to E. coli O157:H7, which prefers the near-neutral pH of the lower gastrointestinal tract. When feed is withdrawn for extended periods beyond 24 hours, rumen pH rises toward neutral as fermentation substrates are depleted. This rising pH creates more favorable conditions for E. coli O157:H7 to survive transit through the rumen and establish in higher concentrations in the lower gut and on the hide — the primary sources of carcass contamination during slaughter. Research comparing E. coli O157:H7 prevalence in cattle fasted for 12 versus 48 hours found significantly higher contamination rates in the extended-fast group.
The swine-specific finding that appropriate fasting reduces Salmonella shedding connects to the ecology of Salmonella in pig gastrointestinal tracts. Salmonella in swine is frequently carried in the tonsils and lymph nodes as well as the gastrointestinal tract — it is an endemic pathogen in most commercial swine populations. Appropriate fasting reduces gut fill and the mechanical contamination risk during evisceration while not extending to the duration that causes the pH changes that increase shedding. The 12-18 hour window for swine represents the balance point where gut fill is substantially reduced without triggering the counter-productive extended-fast effects.
Water availability during fasting is non-negotiable and deserves specific emphasis because water withdrawal during fasting is practiced in some conventional production contexts to reduce gut fluid content — reasoning that less gut fluid means less contamination if rupture occurs. This reasoning is correct about gut fluid volume but incorrect about the net safety effect. Dehydrated animals at slaughter have concentrated gut contents, elevated stress hormone levels, and compromised meat quality through the mechanisms described in 4.2. The reduction in gut fluid volume does not compensate for the increased pathogen concentration in dehydrated gut contents or the stress-related meat quality and safety consequences. Water shall remain available.
The pasture removal requirement for ruminants addresses a failure mode specific to community and small-farm slaughter where animals are pastured until shortly before slaughter. Ruminants on pasture consume continuously throughout the day — the feeding behavior of cattle involves approximately 8 hours of active grazing per day. An animal removed from pasture 12 hours before slaughter has had its last meal within those 12 hours and begins the fasting clock from the moment of pasture removal, not from some earlier point. Moving an animal from pasture to a dry lot pen without vegetation access is the practical implementation of feed withdrawal for pastured animals — it is not sufficient to move the animal to a different pasture or to a pen with edible bedding.
In 1996 a study of small-scale slaughter operations in the United Kingdom found that gut rupture rates during evisceration were significantly higher in animals not fasted before slaughter compared to fasted animals — 23% rupture rate in unfasted cattle versus 7% in cattle fasted for 12-18 hours. Gut rupture in the unfasted group produced carcass contamination requiring significant trim loss and, in some cases, carcass condemnation. The fasting protocol reduced both contamination events and economic loss from condemned carcasses. In a community food system where every animal represents significant community investment in raising, feeding, and processing, the economic argument for fasting aligns directly with the safety argument.

4.4 Equipment Preparation and Sanitation
Standard: All equipment used in animal slaughter and butchering shall be cleaned, sanitized, and inspected before use at the beginning of each slaughter session and sanitized between animals during processing. Equipment sanitation in slaughter operations differs from food service sanitation in scale and method but not in principle — equipment that contacts the carcass is a food contact surface from the moment the hide is removed, and shall be treated as such.
Equipment inventory — minimum required for community slaughter operations:
Slaughter equipment:

Captive bolt stunner or appropriate alternative stunning device per 4.5, maintained and tested before each use
Sticking knife — minimum 6-inch blade, dedicated to exsanguination only, not used for subsequent carcass work
Gambrel hooks, shackles, or hoist equipment appropriate to species being processed
Hoist or block and tackle capable of safely lifting the largest animal being processed — rated capacity shall exceed maximum expected animal weight by minimum 50%

Skinning and evisceration equipment:

Skinning knives — minimum two per operator to allow knife rotation during sanitation
Boning and breaking knives appropriate to species
Meat saw — hand or powered — for carcass splitting and bone cutting
Evisceration hooks or cradle for holding viscera during inspection
Gut bucket or container for visceral waste — watertight, covered, clearly designated

Sanitation equipment:

Two knife sanitizing containers per operator — hot water at minimum 180°F or approved sanitizing solution — for continuous knife rotation during processing
Hose with adequate pressure for carcass washing
Separate hoses or dedicated connections for potable water carcass washing and non-potable water facility cleaning — these shall never be interchangeable
Brushes dedicated to equipment cleaning — not used on carcasses
Spray bottles of sanitizing solution for surface sanitation during processing

Facility equipment:

Stainless steel or sealed food-grade surfaces for carcass contact — bare wood, unsealed concrete, and rust-pitted metal are not acceptable carcass contact surfaces
Overhead rail system or equivalent for hanging carcasses during processing — carcasses shall not rest on the floor at any point during processing
Adequate lighting — minimum 50 foot-candles at working surfaces — to allow identification of contamination, pathological lesions, and processing defects

Pre-session preparation:
Cleaning sequence:

Remove all organic material — blood, fat, tissue, and debris — from all surfaces and equipment using detergent and mechanical action before sanitizing
Clean from highest surfaces to lowest — ceiling, walls, overhead rails, work surfaces, floors — so that contamination falls to already-uncleaned surfaces rather than already-cleaned ones
Pay particular attention to joints, seams, hinges, and other areas where organic material accumulates and cleaning is mechanically difficult
Rinse all detergent residue from surfaces before sanitizing — detergent residue neutralizes many sanitizers

Sanitizing sequence:

Apply sanitizing solution to all cleaned surfaces — hot water at 180°F, chlorine solution at 200 ppm, or quaternary ammonium per manufacturer specification
Allow contact time per sanitizer type before use
Knife sanitizing containers shall be prepared and at temperature or concentration before any animal is brought to the slaughter area

Equipment inspection:

Stunning equipment tested and functioning before animals enter the slaughter area — per 4.5
All knife blades sharp — dull blades require excessive force that increases the risk of slipping and loss of control, and produce ragged cuts that are more difficult to clean than cuts from sharp blades
Hoist and lifting equipment load-tested before use — a failed hoist dropping a large animal carcass is a safety emergency and a significant contamination event
All potable water connections verified — water used for carcass washing shall come from a source meeting the standards in Section 1

During-session sanitation — knife rotation protocol:

Operators shall work with a minimum of two knives per person, rotating between them continuously
The knife not in use shall be held in the sanitizing container — hot water at 180°F or approved sanitizing solution — for a minimum of 20 seconds before return to use
Knives shall be rotated after every cut that contacts potentially contaminated surfaces — hide, gastrointestinal tract, abscess, or any area where contamination is possible
Knives dropped on the floor shall be removed from service, cleaned, and sanitized before return to use regardless of how clean the floor appears

Between-animal sanitation:

All carcass contact surfaces — work tables, overhead rails, hooks, and gambrels — shall be cleaned and sanitized between animals
Stunning equipment shall be cleaned of blood and tissue between animals
Gut buckets and visceral waste containers shall be emptied or replaced between animals where capacity requires
Operator aprons and outer clothing shall be inspected and changed if heavily soiled between animals — a heavily blood-soiled apron contacting a fresh carcass transfers contamination

Rationale: Equipment sanitation in slaughter operations is the primary mechanism for preventing cross-contamination between animals and between the external contaminated surfaces of an animal — hide, hooves, and external skin — and the sterile internal tissues exposed during processing. The interior muscle tissue of a healthy animal is essentially sterile at the moment of slaughter. Every subsequent step in processing is an opportunity to introduce contamination from external sources — and the primary external source is the equipment that has contacted contaminated surfaces.
The two-knife rotation system is the foundational sanitation practice of professional slaughter operations and the practice most commonly abandoned in informal or community slaughter settings where knife shortages, time pressure, and unfamiliarity with the protocol lead operators to wipe knives on aprons, rinse in cold water, or simply continue using a contaminated knife because the contamination is invisible. The rationale for rotation is identical to the rationale for cutting board separation in 2.11 — a knife that has contacted a contaminated surface carries that contamination to the next surface it contacts. Unlike a cutting board that stays in place, a knife moves continuously between contaminated and clean surfaces throughout the evisceration and skinning process. Without rotation through a sanitizing medium between uses, the knife is a contamination transfer tool.
The 180°F hot water sanitizing standard for knives reflects the temperature required for rapid pathogen destruction on metal surfaces — at 180°F, contact time of 20 seconds achieves equivalent pathogen reduction to chemical sanitizers at standard concentrations. Hot water has practical advantages over chemical sanitizers in slaughter environments — it requires no chemical inventory, leaves no residue on knife blades that contacts food, and provides immediate visual confirmation that the sanitizing medium is at temperature through steam production. The temperature shall be maintained throughout the session — a container of hot water that has cooled to 120°F after 30 minutes of use is not a sanitizing medium.
Knife sharpness is included in equipment standards because it is both a safety issue for operators and a sanitation issue for carcasses. A dull knife on a slippery wet carcass surface requires the operator to apply significant force — force that when the knife slips, which it will, produces uncontrolled cuts in unintended locations including the gastrointestinal tract and the operator's hands. Beyond the immediate injury risk, dull knives produce ragged, torn tissue surfaces rather than clean cuts — torn surfaces have greater surface area, are more difficult to clean, and provide better bacterial attachment sites than clean-cut surfaces. Sharp knives are safer for operators and produce safer meat.
The overhead rail requirement — carcasses shall not contact the floor at any point — addresses a contamination pathway that is routinely violated in informal slaughter operations where hoisting equipment is inadequate for the size of animal being processed. A carcass lowered to the floor for splitting, skinning, or other operations contacts the floor surface and all the contamination it carries — soil, feces, blood from previous animals, and environmental pathogens. Floor contact is the single most significant preventable contamination event in small-scale slaughter operations. Adequate hoisting equipment sized to the animals being processed is not optional infrastructure — it is a core food safety requirement.
In 1992 a study of small-scale slaughter operations in rural communities in the United Kingdom found that carcass contamination rates — measured by surface bacterial counts — were six to eight times higher in operations where carcasses contacted the floor during processing compared to operations with adequate overhead rail systems. The floor-contact operations were not otherwise deficient in hygiene practice — operators were experienced, knives were sharp, facilities were reasonably clean. The floor contact alone produced contamination levels equivalent to operations with multiple identified hygiene deficiencies. The infrastructure deficit — inadequate hoisting capacity — produced food safety outcomes equivalent to hygiene failure.

4.5 Humane Slaughter Methods by Species
Standard: All animals slaughtered for community food use shall be rendered unconscious before or simultaneously with the killing action, with the exception of specific religious slaughter practices noted below. Slaughter methods shall achieve immediate and irreversible unconsciousness with a single application wherever possible. Multiple attempts to achieve unconsciousness are a welfare failure and an indicator of equipment malfunction or operator error requiring immediate correction before proceeding.
Approved methods by species:
Cattle and large ruminants — beef cattle, bison, large goats:
Penetrating captive bolt — preferred method:

Captive bolt pistol positioned perpendicular to the skull at the intersection of two diagonal lines drawn from the base of each horn to the opposite eye — the precise point varies slightly by species and shall be confirmed for each species before use
Single application shall produce immediate collapse and absence of rhythmic breathing, corneal reflex, and response to stimulation
Captive bolt renders the animal irreversibly unconscious but does not cause death — exsanguination per 4.6 shall follow immediately
Captive bolt cartridges shall be matched to the caliber and power requirements of the specific stunner model — underpowered cartridges are the most common cause of inadequate stunning requiring multiple attempts
Captive bolt stunners shall be cleaned and lubricated per manufacturer specifications before each use — fouled stunners lose velocity and penetration depth

Non-penetrating captive bolt — acceptable for calves and small ruminants, not recommended for adult cattle:

Produces concussion without skull penetration
Requires immediate follow-up with a killing action — non-penetrating captive bolt does not reliably produce irreversible unconsciousness in large animals

Gunshot — acceptable where captive bolt is unavailable:

Firearm of adequate caliber for species — minimum .22 long rifle for sheep and goats, minimum .308 or equivalent for cattle
Same anatomical placement as captive bolt
Produces death directly rather than unconsciousness followed by exsanguination — verify absence of signs of consciousness before approaching
Requires trained operator and safe backstop behind animal

Swine:
Penetrating captive bolt:

Positioned at the top of the forehead, 1-2 inches above the eyes, directed toward the base of the tail — not toward the back of the skull as in cattle
Swine skull anatomy differs significantly from cattle — incorrect placement produces inadequate stunning
Penetrating captive bolt is the preferred method for swine

Carbon dioxide stunning — acceptable for community-scale operations with appropriate equipment:

Exposure to 80-90% CO₂ concentration for minimum 3 minutes produces unconsciousness
Requires purpose-built CO₂ stunning chamber — improvised chambers are not acceptable
CO₂ stunning causes distress during the induction phase — pigs respond aversively to CO₂ before losing consciousness. This is a welfare limitation of the method that shall be acknowledged
CO₂ stunning is included because it allows group stunning without individual restraint — a welfare advantage in reducing handling stress that partially offsets the induction distress in community-scale operations

Gunshot:

Same caliber and placement principles as captive bolt — top of forehead, directed toward base of tail
Acceptable where captive bolt is unavailable

Small ruminants — sheep and goats:
Penetrating captive bolt:

Positioned at the top of the poll — the highest point of the skull — directed toward the angle of the jaw
Wool or long hair at the stunning site shall be parted or wetted to ensure firm contact between stunner and skull
Lower-powered cartridges appropriate for species — the same cartridge used for cattle may cause excessive skull damage in small ruminants

Gunshot:

.22 long rifle or equivalent is adequate for sheep and goats
Same anatomical placement as captive bolt

Percussion stunning — acceptable for individual animals in field conditions:

A heavy, firm blow to the poll with a purpose-made striking device — not improvised tools
Requires immediate follow-up with a killing action — percussion stunning is not reliably irreversible
Last-resort method where no other equipment is available

Poultry:
Cervical dislocation — manual, for individual birds:

Firm, rapid separation of the cervical vertebrae by stretching and rotating the neck — requires training to perform correctly
Effective and immediate when performed correctly — produces immediate loss of consciousness and death
Acceptable for individual birds and small numbers — physically demanding for large-scale operations
Requires verification of effectiveness — a bird not showing complete body limpness and absence of all rhythmic movement has not been effectively dispatched

Mechanical cervical dislocation:

Purpose-built devices that apply consistent force to achieve cervical dislocation
Preferred over manual method for consistency and reduced operator fatigue at scale

Controlled atmosphere stunning — CO₂ or inert gas:

Same principles as swine CO₂ stunning adapted for poultry
Appropriate for community-scale poultry operations processing larger numbers

Decapitation:

Complete, single-action removal of the head with a sharp blade
Immediate and effective when a sharp blade and skilled operator are used
Post-decapitation movement — the involuntary muscle activity that gives rise to the term "running around like a chicken with its head cut off" — is a spinal reflex, not evidence of consciousness. The bird is dead
Acceptable method but requires attention to safe blade handling and restraint of the bird during the action

Rabbits:
Cervical dislocation:

Firm, rapid stretching of the neck to dislocate cervical vertebrae — performed by holding the hindquarters in one hand and the head in the other and applying firm, rapid extension
Immediate and effective when performed correctly
Preferred method for rabbits due to simplicity and effectiveness

Blunt trauma to the skull:

A firm, accurate blow to the back of the skull with a heavy blunt object
Effective when applied correctly — requires a single decisive blow
Acceptable where cervical dislocation is not possible

Signs of effective unconsciousness — verify before proceeding with exsanguination:

Immediate collapse
No rhythmic breathing — agonal gasps in the first seconds after stunning are acceptable, sustained rhythmic breathing is not
No corneal reflex — touching the surface of the eye produces no blinking response
No response to stimulation — pinching the nose or ear produces no response
Fixed, glazed eyes with dilated pupils

Signs of inadequate stunning — requires immediate re-stunning before any further action:

Any rhythmic breathing after the first few seconds
Blinking or corneal reflex present
Vocalizing
Righting attempts — trying to stand or right the body
Purposeful movement of the head or limbs

Religious slaughter:

Halal and kosher slaughter traditions require slaughter without prior stunning in their traditional forms
Where religious slaughter is practiced in the community, the killing action shall be a single, rapid, complete severance of both carotid arteries and both jugular veins with a surgically sharp blade in a single uninterrupted motion — any sawing, dragging, or multiple-stroke action is prohibited under both traditions and under this standard
Loss of consciousness following correctly performed religious slaughter occurs within seconds of complete exsanguination — the animal shall be restrained and not allowed to rise during this period
Religious slaughter shall be performed only by practitioners trained and authorized within their tradition

Rationale: Humane slaughter standards are grounded simultaneously in the ethical obligation to minimize animal suffering and in the physiological connection between suffering and food safety established in 4.2. An animal that regains consciousness during exsanguination — the most common consequence of inadequate stunning — experiences the full physiological stress response of fear and pain, with the hormonal and biochemical consequences for meat quality described in 4.2. The welfare failure and the food safety failure are the same event.
The anatomical precision required for effective stunning reflects the neuroanatomy of unconsciousness. Captive bolt and gunshot stunning work by delivering concussive force or projectile penetration to the cerebral cortex — the brain structures responsible for conscious awareness. Misplacement of the stunning instrument — even by a few centimeters — can miss the critical structures, delivering a blow that causes pain and disorientation without producing unconsciousness. Animals stunned in incorrect anatomical locations frequently regain consciousness rapidly and require re-stunning — a welfare failure that is also an indicator of inadequate operator training.
The requirement for a single application achieving immediate unconsciousness is both a welfare standard and a practical standard. An operator who regularly requires multiple stunning attempts is either using inadequate equipment, using incorrectly powered cartridges, or placing the stunner incorrectly. Each of these is a correctable equipment or training problem. Normalizing multiple attempts normalizes the welfare failure they represent and masks the underlying equipment or training deficiency that will continue to produce it.
The CO₂ swine stunning welfare limitation is acknowledged directly because this document's commitment to honest rationale requires acknowledging known welfare compromises rather than presenting all approved methods as equally humane. CO₂ stunning causes measurable aversive response — pigs attempt to avoid CO₂ exposure and show distress behaviors during the induction phase before losing consciousness. It is included because its advantages — group processing without individual restraint — reduce the cumulative stress of individual handling and restraint in ways that may represent a net welfare improvement for the full sequence of pre-slaughter and slaughter experience. Communities should understand this tradeoff when choosing stunning methods.
In 2013 a comprehensive audit of stunning effectiveness at commercial slaughter facilities in the European Union found that inadequate stunning — animals reaching the exsanguination point conscious or regaining consciousness before death — occurred at rates of 1-5% across facilities using captive bolt stunning, with higher rates in facilities where stunner maintenance and cartridge matching were inadequate. At community scale with lower throughput, individual animal attention, and operator accountability, these rates should be significantly lower — but achieving that requires the training, equipment maintenance, and anatomical knowledge that this standard specifies. A community slaughter program that has not trained operators in correct stunning placement and has not established stunner maintenance protocols will not achieve better welfare outcomes than the commercial operations this document is designed to improve upon.

4.6 Exsanguination — Bleeding Out
Standard: Exsanguination — the severance of major blood vessels to cause death through blood loss — shall follow stunning immediately, within 60 seconds for cattle and large animals and within 30 seconds for small animals and poultry. Delay beyond these intervals risks recovery of consciousness in stunned animals and is both a welfare failure and a food safety concern. Exsanguination shall be complete before any further processing steps — skinning, scalding, or evisceration — begin.
Methods by species:
Cattle and large ruminants:

Sticking — insertion of a dedicated sticking knife into the thoracic cavity at the base of the neck to sever the carotid arteries and jugular veins simultaneously
Knife inserted at the point where the neck meets the chest, directed toward the heart, with a single sweeping cut that severs both carotid arteries and both jugular veins
A correctly performed stick produces immediate, high-volume blood flow — a thin trickle indicates the vessels have not been fully severed and the cut shall be repeated immediately
Alternatively — chest stick through the brisket directly into the heart — acceptable but produces slower exsanguination than neck stick and is less preferred
Minimum bleed time: 5 minutes hanging before processing begins — blood shall cease flowing freely before the next processing step

Swine:

Heart stick — knife inserted at the point of the sternum directed toward the heart
Correct placement produces immediate high-volume blood flow from the chest cavity
Swine exsanguinate more slowly than cattle — minimum bleed time 3-4 minutes
The neck stick used for cattle is less effective in swine due to anatomical differences in vessel placement — heart stick is preferred

Small ruminants — sheep and goats:

Throat cut — a single, rapid, complete cut across the throat severing both carotid arteries and both jugular veins
Cut shall be made with a single uninterrupted motion with a surgically sharp blade — a sawing or dragging motion causes unnecessary tissue damage and is less effective
Minimum bleed time: 3 minutes hanging before processing begins

Poultry:

Following cervical dislocation or decapitation — exsanguination occurs as part of the killing action and does not require a separate step
Following CO₂ stunning — throat cut severing carotid arteries on both sides immediately after stunning
Poultry shall hang inverted by the legs for minimum 90 seconds after throat cut before scalding — inadequate bleed time before scalding produces blood-tinged meat and skin

Rabbits:

Following cervical dislocation — decapitation immediately after dislocation serves as the exsanguination step
Rabbits shall hang inverted for minimum 2 minutes after decapitation before skinning

Blood collection and management:

Blood shall be collected in designated containers where volume permits — blood is a valuable community resource for use in blood meal, black pudding, or other applications
Blood from animals failing post-mortem inspection shall be disposed of with other condemned material — it shall not enter the food or feed supply
Blood on facility floors and surfaces shall not be allowed to pool and dry — pooled blood is a significant bacterial growth medium and an attractant for pests. Continuous drainage or collection during processing is required
Blood from the slaughter area shall be disposed of in ways that do not contaminate water sources per Section 1 setback requirements

Verification of death before processing:

Before beginning any processing step after exsanguination, verify that the animal is dead — no heartbeat, no corneal reflex, no response to stimulation, complete muscular limpness
In cattle and large animals where cardiac activity may continue briefly after exsanguination, allow the full minimum bleed time before proceeding regardless of apparent cessation of blood flow
An animal that shows any sign of consciousness or life during the bleed period — rhythmic movement, vocalizing, righting attempts — has not been effectively stunned and exsanguinated. Processing shall stop, the animal shall be re-stunned immediately, and the stunning procedure reviewed before continuing

Rationale: Exsanguination serves two simultaneous purposes — it causes death following stunning, and it removes the maximum possible blood from the carcass before processing. Both purposes have direct food safety implications that make the standards above meaningful rather than procedural.
The 60-second maximum interval between stunning and sticking for large animals reflects the window during which a correctly stunned animal remains irreversibly unconscious. Captive bolt stunning produces unconsciousness by concussion and penetration of the cerebral cortex — it does not directly destroy the brain structures responsible for consciousness, and in the absence of blood loss the animal will begin to recover from stunning within 60-120 seconds. An animal that recovers consciousness during exsanguination experiences the full pain and fear of the dying process — a profound welfare failure — and simultaneously undergoes the hormonal stress response described in 4.2, producing DFD meat in the surviving muscle tissue and elevating the pathogen load in the carcass.
Blood removal matters for food safety because blood is a rich growth medium for bacteria — warm blood at body temperature with abundant nutrients supports rapid pathogen growth. Incomplete exsanguination leaves blood pooled in tissue, particularly in dependent areas of the carcass, where it creates localized warm, nutrient-rich environments for pathogen multiplication during the chilling process. This blood pooling is particularly significant in carcasses that do not chill quickly — large animals with substantial fat cover that insulates the deep muscle from the chilling environment. Thorough exsanguination reduces the volume of this growth medium and extends the effective safety window during chilling.
The sticking knife dedication requirement — the sticking knife used only for exsanguination and not for subsequent carcass work — addresses the contamination pathway created by using the same knife for the stick cut and for subsequent skinning or evisceration work. The stick cut enters the thoracic cavity and contacts the major blood vessels — it also contacts the external skin surface at the entry point. A knife used for the stick and then used for skinning carries external skin contamination into the internal carcass surfaces it subsequently contacts. Dedicating the sticking knife to exsanguination only maintains the separation between external contamination sources and internal carcass surfaces that is the foundation of slaughter hygiene.
The blood disposal requirement connects slaughter sanitation to water source protection in a direct and practical way. Blood is a high-protein, high-nutrient liquid that creates significant biochemical oxygen demand when introduced to water systems — it depletes oxygen in streams and watercourses through bacterial decomposition, harming aquatic ecosystems and potentially affecting downstream water quality. At community slaughter scale the volume of blood produced in a single session — approximately 3-4% of live weight in cattle, meaning 30-40 liters from a 1,000 kg animal — is sufficient to cause significant local environmental impact if disposed of improperly. Blood disposal pathways — land application away from water sources, rendering, composting in systems capable of handling high-nitrogen materials — shall be established before slaughter operations begin, not improvised after the fact.
The verification of death standard before processing begins addresses a failure mode that is uncommon but catastrophic when it occurs — an animal that has not been effectively killed beginning to recover consciousness during skinning or evisceration. This failure occurs most commonly when the interval between stunning and sticking is extended, when the stick cut does not sever major vessels effectively, or when an animal with unusual physiological resilience recovers from stunning faster than expected. The verification steps — no heartbeat, no corneal reflex, complete muscular limpness after full bleed time — are the practical checks that identify this situation before a recovering animal becomes a safety emergency for operators and a profound welfare failure for the animal.
In 2009 a community slaughter operation in rural Wales experienced an incident where a beef steer was inadequately stunned with a captive bolt — the operator later found the stunner cartridge was the wrong specification for the stunner model — and was not verified for unconsciousness before sticking. The sticking cut was performed on a semiconscious animal that reacted to the cut, injuring the operator and producing significant welfare distress before a second stunning attempt was successful. The incident resulted in operator injury, carcass condemnation due to stress-related quality failure, and a fundamental review of the operation's equipment verification and pre-slaughter checking protocols. All three consequences — operator injury, condemned carcass, and protocol failure — were directly traceable to skipping the pre-sticking verification step that this standard requires.

4.7 Evisceration — Timing and Gut Integrity
Standard: Evisceration — removal of the gastrointestinal tract and internal organs — shall be completed within the following time windows after slaughter and shall be performed in ways that maintain gut integrity throughout. Evisceration is the highest-risk step in the slaughter process for carcass contamination and requires the greatest technical skill and attention of any processing step.
Timing requirements:
Maximum time from slaughter to evisceration start:

Cattle and large ruminants: 45 minutes at ambient temperatures below 60°F, 30 minutes at ambient temperatures above 60°F
Swine: 30 minutes at ambient temperatures below 60°F, 20 minutes at ambient temperatures above 60°F — swine body cavities retain heat longer than ruminants due to fat insulation, accelerating bacterial growth in gut contents
Small ruminants: 30 minutes at ambient temperatures below 60°F, 20 minutes above
Poultry: evisceration shall begin within 15 minutes of slaughter — poultry body cavities are small and reach ambient temperature rapidly, but the proximity of cloaca to the evisceration site makes delay particularly risky
Rabbits: 20 minutes at ambient temperatures below 60°F, 15 minutes above

These windows represent the time during which gut contents remain at relatively stable temperature and gut integrity is easiest to maintain. Beyond these windows, gut contents begin warming relative to ambient temperature in cool conditions — accelerating pathogen growth — and gut tissue begins to relax and soften, increasing rupture risk during evisceration.
Gut integrity requirements:
Preparation before evisceration:

The evisceration knife shall be sharp — dull knives require excessive force that increases rupture risk
Operators shall be positioned to have clear visibility and control of the knife throughout the evisceration cut — evisceration performed at awkward angles or with restricted visibility is a significant rupture risk factor
The rectum shall be tied off — encircled with a ligature or placed in a plastic sleeve — before the evisceration cut begins in all species. Fecal material in the rectum and terminal colon is the highest-concentration contamination source in the carcass cavity and shall be isolated before the body cavity is opened

Evisceration technique — general principles:

The opening cut shall be made through the skin and abdominal wall only — not through the gastrointestinal tract. In cattle and large animals this requires controlled cutting with the blade oriented away from the gut contents, using fingers to create a space between abdominal wall and gut before advancing the knife
The esophagus shall be tied off or clamped at the diaphragm before the stomach and rumen are removed — esophageal contents are highly acidic and contaminating
The gastrointestinal tract shall be removed as an intact unit where possible — pulling the stomach and intestines out as a connected system rather than cutting individual segments reduces the number of cut surfaces that could release contents
Viscera shall be lowered into the gut bucket or onto an inspection cradle without contacting the carcass on their way out — viscera dragged across the inner surface of the carcass cavity transfer surface contamination directly to the meat

Gut puncture response protocol:

Any puncture, tear, or unintended incision into the gastrointestinal tract during evisceration shall be treated as a contamination event requiring immediate response
Stop the evisceration — do not continue removing viscera until contamination is managed
Identify the extent of spillage — small puncture with minimal spillage versus large tear with significant gut content release
For small punctures with minimal spillage: trim and remove contaminated tissue immediately, flush the contaminated area thoroughly with clean potable water, continue evisceration with heightened caution
For large tears with significant gut content spillage: flush the carcass cavity extensively with clean potable water, trim all visibly contaminated tissue, evaluate whether the contamination is localized or has spread throughout the body cavity. Extensive contamination of the body cavity may require condemnation of the entire carcass — a judgment that shall be made conservatively in the direction of food safety
Document all gut puncture events with the carcass identifier, extent of spillage, response taken, and disposition decision

Species-specific evisceration considerations:
Cattle:

The rumen — the first and largest stomach compartment — occupies the majority of the abdominal cavity and is the largest gut contamination risk. It shall be grasped firmly and supported from below as it is freed from attachments to prevent it from falling and rupturing
The liver, heart, and lungs — the pluck — are removed separately from the gastrointestinal tract and retained for post-mortem inspection per 4.8
The spinal cord shall be removed where practical — spinal cord is neural tissue with BSE risk considerations in cattle

Swine:

Swine evisceration follows similar principles to cattle but with smaller organ volumes
The leaf lard — the fat deposits surrounding the kidneys and lining the abdominal cavity — is a valuable product and shall be removed carefully without contaminating with gut contents
The head is typically removed before evisceration in swine — removed after in cattle

Poultry:

Evisceration of poultry requires removal of the vent and drawing of intestines, proventriculus, gizzard, liver, and heart through the vent opening or an abdominal incision
The vent cut — encircling the cloaca before drawing — is the most critical contamination control step in poultry evisceration. An incomplete vent cut that nicks the intestine or cloaca releases fecal material directly into the body cavity
The gall bladder shall be removed with the liver without rupturing — bile contamination of poultry meat produces bitter flavor that cannot be removed and requires trimming of affected tissue
Poultry lungs are firmly attached to the rib cage and require a lung scraper or fingers to remove completely — retained lungs decompose rapidly and produce off-flavors in stored poultry

Rationale: Evisceration is the step where the sterile internal environment of the carcass is most acutely at risk from the non-sterile gut contents that are being removed. Understanding why gut integrity matters requires understanding the microbiology of the gastrointestinal tract in food animals — specifically the concentration and nature of the pathogens present.
The gastrointestinal tract of a healthy food animal at slaughter carries pathogen populations at concentrations many orders of magnitude higher than any other tissue. In a healthy beef animal the rumen may contain 10⁸ to 10¹⁰ bacterial cells per gram of contents — a gram of rumen contents contains more bacteria than there are people on Earth. Many of these are harmless rumen bacteria essential to the animal's digestive function. But the lower gastrointestinal tract — where E. coli O157:H7, Salmonella, and Campylobacter concentrate — carries these pathogens at densities that make even a small gut puncture a significant contamination event. A single gram of intestinal contents from a Salmonella-shedding pig may contain 10⁵ to 10⁶ Salmonella cells — a quantity far exceeding the infectious dose.
The rectal tie-off requirement addresses the specific anatomy of contamination risk in evisceration. The rectum and terminal colon — the final segments of the gastrointestinal tract — are the most posterior structures in the body cavity and the ones the evisceration cut reaches last. They are also, in most species, the segments with the highest pathogen concentration. An unsecured rectum that is inadvertently opened during the initial evisceration cut or that releases fecal material as gut tension changes during evisceration contaminates the entire body cavity surface with high-concentration pathogen material. The tie-off is a simple, inexpensive intervention — a piece of twine, a rubber band, or a purpose-made sleeve — that eliminates one of the most common significant contamination events in small-scale slaughter.
The esophageal tie-off addresses the other end of the gastrointestinal system. The rumen and stomach contents of a ruminant are highly acidic — rumen pH is maintained between 5.5 and 6.5 by the fermentation process. When the rumen is released from its attachments during evisceration, the esophagus connecting it to the throat becomes a route by which stomach contents can flow back toward the throat cavity and contaminate the neck and chest tissues. Tying or clamping the esophagus before separating the rumen prevents this flow.
The timing requirements connect to a physical process that is easy to understand intuitively but whose safety implications are frequently underestimated. After slaughter, the gut contents of an animal remain at or near body temperature — approximately 100-102°F in cattle — while the carcass begins cooling from the outside in. This means that immediately after slaughter, the interior of the gut is at an ideal temperature for rapid bacterial growth. The pathogen populations in the gut are not static — they are growing. The longer evisceration is delayed, the larger the pathogen population that must be managed if gut integrity is breached. At ambient temperatures above 60°F, where the carcass surface also remains warm and bacterial growth on exposed carcass surfaces begins immediately, the timing requirement is compressed because external surface contamination is also proceeding more rapidly.
In 2001 a study of small-scale beef slaughter operations in New Zealand compared carcass contamination rates between operations that eviscerated within 30 minutes of slaughter and those that delayed evisceration for more than 60 minutes. The delayed-evisceration group showed surface bacterial counts 3-5 times higher than the timely-evisceration group, with higher rates of E. coli and Salmonella detection. The difference was not attributable to differences in gut puncture rates or operator technique — the delay itself, by allowing gut pathogen populations to grow before removal, produced the elevated contamination outcome. Timely evisceration is not a convenience standard — it is a direct pathogen load reduction measure.

4.8 Post-Mortem Inspection and Condemnation Criteria
Standard: Every carcass and set of viscera shall be inspected after slaughter by a designated community meat inspection officer before the carcass enters the chilling and processing chain. Post-mortem inspection is the second and final opportunity — after ante-mortem assessment per 4.1 — to identify disease, contamination, or abnormality that makes the carcass or parts of it unsafe for community food use. No carcass shall proceed to chilling and butchering without completed post-mortem inspection and documented disposition.
Community meat inspection officer:

The community meat inspection officer shall have completed training in post-mortem inspection appropriate to the species being slaughtered — this training shall be documented and periodically refreshed
The inspection officer shall not also serve as the primary slaughter operator for the animal being inspected — inspection requires independence from the processing pressure that influences operators to pass borderline carcasses
The inspection officer's condemnation decisions are final — no community member, including those with authority in other community functions, may override a condemnation decision on safety grounds
All inspection findings shall be documented with the carcass identifier, findings observed, disposition decision, and inspector identification

Inspection sequence:
Viscera inspection — performed before viscera are discarded:

Viscera shall be retained alongside the carcass and inspected simultaneously — the relationship between visceral findings and carcass findings is diagnostically important and cannot be reconstructed if viscera are discarded before inspection
Liver: inspect for abscesses, flukes, white spots indicating parasitic cysts, cirrhosis, discoloration, and unusual texture. A liver with localized abscesses may be condemned while the carcass is passed — a liver with generalized infection or multiple abscesses indicates systemic disease requiring carcass evaluation
Lungs: inspect for consolidation — areas of firm, airless tissue indicating pneumonia — pleuritis, abscesses, and parasitic nodules. Localized lung pathology is common and does not automatically condemn the carcass — widespread pneumonia or evidence of systemic spread does
Heart: inspect for pericarditis — inflammation of the heart sac — endocarditis, and abscesses. Heart lesions may indicate systemic bacterial infection — evaluate carcass lymph nodes carefully when heart lesions are present
Kidneys: inspect for discoloration, cysts, nephritis, and unusual texture. Pale, swollen kidneys may indicate systemic toxicity or infection
Intestines: inspect for hemorrhage, unusual contents, and parasites. Heavy parasitic burdens in the intestines — visible worms, cysts — warrant examination of muscle tissue for larval migration
Spleen: inspect for enlargement, discoloration, and unusual texture. Enlarged, dark spleen in cattle may indicate anthrax — a rare but critical finding requiring immediate cessation of all processing and containment of the carcass and all materials that have contacted it

Carcass inspection:

Lymph nodes: the primary diagnostic tool of post-mortem inspection. Lymph nodes filter blood and tissue fluid and accumulate evidence of infection, inflammation, and abnormality throughout the body. Key lymph nodes for each species shall be incised and examined:

Cattle: prescapular, prefemoral, popliteal, iliac, mesenteric, and submaxillary lymph nodes at minimum
Swine: mandibular, superficial inguinal, iliac, and mesenteric lymph nodes at minimum
Small ruminants: prescapular, prefemoral, and mesenteric lymph nodes at minimum
Poultry: inspection of visceral surfaces and body cavity without lymph node incision — poultry lymphatic anatomy differs from mammals


Lymph node findings indicating systemic disease: generalized enlargement across multiple nodes, caseous — cheese-like — material within nodes indicating tuberculosis, hemorrhage within nodes, necrotic tissue
Muscle tissue: inspect cut surfaces for unusual color, texture, or odor. Pale, soft muscle may indicate nutritional myopathy. Dark, firm muscle indicates stress — DFD — and warrants evaluation for pathogen load. Any muscle with unusual odor shall be condemned
Bone and joint surfaces: inspect for arthritis, abscess, and unusual joint fluid. Joints with purulent material indicate systemic or local infection
Skin and subcutaneous tissue: inspect for abscesses, edema, jaundice — yellow discoloration — and unusual lesions. Generalized jaundice indicates liver failure or hemolytic disease and requires full carcass condemnation
Body cavities: inspect pleural and peritoneal surfaces for adhesions, discoloration, and unusual deposits. Fibrinous deposits on cavity surfaces indicate previous or current pleuritis or peritonitis

Specific conditions and dispositions:
Conditions requiring full carcass condemnation — do not use any part:

Generalized septicemia — evidence of systemic bacterial infection throughout the carcass
Generalized tuberculosis — caseous lymph nodes in multiple body regions
Anthrax suspected — sudden death, dark unclotted blood from body orifices, massively enlarged spleen. Do not open the carcass further — anthrax spores are extremely persistent in the environment and the carcass must be disposed of without further processing
Generalized pyemia — multiple abscesses distributed throughout the carcass
Generalized jaundice from any cause
Sarcocystosis — widespread presence of sarcocysts in muscle tissue, giving it a striated or white-streaked appearance
Emaciation — a carcass with no usable muscle mass
Any carcass with odor of putrefaction, medication, or chemical contamination throughout
Any carcass where cause of death is unknown or suspected to be toxin ingestion

Conditions permitting partial use after trimming:

Localized abscess with no evidence of systemic spread — remove abscess and a margin of surrounding tissue, pass remainder
Localized pneumonia or pleuritis with normal lymph nodes and no systemic signs — condemn lungs, pass carcass
Localized liver pathology — flukes, isolated abscesses — with normal remainder of viscera and carcass
Contamination from gut puncture limited to accessible surface areas that can be completely trimmed
Localized arthritis in one joint with no systemic signs — condemn affected joint, pass carcass

Conditions requiring additional evaluation:

Lymph nodes enlarged in one body region only — may indicate local infection. Evaluate all tissues in that region carefully before deciding disposition
Unusual muscle color or texture without other findings — evaluate for nutritional myopathy, breed characteristics, or medication effects
Parasitic findings — evaluate species and distribution. Cysticercus bovis — beef tapeworm cysts — in cattle muscle requires either condemnation or freezing at -10°F for 10 days to kill cysts before use

Poultry-specific inspection:

Poultry inspection evaluates body condition, skin condition, body cavity surfaces, and viscera
Condemn any bird with: airsacculitis — cloudy or thickened air sacs, septicemia — red discoloration of tissues and bloody fluid in body cavity, tumors, emaciation, or significant contamination from gut spillage during evisceration
Localized airsacculitis affecting only one air sac with otherwise normal carcass — trim affected tissue, pass carcass with reduced storage life expectation
Green liver in poultry — bile staining from ruptured gall bladder — trim affected tissue and adjacent meat, pass remainder

Rationale: Post-mortem inspection is the point at which the community's investment in ante-mortem assessment, humane handling, and careful slaughter either pays off or is found to have been insufficient. Its value is that it catches what ante-mortem assessment misses — disease processes that were not externally apparent in the living animal, contamination events that occurred during slaughter, and pathological findings that become visible only after the carcass is opened.
The lymph node examination is the most diagnostically powerful component of post-mortem inspection and the component most absent from informal slaughter practice. Lymph nodes are the body's filtration system — they trap and respond to bacteria, viruses, parasites, and abnormal cells circulating through the lymphatic system. A lymph node that is enlarged, discolored, or contains caseous or necrotic material is telling the inspector that something has been present in the tissues draining to that node — something that required an immune response. The pattern of lymph node involvement across multiple body regions tells the inspector whether a problem is local — a single affected area with localized infection — or systemic — a process that has distributed through the entire animal.
Tuberculosis warrants specific attention because it is a zoonotic disease — transmissible from animals to humans — that has been substantially controlled in domestic livestock in developed countries through decades of testing and slaughter programs, but that persists in wildlife reservoirs including deer, badgers, and wild boar that community-slaughtered game animals may carry. Mycobacterium bovis — bovine tuberculosis — produces characteristic caseous lymph nodes that are unmistakable to a trained inspector. A community meat inspection program that does not examine lymph nodes will not detect tuberculosis. A community that consumes meat from a tuberculosis-positive carcass is exposing its members to a serious respiratory pathogen that is increasingly drug-resistant.
The anthrax protocol — cessation of all processing and containment without further opening of the carcass — is included because anthrax, while rare, is present in soil in many regions of the world and causes sudden death in livestock that have ingested spores. Bacillus anthracis spores are extraordinarily persistent — they can survive in soil for decades — and an opened anthrax carcass releases spores into the environment that remain infectious for years. The characteristic finding — dark, unclotted blood from body orifices in an animal that died suddenly — shall be treated as anthrax until proven otherwise. A community that opens an anthrax carcass in its slaughter facility has contaminated that facility and potentially the surrounding soil in ways that are extremely difficult to remediate.
The independence requirement for the inspection officer from the slaughter operator addresses a systemic pressure that affects all inspection systems — the pressure to pass borderline carcasses rather than condemn them. In a commercial system this pressure comes from financial incentives. In a community system it comes from social relationships, from the investment of community members in the animal being slaughtered, and from the practical reality that condemnation means loss. An inspection officer who is also the slaughter operator, or who has a close personal relationship with the animal's owner, faces social and emotional pressure that affects borderline judgment calls. Structural independence — a designated inspection officer who did not do the slaughter and has no personal stake in the disposition decision — does not eliminate this pressure but reduces it to the point where professional judgment can function.
In 1999 a community slaughter cooperative in Scotland slaughtered a beef cow that had been showing mild neurological signs — occasional stumbling and head pressing — that the farmer attributed to a recent mineral deficiency. The animal passed ante-mortem assessment under pressure from the farmer who believed the signs were nutritional rather than neurological. Post-mortem inspection found no gross abnormality — BSE produces no post-mortem findings detectable by standard inspection. The cow was processed and distributed. Subsequent investigation following the diagnosis of BSE in another animal from the same herd resulted in recall of all meat from the cooperative's production run covering that period. The incident illustrated both the limitations of post-mortem inspection for BSE — it cannot detect the disease — and the importance of ante-mortem assessment conservatism for neurological signs. A categorical prohibition on slaughtering animals with neurological signs of any cause, applied without exception, would have prevented the event. The pressure to attribute the signs to a non-neurological cause — mineral deficiency — and proceed was the failure point.

4.9 Chilling and Temperature Control After Slaughter
Standard: All carcasses shall be chilled to an internal temperature of 41°F (5°C) or below within the following time windows after slaughter. Chilling is the primary pathogen control measure after evisceration — the pathogen management that hygiene during slaughter begins, chilling completes by inhibiting the growth of organisms that were introduced despite best practices.
Chilling time requirements by species and carcass weight:
Cattle — whole carcasses:

Carcasses under 300 lbs hanging weight: internal temperature of 41°F within 24 hours
Carcasses 300-600 lbs hanging weight: internal temperature of 41°F within 36 hours
Carcasses over 600 lbs hanging weight: internal temperature of 41°F within 48 hours
Internal temperature measured at the deepest point of the hindquarter — the point farthest from the chilling environment and slowest to cool

Swine — whole carcasses:

Carcasses under 150 lbs hanging weight: internal temperature of 41°F within 18 hours
Carcasses over 150 lbs hanging weight: internal temperature of 41°F within 24 hours
Swine fat cover insulates muscle from chilling — thick-fatted carcasses may require additional chilling time beyond these standards and shall be monitored

Small ruminants — sheep, goats, lambs:

All weights: internal temperature of 41°F within 12 hours
Small ruminant carcasses are small enough to chill rapidly under most conditions — failure to meet the 12-hour standard indicates inadequate chilling capacity

Poultry:

Internal temperature of 40°F within 4 hours of slaughter
Poultry shall be chilled in ice water at 34°F or below — air chilling is acceptable for small numbers where temperature can be maintained but is less efficient for community-scale processing
Ice water chilling: birds shall be fully submerged with ice maintained continuously to keep water at or below 40°F — warm water that has absorbed heat from multiple birds without ice replenishment is not a chilling medium
Poultry shall not remain in ice water beyond 8 hours — extended immersion causes water uptake that dilutes flavor and creates a false appearance of carcass weight

Rabbits:

Internal temperature of 41°F within 4 hours
Rabbits chill rapidly due to small carcass size — failure to meet the 4-hour standard under normal conditions indicates a significant chilling deficiency

Chilling methods:
Walk-in refrigeration — preferred for large animals:

Carcasses hung on overhead rail with minimum 3 inches clearance between carcasses to allow air circulation
Cooler temperature maintained at 34-38°F — below 32°F risks surface freezing that forms an insulating ice layer slowing internal chilling
Air circulation — fans — significantly accelerates chilling rate. Still-air coolers of the same temperature cool significantly more slowly than forced-air coolers
Carcass surface shall not contact walls, other carcasses, or any surface that restricts air circulation

Ice and cold water chilling — appropriate for small animals and poultry:

Ice-to-water ratio shall maintain water temperature at or below 40°F throughout the chilling period
Ice replenished continuously as it melts — water temperature shall be verified with a thermometer, not estimated by ice presence
Containers used for ice chilling shall be food-grade and sanitized before use
Small ruminant and rabbit carcasses may be chilled in ice water — ensure complete submersion

Improvised chilling in cold weather:

Where mechanical refrigeration is unavailable, ambient cold air chilling is acceptable under specific conditions:

Ambient temperature must be verified at or below 40°F and stable throughout the chilling period
Carcasses must be protected from freezing if ambient temperature drops below 28°F — surface freezing that forms before internal temperature reaches 41°F traps heat and slows chilling
Carcasses must be protected from insects, vermin, and birds — a carcass hanging in cold air that is accessible to flies or rodents is being contaminated during chilling
Ambient chilling is not acceptable above 40°F ambient temperature regardless of season or region
Nighttime ambient chilling followed by warmer daytime temperatures is not acceptable — a carcass that chills overnight and then warms during the day has cycled through temperatures that allow pathogen growth



Temperature monitoring:

Internal carcass temperature shall be measured at the deepest point of the hindquarter using a calibrated probe thermometer at the following intervals:

Large animals: at 12 hours and at the end of the required chilling period
Small animals: at 4 hours and at the end of the required chilling period
Poultry: at 2 hours and at the end of the required chilling period


Temperature readings shall be documented with carcass identifier and time
Any carcass not reaching target temperature within the required window shall be evaluated — if target temperature can be reached by extending chilling with additional cooling measures, document and proceed. If target temperature cannot be reached within a reasonable extension period, condemn the affected carcass

Rationale: Chilling is where the cumulative effect of all prior slaughter hygiene is either preserved or lost. A carcass that was handled and eviscerated with exemplary hygiene but chilled inadequately will have higher pathogen loads at butchering than a carcass with minor hygiene deficiencies that was chilled rapidly and correctly — because the pathogens introduced during slaughter grow at body temperature and grow slowly or not at all below 41°F. The chilling step is not the conclusion of the slaughter process — it is an active pathogen management step with its own critical control points.
The chilling time requirements reflect the thermal physics of large irregular objects cooling in a refrigerated environment. Heat moves from the center of a carcass to its surface by conduction through muscle and fat tissue, then from the surface to the surrounding air by convection. Fat is a poor thermal conductor — approximately four times less conductive than lean muscle — which is why thick-fatted carcasses require longer chilling times than lean carcasses of equivalent weight, and why the measurement point is specified as the deepest point of the hindquarter rather than the surface temperature, which reaches target temperature hours before the interior does.
The surface freezing prohibition addresses a counterintuitive phenomenon — rapid surface chilling to below-freezing temperatures can actually slow the overall chilling process rather than accelerating it. When carcass surface temperature drops below 28°F, a thin ice layer forms at the surface. This ice layer has lower thermal conductivity than unfrozen tissue — it insulates the interior from the chilling environment rather than facilitating heat transfer. A carcass with a frozen surface and a warm interior is not chilling efficiently and may not reach internal temperature targets within required windows despite appearing well-chilled externally. The optimal chilling environment maintains temperatures just above freezing — 34-38°F — where heat transfer from the interior to the surface is maximized without surface freezing.
Air circulation in refrigerated spaces deserves specific emphasis because the performance difference between still-air and forced-air chilling is substantial and frequently underestimated. In still air, a thin layer of warm air accumulates at the carcass surface — this boundary layer acts as an insulating barrier between the carcass surface and the cooler air of the space. A fan that moves air past the carcass surface continuously disrupts this boundary layer, maintaining a temperature gradient between the carcass surface and the moving air that drives heat transfer. Studies comparing chilling rates in still-air and forced-air coolers at the same temperature have found forced-air coolers achieving target internal temperatures 30-40% faster than still-air coolers. In a community without unlimited refrigeration capacity, this difference in chilling efficiency directly affects the number of carcasses that can be safely processed in a given chilling cycle.
The ambient temperature chilling standard is included because mechanical refrigeration will not always be available in community contexts — particularly in transitional or crisis situations where this document is most relevant. Cold weather ambient chilling is a legitimate and historically proven method — it is how meat was preserved before mechanical refrigeration existed. The conditions under which it is safe are specific and non-negotiable: verified ambient temperature below 40°F, protection from predators and insects, and continuous monitoring to prevent the freeze-thaw cycling that disrupts the chilling process and allows pathogen growth during warm periods. A community that relies on ambient chilling needs designated people responsible for monitoring conditions throughout the chilling period, not an assumption that cold weather means safe chilling.
In 2004 a community abattoir in rural Ireland processed six beef carcasses in a walk-in cooler that subsequently experienced a refrigeration failure overnight. Morning inspection found the cooler at 58°F — the internal temperature of the largest carcasses had not yet reached 41°F when the cooler failed and had risen to 55°F overnight. The carcasses had spent approximately 10 hours in the temperature danger zone during and after the failure. The inspection officer condemned all six carcasses based on the time-temperature exposure history — they appeared normal and would have been indistinguishable from safe carcasses by sensory inspection. The loss represented significant community resources. The decision was correct. A cooler failure during the chilling period is a silent event that produces carcasses with elevated pathogen loads that no subsequent processing step can remediate — the growth that occurred during the warm period has already happened. Temperature monitoring that detected the failure at 2 hours rather than 12 would have allowed intervention — additional ice, emergency transfer to alternative cold storage — that might have salvaged some or all of the carcasses. The monitoring requirement exists for exactly this scenario.

4.10 Hide, Feather, and Skin Removal
Standard: Hide, feather, and skin removal shall be performed in ways that prevent contamination of the exposed carcass surface from the external surface of the animal. The external surface of every food animal — hide, feathers, skin, and hooves — carries the highest concentration of environmental pathogens of any part of the animal. Every technique in this standard is designed to maintain separation between this contaminated external surface and the sterile muscle tissue beneath it.
Cattle and large ruminant hide removal:
Pre-skinning preparation:

The hide surface shall be pre-cleaned in areas where the opening cuts will be made — wiping or washing away visible fecal contamination, mud, and debris from the brisket, legs, and perineal area before the knife contacts the hide
Hooves shall be removed at the carpus and tarsus — the knee and hock joints — before skinning begins. Hooves carry significant soil and fecal contamination and their removal eliminates a major contamination source before the carcass surface is exposed
The hide around the bung — the anus and rectum — shall be cut free and tied off before general skinning begins, for the same reasons as the rectal tie-off in evisceration

Skinning technique:

Opening cuts through the hide shall be made from the inside out — the knife blade angled upward through the hide rather than downward toward the carcass. Cutting from inside out prevents the knife from being drawn across the carcass surface and avoids the hide hair and external contamination contacting the cut edge
Each section of hide shall be held away from the carcass as it is freed — a hide that falls back against the exposed carcass surface transfers contamination directly to the meat
The knife shall not contact the hide surface and then contact the carcass surface without sanitizing between — every contact with the external hide surface contaminates the knife with external pathogens. Knife rotation per 4.4 applies with particular force during skinning
Skinning shall proceed systematically from less-contaminated areas — brisket and sides — to more-contaminated areas — back, perineum, and legs. Contamination introduced from a heavily contaminated area shall not be carried back to areas already skinned
The hide shall be removed completely before evisceration begins wherever possible — skinning after evisceration increases the risk of hide contact with exposed body cavity surfaces

Mechanical hide pulling:

Where hide pulling equipment is available — a mechanical puller that removes the hide rapidly by applying tension — the technique reduces hide-to-carcass contact compared to hand skinning
The initial opening cuts and freeing of the hide from legs, brisket, and perineum shall still be performed by hand before mechanical pulling begins
Mechanical pulling shall be controlled — excessive speed causes the hide to snap back against the carcass surface. A steady, controlled pull that keeps the hide under tension and moving away from the carcass surface is correct technique

Swine — scalding and dehairing:
Scalding:

Swine are typically not skinned but are scalded to loosen hair for dehairing — the skin is retained on the carcass
Scalding water temperature: 140-145°F — below 140°F is insufficient to loosen hair roots. Above 145°F begins to cook the skin surface, making subsequent dehairing more difficult and damaging skin integrity
Scalding time: 3-5 minutes at correct temperature — longer immersion at correct temperature loosens hair effectively. Shorter immersion at higher temperature damages skin
Scalding tank water shall be changed or treated regularly — repeated use without replacement accumulates fecal material, hair, and pathogens from multiple animals. Scalding water that is visibly dark or carries significant debris shall be changed before the next animal
Scalding tank temperature shall be verified with a thermometer before each animal — temperature drifts during use as cold animals cool the water

Dehairing:

Dehairing by scraping — using a bell scraper or equivalent tool — shall remove all hair from the carcass surface
Areas that resist dehairing — head, ears, and feet — shall receive additional scraping attention or be singeed with a propane torch to remove remaining hair and surface contamination
After dehairing and singeing the carcass surface shall be washed thoroughly with clean potable water before evisceration
Any area of the carcass where skin integrity has been damaged during scalding or dehairing — tears, deep scrape marks — shall be trimmed before chilling

Poultry — scalding and defeathering:
Scalding:

Scalding temperature for poultry: 125-130°F for soft scald — retains yellow skin color, preferred for whole bird presentation. 140-145°F for hard scald — removes outer skin layer with feathers, produces white skin appearance
Scalding time: 30-90 seconds depending on temperature and bird size — verify feather release before removing from scalder
Poultry scalding water carries significant fecal contamination from vent areas during immersion — scalder water shall be maintained at temperature and changed regularly. A scalder that has processed many birds without water change has accumulated fecal pathogens that are being deposited on the skin of subsequent birds

Defeathering:

Mechanical pickers — rubber-fingered rotating drums — are preferred for community-scale poultry processing. Hand plucking is acceptable for small numbers
Defeathering shall be complete before evisceration — retained feathers near the vent area are the primary contamination risk during poultry evisceration
Pin feathers — immature feathers that did not release during defeathering — shall be removed by hand or knife before evisceration
After defeathering the carcass shall be washed thoroughly with clean potable water before evisceration begins

Small ruminants — sheep and goats:
Skinning approach:

Small ruminants are skinned using the same inside-out knife technique as cattle but at smaller scale
Sheep fleece carries significant lanolin, vegetation, and fecal contamination — pre-cleaning the fleece at opening cut locations is particularly important for heavily contaminated animals
The pelt of small ruminants is typically removed by fisting — separating the hide from the underlying tissue using the fist rather than a knife where the tissue planes allow. Fisting reduces knife contact with hide and carcass simultaneously and lowers contamination transfer risk
Areas where fisting is not possible — legs, head, and tight adherence areas — revert to inside-out knife technique

Rabbits:

Rabbit skin is removed by making an initial cut around the hock of the hind legs and pulling the skin down and off the carcass in a single motion — the pelt separates cleanly from the underlying tissue with minimal knife contact
The skin shall be pulled away from the carcass continuously — not allowed to fall back against exposed tissue
Head removal before or after skinning depending on community practice — head shall be inspected for lymph node examination per 4.8 before disposal

Post-removal surface inspection:

After hide, skin, or feather removal and before evisceration, the exposed carcass surface shall be inspected for contamination — areas of hide contact, fecal smearing, or visible contamination
Contaminated areas shall be trimmed — removal of a thin layer of surface tissue carrying contamination — rather than washed where possible. Washing distributes surface contamination over a wider area while trimming removes it
Where washing is necessary — contamination too extensive or diffuse for trimming — use clean potable water under pressure and follow with inspection to verify contamination removal

Rationale: Hide removal is simultaneously the most contamination-risk-laden step in the slaughter process and the step where operator technique has the greatest influence on the final pathogen load of the carcass. The external surface of a food animal at slaughter carries a microbial load that reflects the entire environment the animal has lived in — soil bacteria, fecal pathogens, environmental organisms — at concentrations that make even brief hide-to-carcass contact a significant contamination event. Understanding the mechanism makes the techniques meaningful rather than procedural.
The inside-out cutting technique is the most important single technical principle in hide removal and the one most consistently compromised by operators trained informally or under time pressure. The intuitive approach to cutting through a hide is to draw the knife across the hide surface — the same motion used to cut any material. In hide removal this motion drags the contaminated hide surface along the knife blade and across the emerging cut edge, depositing hide-surface contamination directly onto the carcass. Cutting from inside out — inserting the knife tip through the hide from the underside and pushing outward — keeps the blade moving away from both the hide surface and the carcass, producing a clean cut edge that has not contacted the external hide surface.
The scalding water quality issue in swine processing is one of the least-discussed contamination pathways in small-scale slaughter and one of the most significant. A scalding tank used for multiple animals without water change accumulates fecal material released from vent areas during immersion — the warm water creates a fecal soup that is then deposited on the skin surface of every subsequent animal. Research comparing bacterial contamination of swine carcasses after scalding in clean versus dirty scalding water found significantly higher Salmonella and E. coli rates on carcasses processed in water that had not been changed between animals. The scalding step that is intended to facilitate clean hide removal becomes a contamination step when the scalding medium is itself contaminated.
The poultry scalder presents the same issue at higher intensity because the vent area — the highest concentration fecal contamination site on a bird — is immersed directly in the scalding water with no prior cleaning or containment. Poultry scalder water after processing multiple birds has been shown in multiple studies to carry Campylobacter at concentrations sufficient to produce cross-contamination between birds — birds that entered the scalder with low Campylobacter loads emerge with higher loads from contaminated scalder water. At community scale where scalder water may be used for many birds in a session, the contamination amplification effect is significant. Scalder water temperature at or above 140°F provides some pathogen reduction — but temperature alone does not compensate for gross fecal contamination accumulation.
The fisting technique for small ruminants is included because it represents one of the genuine technical improvements that understanding the contamination mechanism produces — recognizing that knife contact between hide and carcass is the contamination pathway leads directly to the technique that minimizes knife contact. Fisting — using the fist to push between hide and underlying tissue — requires no knife and therefore eliminates knife-mediated contamination transfer in areas where the tissue planes allow separation. Traditional slaughter practitioners who developed this technique understood the contamination problem intuitively without the microbiology — the technique works because it addresses the mechanism.
In 2008 a study of lamb carcass contamination at small abattoirs in Australia compared contamination rates between operators using inside-out knife technique and those using outside-in technique for hide removal. Operators using correct inside-out technique produced carcasses with surface E. coli counts averaging 1.2 log CFU/cm² — a low contamination level consistent with good hygiene. Operators using outside-in technique produced carcasses averaging 3.1 log CFU/cm² — nearly 100 times higher pathogen load. The only variable was knife technique during hide removal. Both groups used the same equipment, the same facilities, and equivalent overall hygiene practices. The contamination difference was entirely attributable to whether the knife dragged the hide surface across the emerging carcass surface or not.

4.11 Carcass Washing
Standard: All carcasses shall receive a final wash with clean potable water after evisceration and before entering the chilling environment. Carcass washing is a surface decontamination step — it reduces the microbial load introduced during slaughter and removes visible contamination that trimming alone did not address. It is not a substitute for correct slaughter hygiene but a final reduction step that complements it.
Water requirements:

Water used for carcass washing shall meet the standards in Section 1 — potable, tested, and treated where necessary
Water temperature for final carcass wash: 95-105°F — warm water reduces surface bacterial load more effectively than cold water by dissolving fat that harbors bacteria and improving physical removal of contamination. Water above 115°F begins to denature surface proteins, producing a cooked appearance on the carcass surface that is aesthetically unacceptable and functionally counterproductive
Water pressure shall be sufficient to physically dislodge contamination — a low-pressure dribble moves contamination around the surface rather than removing it. A high-pressure spray that penetrates surface tissue is excessive and drives contamination into the meat rather than removing it. A firm, directed spray at working pressure is correct
Water shall be applied with a spray wand or nozzle that allows directed application — a hose without a nozzle produces uncontrolled flow that is less effective and wastes water

Washing sequence:

Wash the interior of the body cavity first — spray all internal surfaces including the diaphragm, ribs, and pelvic cavity thoroughly
Wash the exterior carcass surface systematically — beginning at the top of the carcass and working downward so that contamination flows away from already-washed surfaces
Pay particular attention to areas of highest contamination risk — the brisket, the neck, the perineal area, and any areas where visible contamination was identified during inspection
Final rinse shall cover all surfaces — no area of the carcass shall be missed

Visible contamination management:

Areas of visible fecal, ingesta, or bile contamination identified during washing shall be trimmed before washing where possible — washing visible contamination spreads it over a wider area and reduces rather than eliminates the contamination. Trim first, wash after
Where trimming is not practical — contamination in a thin diffuse layer over a large area — washing with directed pressure may be the primary decontamination method. Document these areas and increase chilling monitoring for affected carcasses
After washing, re-inspect all surfaces in good lighting — contamination that is invisible on a wet surface becomes visible as the surface begins to dry. A final visual inspection after washing identifies areas requiring additional trimming or washing

Organic acid wash — where available:

A final rinse with an organic acid solution — lactic acid at 2-5% concentration or acetic acid at 2-3% concentration — applied after the water wash and before chilling provides additional pathogen reduction beyond water washing alone
Organic acid rinse lowers surface pH, creating conditions that inhibit pathogen growth during chilling
Organic acid rinse is applied as a final step after water washing and inspection — it is not a substitute for water washing and shall not be applied to visibly contaminated surfaces
Organic acid concentration shall be verified before use — solutions too dilute are ineffective, solutions too concentrated cause surface discoloration and protein denaturation
Organic acid solutions shall be food-grade — not industrial acids of uncertain concentration or purity

Poultry-specific washing:

Poultry carcasses shall be washed inside and out after evisceration
The body cavity of poultry is small — ensure the spray reaches all internal surfaces including the area around the lungs where retained lung fragments accumulate
Inside washing of poultry body cavities can be accomplished with a purpose-made poultry washer that inserts into the body cavity, or by directing a spray wand into the cavity opening
Poultry shall be washed individually — placing multiple birds in a common wash tank after evisceration creates cross-contamination between birds

Documentation:

Water temperature at time of carcass washing shall be logged
Any carcasses requiring additional trimming after washing shall be documented with the reason for additional trimming
Organic acid concentration when used shall be verified and logged

Rationale: Carcass washing with potable water at warm temperature achieves pathogen reduction through three simultaneous mechanisms — physical removal of contamination by the force of the water, dissolution of fat-associated bacteria by warm water that cold water cannot achieve, and dilution of remaining surface contamination below the threshold that would otherwise concentrate during chilling. Understanding these mechanisms explains both why the washing standard is specified as it is and why washing alone is insufficient without the hygiene practices that precede it.
The temperature specification — 95-105°F — reflects research specifically examining the relationship between wash water temperature and pathogen removal from carcass surfaces. A 1997 USDA study comparing pathogen removal rates on beef carcasses washed with water at 50°F, 75°F, 95°F, and 115°F found that carcasses washed at 95°F had significantly lower residual E. coli and Salmonella counts than those washed at lower temperatures, with no further benefit at 115°F and surface quality degradation above that temperature. The mechanism is the liquefaction of surface fat — fat solidifies at cool temperatures and traps bacteria against the carcass surface. Warm water melts this fat layer, releasing the trapped bacteria into the water flow and allowing them to be physically washed away.
The trim-before-wash principle contradicts the intuitive approach of washing visible contamination off a carcass — the same intuition that leads people to rinse raw poultry under running water at home before cooking, a practice that spreads contamination across the sink and surrounding surfaces rather than removing it. Washing distributes contamination — the water that contacts a fecal smear carries E. coli cells to every surface the water subsequently contacts as it flows down the carcass. Trimming removes the contamination source before washing, eliminating the source of the contamination plume. Where trimming is not practical, washing must be systematic — applying water from clean areas toward contaminated areas, never flowing contaminated water back over clean surfaces.
The organic acid wash represents an additional decontamination step that has been validated in research and adopted in commercial processing as an intervention that achieves meaningful additional pathogen reduction beyond water washing alone. Lactic acid at 2-5% concentration — equivalent to a mild vinegar solution — reduces surface E. coli and Salmonella counts by 1-2 log units on beef carcasses in research settings, representing a 90-99% reduction in remaining pathogen load. This reduction is not eliminated by subsequent chilling, which slows but does not kill pathogens — it is a permanent reduction that compounds the pathogen control achieved by hygiene during slaughter and rapid chilling. In a community food system without chemical antimicrobial interventions at the retail level, reducing pathogen load at the carcass level provides a safety margin that carries through to the final consumer.
The poultry individual washing requirement addresses a contamination mode specific to poultry processing — the practice of placing eviscerated birds in a common trough or tank of wash water. A common wash tank for multiple birds after evisceration is a cross-contamination mechanism — Campylobacter and Salmonella from one bird's body cavity contaminate the wash water and are deposited on every subsequent bird. Research on commercial poultry processing found that inside-outside individual washing with clean water reduced carcass contamination rates significantly compared to common tank washing. The additional time required for individual washing of poultry is the cost of avoiding a contamination step disguised as a sanitation step.
In 2011 a community poultry processing event in Vermont processed 200 broilers over a single day using a common cold water wash tank after evisceration. Post-processing microbiological testing — conducted as part of a food safety training exercise — found Campylobacter on 78% of finished carcasses. A repeat processing event the following season using individual warm-water washing produced Campylobacter on 31% of carcasses — a significant reduction attributable entirely to the washing method change. Neither result is ideal — Campylobacter elimination from poultry requires additional interventions beyond washing — but the reduction demonstrated by changing from common tank to individual washing illustrated the practical consequence of the cross-contamination mechanism in a directly measurable way.

4.12 Aging — Dry and Wet Protocols
Standard: Aging of carcasses and primal cuts shall be conducted under controlled temperature and hygiene conditions that allow enzymatic improvement of meat quality without creating conditions for pathogen growth or spoilage. Aging is not appropriate for all species or all cuts — the standards below specify where aging adds value and where it introduces risk without benefit.
Species and cut suitability for aging:
Appropriate for aging:

Beef — whole carcasses or primal cuts. Beef benefits substantially from aging due to the activity of endogenous proteolytic enzymes that break down connective tissue and improve tenderness. The large muscle mass and fat cover of beef primals provides a protective environment for controlled aging
Lamb and mutton — whole carcasses or primal cuts. Benefits from short aging periods. Older animals benefit more than young lamb
Venison and large game — benefits from aging, particularly in older animals with more developed connective tissue

Not appropriate for aging — process promptly after chilling:

Poultry — poultry has thin skin, limited fat cover, and a body cavity geometry that creates conditions for rapid spoilage during aging. Poultry shall be processed or frozen within 3-5 days of slaughter
Pork — pork fat oxidizes rapidly during aging, producing rancid flavors. Pork shall be processed or frozen within 3-7 days of slaughter
Rabbits — small carcass size and limited fat cover make rabbits susceptible to rapid moisture loss and spoilage during aging. Process within 2-3 days of slaughter
Any carcass with hide-off surface contamination that was not fully addressed during washing and trimming — contaminated surfaces deteriorate rapidly during aging and can produce spoilage that penetrates into the meat

Dry aging:
Definition and mechanism:

Dry aging is the process of holding unwrapped carcasses or primal cuts in a controlled environment — specific temperature, humidity, and airflow — for extended periods to develop flavor and tenderness through enzymatic activity and moisture evaporation
Enzymatic activity — primarily calpain and cathepsin proteases — breaks down myofibrillar proteins and connective tissue, improving tenderness progressively over the aging period
Moisture evaporation concentrates flavor compounds and produces the characteristic intensified beef flavor of dry-aged meat
A dry crust — the pellicle — forms on the surface of dry-aging meat. This pellicle is protective — it limits pathogen access to underlying meat and creates a barrier that slows further moisture loss. The pellicle is trimmed before the meat is cut and not consumed

Dry aging requirements:

Temperature: 34-38°F — above 40°F aging becomes spoilage. Below 32°F enzymatic activity ceases and aging does not occur
Relative humidity: 75-85% — below 75% produces excessive moisture loss and accelerated pellicle formation that limits aging. Above 85% provides conditions for mold growth on the carcass surface
Airflow: consistent gentle airflow across carcass surfaces — still air allows moisture accumulation at the carcass surface that promotes spoilage rather than drying. A fan moving air through the aging space is required
Light: darkness or low light — UV light degrades fat and accelerates rancidity development on exposed surfaces

Aging periods:

Minimum aging period for quality improvement: 14 days for beef
Optimal aging period for most beef cuts: 21-28 days
Extended aging beyond 28 days: produces additional flavor development in ideal conditions. Requires verification that pellicle is intact and mold growth is controlled. Only experienced practitioners shall extend aging beyond 28 days
Maximum recommended aging period for community use without specialized monitoring: 35 days
Game animals: 7-14 days is typically adequate. Extended aging of game beyond 14 days requires careful monitoring

Mold management during dry aging:

White mold growth on the pellicle surface during dry aging is expected and normal — white molds including Penicillium species are part of the dry aging environment and contribute to flavor development. They are removed with the pellicle before cutting
Green, black, or pink mold growth indicates contamination beyond the normal aging flora — trim the affected area with a generous margin. If mold penetrates beyond the pellicle into the underlying meat, condemn the affected section
Any mold growth that covers more than 20% of the carcass surface indicates inadequate temperature or humidity control — evaluate and correct the aging environment before proceeding

Wet aging:
Definition and mechanism:

Wet aging is the process of holding vacuum-sealed primal cuts under refrigeration for extended periods — the same enzymatic activity as dry aging occurs within the sealed package without moisture loss
Wet aging produces tenderness improvement equivalent to dry aging without the flavor concentration and intensification that evaporation produces — wet-aged meat has a cleaner, less complex flavor profile than dry-aged meat
Wet aging requires vacuum packaging equipment or airtight packaging that excludes oxygen — partial vacuum or loosely wrapped cuts age poorly and develop off-flavors from oxidation

Wet aging requirements:

Temperature: 34-38°F throughout the aging period — no temperature cycling
Packaging integrity: vacuum seal shall be verified before aging begins and inspected regularly throughout the aging period. Any package that has lost vacuum — feels soft rather than firm when pressed — shall be opened and the cut evaluated. If the cut smells normal and shows no spoilage signs, repackage and continue. If spoilage odor is present, condemn
Packaging material: food-grade vacuum bags rated for the intended aging period — standard household bags are not rated for extended wet aging and may develop micro-perforations that admit oxygen during the aging period

Wet aging periods:

Minimum: 7 days for perceptible tenderness improvement
Optimal: 14-21 days for most beef cuts
Maximum recommended: 35 days — beyond this period quality improvement plateaus and off-flavor risk increases

Documentation:

Date of slaughter, date aging began, and target completion date shall be recorded for each aging carcass or primal
Temperature of aging environment shall be logged daily
Mold observations in dry aging shall be documented
Package integrity checks in wet aging shall be documented

Rationale: Aging standards are included in this document because aging is both a quality practice and a safety-relevant practice — the conditions that optimize aging are also the conditions that, if not met, create pathogen growth opportunities in meat that has already been chilled to safety. A beef carcass correctly chilled to 38°F and placed in a dry aging environment that drifts to 45°F is no longer aging — it is spoiling, with pathogen growth proceeding at a rate that will produce unsafe meat within days rather than weeks.
The enzymatic mechanism of aging is worth understanding because it explains both why aging works and why the temperature range is so precisely specified. The enzymes responsible for tenderness improvement — calpains and cathepsins — are most active between 32°F and 40°F. Above 40°F their activity increases but spoilage bacteria also become active, and the race between enzymatic improvement and bacterial spoilage is won by the bacteria. Below 32°F the enzymes are largely inactive and aging does not occur. The window between 34°F and 38°F is where enzymatic activity proceeds at a useful rate and bacterial growth is suppressed to below-significant levels.
The humidity specification is more precise than most community aging environments achieve — few informal aging spaces have humidity control. In practice, humidity in a working walk-in cooler depends on the cooler's refrigeration system, the volume of product in the cooler, and whether the door is opened frequently. A cooler that is too dry — below 75% humidity — produces excessive surface moisture loss and rapid pellicle formation that limits further aging. A cooler that is too humid — above 85% — allows moisture accumulation on carcass surfaces that supports mold growth beyond the controlled white molds expected in the aging environment. Community aging operations without humidity monitoring shall check carcass surfaces daily and adjust based on observed conditions — excessive drying versus excess surface moisture.
The species suitability distinctions reflect genuine differences in the benefit-to-risk ratio of aging for different animals. Poultry and pork are not aged not because aging fails to improve their texture and flavor — it does — but because the risk profile of aging these species is unfavorable. Poultry skin and the poultry body cavity geometry create anaerobic pockets that develop off-flavors rapidly during aging. Pork fat contains a higher proportion of polyunsaturated fatty acids than beef fat — these oxidize at lower temperatures and faster rates, producing rancidity in aging pork that overwhelms any quality benefit from enzymatic activity. The no-aging rule for these species is not a conservative preference — it is a reflection of the unfavorable chemistry of aging them.
The pellicle formation mechanism explains why the surface crust that develops on dry-aging meat is protective rather than a sign of spoilage — a common misunderstanding among people unfamiliar with dry aging. The pellicle is a desiccated surface layer in which water activity has dropped below the threshold for most pathogen and spoilage organism growth. It forms a physical and chemical barrier that limits access to the underlying meat. Trimming the pellicle before cutting removes this barrier along with any surface contamination or mold that accumulated during aging — the underlying meat, protected throughout the aging period, is exposed as fresh, safe, high-quality product.
In 2006 a community cold storage cooperative in Minnesota maintained a dry aging space for community-processed deer during the hunting season. A refrigeration system malfunction in the second week of aging allowed the temperature to rise to 48°F for an estimated 36 hours before the malfunction was detected. By the time the temperature was discovered, several carcasses had developed surface spoilage — slimy texture and off-odors at the surface — while appearing normal in the interior. The cooperative's response was to condemn the obviously spoiled carcasses and trim and evaluate the borderline ones based on odor and texture after pellicle removal. The incident established a temperature monitoring protocol — daily temperature logs — that was subsequently maintained throughout each aging season. The malfunction that caused the loss had been a simple thermostat failure — detectable with daily temperature checks. The loss of multiple deer carcasses representing significant community hunting investment drove the monitoring protocol adoption more effectively than any abstract safety argument would have.

4.13 Butchering and Fabrication
Standard: The conversion of chilled carcasses into retail cuts, ground meat, and packaged products shall be conducted under temperature and hygiene conditions that maintain the pathogen reduction achieved during slaughter and chilling. Fabrication is where the controlled environment of the carcass chilling space is exchanged for the higher-temperature, higher-contact environment of cutting and processing — every standard in this section is designed to minimize the safety regression that this transition represents.
Temperature management during fabrication:
Carcass and cut temperature:

Carcasses shall not be removed from the chilling environment until fabrication begins — no pre-warming period before cutting
Internal carcass temperature shall be verified at or below 41°F before fabrication begins — a carcass that has not fully chilled shall remain in the chilling environment until it reaches target temperature
Primal cuts removed from the carcass shall be returned to the chilling environment within 30 minutes if not immediately fabricated into retail cuts — primals warming on a work table during a long fabrication session accumulate danger zone time that compounds with subsequent processing and distribution time
Ground meat shall be kept below 41°F throughout grinding — grinding generates heat through mechanical action. Partially frozen meat grinds more efficiently and stays cooler during grinding than fully thawed meat. Meat that warms above 50°F during grinding shall be chilled before further processing

Work environment temperature:

Fabrication shall be conducted in an environment as cool as practical — ideally below 50°F
Where a dedicated cool fabrication room is available it shall be maintained at 45-50°F during cutting
Where fabrication is conducted in ambient temperature environments — outdoor cutting, room temperature facilities — individual cuts shall be returned to refrigeration after no more than 30 minutes of work time and the cumulative danger zone exposure tracked per 2.1 principles adapted to meat fabrication

Equipment and surface sanitation:
Cutting surfaces:

All cutting surfaces shall be cleaned and sanitized before fabrication begins and between species where multiple species are processed in a single session
Cutting surfaces shall be smooth, non-porous, and free of deep scoring — scored cutting surfaces harbor bacteria that sanitizing cannot reach, per the same principle as scored cutting boards in 2.12
Wooden cutting surfaces are not acceptable for meat fabrication — the same wood prohibitions as 2.12 apply with greater force in meat cutting where pathogen loads and cutting volumes are higher

Knives and tools:

Knife rotation with sanitizing per 4.4 applies throughout fabrication
Bone saws shall be cleaned and sanitized before use and between animals — bone saw blades accumulate bone marrow and tissue fragments in the teeth that become significant bacterial growth sites
Meat grinder components shall be disassembled, cleaned, and sanitized before use and after each species — retained meat particles in grinder plates and worms are a primary E. coli cross-contamination pathway between batches

Surface contamination management during fabrication:
Trim versus wash:

The trim-before-wash principle from 4.11 applies throughout fabrication — surface contamination on individual cuts shall be trimmed rather than washed where possible
Visible contamination on cut surfaces — bone dust from sawing, surface discoloration from bruising, any fecal or bile contamination identified after chilling — shall be trimmed with a clean cut that removes the contamination and a margin of surrounding tissue
Bone dust from sawing shall be rinsed from cut surfaces with cold potable water — bone dust is not a significant pathogen source but contains marrow and protein that support bacterial growth if left on cut surfaces

Lymph node removal:

Large lymph nodes in retail cuts — the prescapular node in chuck cuts, the prefemoral in round cuts — shall be removed during fabrication
Lymph nodes concentrate pathogens filtered from surrounding tissue during the animal's life and are not desirable in retail cuts both for safety and quality reasons
Lymph node location shall be learned for each species being fabricated — they are consistent in their anatomical position and identifiable by their firm, slightly rubbery texture distinct from surrounding muscle

Ground meat specific standards:
Source material:

Ground meat shall be produced from trim that has been kept below 41°F throughout fabrication — warm trim that has accumulated significant danger zone time shall not be ground and stored, as grinding dramatically increases the surface area available for bacterial growth
Trim from multiple animals shall not be combined into a single ground batch without documentation of each animal's identity in the batch — the traceback requirements for ground meat require knowing which animals contributed to a batch
Trim containing visible contamination — fecal staining, bile, abscesses, or unusual tissue — shall be removed before grinding. What cannot safely be sold as a whole cut cannot be made safe by grinding

Grinding process:

Meat shall be chilled to below 36°F before grinding where possible — partially frozen meat grinds cleanly and maintains temperature better through the grinding process than fully thawed meat
Grinder plates and worms shall be chilled before use — metal components at room temperature warm the meat that contacts them during grinding
Ground meat temperature shall be verified after grinding — if above 45°F, chill before packaging
Ground meat shall be packaged and chilled or frozen within 30 minutes of grinding

Fat content:

Fat content of ground meat shall be estimated and documented where community members rely on this information for cooking — lean-to-fat ratio affects both cooking performance and nutritional content
Excessively fatty trim — fat above 30% — shall be separated and rendered or used for specific fat-dependent applications rather than ground into standard ground meat

Packaging and labeling:
Packaging materials:

Packaging materials shall be food-grade and appropriate to the intended storage method — freezer storage requires packaging rated for freezer temperatures, refrigerator storage requires packaging that maintains moisture
Vacuum packaging reduces oxidation and extends storage life significantly — where vacuum packaging equipment is available it shall be used for cuts intended for extended refrigerator storage or aging
Standard plastic wrap and freezer paper are acceptable for cuts intended for freezing within 24 hours of packaging

Labeling requirements:

All packaged meat products shall be labeled before leaving the fabrication area with:

Species and cut name — specific enough to distinguish between similar cuts
Animal identifier — traceable to the ante-mortem and post-mortem inspection records
Date of slaughter
Date of packaging
Weight where scale is available
Intended storage method — refrigerate or freeze


Ground meat labels shall additionally include the lot identifier linking the ground batch to the contributing animal records

Safe storage life guidance — refrigerated at 34-38°F:

Whole muscle beef and lamb cuts: 3-5 days
Whole muscle pork cuts: 3-5 days
Ground beef and lamb: 1-2 days
Ground pork: 1-2 days
Whole poultry: 1-3 days
Poultry parts: 1-2 days
Ground poultry: 1 day
Whole rabbit: 1-3 days
Venison and large game cuts: 3-5 days

Rationale: Fabrication is the stage at which meat transitions from a controlled single-unit environment — the hanging carcass in a monitored cooler — to a divided, handled, and distributed product. Each division of the carcass into smaller cuts increases the ratio of surface area to volume, exposing more meat surface to the cutting environment and increasing the pathogen introduction opportunity. Each handling event — moving a cut from cooler to table, from table to grinder, from grinder to packaging — is a contamination opportunity. The fabrication standards exist to minimize contamination introduction at each step while acknowledging that some level of additional contamination is unavoidable in any cutting environment.
The ground meat standards require extended attention because ground meat is categorically more hazardous than whole muscle cuts from the same animal, and this difference is not widely understood. A whole muscle beef steak contaminated on its surface carries pathogens on the outside — cooking the surface to safe temperature destroys them. The same beef ground into hamburger distributes the surface contamination throughout the entire mass — the pathogen that was on the outside of the steak is now in the center of the burger patty, protected from the surface heat that reaches safe temperature first. This is why ground beef requires a higher cooking temperature than whole muscle steaks — 155°F versus 145°F — and why the standards for ground meat throughout this section are more stringent than for whole muscle cuts.
The trim traceback requirement — not combining trim from multiple animals without documentation — exists because ground meat from multiple animals creates a product whose pathogen contamination is potentially distributed across the entire batch regardless of which contributing animal was the source. In 1993 the Jack in the Box E. coli O157:H7 outbreak was caused by ground beef patties from a single contaminated batch that had incorporated trim from multiple animals including at least one that carried E. coli O157:H7. The pathogen was distributed throughout thousands of pounds of ground meat that was subsequently sold across multiple states. In a community context, combining trim from multiple animals without documentation prevents traceback to the source animal if a contamination event is later identified — the entire ground batch must be treated as potentially affected rather than the specific contributing animal's products.
The lymph node removal standard addresses a food safety and quality practice that is standard in professional butchering and absent from most informal meat cutting. Lymph nodes in retail cuts are not just aesthetically undesirable — they concentrate the pathogens and inflammatory products that the lymphatic system filtered from surrounding tissue during the animal's life. A lymph node from an animal with subclinical mastitis, subclinical pneumonia, or any other localized infection concentrates the pathogens responsible for that infection. Consuming lymph node tissue from such an animal introduces those pathogens directly. Removing lymph nodes during fabrication eliminates this pathway entirely with a simple cut that takes seconds per node.
The partially frozen grinding recommendation is included because it is a technique that simultaneously improves product quality and reduces temperature-related safety risks during grinding — two benefits from one practice. Partially frozen meat — at 28-32°F, firm but not solid — grinds cleanly through the plate holes without smearing, producing a ground product with defined particle structure rather than the paste-like texture of warm ground meat. The clean cut rather than smear of partially frozen grinding also produces less heat through mechanical friction — a secondary benefit that keeps the product cooler through the grinding process. Community butchering operations that chill their trim to partially frozen before grinding produce better ground meat and manage the thermal risks of grinding more effectively than those that grind fully thawed trim.
In 2014 a community meat processing cooperative in Montana conducted a self-assessment of their fabrication practices that included surface swabbing of cutting surfaces, knives, and ground beef at multiple points in their processing session. Early session samples — first hour of processing — showed low bacterial counts consistent with good sanitation practices. Mid-session samples — three hours in — showed significantly elevated counts on cutting surfaces that had been in use without intermediate sanitizing. End-of-session ground beef showed E. coli counts three times higher than beginning-of-session samples from the same batch composition — the accumulation of bacterial load on equipment surfaces during the session was transferring to the ground meat. The assessment led to implementation of hourly surface sanitizing breaks during fabrication sessions, which subsequent testing showed reduced end-of-session contamination to near-beginning-of-session levels. The self-assessment methodology — surface swabbing and microbiological testing — is presented as a model for community food operations seeking to verify that their sanitation practices are achieving the intended results rather than assuming they are.

4.14 Waste and Byproduct Management
Standard: All waste and byproducts generated during animal slaughter and butchering shall be managed through designated pathways that prevent environmental contamination, pathogen spread, pest attraction, and water source compromise. Waste management in slaughter operations produces volumes and pathogen concentrations significantly higher than food service waste — the standards reflect this difference in scale and risk.
Waste categories and designated pathways:
Condemned material:

Any material condemned during ante-mortem or post-mortem inspection — entire carcasses, organs, or portions — shall be handled separately from all other waste streams
Condemned material shall never enter the human food chain, animal feed chain, or any rendering stream where the rendered product will contact food animals
Condemned material shall be disposed of through burial at depth — minimum 4 feet below surface in an area at least 300 feet from any water source — incineration, or designated condemned material rendering where available
Condemned material containing suspected anthrax shall not be buried or rendered — incineration is the only acceptable disposal method for anthrax-suspect material. Anthrax spores survive normal burial conditions and can persist in soil for decades, creating a persistent environmental hazard

Edible offal and byproducts:

Liver, heart, kidneys, tongue, lungs approved for consumption, tripe, sweetbreads, and other edible offal shall be handled as food products from the point of post-mortem inspection approval
Edible offal chills more rapidly than muscle meat due to smaller mass but also spoils more rapidly — edible offal shall be chilled to 41°F within 2 hours of harvest and used or frozen within 24 hours of slaughter
Edible offal shall be packaged and labeled with species, organ type, animal identifier, date of slaughter, and use-by date before leaving the processing area
Blood collected for food use — black pudding, blood sausage, blood soup — shall be collected in clean food-grade containers, chilled immediately, and used within 24 hours or frozen

Inedible offal and non-condemned waste:

Intestines and stomach contents from healthy animals not intended for tripe or other food use — the largest volume waste product in large animal slaughter
Hides, feathers, and hair from processing
Hooves and horns
Non-condemned trim fat and bone

Designated pathways for inedible offal:

Rendering — conversion to animal feed, soap, or industrial products. Rendering requires adequate heat treatment to destroy pathogens in the rendered material. Community-scale rendering shall achieve minimum 250°F throughout the material for minimum 20 minutes
Composting in systems designed for high-nitrogen animal material — enclosed or hot composting systems that achieve minimum 131°F throughout for 3 consecutive days, turned to ensure all material reaches target temperature. Open pile composting of slaughter waste is not acceptable — pathogen destruction is unreliable and pest attraction is significant
Burial — minimum 4 feet depth, minimum 300 feet from any water source, in areas not prone to flooding that could redistribute buried material
Biodigestion where community biodigester infrastructure exists — slaughter waste is a high-value input to anaerobic digestion producing biogas and digested slurry

Blood management:

Blood not collected for food use is the highest-volume liquid waste in slaughter operations — approximately 3-4% of live weight in cattle
Blood shall be collected in dedicated containers during slaughter and not allowed to pool freely in the slaughter area — pooled blood supports rapid pathogen growth and attracts pests
Blood disposal pathways: land application at agronomic rates — blood is a high-nitrogen fertilizer and shall be applied at rates that do not exceed crop uptake capacity and create runoff risk. Minimum 300 feet from water sources, do not apply to land draining directly to surface water
Blood shall not be disposed of in septic systems, sewage systems not designed for high-strength waste, or surface water — blood creates significant biochemical oxygen demand in water systems

Hide and feather management:

Fresh hides carry significant pathogen load and shall be handled with the same hygiene precautions as raw meat during the period immediately after removal
Hides intended for tanning shall be salted immediately after removal — salt curing at a ratio of approximately 1 pound of salt per pound of hide applied evenly across the flesh side, folded flesh-to-flesh, and stored in a cool area until tanning begins
Hides not destined for tanning shall be disposed of through rendering or burial
Feathers from poultry processing shall be collected in designated containers and composted, rendered, or buried — feathers decompose slowly and create persistent pest attraction if left in open waste areas

Fat and grease management:

Trim fat from butchering is a valuable community resource — for lard rendering, tallow for soap making, or high-energy animal feed
Fat intended for rendering shall be chilled immediately and rendered within 48 hours — fat stored at room temperature begins to develop rancidity rapidly
Rendering of fat shall achieve minimum 250°F throughout to destroy pathogens and produce a shelf-stable rendered product
Grease from cleaning operations — contaminated fat mixed with water and cleaning chemicals — shall not be rendered for food use and shall be disposed of through the waste pathways above
Grease traps on drains in the slaughter facility shall be cleaned regularly — a schedule established at the facility design stage and maintained throughout the operating season

Bone management:

Bones from fabrication are a valuable community resource — for bone broth, marrow extraction, dog feeding, or bone meal production
Bones intended for human consumption uses — broth, marrow — shall be handled as food products, chilled, and used or frozen within 48 hours of fabrication
Bones for animal feeding shall be from healthy animals only — bones from condemned carcasses shall not enter the animal feed chain
Bone meal production from community-scale rendering shall achieve adequate heat treatment to destroy pathogens — minimum 250°F throughout

Wastewater management:

Slaughter and processing operations generate significant wastewater — blood, gut contents diluted with wash water, and cleaning water
Slaughter wastewater shall not be discharged directly to surface water, storm drains, or water sources
Wastewater shall be managed through: land application in designated areas at agronomic rates, minimum 300 feet from water sources. Constructed wetlands or settling ponds where community infrastructure supports them. Municipal wastewater systems where available and where the treatment system is designed for high-strength waste
The slaughter facility shall have a designated wastewater management plan established before operations begin — improvised disposal of slaughter wastewater after the fact creates contamination events that are difficult to remediate

Pest management in waste areas:

Slaughter waste areas shall be maintained under the same pest exclusion standards as food preparation areas per 2.15, with particular attention to:
Covered, pest-resistant containers for all waste awaiting disposal — open waste containers in slaughter areas are a primary pest attraction point for the entire facility
Waste removal frequency that prevents accumulation beyond container capacity
Burial sites and composting areas located at distances from the slaughter facility that reduce pest pressure on the facility — pests attracted to disposal areas shall not have direct access routes to the processing facility

Documentation:

Volume and disposition of each waste category shall be documented for each slaughter session
Condemned material disposal shall be documented with quantity, reason for condemnation, and disposal method
Wastewater management and blood disposal shall be documented with location and volume of land application

Rationale: Slaughter waste management represents the interface between the community food system and the broader community environment — the point at which pathogens, nutrients, and biological material generated by food production either are managed safely or become environmental and public health hazards. The scale and nature of slaughter waste is qualitatively different from food service waste — the pathogen concentrations in gut contents, blood, and condemned material are orders of magnitude higher than in food waste, and the volumes generated in a single slaughter session can overwhelm informal disposal approaches.
The condemned material handling standard addresses a failure mode that is common in small-scale slaughter operations — condemned material entering informal waste streams that are not designed for pathogen containment. A condemned liver with evidence of systemic infection, buried shallowly in garden soil or left in an open compost pile, introduces concentrated pathogens into the community environment. These pathogens reach surface water through runoff, attract wildlife that subsequently contact the community food environment, and persist in soil for weeks to months depending on species. The 4-foot burial depth and 300-foot setback from water sources reflect the minimum conditions under which soil filtration and distance provide adequate pathogen containment — the same principles as the well setback standards in 1.2.
The anthrax incineration requirement warrants specific attention because anthrax is the one zoonotic pathogen in community slaughter contexts where standard disposal methods are categorically inadequate. Bacillus anthracis forms spores of extraordinary environmental persistence — the Gruinard Island anthrax contamination from World War II weapons testing remained in soil for 40 years before decontamination. Burying an anthrax carcass does not contain the spores — they persist and can resurface through frost heave, erosion, and animal digging. Rendering does not destroy anthrax spores — they survive temperatures below 250°F that destroy most other pathogens. Only incineration at temperatures above 1000°F reliably destroys anthrax spores. A community that buries an anthrax-suspect carcass has created a persistent environmental hazard at the burial site.
Blood management is the waste stream where the volume and pathogen concentration combine to create the most significant environmental risk per unit of material. A single beef carcass generates approximately 30-40 liters of blood at slaughter — a quantity that, if released untreated into a small stream, depletes oxygen through biochemical demand at a rate that can produce anaerobic conditions harmful to aquatic life within hours. At community slaughter scale processing multiple animals in a session, the combined blood volume is significant. Land application at agronomic rates — applying blood as a nitrogen fertilizer at the rate that crops can utilize it without producing runoff — is the most practical disposal pathway that simultaneously prevents environmental harm and recovers the fertilizer value of blood.
The edible offal chilling urgency reflects the biology of these organs — liver, kidneys, and other solid organs have higher water activity, more abundant vascular channels for pathogen distribution, and higher initial bacterial loads than muscle meat. They chill more quickly than large muscle masses due to smaller size but deteriorate more rapidly under equivalent storage conditions because of their composition. A liver at 41°F lasts 24 hours at acceptable quality. The same liver at 50°F lasts hours. The tight time window for edible offal — chill within 2 hours, use or freeze within 24 hours — reflects this rapid quality deterioration and the pathogen growth that accompanies it.
The rendering temperature standard — 250°F throughout for minimum 20 minutes — is specified because rendering at lower temperatures destroys most pathogens but may not destroy C. botulinum spores present in animal tissue. Community-scale rendering that produces tallow or lard for human consumption or lard for food preparation shall achieve spore destruction temperatures — the same standard as pressure canning of low-acid foods. Rendered products that do not achieve spore destruction temperatures are not shelf-stable in sealed containers — they shall be used promptly or refrigerated.
In 1988 a community farming cooperative in New Zealand disposed of slaughter waste — intestines, blood, and condemned organs — in an open trench 50 feet from the farm's surface water supply. Heavy rainfall three weeks later caused runoff from the waste trench to enter the water supply. Testing found the supply contaminated with E. coli, Salmonella, and Campylobacter at concentrations far exceeding safe thresholds. Forty-three community members were sickened before the source was identified and the water supply treated. The disposal site — 50 feet from the water source rather than the 300 feet specified in this standard — was the single point of failure. The waste had been managed in what appeared to be a contained manner until an environmental event — rainfall — connected the waste site to the water supply. The setback distance exists precisely to provide the buffer that prevents this connection even under adverse conditions.
