Structural Integrity Standards
1.1 How Structures Fail — The Physics of Structural Collapse
Standard: This standard exists as a foundational rule because structural failure modes are not random — they follow predictable physical patterns that a community member who understands them can recognize before failure occurs and can design against using whatever materials are available. A community member who understands why structures fail makes better construction decisions than one who knows only that certain dimensions and materials are required.
The fundamental mechanisms of structural failure:
Overload:

Every structural element has a capacity — the maximum force it can resist before failing
Failure occurs when applied load exceeds capacity
The load that causes failure may be a single extreme event — a heavy snow load, an earthquake, a wind storm — or it may be the cumulative addition of loads over time — a floor that was adequate for one family becomes inadequate when a second family moves in and adds furniture, equipment, and occupants
Overload failure is the most straightforward failure mode — it is prevented by ensuring that structural elements have adequate capacity for all loads they will be asked to carry, with a margin for uncertainty

Buckling:

Slender structural elements loaded in compression fail not by crushing but by buckling — bending sideways under compressive load
A long, slender column carries far less compressive load than a short, stocky column of the same cross-section because the long column buckles before it crushes
Buckling is the failure mode of: tall, slender wall sections, long, unbraced compression members in roofs and floors, and thin-web elements in built-up structural members
The critical factor in buckling resistance is the ratio of length to cross-section — the slenderness ratio. Reducing length (through intermediate bracing) or increasing cross-section dramatically improves buckling resistance
In folk construction, long unbraced wall sections and roof members are the most common buckling risk — a wall that is 10 feet tall with no lateral support buckles under far less load than the same wall with intermediate bracing

Connection failure:

The connection between structural elements is almost always the weakest point in a structural system
A beam may be strong enough to carry its load, but if the connection at its end fails the beam falls regardless of its own strength
Connection failures are the most common structural failure mode in real-world construction — they are the failure mode most frequently observed in post-earthquake and post-hurricane damage surveys
Connection failure occurs at: the joint between a roof rafter and a wall, the anchor between a wall and its foundation, the fasteners joining wall sheathing to framing, the connection between floor joists and their supporting beams
In folk construction where connection hardware is unavailable, connection design using lashing, joinery, and geometric interlocking becomes the critical structural skill

Shear failure:

Shear is a sliding force — it acts parallel to a surface rather than perpendicular to it
Shear failure occurs when a structural element is forced to slide along a plane — a bolted connection shearing through the bolt, a wall sliding off its foundation, a floor diaphragm distorting under lateral load
The most common shear failure at community scale is wall racking — a wall that is strong vertically but has no resistance to horizontal forces deforms in a parallelogram shape under wind or seismic load, shearing the connections at the top and bottom of the wall and collapsing

Progressive collapse:

Progressive collapse occurs when the failure of one structural element removes support from adjacent elements, which then fail, which removes support from further elements, and so on
A single column failure that causes a floor to collapse, which loads the floor below beyond its capacity, which then collapses, which loads the next floor — this progressive sequence is progressive collapse
Progressive collapse is the most catastrophic failure mode because a localized initial failure becomes a total structural failure
It is prevented by structural redundancy — multiple load paths so that if one element fails the load it was carrying is redistributed to other elements rather than causing progressive overload

Fatigue:

Repeated loading below the failure threshold progressively weakens structural elements
A connection that can survive a single application of a given load may fail after thousands of cycles of that load — the material accumulates microscopic damage with each cycle until fracture occurs
Fatigue is relevant to: roof structures subject to repeated wind loading, floors subject to dynamic loading from machinery or heavy foot traffic, and connections subject to repeated thermal expansion and contraction
In folk construction, the connections that are repeatedly loaded by wind are the most common fatigue failure sites

Rationale: The connection failure emphasis is the most important take-away from this section and the one most consistently validated by structural failure investigations. The 1981 Hyatt Regency walkway collapse killed 114 people not because the walkway beams were inadequate but because a connection design change during construction reduced the connection capacity by half. The 1995 Sampoong department store collapse in Seoul killed 502 people not because the structural members were undersized but because connections were inadequate and columns were added and removed during construction without structural analysis. Post-earthquake damage surveys consistently find that structures with good connection detailing survive with minimal damage while adjacent structures of equivalent or better member size collapse because their connections failed first.
The progressive collapse mechanism is included because it explains why the most catastrophic structural failures — the ones that kill many people simultaneously — are qualitatively different from ordinary structural distress. A structure that sags, cracks, and shows distress over time gives warning. A structure that experiences progressive collapse gives almost no warning — the initial failure triggers the cascade, and the entire structure may collapse in seconds. Progressive collapse in multifamily buildings is the mechanism that turns a localized structural failure into a mass casualty event. The structural redundancy principle — multiple load paths — is the primary prevention strategy and is achievable even in folk construction through simple planning decisions.
The buckling concept is included because it explains why the dimensions of structural elements are not intuitive — why a column that feels solid and strong can fail at loads far below what its material strength would suggest. A community member who understands buckling understands why a long, slender post braced at its midpoint carries far more load than the same post unbraced — and can apply that understanding to any structural element in any construction type without needing to look up a table.

1.2 Loads — Dead, Live, Wind, Snow, and Seismic
Standard: Every structural element in a building must be designed to carry the loads applied to it. Load estimation is the starting point for any structural decision — determining whether a proposed element is adequate, whether an existing element is overstressed, or whether a modification will create a problem. This standard provides practical load estimation methods for the construction types and occupancy levels relevant to community building.
Dead load — the weight of the structure itself:
Dead load is the permanent weight of structural elements, finishes, and fixed equipment — everything that remains in place permanently.
Typical dead loads:

Light wood frame floor with wood subfloor and finish: 10-15 pounds per square foot (psf)
Heavy timber floor with thick plank decking: 15-25 psf
Concrete slab floor — 4 inches thick: 50 psf
Light wood frame roof with asphalt shingles: 10-15 psf
Metal roofing on light framing: 8-12 psf
Clay tile roof: 25-35 psf
Earthen (sod) roof: 80-120 psf — the heaviest common roof type, requiring significantly heavier structural support than any other roof type
Light wood frame wall: 8-12 psf of wall area
Masonry wall — 8-inch solid brick: 80-100 psf of wall area
Adobe wall — 12-inch thick: 90-110 psf of wall area

Dead load estimation for folk construction:

Weigh a representative sample of the material — a square foot section — to estimate the dead load per square foot
Multiply by the area supported to get total dead load on a given element
When in doubt, estimate high — underestimating dead load is the more dangerous error

Live load — the weight of occupants, furniture, and movable equipment:
Live load varies with occupancy type and is applied to floors and roofs.
Residential live loads:

Sleeping rooms: 30 psf
Living areas and corridors: 40 psf
Stairs: 40 psf
Storage areas: 125 psf minimum — storage areas are frequently the most heavily loaded spaces in a structure and are frequently underestimated

Community building live loads:

Assembly areas — markets, meeting halls: 100 psf — a crowd of people standing is one of the heaviest live loads a structure can experience
Community kitchens: 60-100 psf depending on equipment
Medical facilities: 40-60 psf

Live load distribution:

Live load is not always uniformly distributed — a heavy piece of equipment or a concentrated group of people creates a point load far exceeding the average live load
Point loads from heavy equipment shall be analyzed separately — the floor element directly under the equipment may be carrying many times the average live load

Snow load:

Snow accumulates on roofs and can reach very high loads — wet snow at 20 psf per foot of depth is common in many climates
Ground snow load — the reference value for roof snow load design — varies significantly by location and elevation
Practical estimation: observe the maximum historical snow depth in the community's location and multiply by 12-20 psf per foot of depth for wet snow, 5-10 psf for dry snow
Roof snow load is typically 70-80% of ground snow load for sloped roofs — snow slides off sloped roofs partially
Flat roofs retain full snow load and also accumulate drifted snow from wind — flat roofs in snow climates shall be designed for 1.5 times the ground snow load minimum
Roof snow load shall be included in every structural analysis for communities in climates with regular snowfall

Wind load:

Wind exerts pressure on windward surfaces and suction on leeward surfaces — both forces must be considered
Basic wind pressure: approximately 25 psf at 100 mph wind speed, 55 psf at 150 mph
Wind load on a structure is not simply pressure on the windward wall — it includes uplift on roof surfaces, suction on leeward walls, and internal pressure through openings
Roof uplift is frequently the governing wind load for low-rise structures — the roof acts like an airplane wing and attempts to lift off the walls. Wind uplift on a roof can exceed 40 psf in high-wind regions
The most important wind load design decision for folk construction is the roof-to-wall connection — this connection resists the uplift force trying to separate the roof from the walls

Seismic load:

Earthquakes impose horizontal accelerations on structures — the ground moves and the structure, due to its inertia, tends to stay in place. The result is a horizontal force proportional to the structure's weight
Seismic force estimation: in moderate seismic zones, assume a horizontal force of 10-20% of the structure's total weight. In high seismic zones, 20-30% or higher
Seismic loads act in any horizontal direction — structural resistance to lateral loads must be provided in both principal directions of the structure
Seismic load is the primary concern for masonry and earthen construction — these materials are strong in compression but brittle in tension, making them vulnerable to the tensile forces generated by seismic loading

Load combinations:

Structural elements are rarely subjected to only one load type — they carry dead load constantly, live load when occupied, and wind or snow loads during weather events
The critical load combination must be evaluated: dead plus live plus snow, dead plus live plus wind, dead plus wind uplift (which may reduce the downward load and create net uplift)
For folk construction without formal structural analysis: size structural elements for the maximum expected combination of loads with a safety factor of at least 2 — if the element can carry twice the maximum expected load, it has adequate capacity for the loads it will actually experience

Rationale: The storage area live load — 125 psf — is emphasized because it is the most consistently underestimated live load in community construction and the one most frequently responsible for floor failures in converted structures. A community that converts a barn loft, a commercial warehouse space, or an attic into a grain storage area is applying loads that may be 5-10 times the live load the floor was designed for. A grain bin on a floor — even a relatively small bin — can easily apply 500-1000 pounds per square foot to the floor area directly below it. Storage area overloading is a slow failure mode — the floor sags progressively before failing — but the collapse when it comes is sudden and can be catastrophic if the space below is occupied.
The earthen roof dead load — 80-120 psf — is included because earthen roofs are traditional in many folk construction traditions and are genuinely excellent roofs for thermal mass and longevity. Their structural implication — that the supporting structure must carry 6-8 times the dead load of a metal or shingle roof — is frequently not accounted for in folk construction. An earthen roof on a structure designed for light roofing will fail progressively as the earthen layer is built up, often without any visible warning until a sudden collapse event. The structural support for an earthen roof must be designed specifically for that load — it cannot be a modified version of a light roof structure.
The wind uplift emphasis is included because it is the least intuitively understood structural load and the most consistently responsible for roof failures in wind events. Building owners and builders think of wind as a horizontal force pushing against walls — this is correct but it is not the primary structural failure mechanism in most wind events. The primary mechanism is uplift — the Bernoulli effect as wind accelerates over the roof surface creates a low-pressure zone above the roof that attempts to lift the roof off the walls. The roof-to-wall connection that resists this uplift is the critical element, and it is the connection most frequently under-designed in folk construction where the intuitive design focus is on keeping the roof up rather than on keeping it down.

1.3 The Load Path Concept
Standard: Every load applied to a structure must travel through a continuous chain of structural elements from its point of application to the foundation and ultimately to the ground. This chain is the load path. A structural system is only as strong as its weakest link in the load path — a strong beam connected to an inadequate wall, connected to an inadequate foundation, provides no more structural capacity than the weakest element in the chain. Understanding load paths enables a community member to evaluate any structural system by tracing the path from load to ground and identifying where the chain is weakest.
Tracing a load path — vertical loads:
A person standing on the second floor of a building:

Their weight is applied to the floor surface
The floor surface transfers load to floor joists — the beams spanning between supports
The floor joists transfer load to the beams or walls that support them at their ends
The beams transfer load to columns or bearing walls
The bearing walls transfer load to the foundation
The foundation transfers load to the soil beneath it
The soil supports the load through bearing pressure distributed over the foundation area

Every link in this chain must be adequate — if the floor joists are strong but the connection between the joists and the supporting wall is inadequate, the joists will pull free from the wall under load. If the wall is strong but the foundation is inadequate, the wall will sink or overturn. The load path analysis asks: where is this chain weakest?
Tracing a load path — lateral loads:
Wind pushing against the wall of a building:

Wind pressure is applied to the wall surface
The wall transfers horizontal load to the floor and roof diaphragms — the horizontal planes of the floor and roof act as deep horizontal beams resisting lateral load
The floor and roof diaphragms transfer lateral load to shear walls — vertical structural elements that resist horizontal forces
The shear walls transfer lateral load to the foundation
The foundation transfers lateral load to the soil through friction and bearing

The lateral load path is more complex than the vertical load path and is more frequently incomplete in folk construction. A structure may have excellent vertical load path — the weight is fully supported — but an incomplete lateral load path — there is no continuous path for horizontal forces from the wall to the ground. This structure will resist gravity perfectly and fail in a wind or earthquake event.
Load path continuity in folk construction:
Folk construction frequently creates load path discontinuities — gaps in the chain that prevent loads from reaching the ground. Common discontinuities:

A beam bearing on a wall that is not connected to the wall — the beam can lift off the wall under wind uplift, breaking the load path
A wall that sits on the floor rather than on the foundation — under lateral load the wall slides along the floor rather than transferring load to the foundation
A roof that bears on the top of walls but is not anchored to them — under wind uplift the roof separates from the walls
A floor that spans between walls but is not connected to them — under lateral load the floor slides relative to the walls

Load path continuity is achieved through connections — the connections that tie each structural element to the next in the chain. This is why connections are the critical failure point — a missing or inadequate connection creates a load path discontinuity that turns a strong structure into a vulnerable one.
The simple load path test:

For any structural element, ask: where does the load go when this element deflects or deforms?
If the answer is "into the next element in the chain" — the load path is continuous
If the answer is "there is nothing to receive the load" or "the next element is not connected to this one" — there is a load path discontinuity requiring correction
Apply this test to every major structural element in a community building before occupancy

Rationale: The load path concept is the single most powerful structural analysis tool available to non-engineers because it converts the complex question "is this structure adequate?" into a series of simpler questions — "is this element adequate, and is this connection adequate, and is the next element adequate?" — that can be evaluated one at a time without simultaneous consideration of the entire structural system.
The lateral load path discontinuity in folk construction is emphasized because it is the failure mode most consistently observed in post-disaster structural surveys and the one most amenable to simple correction. A wall that sits on a floor without being anchored to it is a common folk construction condition — the wall is placed, the weight holds it down under gravity, and it appears stable. Under lateral load from wind or earthquake, it slides. Adding anchor bolts or metal straps that tie the wall sill to the floor or foundation costs almost nothing in materials and labor and converts a sliding failure mode into a resistant one. The community structural knowledge service shall specifically address lateral load path continuity as the highest-priority structural improvement available for existing folk construction.

1.4 Safety Factors and Structural Redundancy
Standard: Structural elements shall be sized with a safety factor — a ratio of actual capacity to design load — that accounts for uncertainty in load estimation, material variability, construction quality, and degradation over time. The appropriate safety factor varies with the consequences of failure and the confidence in the load and material estimates. Structural redundancy — multiple load paths — provides additional safety beyond individual element safety factors by ensuring that the failure of one element does not cause progressive collapse.
Why safety factors exist:
Uncertainty in loads:

The actual loads a structure will experience over its lifetime cannot be predicted precisely
The number of people who will occupy a space, the weight of snow in an extreme storm, the wind speed in an unusual weather event — all of these exceed the design assumptions at some point in the structure's life
A safety factor of 2 means the structure can carry twice the design load before failure — it can accommodate a storm twice as severe as the design storm, or an occupancy twice as dense as the design occupancy

Material variability:

The strength of wood, masonry, and earthen materials varies significantly from piece to piece and location to location
A nominal 2x10 floor joist may have significant knots, checks, or slope of grain that reduces its actual strength below the published design value
A masonry wall may have weak mortar joints or voids that reduce its actual capacity below what a perfect wall would provide
Safety factors account for this variability — they ensure that even below-average material performs adequately

Construction quality:

Real construction rarely achieves the theoretical perfection assumed in structural analysis
Connections may be imperfectly made, members may be slightly misaligned, materials may be stored improperly before use
A safety factor of 2 means that construction that achieves 70% of theoretical perfection still provides adequate structural capacity

Degradation over time:

Wood rots, masonry mortar erodes, metal corrodes — structural capacity decreases over time
A safety factor that provides adequate capacity at construction maintains some margin as the structure ages and degrades

Recommended safety factors for community construction:
Single-family owner-occupied structures:

Safety factor of 2.0 minimum — a structural element shall have at least twice the capacity required to carry its design load
This is the minimum — a safety factor of 3.0 is preferable where material quality and construction precision cannot be assured

Multifamily structures:

Safety factor of 2.5 minimum — the consequences of failure affect multiple households
Critical elements — primary columns, main beams, foundation elements: safety factor of 3.0

Community and public buildings:

Safety factor of 3.0 minimum for all elements
Critical elements in assembly spaces: safety factor of 4.0 — the consequences of failure in an occupied assembly space are catastrophic

Practical application of safety factors:
In the absence of formal structural analysis, safety factors can be applied by:

Estimating the maximum load the element will carry
Multiplying by the safety factor to get the required capacity
Selecting an element with actual capacity exceeding this value

For folk construction where material properties are uncertain:

Use conservative estimates of material strength — assume the weakest likely material, not the average
Increase safety factors above the minimums when material quality is unknown
Prefer over-sizing structural elements to under-sizing — the cost of a larger beam is small compared to the cost of a structural failure

Structural redundancy:
Redundancy means that if one structural element fails, the load it was carrying is redistributed to other elements rather than causing progressive collapse.
How to achieve redundancy:

Multiple columns rather than a single column — if one column is damaged, adjacent columns provide alternate support
Multiple load paths for lateral forces — shear walls on multiple sides of the structure rather than concentrated on one side
Continuous connections throughout the structure — if one connection fails, adjacent connections still provide some restraint
Tied construction — elements connected to each other so that partial failure in one area is resisted by adjacent elements

Minimum redundancy requirements:
All occupied structures:

No single structural element whose failure would cause total collapse of the structure without warning — every critical element shall have at least one backup load path
Primary columns and bearing walls shall be designed so that the loss of any one does not cause progressive collapse of more than the directly supported area

Multifamily and community buildings:

Structural elements shall be designed with specific progressive collapse resistance — the structure shall be capable of bridging over a failed element without collapsing
Connections shall be designed to maintain structural integrity even when subjected to loads significantly exceeding design loads — ductile connections that deform rather than fracture provide warning before failure

Rationale: The safety factor framework is included as an explicit standard because it provides the non-engineer with a systematic approach to structural adequacy evaluation that does not require detailed structural analysis. The question "is this structural element adequate?" is difficult to answer without engineering knowledge. The question "does this structural element have at least twice the capacity needed to carry the loads applied to it, based on conservative estimates of both load and capacity?" is answerable by a community member with basic measurement skills and access to material property tables. The safety factor converts a qualitative judgment into a quantitative comparison.
The progressive collapse redundancy requirement for multifamily and community buildings is the standard with the most direct historical justification. The Champlain Towers collapse in Surfside, Florida in 2021 killed 98 people — the initial structural failure in the parking garage triggered progressive collapse through the entire residential tower. The investigation found that the original structure lacked the redundancy required to arrest progressive collapse once it began. The Ronan Point collapse in London in 1968 killed 4 people and injured 17 when a gas explosion in one apartment caused progressive collapse through the corner of a 22-story building — the structure had no mechanism to redistribute load after the initial panel failure. Progressive collapse provisions emerged from these and similar disasters and represent hard-won knowledge about the difference between a structure that fails locally and one that fails totally.

1.5 Site Assessment — Soil, Drainage, and Slope Stability
Standard: The ground beneath a structure is a structural element — it must support the building loads applied to it without excessive settlement, without differential movement that distorts the structure, and without failure modes specific to the soil type and site conditions. Site assessment before construction is among the most important structural decisions — a structure built on inadequate ground will fail regardless of how well the structure itself is built.
Soil bearing capacity:
What soil bearing capacity means:

Soil bearing capacity is the maximum pressure the soil can support without excessive settlement or shear failure
Expressed in pounds per square foot (psf) or tons per square foot — the load per unit area that the soil can carry
Foundation size is determined by dividing the total structural load by the allowable soil bearing capacity — a heavier structure on weaker soil requires a larger foundation to distribute load over more soil area

Soil types and approximate bearing capacities:
High bearing capacity soils — 3,000-8,000 psf:

Bedrock — the highest bearing capacity, essentially unlimited for building loads
Dense gravel and gravelly sand — excellent bearing, minimal settlement
Dense sand — good bearing capacity, low compressibility
Stiff to hard clay — moderate to good bearing capacity when not subject to moisture change

Moderate bearing capacity soils — 1,000-3,000 psf:

Medium density sand
Firm clay
Mixed sand and clay

Low bearing capacity soils — below 1,000 psf:

Loose sand — compresses significantly under load, settles
Soft clay — very compressible, subject to long-term consolidation settlement
Organic soils — peat, muck, fill containing organic material — extremely compressible, subject to large settlements and ongoing consolidation

Problem soils requiring special treatment:

Fill material — ground that has been raised with added material of any type. Fill settlement is unpredictable and can be large. Never build on uncontrolled fill without investigation
Expansive clay — soils that swell when wet and shrink when dry, applying large forces to foundations and causing differential movement. Common in hot climates with seasonal wet-dry cycles
Liquefiable sand — loose saturated sand that loses bearing capacity during earthquake shaking, behaving like a liquid. Structures on liquefiable soil can sink or overturn during earthquakes

Field assessment of soil bearing capacity:
In the absence of laboratory testing:

Probe the ground with a metal rod — the resistance to penetration gives a rough indication of soil density. A rod that penetrates easily under hand pressure indicates soft soil. A rod that requires significant effort to penetrate 6 inches indicates firm soil
Examine soil from a hand-dug test pit — dark, spongy, or fibrous material indicates organic content. Gray or mottled gray-blue coloring indicates seasonal saturation. Layered deposits indicate variable conditions
Observe existing structures nearby — settlement cracks in neighboring buildings indicate soil problems. Tilted or settled structures are definitive indicators of bearing capacity problems
Look for springs, wet areas, and areas where vegetation is lush and wet — these indicate high groundwater and potentially saturated soils with low bearing capacity

Drainage:
Why drainage matters structurally:

Water is the primary enemy of foundations and below-grade construction
Hydrostatic pressure from saturated soil applies horizontal forces to foundation walls — forces that can exceed the wall's lateral resistance and cause foundation wall failure
Frost heave — the expansion of frozen saturated soil — applies large upward forces to foundations, lifting the structure and causing differential movement
Saturated soil has dramatically lower bearing capacity than the same soil dry — a site that is adequate in dry season may be inadequate during wet season if drainage is not managed

Site drainage standards:

Ground surface shall slope away from all structures — minimum 2% slope (2 feet of drop per 100 feet of horizontal distance) for at least 6 feet from the structure in all directions
Surface water from roofs shall be collected and directed away from the structure — downspouts discharging adjacent to the foundation create the concentrated saturated soil conditions that cause the most foundation problems
Subsurface drainage — perforated pipe in gravel trench at or below foundation level — shall be provided where the natural drainage is inadequate or where the water table is high relative to foundation depth

Slope stability:
When slopes are a structural concern:

Building on or near slopes introduces failure modes that flat-site construction does not face
Slopes can fail — slide, slump, or erode — under conditions that include heavy rainfall, earthquake loading, and removal of vegetation that provides root reinforcement

Slope stability indicators — avoid building on or immediately below slopes showing:

Fresh scarps — abrupt slope breaks where material has recently moved
Hummocky topography — irregular, lumpy terrain indicating previous sliding
Curved or tilted trees — leaning trees indicate ongoing slow soil movement
Springs emerging from the slope face — indicates internal water pressure that reduces soil shear strength
Tension cracks — cracks in the ground surface running parallel to the slope at the top — indicate active separation of a sliding mass from stable ground

Minimum setback from unstable slopes:

No structures within the potential slide runout area — the area a slope failure would reach if it occurred
Setback from the top of any slope steeper than 30 degrees: minimum equal to the height of the slope
Setback from the base of any slope steeper than 30 degrees: minimum equal to twice the height of the slope

Rationale: The fill soil warning is included with particular emphasis because building on uncontrolled fill is among the most consistently dangerous construction decisions made in informal community building. Fill is placed to level terrain, raise low areas, or dispose of excavated material — the composition of the fill, its density, and its long-term settlement behavior are unknown without investigation. Fill containing organic material continues to compress and settle for years as the organic material decomposes. Fill placed without compaction has variable density and settles differentially — some areas settle more than others, distorting the structure above. In community contexts where site preparation involves raising ground with available material, the composition and compaction of that material must be known before foundations are placed on it.
The slope stability indicators are included as field-assessable criteria because slope failures are among the most catastrophic and most preventable geotechnical disasters. The indicators — curved trees, hummocky topography, tension cracks, springs — are visible without instrumentation and reliably indicate sites where construction is hazardous. A community that knows to look for these indicators before selecting a building site avoids the most preventable category of site-related structural failures.

1.6 Foundation Types and Their Appropriate Applications
Standard: The foundation transmits all structural loads from the building to the soil. An inadequate foundation fails regardless of how well the structure above it is built — and foundation failure is the most difficult and expensive structural problem to correct after construction is complete. Foundation selection shall match the soil conditions, the structural loads, and the frost conditions of the site.
Foundation types:
Continuous wall foundation — spread footing:

A continuous concrete, masonry, or stone wall running beneath all load-bearing walls
The wall is widened at its base — the footing — to spread load over a greater soil area
The footing width shall be sufficient to reduce bearing pressure below the allowable soil bearing capacity
Footing width calculation: total wall load per linear foot ÷ allowable soil bearing capacity = minimum footing width in feet
Example: a bearing wall carrying 2,000 lbs per linear foot on soil with 1,500 psf bearing capacity requires a footing at least 16 inches wide (2,000 ÷ 1,500 = 1.33 feet)
Most common and most appropriate foundation type for masonry and earthen construction where load is distributed along continuous walls

Isolated column footings:

Individual footings beneath columns or posts
Each footing sized for the column load it supports
Used in post-and-beam construction where loads are concentrated at column points rather than distributed along walls
Footing size calculation: column load ÷ allowable soil bearing capacity = minimum footing area

Slab on grade:

A concrete slab poured directly on the ground surface
Appropriate for: warm climates without frost, well-drained sites with adequate bearing capacity
Not appropriate for: sites with frost penetration below the slab, expansive clay soils, sites with poor drainage
Minimum slab thickness: 4 inches for residential, 5-6 inches for community buildings
Reinforcement: a minimum of wire mesh or rebar in a 12-inch grid improves slab resistance to cracking from differential settlement and shrinkage

Rubble trench foundation:

A trench filled with angular rubble stone — without mortar — below frost depth, providing drainage and frost-heave resistance
Traditional and effective in folk construction contexts where concrete is unavailable
The rubble trench drains water away from the foundation zone, preventing the saturated soil conditions that cause frost heave
A concrete grade beam or timber sill at the top of the rubble trench distributes structural loads across the trench width
Appropriate for: well-drained sites in frost climates, folk construction contexts, sites where concrete is unavailable
Not appropriate for: sites with poor drainage where the rubble trench would collect and hold water, sites with expansive clay that requires continuous bearing rather than point contact

Earthen foundation — rammed earth or cob foundation walls:

Appropriate for: dry climates with minimal frost, sites above the water table, low-load structures
Earthen foundations shall be protected from water infiltration — a stone or concrete base layer above grade that prevents rain splash and ground moisture from reaching the earthen wall, and a roof overhang that keeps rain off the wall face
Not appropriate for: wet climates, frost climates without adequate protection, high-load structures

Pier and beam foundation:

Concrete, masonry, or treated wood piers at intervals supporting a beam structure above
The space between the ground and the floor structure provides ventilation that reduces moisture accumulation beneath wood floors
Appropriate for: sites with moderate soil conditions, areas where the ground surface is uneven, sites where the floor needs to be elevated above flood level
Pier depth shall reach frost depth in frost climates — shallow piers in frost climates heave differentially, distorting the structure above

Frost depth:

In climates with freezing temperatures, foundations shall extend below the depth at which soil freezes
Soil water that freezes expands 9% — saturated soil below a shallow foundation can heave the foundation upward with forces exceeding any structural resistance
Differential frost heave — more heave in one area than another — distorts the structure above, cracking walls and floors and misaligning doors and windows
Frost depth by climate:

No frost (tropical and subtropical): 0 inches — foundations may be at grade
Mild frost (temperate): 12-24 inches
Moderate frost (continental): 24-48 inches
Severe frost (subarctic): 48-72 inches or more


The community shall determine the local frost depth from historical records, neighboring construction practice, or soil temperature observation

Foundation moisture management:

Foundation walls below grade shall be protected from water infiltration
Waterproofing or damp-proofing applied to below-grade foundation wall exterior surfaces reduces water infiltration
In folk construction where commercial waterproofing is unavailable: a layer of clay or compacted earth against the foundation exterior, combined with drainage that directs water away from the foundation, provides meaningful moisture protection
A capillary break — a layer of gravel or a physical barrier — between the foundation and any wood sill plate prevents moisture wicking from the foundation into the wood structure above

Rationale: The rubble trench foundation is included with specific detail because it is the foundation type most appropriate for folk construction in frost climates where concrete is unavailable — and it is a genuinely effective traditional technology that is not widely known. The rubble trench was extensively documented and advocated by Frank Lloyd Wright's apprentice architect Walter Segal, who used it successfully in community self-build housing projects in the United Kingdom. Its effectiveness derives from drainage physics — a trench of angular rubble has a high void ratio that allows water to drain freely through it, preventing the saturated soil conditions that cause frost heave. Communities in frost climates without access to concrete who are aware of the rubble trench foundation have a viable foundation option that conventional construction knowledge rarely provides.
The capillary break requirement addresses one of the most consistently observed moisture-related structural failures in folk construction — wood rot at the sill plate. The sill plate is the lowest wood member of a wall structure, sitting on top of the foundation. Masonry and concrete are capillary — they wick moisture upward from the soil into the foundation wall and from the foundation wall into the wood sill plate resting on it. A sill plate that is continuously wetted by capillary moisture decays within years to decades, removing the base of the wall from its support and creating the conditions for progressive wall settlement and distortion. A capillary break — a layer of gravel, a sheet of polyethylene, or historically a layer of roofing felt — between the foundation and the sill plate costs almost nothing and prevents a failure mode that has destroyed the bases of countless folk construction buildings.

1.7 Wood Framing — Principles, Spanning Limits, and Connections
Standard: Wood framing is the most common structural system in community and folk construction — it is workable with hand tools, available from local timber resources, and adaptable to a wide range of structural applications. The standards below provide practical spanning limits and connection requirements for the most common wood framing applications, with specific attention to the failure modes most common in folk construction.
Wood as a structural material:
Wood properties relevant to structural use:

Wood is strong in both tension and compression along the grain — a wood member loaded parallel to its grain can carry significant loads in either direction
Wood is much weaker perpendicular to the grain — loading wood across the grain crushes the fibers and produces large deformations at relatively low loads
Wood is weakest in shear — sliding forces parallel to the grain at connections and notches
Knots, checks, and slope of grain dramatically reduce wood strength — a knotty board may have 50-60% of the strength of a clear board of the same species and size
Green wood (unseasoned) is approximately 40% weaker than dry wood of the same species and size, and it shrinks and distorts as it dries in place — green wood construction requires larger members and looser connections that accommodate movement

Practical wood strength for folk construction:

Without species identification and grade, assume the lower end of typical wood strength ranges
For structural calculations where formal values are unavailable: assume 1,000 psi allowable bending stress for ungraded softwood lumber — this is conservative for most common construction softwoods in good condition
Increase member sizes by 25% when using green wood to account for the strength reduction during seasoning

Spanning limits — practical rules of thumb:
These rules of thumb provide reasonable starting points for folk construction. They incorporate a safety factor of approximately 2 for residential loads (40 psf live load plus 15 psf dead load). Increase member sizes where loads are higher, spans are longer, or material quality is uncertain.
Floor joists — depth as a fraction of span:

Minimum joist depth: span in feet ÷ 1.5 = minimum depth in inches
Example: a 12-foot span requires a minimum joist depth of 8 inches (12 ÷ 1.5 = 8)
Joist spacing: 16 inches on center for standard residential loads, 12 inches for heavier loads or lower-quality lumber
This rule applies to solid sawn lumber joists at standard residential loads — heavier loads or lower-quality lumber require larger members or closer spacing

Roof rafters:

Minimum rafter depth: span in feet ÷ 2.0 = minimum depth in inches for moderate snow loads
In heavy snow regions: span in feet ÷ 1.5 = minimum depth in inches
Rafter spacing: 16-24 inches on center depending on load and material quality

Beams supporting joists or rafters:

Minimum beam depth: span in feet ÷ 1.0 = minimum depth in inches for moderate loads
Beam width: minimum 1/3 of depth
Beams carrying heavy loads or spanning long distances require larger dimensions — increase depth by 50% for heavy storage loads or long spans

Posts and columns:

Minimum post dimension: height in feet ÷ 10 = minimum dimension in inches for unbraced posts
Example: an 8-foot unbraced post requires a minimum dimension of 0.8 inches — which is clearly inadequate for structural purposes. This rule reveals that unbraced posts of significant height require large dimensions — an 8-foot post would need to be 6x6 or larger for significant loads
Intermediate bracing at the midpoint of a post's height doubles its load capacity — a 4x4 post braced at mid-height carries as much as an 8x8 post of the same height unbraced

Connections in wood framing:
Nail connections:

Nails are the primary fastener in light wood frame construction
Nail capacity in withdrawal (pulling out): approximately 20-40 lbs per nail per inch of penetration in the receiving member, depending on wood density
Nail capacity in shear (sliding): approximately 60-100 lbs per nail, depending on nail diameter and wood density
Standard connection requirements:

Joist to top plate (toenail): 3 nails minimum per joist
Rafter to top plate (toenail): 3 nails minimum per rafter
Double top plate splice: 8 nails minimum at each splice
Stud to top and bottom plate: 2 nails minimum at each end



The toenail weakness:

Toenailing — driving nails at an angle through one member into another — is the most common connection method in light frame construction and the weakest
A toenail connection resists withdrawal at the angle of the nail but provides minimal resistance to forces perpendicular to the nail angle
For roof-to-wall connections where wind uplift tries to lift the roof straight off the wall, toenail connections provide almost no resistance — they pull out readily under uplift forces
Metal hurricane ties or rafter ties at each rafter-to-plate connection provide 10-20 times the uplift resistance of toenail connections at minimal cost

Metal connector hardware:

Joist hangers, rafter ties, hurricane ties, hold-down anchors, and post caps dramatically improve connection strength at relatively low material cost
Where metal connector hardware is available, it shall be used at critical connections — particularly roof-to-wall connections in Tier 2 and Tier 3, and at all connections in multifamily and community buildings
Where metal connector hardware is unavailable: wood blocking, lap joints, and through-bolted connections provide improved alternatives to simple toenailing

Folk construction connections:

Traditional joinery — mortise and tenon, lap joints, wedged joints — provides connections of adequate strength when properly executed
The critical requirement for traditional joinery is geometric precision — a loosely fit mortise and tenon has a fraction of the strength of a tight-fit joint
Lashing connections using rope or rawhide are appropriate for temporary or light-load applications — they provide flexibility that allows movement under load without fracture, but they degrade with moisture and UV exposure and require regular inspection and replacement
Through-bolted connections — a bolt passing completely through both members being joined — provide strong, reliable connections using simple hardware

Lateral bracing — the most overlooked wood frame requirement:
A wood frame structure without adequate lateral bracing is a stack of sticks held together by friction and a few nails — it will stand in still air and collapse in a wind or earthquake. Lateral bracing provides the structural resistance to horizontal forces that prevents racking failure.
Types of lateral bracing:

Diagonal bracing: a diagonal member from corner to corner of a wall frame, in tension or compression under lateral load. Let-in diagonal bracing — a 1x4 notched into the face of the studs — is the traditional approach. A full-depth diagonal member is more effective
Shear wall: a wall surface sheathed with a rigid material — plywood, boards laid diagonally, woven bamboo, or earthen plaster of adequate thickness — that acts as a deep vertical beam resisting lateral force
Cross-bracing: two diagonal members crossing each other, one in tension and one in compression under lateral load. More effective than single diagonal bracing because it resists lateral loads in both directions

Minimum lateral bracing for folk construction:

Every rectangular wall panel shall have at least one diagonal brace or shear wall element
Diagonal bracing shall connect from the top corner to the bottom corner of the wall panel — half-height bracing provides inadequate resistance
In Tier 2 and Tier 3 structures: formal shear wall design with rated sheathing and specific nail spacing is required for multifamily and community buildings

Rationale: The toenail weakness is included with specific emphasis because it is the most consistently observed inadequate connection in folk wood frame construction and the one responsible for the majority of roof failures in wind events. The physics are simple — a toenail driven at 45 degrees through a rafter into a top plate provides withdrawal resistance in the direction of the nail shaft, but wind uplift acts perpendicular to the roof surface, pulling the rafter straight up and off the plate. The toenail is oriented to resist sliding along the plate, not vertical separation from it. A rafter connected only by toenails will separate from the plate under moderate wind uplift — the nails pull out rather than the wood failing. Hurricane ties that strap across the joint and nail to both the rafter and the stud below the plate provide resistance in the direction that wind uplift acts. The difference between a toenailed roof connection and a hurricane-tied connection is the difference between a roof that stays on in a significant wind event and one that does not.
The diagonal bracing standard addresses what is perhaps the most common structural deficiency in folk construction globally — structures that are entirely adequate for vertical loads and have no lateral resistance. In still conditions these structures appear solid and stable. Under lateral load from wind or earthquake they rack — the rectangular wall frame distorts into a parallelogram — and the connections at the top and bottom of the wall fail as the racking force exceeds the nail shear capacity. The collapse is sudden because once racking begins the connections fail progressively and rapidly. Diagonal bracing converts a structure that is stable only in still conditions into one that can resist lateral loads — the addition of a few diagonal members is the single most significant structural improvement available for existing folk construction buildings in wind or seismic regions.

1.8 Masonry and Earthen Construction — Structural Considerations
Standard: Masonry and earthen construction — brick, stone, concrete block, adobe, cob, and rammed earth — provides excellent compressive strength, fire resistance, and thermal mass. Its structural vulnerability is brittleness in tension and shear — masonry fails suddenly and catastrophically under the tensile and shear forces imposed by seismic loading, differential settlement, and lateral loads that it cannot redistribute through ductile deformation. Understanding this vulnerability is the foundation of safe masonry construction in the Ben Franklin Society context.
Masonry structural characteristics:
Compressive strength — the strength:

Masonry is strong in compression — brick, stone, adobe, and cob can carry large compressive loads without distress
Compressive strength of common masonry materials:

Solid fired brick: 3,000-8,000 psi (very strong)
Concrete block: 1,500-3,000 psi
Adobe: 300-600 psi (adequate for low-rise construction)
Cob: 150-400 psi (adequate for low-rise construction)
Rubble stone: variable, 500-3,000 psi depending on stone type and mortar



Tensile strength — the weakness:

Masonry has very low tensile strength — the mortar joint between units is the weak point and fails under relatively small tensile forces
Unreinforced masonry will crack and fail under tensile stresses as low as 50-100 psi — far below the compressive strength
Lateral loads — wind and earthquake — produce tensile stresses in masonry walls that can easily exceed this threshold
Differential settlement — one part of the foundation settling more than another — also produces tensile stresses that crack masonry

Unreinforced versus reinforced masonry:
Unreinforced masonry:

Traditional and common in folk construction — no steel reinforcement
Adequate for: low-rise construction in low-seismic zones, walls loaded primarily in compression with minimal lateral load, structures with regular geometry and symmetric load distribution
Not appropriate for: seismic zones above low, tall slender walls, irregular structures with asymmetric loads, walls with large openings that create stress concentrations

Reinforced masonry:

Steel reinforcement embedded in grout-filled cells of concrete block or in reinforced concrete bond beams at intervals in brick or adobe construction
Dramatically improves lateral load resistance and tensile capacity — the steel carries tension while the masonry carries compression
Required for: any masonry structure in moderate or high seismic zones, masonry structures above two stories, masonry walls taller than 10 feet without lateral support

Earthen construction — adobe and cob:
Structural similarity to masonry:

Adobe and cob behave structurally similarly to unreinforced masonry — strong in compression, weak in tension and shear
The same vulnerabilities apply — susceptibility to seismic damage, need for lateral bracing, sensitivity to differential settlement

Additional earthen construction considerations:
Moisture sensitivity:

Earthen construction is highly vulnerable to moisture — water dissolves the clay binder and reduces both compressive and tensile strength dramatically
Earthen walls exposed to driving rain or standing water at their base lose structural capacity progressively
Protection is mandatory: wide roof overhangs that prevent rain from reaching the wall face, a non-earthen base course (stone, brick, or concrete) that elevates the earthen wall above rain splash and ground moisture, and plaster finishes that shed water

Wall thickness and height:

Adobe and cob walls shall be proportioned so that their height does not exceed 8-10 times their thickness for single-story construction
A 12-inch thick adobe wall should not exceed 8-10 feet in height without lateral support
Above this ratio, buckling becomes the governing failure mode — the wall buckles outward under compressive load before it crushes

Openings:

Openings in earthen walls concentrate stress at their corners — diagonal cracks radiating from window and door corners are the most common earthen wall distress pattern
Lintels above openings shall be robust — timber lintels spanning the opening and extending minimum 12 inches into the wall on each side
Total opening width shall not exceed 50% of any wall length — excessive openings weaken the wall to the point where it cannot resist lateral loads

Bond beams:

A continuous reinforced concrete or timber bond beam at the top of masonry and earthen walls ties the wall together and provides the connection point for roof framing
Without a bond beam, roofs bear directly on the masonry — the roof loads create point stresses that crack the masonry, and there is no positive connection between roof and wall to resist wind uplift
A timber bond beam — continuous timber members at the top of the wall, anchored into the masonry at intervals — is achievable in folk construction without concrete and provides significant improvement over direct roof bearing

Masonry in seismic zones:
The most dangerous construction type in seismic zones:

Unreinforced masonry — brick, stone, concrete block, adobe — is the construction type responsible for the majority of earthquake deaths globally
The brittleness of masonry means that once the tensile capacity is exceeded, the wall fails suddenly and completely — there is no ductile deformation that provides warning or allows occupant escape
The 2010 Haiti earthquake killed approximately 220,000 people — the majority in unreinforced concrete and masonry construction
The 2023 Turkey earthquake killed approximately 60,000 people — unreinforced and poorly reinforced masonry was the primary failure mode

Seismic precautions for masonry in communities at risk:
Minimum precautions — all masonry in any seismic zone:

Regular wall geometry — no complex plan shapes that create stress concentrations
Adequate bond beam at wall top — ties the structure together under seismic loading
Roof construction connected to walls — not simply resting on them
No heavy masses (water tanks, storage) at upper levels — reduces seismic inertia forces

Additional precautions for moderate seismic zones:

Steel reinforcement at all corners and opening edges minimum
Grouted cells in concrete block construction at corners and opening edges
Seismic ties — horizontal reinforcement in bed joints at regular intervals

High seismic zones:

Fully engineered reinforced masonry design required for multifamily and community buildings
Single-family owner-occupied structures: the community knowledge service shall strongly advise against unreinforced masonry and earthen construction in high seismic zones and provide specific guidance on reinforcing techniques

Rationale: The seismic death toll from unreinforced masonry is presented directly and with specific numbers because it is the most important structural safety fact in this document — and the one most frequently ignored because masonry feels solid and permanent. The 2010 Haiti earthquake death toll — approximately 220,000 people — is the most extreme example of what unreinforced masonry does in a seismic event. Haiti's construction was predominantly unreinforced concrete and masonry — materials that are individually strong but fail catastrophically under the tensile and shear demands of seismic loading. A community in a seismic zone that builds primarily in unreinforced masonry based on the material's compressive strength, its fire resistance, and its availability is making a decision that could be catastrophic if a significant seismic event occurs during the community's lifetime. The Ben Franklin Society structural knowledge service shall ensure that this risk is clearly communicated to community members considering masonry construction in seismic regions.
The moisture protection for earthen construction is included with specific technical detail because it is the most consistently misunderstood aspect of earthen construction — the material's apparent fragility in moisture leads to undervaluation of its genuine structural capabilities when properly protected. A well-built adobe or cob structure with adequate roof overhang, non-earthen base course, and plaster protection can last centuries — there are earthen structures in Yemen, Morocco, and New Mexico that are hundreds of years old and structurally sound. The same material without protection deteriorates within years to decades. The protective measures are simple, inexpensive, and entirely within the capability of folk construction — the knowledge of why they are necessary and what they consist of is the missing element.

1.9 Lateral Forces — The Most Overlooked Structural Concern
Standard: Lateral forces — horizontal loads from wind and earthquakes — are the structural concern most consistently underestimated in folk and community construction, and the one responsible for the most dramatic and sudden structural failures. A structure that has stood for years under gravity loads and shown no distress can fail in minutes under lateral loads it has no capacity to resist. Every occupied structure shall have explicit lateral load resistance — not as an afterthought but as a primary structural design consideration.
What lateral loads do to structures:
The racking failure mode:

A rectangular wall frame subjected to horizontal force at its top distorts into a parallelogram — a shape called racking
The nails at the top and bottom of each stud shear as the frame distorts
Once racking begins, the connections fail progressively and rapidly — the structure collapses not because any individual member failed but because the connections that held the geometry together lost their ability to resist the distortion
Racking failure is the most common structural collapse mode in both wind and earthquake events for light frame construction

How lateral loads enter the structure:

Wind: pressure on the windward wall and suction on the leeward wall create a net horizontal force that tries to push the structure sideways
Earthquake: the ground moves horizontally — the structure's inertia keeps it stationary while the ground moves under it, creating a horizontal force between the ground and the structure proportional to the structure's weight

The lateral load path:

Lateral loads must travel from the surface they are applied to — the wall surface for wind, the mass of the structure for earthquake — through the structure to the foundation
The path: wall surface → horizontal diaphragm (floor or roof) → shear walls → foundation → soil
Each element in this path must be adequate and each must be connected to the next

Diaphragms:

Floors and roofs act as horizontal diaphragms — rigid planes that collect lateral loads and distribute them to the vertical resisting elements (shear walls)
A diaphragm must be rigid enough to distribute loads — a flexible diaphragm allows the structure to distort before distributing loads
Board sheathing laid diagonally, plywood, or woven bamboo panels all provide diaphragm action
Bare joists without sheathing provide no diaphragm action — the joists can rotate relative to each other under lateral load

Shear walls:

Shear walls are the vertical elements that resist lateral loads — they act as deep vertical cantilever beams fixed at their base
A shear wall must be connected to the diaphragm above it (to receive lateral load) and to the foundation below it (to transfer lateral load to the ground)
The connection at the base of a shear wall — the hold-down — is the most critical connection for lateral resistance. Without an adequate hold-down, the shear wall overturns rather than shearing, providing no lateral resistance

Shear wall distribution:

Shear walls shall be distributed throughout the structure — concentrated shear walls on one side create a structure that resists lateral loads in one direction but not perpendicular
Shear walls shall be provided in both principal horizontal directions of the structure
A structure without shear walls on the windward and leeward faces for wind in any direction has no lateral resistance for that wind direction

Lateral resistance in folk construction:
Available methods in order of effectiveness:
Diagonal bracing:

A diagonal member from the upper corner to the lower corner of a wall panel — wood, bamboo, or metal rod
Effective in tension (rod bracing) or compression (wood or bamboo bracing)
Cross-bracing — two diagonals crossing — resists lateral loads in both directions
Minimum brace size: 1x4 notched into the studs for let-in bracing, or 2x4 full-depth diagonal for stronger applications

Earthen plaster shear walls:

Thick earthen plaster — 2 inches or more — applied to both faces of a light frame wall panel creates a composite wall with meaningful shear resistance
The plaster stiffens the frame and distributes shear across the panel area
Requires adequate connection between plaster and frame — wire mesh or wood lath embedded in the plaster and nailed to the frame provides this connection
Less effective than structural sheathing but significantly better than unbraced light framing

Woven bamboo or reed panels:

Traditional lateral bracing system in many folk construction traditions
Bamboo or reed woven between the studs of a wall frame provides both shear resistance and a base for earthen plaster
Effective as shear resistance when the weaving is tight and the connection to the frame is secure

Log or heavy timber walls:

Solid log construction and heavy timber plank walls provide inherent lateral resistance through their mass and the interlocking of corner connections
The lateral resistance of log construction is sufficient for moderate wind loads without additional bracing — the mass and corner interlocking provide shear resistance
Not adequate without additional bracing for significant seismic loads

Minimum lateral bracing requirements by occupancy:
Single-family owner-occupied — knowledge service:

The community knowledge service shall specifically address lateral bracing as the highest-priority structural improvement for folk construction
Minimum recommended: diagonal bracing in at least one wall panel per exterior wall face
In seismic zones: diagonal bracing in all wall panels and positive hold-down connections at all shear wall bases

Multifamily structures — mandatory:

Formal shear wall design required — shear wall length, location, and connections calculated for the design lateral loads
Hold-down anchors at all shear wall ends — connections from the end studs of shear walls to the foundation that resist overturning
Diaphragm connections — positive connections between the floor and roof diaphragms and the shear walls below them

Community buildings — mandatory:

Full lateral analysis required — lateral loads calculated, shear wall locations and sizes determined by calculation, all connections designed
Community buildings with assembly occupancy shall have lateral resistance reviewed by the most structurally knowledgeable community member available before occupancy

Rationale: The racking failure description is detailed here because it is the most important structural failure mode for community members to understand and the most amenable to prevention through simple measures. Racking is not a failure of the structural members — the studs, joists, and rafters remain intact through a racking failure. It is a failure of the structural system's ability to maintain its geometry under lateral load. This means that adding lateral bracing to an existing structure — even a structure that has been standing for years without visible distress — provides genuine structural improvement that can prevent collapse in the next wind or earthquake event. The structural members are already there and adequate. What is often missing is the system that holds them in the correct geometric relationship under lateral load.
The hold-down anchor emphasis addresses the failure mode that converts a structurally adequate shear wall into an ineffective one — overturning without adequate base anchorage. A shear wall that is not positively connected to its foundation overturns like a tall thin book being pushed over, rather than shearing like a deep beam — the overturning mode provides no lateral resistance. The hold-down is the connection that makes the shear wall work as intended. In post-earthquake damage surveys it is consistently found that structures with shear walls but inadequate hold-downs fail at hold-downs rather than in the shear walls themselves — the investment in shear wall construction is wasted by inadequate hold-down detail.

1.10 Roofs — Structural Standards
Standard: The roof is simultaneously the structural element most exposed to environmental loads — snow, wind uplift, and seismic forces from the roof mass — and the element most commonly under-designed in folk construction. Roof failures in wind and snow events are the most frequent structural failures in community settings. The standards below address the structural adequacy of roof framing, the critical roof-to-wall connection, and the specific failure modes most common in folk construction.
Roof structural systems:
Simple rafter framing:

Individual rafters spanning from the ridge to the wall plate — the simplest and most common roof structure in folk construction
The rafter carries the roof load in bending — it acts as an inclined beam
The rafter also creates an outward horizontal thrust at the wall plate — rafters push the walls outward as they try to flatten under load. This outward thrust must be resisted either by the ceiling joist (which ties the two opposite wall plates together, preventing them from spreading) or by a structural ridge beam at the apex
A simple rafter roof without ceiling joists or a structural ridge beam will spread its walls outward progressively under load — this is the most common folk construction roof failure mode other than wind uplift

Collar ties:

Horizontal members connecting opposing rafters at mid-height — they resist the spreading tendency of rafter pairs
Less effective than ceiling joists at resisting outward thrust because they are at mid-height rather than at the plate level
Useful as supplementary bracing but not adequate as the sole thrust resistance in most rafter roof systems

Ridge beam:

A structural beam at the ridge that supports the upper end of the rafters — it eliminates the outward thrust by converting the rafter from a compression arch into a simple beam
Requires adequate support — the ridge beam carries vertical load and must be supported at its ends by posts, gable walls, or interior structure
More material-intensive than a collar tie solution but eliminates the outward thrust problem entirely

Truss:

A triangulated structural assembly that carries roof loads efficiently by converting all members to pure tension or pure compression — no bending
Trusses can span large distances with relatively light members
Folk trusses — constructed from available timber with simple connections — can achieve spans of 20-40 feet that would require very heavy single members
The critical element in a folk truss is the bottom chord connection — the joint at the corners where the rafters meet the bottom horizontal member. This joint is in high tension and must be positively connected

Roof-to-wall connections — the critical failure point:
Wind uplift is the governing load for roof-to-wall connections — the roof wants to lift off the walls under wind loading. The connection that resists this uplift is the most critical structural connection in the entire building for wind resistance.
Toenail connections — inadequate for wind uplift:

As established in Section 1.7, toenail connections provide minimal resistance to direct withdrawal — the direction of wind uplift force
A roof connected to its walls only by toenails will separate from the walls in a significant wind event
Post-hurricane damage surveys consistently find roofs sitting next to houses rather than on them — the failure is almost always at the rafter-to-plate toenail connection

Adequate roof-to-wall connections:
Hurricane ties (metal rafter ties):

Prefabricated metal straps that connect the rafter to the wall plate and to the stud below the plate
Provide 500-1,500 lbs of uplift resistance per connection depending on the specific hardware
The most reliable and cost-effective roof-to-wall connection improvement available

Manufactured connector substitutes in folk construction:

Where metal hurricane ties are unavailable: through-bolted wood blocks at each rafter-to-plate connection, or wire wrapping from the rafter around the stud below the plate
Wire wrapping — twisted wire from the rafter, down around the stud, and twisted tight — provides meaningful uplift resistance using only wire that may be available in a folk construction context
Plywood gussets nailed to both the rafter and the stud below the plate — plywood gussets at each rafter-to-plate connection provide shear and withdrawal resistance significantly exceeding toenails

Roof spanning limits:
Rafters:

The spanning limit rule of thumb from Section 1.7 applies: span in feet ÷ 2.0 = minimum depth in inches for moderate loads
In snow regions: span in feet ÷ 1.5 = minimum depth in inches
Rafter sizing shall account for the actual slope — a very low-slope roof carries snow load at nearly full ground snow load. A steep roof sheds snow and carries lower load

Ridge beam:

Ridge beam depth: tributary span in feet ÷ 1.0 = minimum depth in inches
The tributary span for a ridge beam is the full span of the roof from wall to wall — the ridge beam carries half the load from each side of the roof

Overhang structural considerations:

Overhangs — roof extension beyond the wall — are cantilevered from the rafter tails
Large overhangs create concentrated uplift at the wall plate as wind load on the overhang surface acts as a lever
Maximum unsupported overhang for light framing: 2 feet for 2x6 rafters, 3 feet for 2x8 rafters
Overhangs beyond these limits require larger rafter members or structural support at the overhang tip

Snow load management:

Steep roofs shed snow — slopes above 30 degrees shed most snow and carry approximately 50% of the ground snow load
Low-slope roofs retain snow — slopes below 15 degrees retain essentially all snow and shall be designed for full ground snow load plus drift accumulation
Valley areas where two roof slopes meet are drift collection zones — snow accumulates to depths exceeding surrounding roof areas. Valley framing shall be sized for 1.5 times the design roof snow load

Flat roof structural considerations:

Flat roofs are the most structurally demanding roof type because they:

Carry full snow load without shedding
Accumulate drifted snow from wind
Collect ponded water if drainage is inadequate — ponded water load can cause progressive collapse as the water load deflects the roof, creating a deeper pond, adding more load, increasing deflection
Have no inherent structural depth advantage of slope


Flat roof framing shall be designed for full snow load plus 10 psf for ponded water
Flat roofs shall drain completely with no ponding areas — drainage shall be verified before the first rain season

Earthen roofs — sod and planted roofs:

Earthen roofs have extremely high dead loads — 80-120 psf — that dominate the structural design
Every structural element supporting an earthen roof shall be sized specifically for this load — no standard sizing rules for light roofing apply
The structural support for an earthen roof is typically heavy timber with thick plank decking — light framing is inadequate
The community knowledge service shall specifically advise any community member proposing an earthen roof that the structural support requirements are significantly greater than for any other roof type

Rationale: The outward thrust of simple rafter roofs is the structural behavior most commonly misunderstood in folk construction and the one responsible for the widest range of roof-related problems. A rafter roof without ceiling joists or a structural ridge beam continuously pushes its walls outward — the walls bow, connections fail progressively, and eventually the roof sags at the ridge as the walls spread. This failure mode takes years to develop and is frequently misdiagnosed as wall settlement, foundation movement, or material deterioration when it is actually a rocking structural system. The fix — installing ceiling joists that tie the opposing wall plates together — is simple and inexpensive if done during construction but very difficult and expensive to add to a completed structure. The community knowledge service shall address this issue specifically in any consultation with community members planning rafter roofs.
The ponding water progressive collapse mechanism is included because it is the most dangerous flat roof failure mode and the one least visible during construction. A flat roof that appears structurally adequate under its design loads may collapse under the combination of snow load and the additional load of ponded meltwater — the meltwater accumulates in the low point created by the initial deflection, adding more load, increasing deflection, and creating a progressively deeper pool until the structural capacity is exceeded. This positive feedback mechanism has caused numerous flat roof collapses in commercial and community buildings after snowstorms, when the snow melts but the drainage is blocked by ice at the drains or perimeter. Ensuring that flat roof drainage is functional — drains unblocked, no ponding zones — is a maintenance requirement with direct structural safety implications.

1.11 Multifamily Structural Standards
Standard: Multifamily structures — buildings housing two or more independent households — carry mandatory structural standards because the occupants of each unit bear the structural risk of decisions made by the builder and by the occupants of other units. The standards below establish minimum structural requirements for multifamily construction that apply regardless of construction type or resource constraints.
Floor system requirements:
Minimum floor structural capacity:

All floors in multifamily buildings shall be designed for a minimum live load of 40 psf for living areas and 125 psf for storage areas
Floor framing shall be sized using the spanning rules in Section 1.7 with a safety factor of 2.5 — greater than the 2.0 recommended for single-family construction because the consequences of floor failure in a multifamily building affect multiple households

Floor-to-floor separation:

Floor-ceiling assemblies between units in vertically stacked multifamily construction shall provide minimum structural separation — the failure of one floor shall not cause the collapse of the floor above or below it
Double joists or deeper joists at unit boundaries provide structural separation — if fire or overload compromises one unit's floor, the adjacent unit's floor framing remains structurally independent
Progressive collapse prevention: each floor shall be designed to carry its own loads plus a portion of the load from the floor above if the floor above fails — minimum 25% of the floor above's dead load as additional load on each floor

Shared walls — party walls:

Party walls between units shall be structurally independent from both units they separate — the structural integrity of one unit's party wall shall not depend on the structural elements of the adjacent unit
A party wall that serves as a bearing wall for both adjacent units creates a structural dependency — if one unit modifies or removes structural elements, the other unit's structure is affected
Preferred: each unit has its own structural wall with a gap between them — the double wall construction referenced in the fire safety document also achieves structural independence

Stair structural requirements:

Stairs in multifamily buildings shall be structurally designed for a minimum live load of 100 psf — stair loads during emergency evacuation with multiple occupants simultaneously on the stair can significantly exceed normal residential stair loading
Stair stringers — the inclined framing members that support the treads — shall be sized for this load: minimum 3x12 lumber at 16-inch spacing for stairs up to 12 feet in height
Stair connections to the floor structure above and below shall be positively anchored — not simply resting on a bearing
Handrails shall be structurally capable of resisting a 200-lb horizontal force at any point — the force a panicking occupant may apply during emergency egress

Community review before occupancy:

All multifamily structures shall be reviewed by the community structural officer or the most structurally knowledgeable community member available before first occupancy
The review shall include: visual inspection of all accessible structural elements, verification that spanning limits are not exceeded, verification that lateral bracing is present in all wall faces, verification that roof-to-wall connections are adequate, and verification that the foundation appears sound
Structures that fail the review shall not be occupied until identified deficiencies are corrected
The review shall be documented with findings recorded and retained

Rationale: The progressive collapse prevention standard — designing each floor to carry 25% of the floor above's dead load — is the minimum provision for preventing cascading floor collapse in multifamily construction. Without this provision, a floor failure in one unit removes support from the floor above, which then fails, which removes support from the next floor above, and so on — the entire building collapses sequentially from the initial failure point. The 25% additional dead load provision ensures that each floor has enough reserve capacity to carry a significant portion of the load shed by a failed floor above — arresting the progressive collapse before it extends beyond the immediate failure zone. This provision is achievable in folk construction by sizing floor framing at 125% of the load required for that floor's own loads — a 25% oversize that provides the progressive collapse reserve.
The stair live load standard — 100 psf — is significantly higher than the residential stair design load used in standard building codes (40 psf) and reflects the emergency evacuation loading condition. During a fire or structural emergency in a multifamily building, multiple occupants attempt to evacuate simultaneously using the same stair. A two-story building with four units each housing four occupants can have 16 people on the stair simultaneously during emergency evacuation — at an average of 150 lbs per person concentrated on a stair of 36-inch width and 12-foot length, the average loading is approximately 70 psf with local concentrations significantly higher. A stair designed for 40 psf residential loading may fail under this emergency condition. The 100 psf design load ensures structural adequacy for the emergency condition that determines whether the stair remains functional when it is most needed.

1.12 Moisture and Structural Degradation
Standard: Moisture is the primary cause of long-term structural degradation in wood construction and a significant cause of degradation in masonry and earthen construction. A structure that is structurally adequate at completion may become structurally deficient within years if moisture management is inadequate. Moisture management is therefore both a construction standard and an ongoing maintenance requirement.
How moisture destroys wood structures:
Wood rot:

Wood rot — decay caused by fungi — occurs when wood moisture content exceeds approximately 19% and temperature is above 40°F
Below 19% moisture content, wood does not rot regardless of temperature
The implication: wood that is kept dry does not rot. Wood that is repeatedly wetted and dried, or that remains continuously damp, decays progressively
Decay begins at the surface and progresses inward — visible surface decay indicates significant structural loss has already occurred in the affected member
Common sites of wood rot in community construction: sill plates in contact with moisture-wicking foundations, floor joists in crawl spaces without adequate ventilation, roof rafters at the eaves where roof leaks occur, window and door frames where flashing is inadequate, posts in direct contact with soil or concrete

Structural significance of wood rot:

A floor joist that has lost 25% of its cross-section to rot has lost approximately 44% of its bending strength — the relationship between section loss and strength loss is nonlinear
A post that has rotted through 50% of its cross-section at its base has lost approximately 75% of its compressive capacity
Rot damage is cumulative and progressive — a small area of rot grows under favorable moisture conditions and can eventually compromise the entire member

Inspection for wood rot:

Sound wood produces a solid tone when tapped with a hammer. Rotted wood produces a hollow thud — the decayed interior has lower density
A probe — an ice pick or sharp awl — penetrates sound wood with difficulty. Rotted wood accepts a probe with minimal resistance
Structural members shall be inspected annually for rot at all locations where moisture contact is possible — sill plates, floor joists at their bearing ends, posts at their bases, and roof framing at the eaves and valley areas

Preventing wood rot:
Physical separation from moisture sources:

No wood in direct contact with soil or concrete without a physical moisture barrier — pressure-treated wood, naturally rot-resistant wood species (cedar, redwood, black locust, osage orange, teak), or a physical barrier (metal flashing, roofing felt, polyethylene)
Sill plates on foundations: capillary break required per Section 1.6
Posts: metal post bases that elevate the post above concrete or soil, or post bases that allow water to drain away from the post-to-concrete junction
Floor framing in crawl spaces: minimum 18-inch clearance between the bottom of the floor framing and the ground, ground surface covered with polyethylene to reduce moisture evaporation, cross-ventilation to remove moisture-laden air

Roof and wall moisture management:
Flashing:

Flashing is sheet metal or other impermeable material installed at all joints where water could enter the building envelope — at roof penetrations, at roof-to-wall intersections, at window and door heads, at valley intersections of roof slopes
Flashing failure is the most common cause of building envelope moisture intrusion — water follows the path of least resistance, and an unflashed joint provides a direct path into the structure
Critical flashing locations:

Chimney to roof intersection — water runs down the chimney face and under the roofing without adequate flashing
Window and door heads — water runs down the wall and into the window or door opening without head flashing
Roof-to-wall intersection — where a roof meets a vertical wall above it, water accumulates and penetrates without step flashing and counter-flashing
Valley intersections — where two roof slopes meet, water concentration is highest and flashing failure causes the most concentrated water intrusion



Vapor management:

Warm, moist air moving into a wall or roof assembly deposits moisture when it contacts a cold surface — this is condensation within the wall
In cold climates: vapor barrier on the warm side of insulation (interior face of the wall) prevents warm interior air from carrying moisture into the wall cavity
In hot-humid climates: vapor barrier on the exterior face of the wall prevents exterior moisture from entering the wall
In folk construction without dedicated vapor barriers: thick earthen plaster on both faces of a wall moderates moisture movement through the wall and provides buffering capacity that reduces condensation risk

Foundation drainage:

Water that accumulates adjacent to the foundation creates hydrostatic pressure against foundation walls and saturates the soil, reducing its bearing capacity
Foundation drainage prevents this accumulation — a perforated drain pipe in a gravel trench at the base of the foundation, sloped to drain away from the building
In folk construction where drainage pipe is unavailable: a gravel-filled trench around the foundation perimeter that allows water to drain away from the structure provides meaningful improvement over undrained conditions

Structural inspection program:
Annual inspection requirements for all occupied structures:

Visual inspection of all accessible structural elements — look for: sagging, bowing, cracking, rot, insect damage, settlement
Inspection of foundation perimeter — look for: settlement cracks, water staining, erosion, heaving
Inspection of roof — look for: sagging ridgeline, damaged or missing roofing, blocked gutters and valleys
Inspection of all connections — look for: separated connections, missing fasteners, rust and corrosion on metal connectors

For multifamily and community buildings:

Semi-annual inspection minimum
Documented inspection record with photographs of any observed distress
Any observed structural distress shall be evaluated by the most structurally knowledgeable community member available within 30 days of observation
Structures with significant structural distress shall be evaluated for occupancy restriction until repairs are made

Rationale: The nonlinear relationship between section loss and strength loss in rotted wood is included because it explains why visible rot is more serious than it appears. A floor joist that has lost 25% of its depth to rot at the bottom — visible as soft, discolored wood along the bottom edge — has not lost 25% of its structural capacity. Bending strength is proportional to the square of the section depth — a 10-inch joist that loses 2.5 inches of depth at the bottom retains only (7.5/10)² = 56% of its original bending strength. The visible 25% section loss represents a 44% strength reduction. Community members performing structural inspections shall understand this relationship — what looks like minor surface rot may represent a structurally significant strength reduction.
The flashing emphasis is grounded in the practical observation that the majority of building moisture problems — and the structural deterioration they cause — originate at flashings that were not installed, were installed incorrectly, or have failed over time. A well-built structure with inadequate flashing at a critical location will develop moisture intrusion at that location within years, producing rot and deterioration in structural elements that appear intact from the exterior. A less well-built structure with excellent flashing at all critical locations will remain dry and structurally sound. Flashing is the most cost-effective long-term structural maintenance investment available — the cost of correct flashing at construction is a small fraction of the cost of repairing structural damage from moisture intrusion over the life of the building.
