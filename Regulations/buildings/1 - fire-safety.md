## 1.1 How Fire Spreads — The Physics of Fire Propagation

**Standard:** This standard exists as a foundational rule because every subsequent fire safety standard is an application of fire physics. A community member who understands how fire actually moves makes correct decisions when confronting situations this document did not anticipate — including the improvised construction, limited resources, and degraded response capacity that characterize the context this document addresses.

**The three mechanisms of fire spread:**

**Radiation:**

- Hot burning material emits thermal radiation in all directions — the same mechanism by which the sun warms the earth
- Radiation intensity decreases with the square of distance — double the distance, quarter the radiation intensity. This is why spacing between structures works
- Radiation ignites combustible materials when the heat flux exceeds the ignition threshold of the receiving material — typically 12-20 kilowatts per square meter for wood surfaces
- Glass windows fail under radiant heat before the surrounding wall — a window facing a burning structure becomes an opening that admits flames and hot gases within minutes of exposure to significant radiant heat
- Dark surfaces absorb more radiation than light surfaces — a dark roof absorbs significantly more radiant heat from a neighboring fire than a light-colored roof at equivalent distance
- Radiation is the primary spread mechanism at moderate distances — 10 to 50 feet between structures

**Convection:**

- Hot gases rise — heated air and combustion products carry heat upward and in the direction of wind
- Convection is the primary mechanism of fire spread within a structure — hot gases accumulate at the ceiling, heat the ceiling and upper walls, and eventually ignite the entire upper portion of the room simultaneously in what is called flashover
- Flashover is the transition from a fire in a room to a room on fire — it occurs when the upper gas layer reaches approximately 1100°F and everything in the room simultaneously ignites. Survival after flashover is essentially impossible
- At community scale convection creates the fire plume above a burning structure — a column of hot gas and embers rising hundreds of feet and traveling downwind
- The convective plume is what carries embers to start spot fires ahead of the main fire front — the primary mechanism of rapid fire spread in wind-driven events

**Conduction:**

- Heat transfer through direct material contact
- Less significant for community-scale fire spread than radiation and convection
- Most significant for spread through shared construction elements — a shared floor joist, a party wall with combustible framing, a roof that spans between units
- Metal conducts heat rapidly — a metal roof or metal wall panel conducts heat to combustible materials behind it faster than wood

**Wind — the multiplier:**

- Wind transforms all three spread mechanisms simultaneously
- Wind increases radiation exposure by tilting flames toward unburned structures — a burning structure in 20 mph wind exposes neighboring structures to direct flame contact that would not occur in still air
- Wind dramatically increases convection velocity — the ember transport distance in a 20 mph wind is many times greater than in still air
- Wind dries vegetation and building materials ahead of the fire, reducing ignition resistance
- Wind-driven fires move faster than any community fire response can manage — the 2018 Camp Fire destroyed Paradise, California at a rate of one football field per second at its peak
- Every significant urban fire disaster in history involved wind — the Great Fire of London 1666, the Chicago Fire 1871, the San Francisco Fire 1906, the Tokyo Fire 1923, the Lahaina Fire 2023
- The community fire safety plan shall explicitly address wind — prevailing wind direction, seasonal wind patterns, and the implications for which structures are upwind versus downwind of the community core

**Fire behavior in different construction types:**

**Masonry and earthen construction:**

- Non-combustible walls do not contribute fuel to the fire
- Fire is contained within the structure — the walls remain standing even as interior contents burn
- Provides meaningful separation time for adjacent structures — 30-60 minutes or more depending on wall thickness and construction quality
- Failure mode: roof collapse as roof framing burns, window and door openings admit flames and embers to adjacent structures
- Historical urban fires in masonry cities — London after the Great Fire rebuilt in brick — were significantly more containable than equivalent fires in timber cities

**Thick timber construction:**

- Solid timber members char on the exterior, insulating the interior wood from ignition — a 6-inch timber post will burn for hours before losing structural capacity
- Charring rate approximately 1.5 inches per hour for most wood species
- Provides meaningful fire resistance — more than light framing, less than masonry
- Failure mode: connections fail before members, joints and notches are points of accelerated charring

**Light wood framing — standard dimensional lumber:**

- The most common construction type in the modern built environment and the most fire-vulnerable
- 2x4 and 2x6 framing provides essentially no fire resistance — a standard light wood frame wall fails within 5-15 minutes of direct flame exposure
- Provides no meaningful separation between adjacent structures at close spacing
- Failure mode: rapid structural collapse, high ember production

**Metal cladding over combustible framing:**

- Metal skin does not ignite but conducts heat to the combustible framing behind it
- Provides some protection against radiant ignition from adjacent structures
- Does not prevent internal ignition from embers entering through gaps and openings
- Corrugated metal roofing is common in folk construction — it provides modest protection but gaps at ridges and edges are ember entry points

**Earthen construction — cob, adobe, rammed earth:**

- Non-combustible walls equivalent to masonry for fire separation purposes
- Excellent thermal mass — absorbs radiant heat without igniting
- Failure mode: roof construction, which is typically combustible even when walls are earthen. An earthen wall building with a light wood and thatch roof has excellent wall fire resistance and very poor roof fire resistance
- Adobe and cob buildings that have survived urban fires have typically done so with walls intact and roofs destroyed

**Fabric and tarp structures:**

- Highest fire risk of any construction type
- Ignite almost immediately on exposure to radiant heat or ember contact
- Produce toxic combustion products from synthetic materials
- Provide zero fire separation for adjacent structures
- Acceptable only at maximum spacing distances — treated as open air camping rather than permanent structure for fire safety purposes

**Rationale:** The physics-first approach is adopted here for the same reason it was adopted in the food safety document — a community member who understands why a standard exists can adapt it to circumstances the standard didn't anticipate. In a degraded infrastructure context where people are building with whatever materials are available, prescriptive standards ("use Type X wall assembly") are not actionable. The physics of radiation, convection, and conduction are always actionable — they allow a community member to evaluate any construction type against the fundamental question: how long will this material slow fire spread, and is that long enough for people to escape and for the fire not to reach the neighboring structure?

The wind section is emphasized here because it is the variable most consistently underestimated in community fire planning and most consistently identified in post-disaster investigations as the determining factor in catastrophic fire spread. Fire safety planning that does not account for wind is planning for the easy scenario — the still-air structure fire that stays in one building and is managed by response. The hard scenario — the wind-driven community fire that cannot be stopped — is the one that kills communities. The 2023 Lahaina fire destroyed 86% of the historic town in approximately two hours with winds gusting to 60-80 mph. The structures that survived were not significantly better built than those that burned — they survived because of their position relative to the wind direction and the fire's path. Community fire planning in this context must incorporate wind as a primary planning variable, not an afterthought.

The flashover concept is included because it defines the point of no return in structure fires — the moment after which survival inside the structure is essentially impossible and the character of the fire changes from a contained event to a structure-threatening event. A community member who understands flashover understands why early detection and early egress are the life safety priorities, why fire suppression efforts that begin after flashover are primarily property protection rather than life safety, and why the time between ignition and flashover — typically 5-10 minutes in a furnished room with light wood framing — defines the time budget for detection, warning, and evacuation.

The Great Fire of London is the most instructive historical example for communities in degraded infrastructure scenarios because it occurred in conditions that have direct parallels — a city with no professional fire department, limited water supply, and no coordinated response capacity. The fire burned for four days and destroyed 13,200 houses before being stopped — not by fire suppression but by firebreaks created by demolishing structures in the fire's path. The lesson was so thoroughly learned that the rebuilding regulations enacted afterward — requiring masonry construction, limiting building height, requiring party walls — established the framework that all subsequent building fire codes have elaborated. The fire safety standards in this document are in direct historical continuity with those 1667 London regulations.

## 1.2 Density Tiers and Their Implications

**Standard:** The fire safety standards that apply to any structure depend on its density tier — the concentration of structures and people in the surrounding area. A single folk structure in an open rural landscape presents fire risk primarily to its occupants. The same structure in a dense community core presents fire risk to every structure within fire spread distance. Density tier determines which standards are mandatory and which are offered as community knowledge service.

**Tier classification:**

**Tier 1 — Dispersed:**

- Structures separated by minimum 50 feet from any other structure
- Applies to: rural homesteads, isolated farm structures, structures at the community periphery
- Fire spread risk: primarily to occupants of the specific structure
- Mandatory standards: egress from sleeping areas, ignition source management
- Knowledge service: all structural and fire resistance standards offered but not required

**Tier 2 — Village scale:**

- Structures separated by 15-50 feet from neighboring structures
- Applies to: residential neighborhoods, small settlements, community periphery with moderate density
- Fire spread risk: significant — a structure fire can spread to neighbors under moderate wind conditions
- Mandatory standards: egress, minimum spacing, roof construction requirements, ignition source management
- Knowledge service: wall construction, compartmentalization

**Tier 3 — Dense community core:**

- Structures separated by less than 15 feet, or sharing walls, or in continuous building rows
- Applies to: urban core, market districts, dense residential areas
- Fire spread risk: high — a single structure fire in this tier can spread to an entire block under wind conditions
- Mandatory standards: full fire separation standards, construction material requirements, egress, community fire response infrastructure
- No folk construction exception in Tier 3 — the risk to neighbors is too high

**Tier determination:**

- Tier is determined by the relationship of a specific structure to its neighbors — not by a zone designation
- A structure that is currently Tier 1 becomes Tier 2 if a neighbor builds within 50 feet — the tier is dynamic and reflects actual conditions
- The community planning function shall maintain awareness of density tier transitions as the community develops
- When a community member proposes to build in a way that changes the tier of existing structures, the community review process shall inform affected neighbors

**The folk construction accommodation:**

- Folk construction — salvaged materials, earthen construction, non-standard framing, fabric and tarp structures — is explicitly permitted in Tier 1 and Tier 2 with appropriate spacing
- Folk construction in Tier 3 requires community review and approval — the fire risk to neighbors is too high for unreviewed construction
- The spacing requirements in 1.3 account for construction type — a fabric structure requires more spacing than a masonry structure at equivalent tier

**Rationale:** The density tier system is the mechanism by which this document respects personal autonomy while protecting community interests. The fundamental principle — that your fire risk becomes my fire risk when our structures are close enough for fire to spread between them — is the ethical foundation of mandatory fire standards. In Tier 1, fire spread to neighbors is unlikely — mandatory standards are minimal because the risk is primarily personal. In Tier 3, fire spread is nearly inevitable — mandatory standards are comprehensive because the risk is entirely communal.

The dynamic tier determination — based on actual spacing rather than zone designation — is a departure from conventional zoning practice but is more appropriate for a community building incrementally from a degraded baseline. Conventional zoning assumes a planning authority that designates zones before development occurs. The Society context more often involves existing structures of varying types and ages to which new structures are being added. The tier system that operates on actual conditions rather than planned designations is more honest about the risks that exist and more responsive to the community as it actually develops.

The Tier 3 folk construction restriction is the point where this document draws its firmest line — and the rationale deserves explicit statement. In Tier 1 and Tier 2, the primary risk of folk construction falls on the builder and their household. They can evaluate that risk and accept it. In Tier 3, the primary risk of folk construction falls on neighbors who did not choose the construction and cannot evaluate it. A family living in a dense community core next to a tarp structure has not consented to the fire risk that structure creates — it has been imposed on them. The restriction on folk construction in Tier 3 is not paternalism toward the builder — it is protection of the neighbors.

## 1.3 Spacing and Setbacks

**Standard:** Physical separation between structures is the most reliable and most universally applicable fire safety measure available — it works regardless of construction type, requires no materials, and degrades no faster than the space itself. Spacing requirements shall be understood as the primary fire safety standard for Tier 1 and Tier 2 structures, and as a supplementary standard for Tier 3 where construction type and compartmentalization carry the primary burden.

**Minimum spacing by construction type and tier:**

**Tier 1 — Dispersed:**

- Masonry and earthen construction: 30 feet minimum to any other structure
- Thick timber (minimum 4-inch members): 40 feet minimum
- Light wood framing: 50 feet minimum
- Metal cladding over combustible framing: 50 feet minimum
- Fabric and tarp structures: 75 feet minimum — fabric structures ignite and collapse rapidly, producing a large initial fire that radiates intense heat in all directions

**Tier 2 — Village scale:**

- Masonry and earthen construction: 15 feet minimum
- Thick timber: 20 feet minimum
- Light wood framing: 30 feet minimum
- Metal cladding over combustible framing: 30 feet minimum
- Fabric and tarp structures: 50 feet minimum — fabric structures in Tier 2 require review. Their rapid ignition and intense initial radiation make them problematic neighbors even at substantial spacing

**Tier 3 — Dense community core:**

- Masonry and earthen construction: shared wall or minimum 5 feet with fire-rated wall on the exposed face
- All other construction types: require community review. Minimum 10 feet with fire-resistant wall treatment on exposed faces
- Fabric and tarp structures: not permitted in Tier 3

**How spacing works — the physics:**

- Radiant heat intensity follows the inverse square law — double the distance, quarter the intensity
- At 10 feet from a fully involved structure, radiant heat flux typically exceeds 20 kW/m² — sufficient to ignite most combustible materials within seconds
- At 30 feet, radiant heat flux from a typical residential structure fire falls below 12 kW/m² — approaching but still above the piloted ignition threshold for wood
- At 50 feet, radiant heat flux falls below 5 kW/m² for most residential structure fires — below the ignition threshold for most building materials under most conditions
- These thresholds assume still air — wind tilts the flame toward the exposed structure and increases effective radiation by bringing the flame source closer

**Wind adjustment:**

- In communities with regular wind events above 20 mph, increase all spacing requirements by 50%
- A structure that is adequately spaced in still air may be inadequately spaced under wind conditions that bring flame into direct contact with the neighboring structure
- The prevailing wind direction shall be considered in site planning — structures downwind of potential ignition sources (cooking areas, heating fuel storage, forge and metalwork areas) require greater spacing than structures upwind

**Vegetation and fencing as fire vectors:**

- Dry vegetation between structures can carry fire across spacing gaps that would otherwise be adequate
- Spacing gaps between structures shall be maintained free of dry combustible vegetation — grass, shrubs, and brush that have dried seasonally
- Wooden fences connecting structures are continuous fire pathways — a fence connecting two structures at 30-foot spacing effectively eliminates the fire separation benefit of the spacing. Fences in spacing gaps shall be non-combustible (metal, masonry) or shall be interrupted with non-combustible gaps of minimum 5 feet at each structure
- Debris accumulation in spacing gaps — stored materials, firewood, discarded construction materials — reduces effective spacing. Spacing gaps are not storage areas

**Roof overhang considerations:**

- Roof overhangs extend the effective building footprint for fire spread purposes — spacing is measured from the outermost edge of the roof overhang, not the wall face
- Large overhangs common in vernacular construction — 3 feet or more — reduce the effective spacing between structures
- Overhangs on the fire-exposed face of a structure create a trap for radiant heat and ember accumulation — the underside of an overhang facing a neighboring fire collects heat and embers that ignite the overhang from below. Overhangs on fire-exposed faces shall be enclosed with non-combustible soffit material in Tier 2 and Tier 3

**Height and spacing interaction:**

- Taller structures create larger fire plumes and expose adjacent structures to heat at greater distances
- For structures above one story, increase spacing requirements by 10 feet per additional story above one
- A three-story light wood frame structure in Tier 2 requires 30 + 20 = 50 feet minimum spacing — equivalent to Tier 1 requirements — because its fire plume and radiant heat output is significantly greater than a single-story structure

**Rationale:** The spacing standards in this section are derived from fire physics rather than code convention — the inverse square law governing radiant heat intensity is not a regulatory choice, it is a physical fact that applies equally to a masonry building and a tarp structure. The material-differentiated spacing requirements reflect the different fire intensities and durations of different construction types — a masonry structure fire burns hot but contained while a fabric structure fire burns intensely for a brief period then collapses, radiating intense heat in all directions during its brief burning phase.

The wind adjustment is the most important departure from conventional code practice in this section. Standard building codes address wind in the context of wildland-urban interface fires but rarely require spacing adjustments for wind in urban or suburban contexts. The historical record is unambiguous — every catastrophic urban fire spread in history was wind-driven. The 2023 Lahaina fire killed 101 people and destroyed 2,200 structures in a community that met or exceeded standard code requirements for structure spacing. The wind converted adequate still-air spacing into inadequate wind-driven spacing. A community fire safety plan that does not explicitly account for wind is planning for the easy scenario.

The fence-as-fire-vector point is included because it is the most consistently overlooked spacing gap bridging issue in community fire planning. A wooden fence between two houses at 30-foot spacing is not an aesthetic element — it is a fuel bridge that can carry fire from one structure to the other in under a minute. Fence fires are documented in multiple urban fire disaster investigations as the mechanism by which fires jumped spacing gaps that should have been adequate. The requirement for non-combustible gaps in fences is a simple, inexpensive intervention that preserves the fire separation benefit of spacing.

The Lahaina fire of 2023 is the most directly relevant recent example for this document's context — a community with limited fire response capacity, a degraded water system that failed during the fire, wind conditions that exceeded all planning assumptions, and a built environment that could not contain the spread. The fire moved faster than evacuation could be organized, faster than any manual suppression could address, and faster than any mutual aid could arrive. The lesson is not that fire safety is hopeless in degraded conditions — it is that wind-driven fire in inadequately spaced construction is a catastrophe that no response can stop, and that prevention through spacing, construction type, and community planning is the only effective intervention.

## 1.4 Construction Materials and Fire Resistance

**Standard:** When structures must be closer than the spacing minimums in 1.3 — in Tier 2 and Tier 3 contexts — construction materials on fire-exposed faces determine how long a structure resists fire spread from a neighboring burning structure. This section provides a practical material hierarchy that applies to folk construction using available materials, not a prescriptive list of rated assemblies that assume industrial building materials.

**The material hierarchy — fire resistance from best to least:**

**Class 1 — Non-combustible masonry and earthen:**

- Solid brick, stone, concrete block, adobe, cob, rammed earth — minimum 8 inches thick
- Does not ignite, does not contribute fuel, provides 60+ minutes of fire separation
- Failure mode: mortar joints in unreinforced masonry may fail under sustained heat. Earthen construction may crack and spall under thermal shock from water application
- For folk construction: adobe and cob walls of adequate thickness are equivalent to brick for fire separation purposes — earthen construction is not inferior construction for fire resistance

**Class 2 — Non-combustible masonry and earthen, thinner sections:**

- Brick, stone, concrete block, adobe, cob — 4 to 8 inches thick
- Does not ignite, provides 30-60 minutes of fire separation
- Adequate for Tier 2 exposed walls. Marginal for Tier 3 party walls

**Class 3 — Thick timber:**

- Solid wood members minimum 4 inches in any dimension — posts, beams, log walls, timber planking
- Chars at approximately 1.5 inches per hour — a 6-inch timber provides approximately 2 hours before charring reaches the center
- Provides 30-60 minutes of fire resistance depending on member dimensions
- Failure mode: connections and joints char faster than solid members — a notched joint in a timber wall is a point of accelerated failure

**Class 4 — Metal cladding:**

- Corrugated metal roofing, metal wall panels, flattened metal sheeting over framing
- Does not ignite but conducts heat to combustible framing behind it
- Provides 15-30 minutes of protection against radiant ignition from adjacent fires
- Failure mode: gaps at ridges, edges, and penetrations admit embers. Heat conduction to interior combustibles. Metal expands and gaps open under sustained heat
- Improvement: metal cladding over an earthen plaster layer over light framing approaches Class 2 performance on the exposed face

**Class 5 — Light wood framing with plaster or earthen render:**

- Standard dimensional lumber framing (2x4, 2x6) with earthen plaster, lime plaster, or clay render on the exterior face
- The plaster layer provides 15-30 minutes of protection — it is non-combustible and insulates the framing behind it
- Failure mode: plaster cracks and spalls under thermal shock, exposing framing. Corners and edges are points of early failure
- Significantly better than unprotected light framing — earthen render is an accessible folk construction improvement that meaningfully increases fire resistance

**Class 6 — Unprotected light wood framing:**

- Standard dimensional lumber framing with wood siding, board and batten, or similar combustible exterior
- Provides essentially no fire separation — ignites within minutes of exposure to significant radiant heat
- Acceptable only in Tier 1 at full spacing distances
- Not acceptable on fire-exposed faces in Tier 2 or Tier 3 without treatment to achieve at least Class 4 performance

**Class 7 — Fabric and tarp:**

- Canvas, polyethylene tarp, woven plastic, and similar flexible materials
- Ignites almost immediately on ember contact or significant radiant heat exposure
- Produces toxic combustion products from synthetic materials
- Acceptable only in Tier 1 at maximum spacing distances
- Not acceptable in Tier 2 or Tier 3 for permanent or semi-permanent structures

**Application of the material hierarchy:**

**Tier 1:**

- Any material class acceptable at appropriate spacing distances
- Class 7 requires maximum spacing — 75 feet or more

**Tier 2:**

- Fire-exposed wall faces (walls facing within 30 feet of any other structure) shall achieve minimum Class 4 performance
- A Class 6 structure in Tier 2 shall either: increase spacing to Tier 1 minimums, or apply earthen render, metal cladding, or other treatment to achieve Class 4 on exposed faces
- Rear and side walls not facing other structures: any class acceptable

**Tier 3:**

- Fire-exposed wall faces shall achieve minimum Class 2 performance
- Party walls — walls shared with or directly adjacent to neighboring structures — shall achieve minimum Class 1 performance
- Masonry or earthen construction is the practical standard for Tier 3 — light framing even with treatment does not reliably achieve the separation times required at Tier 3 spacing

**Roof construction — the most vulnerable surface:**

Roofs are the primary surface through which embers enter structures and the primary surface through which fire spreads between structures in wind-driven events.

**Roof material hierarchy:**

- Non-combustible: metal roofing, clay or concrete tile, slate — does not ignite from ember contact
- Semi-combustible: treated wood shingles, asphalt shingles — resistant to ember ignition but will ignite under sustained ember accumulation or direct flame contact
- Combustible: untreated wood shingles, thatch, fabric — ignites readily from ember contact

- In Tier 2 and Tier 3: roof surfaces shall achieve minimum semi-combustible performance
- In Tier 3: non-combustible roof surfaces required
- The most critical roof vulnerability is not the surface material but the gaps and openings — ridge vents, eave gaps, and gutter debris accumulation allow embers to enter the roof structure and ignite from inside. These openings shall be screened with fine metal mesh (1/8 inch or finer) in Tier 2 and Tier 3

**The earthen render upgrade:**

- Throughout this section earthen render — clay plaster, lime plaster, or cob applied to the exterior face of combustible construction — appears as a meaningful improvement
- This deserves explicit emphasis because earthen render is the most accessible folk construction fire resistance upgrade available — it requires no industrial materials, is producible from local soil in most locations, and can be applied to existing light framing structures
- A light wood frame structure with 1-2 inches of earthen render on the exterior moves from Class 6 to approximately Class 5 — a meaningful improvement in communities where light framing is the only available material
- Earthen render application requires: appropriate soil mix (clay content 15-30%, sand balance, fiber reinforcement from straw or grass), adequate thickness (minimum 1 inch, 2 inches preferred), and protection from rain erosion through lime finish coat or roof overhang

**Rationale:** The material hierarchy replaces the rated assembly system of conventional fire codes because rated assemblies assume industrial building materials and laboratory testing — neither of which is available in folk construction contexts. The hierarchy is derived from the same fire physics as rated assemblies — the thermal properties of different materials — but expressed in practical terms that allow evaluation of any construction type including improvised and traditional materials.

The earthen construction equivalence to masonry deserves particular emphasis because it contradicts the prejudice against earthen construction that pervades conventional building codes. Adobe, cob, and rammed earth walls of adequate thickness are genuinely fire-resistant — they do not burn, they conduct heat slowly due to thermal mass, and they have a documented historical record of containing fires. The 1906 San Francisco earthquake and fire destroyed the majority of the city's wood-frame construction. Adobe structures in the Mission District, many of them poorly maintained, survived the fire because their earthen walls provided genuine fire separation. The code prejudice against earthen construction is driven by structural concerns — unreinforced earthen construction performs poorly in earthquakes — not by fire concerns. For fire resistance in non-seismic or low-seismic contexts, earthen construction is an excellent choice.

The roof vulnerability emphasis reflects direct operational experience — structure fires that spread to adjacent structures in urban contexts most commonly do so via roof-to-roof ember transport rather than wall-to-wall radiation. The roof surface material matters, but the gaps and penetrations in the roof structure matter more — an ember that lands on a metal roof cannot ignite the roof surface, but an ember that enters the attic through an unscreened ridge vent ignites the roof structure from inside, where suppression is difficult and spread is rapid. Screening roof openings is a simple, inexpensive intervention that dramatically reduces ember intrusion risk.

## 1.5 Egress — The Life Safety Standard

**Standard:** Egress — the ability of occupants to exit a burning structure safely — is the single most important life safety standard in this document. More people die in building fires from failed egress than from any other cause. Egress standards apply to every occupied structure regardless of tier, regardless of construction type, and regardless of whether the structure is owner-occupied or multifamily. Egress is the one standard where folk construction provides no exception — every person sleeping in a structure has the right to a way out.

**The fundamental egress principle:**

Every sleeping area shall have two independent means of egress. Independent means that if one egress path is blocked by fire, smoke, or structural failure, the other remains available. Two doors from the same room to the same corridor are not independent — both become blocked if the corridor fills with smoke. Independent egress means paths that diverge at the point of origin and lead to different exits from the building or directly to the exterior.

**Why two exits:**

- The primary cause of residential fire death is not burns — it is smoke inhalation during attempted egress through a fire-blocked path
- A person who wakes to a smoke-filled structure and finds their single egress path blocked has no safe option
- The second egress path does not need to be a door — a window of adequate size leading to the exterior is a functional second egress for ground floor and second floor sleeping rooms
- The two-exit requirement is the most consistently validated life safety standard in fire research — structures with two functional egress paths have dramatically lower occupant fatality rates than those with one

**Egress requirements by structure type:**

**Single story structures — all tiers:**

- Minimum one door to the exterior from any sleeping area, or one window meeting the size requirements below
- Preferred: one door plus one window — window provides second egress if door path is blocked by fire
- Door shall swing outward or be a sliding type — inward-swinging doors can be blocked by a collapsed occupant or debris on the interior

**Multi-story structures:**

- Ground floor: same as single story — door to exterior plus window egress
- Upper floors: minimum one stairway to ground floor plus one window meeting egress size requirements
- The window on upper floors is egress of last resort — it requires either a drop to grade that is survivable (maximum 13 feet to grade for a second floor window) or the availability of a ladder or external fire escape
- In Tier 3 multifamily buildings: two separate stairways required for buildings above two stories — one stairway serving as the primary egress for one end of the building and the second stairway serving the other end

**Window egress requirements:**

- Minimum opening size: 5.7 square feet — the minimum through which an adult can pass carrying a child
- Minimum opening height: 24 inches
- Minimum opening width: 20 inches
- Maximum sill height from floor: 44 inches — a sill higher than 44 inches requires a step or furniture to reach, which may be impossible in a smoke-filled room
- The window shall be openable from inside without tools, keys, or special knowledge — a window that requires a specific technique to open will not be opened by a panicked occupant in a smoke-filled room at 3am
- Window bars, grilles, and security screens on egress windows shall have a release mechanism operable from inside without tools — security bars that cannot be opened from inside convert an egress window into a death trap. This is one of the most consistently documented causes of fire death in communities using window bars for security

**Egress path standards:**

**Width:**

- Minimum 28 inches clear width through any egress path — the minimum for a person carrying another person
- Egress paths shall be maintained clear at all times — stored materials, furniture, and equipment in egress paths shall be removed. A code-compliant egress that is blocked by stored firewood or furniture kills people as effectively as no egress at all

**Travel distance:**

- The distance from any point in a sleeping area to an exit shall not exceed 50 feet
- In structures where travel distance exceeds 50 feet, intermediate smoke barriers — doors that close automatically or can be closed — reduce the effective travel distance by compartmentalizing smoke spread

**Stair geometry:**

- Stair width: minimum 36 inches
- Riser height: maximum 8.25 inches — steeper risers cause falls during emergency descent, particularly for elderly occupants and children
- Tread depth: minimum 9 inches
- Handrail: required on at least one side of any stair more than 3 risers — a handrail is the primary means of maintaining control during descent in low-visibility conditions
- Headroom: minimum 6 feet 8 inches — lower headroom causes head strikes during rapid descent
- These stair dimensions are grounded in emergency descent research — they represent the geometry at which falls and injuries during emergency stair descent are minimized. Steeper, narrower stairs with inadequate headroom are documented causes of egress-related injury in fire evacuation

**Egress for folk structures:**

- Folk construction egress shall meet the same functional requirements as standard construction egress — the geometry and independence requirements apply regardless of construction type
- A salvaged-material structure with a door that swings out and a window of adequate size meets egress requirements
- A tarp structure with a single opening does not meet egress requirements regardless of how large the opening is — the opening can be blocked by collapsed fabric

**Early warning — smoke detection:**

- Egress is only useful if occupants are awake and aware of the fire in time to use it
- Every sleeping area shall have a functional smoke alarm or equivalent early warning system
- In the absence of commercial smoke alarms: interconnected bells or whistles that can be triggered by a community fire watch, a dog trained to alert to smoke, or a community member designated as fire watch during high-risk periods (high wind, dry conditions, active cooking fires nearby) are acceptable functional alternatives
- The specific technology matters less than the outcome — occupants in sleeping areas shall have reliable early warning of fire with sufficient time to use egress paths

**Egress discharge:**

- Egress paths shall lead to a location that is safe — not into an enclosed courtyard with no further exit, not into an area between burning structures
- Egress discharge shall provide access to open space away from the structure — a public path, an open area, or a community assembly point
- In Tier 3 dense construction: egress discharge shall not require passing adjacent to another structure's wall — the discharge route shall lead directly to a street, alley, or open space

**Rationale:** The window bar warning is included because window bars on egress windows are among the most consistently documented causes of fire death in communities that use them for security — and communities in degraded infrastructure scenarios are precisely the communities most likely to use window bars. The conflict between security and egress is real — window bars genuinely improve security against intrusion. The solution is release mechanisms operable from inside, not the removal of bars. A quick-release bar system that can be opened from inside in seconds provides security equivalent to fixed bars while preserving egress. The community knowledge service shall include instruction in quick-release bar installation for community members who use window bars.

The stored materials in egress paths issue is addressed here because it is the most common egress failure mode in real fire events and the one most amenable to community-level intervention. People store things in hallways, on stairs, and in front of doors because those spaces are convenient and the fire that will make those storage decisions deadly is abstract and distant. A community that conducts regular egress checks — walking every egress path in every community building to verify it is clear — converts an abstract requirement into a maintained reality. The egress check is a simple, no-cost intervention that the community fire safety officer can conduct quarterly.

The stair geometry standards are derived from injury data rather than code convention — the maximum riser height of 8.25 inches and minimum tread depth of 9 inches represent the geometry at which stair-related injuries during emergency descent are minimized based on research on stair falls in emergency conditions. Steeper stairs that are more space-efficient in folk construction contexts produce significantly higher fall rates during emergency descent, particularly for elderly occupants descending in darkness or smoke. The geometry standards are not arbitrary — they represent the accumulated learning from decades of stair fall injury research.

## 1.6 Compartmentalization

**Standard:** Compartmentalization is the division of a structure into fire-resistant sections that contain a fire within its room or unit of origin long enough for occupants to escape and long enough to prevent spread to adjacent units or structures. In single-family structures compartmentalization is a knowledge service standard — it extends survival time but its absence primarily affects the occupants of that structure. In multifamily structures and community buildings compartmentalization is a mandatory standard — its absence allows fire to spread to units whose occupants had no role in starting the fire.

**What compartmentalization achieves:**

A fire in a single room of a well-compartmentalized building remains in that room for 20-60 minutes before breaching the surrounding walls, floor, and ceiling. During that time:

- Occupants of the fire room have time to escape
- Occupants of adjacent rooms and units are warned and can escape before their spaces are threatened
- Community fire response — however limited — has time to arrive and intervene
- The fire does not simultaneously threaten all occupants of the building

A fire in an unpartitioned open structure — a large open barn, a warehouse converted to multifamily housing without interior walls, a folk structure with no interior doors — reaches every part of the structure simultaneously. All occupants face simultaneous threat with no warning time advantage for those furthest from the fire origin.

**Horizontal compartmentalization — walls between spaces:**

**Within a single unit:**

- Interior walls between sleeping areas and living areas are the most important single-unit compartmentalization element — a closed bedroom door is documented to reduce bedroom temperature during a living room fire by 900°F or more, providing sleeping occupants significant additional escape time
- A closed door is the simplest, most accessible compartmentalization measure available — it costs nothing, requires no materials, and provides 10-20 minutes of protection even in light wood frame construction
- Community fire education shall specifically address the practice of sleeping with bedroom doors closed — it is the single most effective personal fire safety behavior available

**Between units in multifamily buildings:**

- Walls separating units in multifamily buildings shall achieve minimum Class 2 fire resistance — masonry, earthen construction, or equivalent
- Light wood frame party walls between units in multifamily buildings are the most common cause of multi-unit fire losses in residential buildings — a fire in one unit breaches a light frame party wall within 5-15 minutes and simultaneously threatens the adjacent unit
- In folk construction multifamily buildings where masonry party walls are not achievable: double wall construction — two separate light frame walls with an air gap between them — provides improved separation compared to a single shared wall. Each unit's wall stands independently — if one chars and fails the other remains

**Vertical compartmentalization — floors and ceilings:**

**Floor-ceiling assemblies:**

- The floor-ceiling assembly between stories is the primary vertical compartmentalization element
- An unprotected wood joist floor provides approximately 5-10 minutes of vertical separation — fire burns through from below rapidly
- Earthen plaster or lime plaster on the ceiling below the floor joists significantly improves vertical separation — approaching 20-30 minutes for a plastered ceiling
- In multifamily buildings with multiple stories: vertical compartmentalization shall achieve minimum 20-minute separation — achievable with plastered ceiling assemblies over wood joists

**Utility penetrations:**

- Every hole through a fire-resistant wall, floor, or ceiling for pipes, wires, ducts, or other utilities is a compartmentalization breach — fire follows utility runs through fire-resistant assemblies
- Penetrations through compartmentalization elements shall be sealed with non-combustible material — earthen plaster, mortar, or mineral wool packed tightly around the penetration
- This requirement is frequently overlooked in folk construction — a carefully built masonry wall with unsealed pipe penetrations provides significantly less than its potential fire separation because fire travels through the penetrations

**Community buildings — assembly spaces, markets, medical facilities:**

**Higher compartmentalization requirements:**

- Community buildings with assembly occupancy — spaces where people gather in groups — shall have compartmentalization between the assembly space and any adjacent storage, mechanical, or service spaces
- Storage areas adjacent to assembly spaces are the most common ignition source for assembly building fires — combustible storage next to a heat source or electrical connection ignites and spreads to the assembly space where many people are present
- The separation between storage and assembly shall achieve minimum Class 2 fire resistance — masonry or earthen construction preferred

**Exit access corridors:**

- In multifamily buildings and community buildings with interior corridors leading to exits: the corridor walls shall achieve minimum Class 2 fire resistance
- A fire that enters the egress corridor simultaneously threatens all occupants using that corridor for escape — corridor compartmentalization maintains the egress path as a survivable environment during escape

**Rationale:** The closed door research finding deserves emphasis because it is among the most actionable and least-known fire safety facts available to community members. A UL Firefighter Safety Research Institute study using instrumented test rooms found that the temperature in a bedroom with a closed door during a living room fire reached 100°F — uncomfortable but survivable. The temperature in an identical bedroom with an open door reached over 1000°F within the same time period — unsurvivable. The closed door is not merely slowing the fire — it is creating a fundamentally different thermal environment that defines the difference between survivable and unsurvivable conditions for the sleeping occupant. Community fire education that successfully instills the closed-door sleeping habit produces a measurable reduction in fire fatalities with no cost and no materials.

The double wall construction for folk multifamily buildings is included because it provides a meaningful improvement over single shared-wall construction using only the materials and skills required for the original construction. A community building multifamily housing from salvaged light framing cannot afford masonry party walls — but it can build two independent walls with a gap between them. Each wall provides some separation, and the gap between them prevents the thermal bridging that allows a single shared wall to conduct heat from one unit to the other. The improvement is meaningful — not equivalent to masonry but significantly better than a single shared wall — and achievable within the constraints of folk construction.

## 1.7 Ignition Source Management

**Standard:** Most structure fires begin with an identifiable ignition source — an open flame, an overheated surface, an electrical fault, or a smoldering ember. Managing ignition sources is the primary fire prevention standard — preventing fires from starting is categorically more effective than containing fires after they start. In a degraded infrastructure context where heating and cooking rely on open flame more than in modern infrastructure-dependent contexts, ignition source management is particularly critical.

**Cooking fires:**

**Open fire cooking:**

- Open fire cooking areas shall be located minimum 10 feet from any structure in Tier 1, minimum 15 feet in Tier 2, and shall not be permitted within enclosed structures in Tier 3 without a dedicated fire-safe cooking structure meeting the standards below
- Open cooking fires shall have a designated non-combustible base — stone, brick, or bare earth minimum 3 feet in all directions from the fire
- A fire tender shall be present at all times during open fire cooking — unattended cooking fires are the leading cause of outdoor fire spread in community settings
- Fire suppression capability — a bucket of water or sand — shall be immediately available at every open cooking fire

**Enclosed cooking — stoves, ranges, and ovens:**

- Cooking appliances shall have minimum 18 inches clearance to any combustible material on sides and rear, 30 inches clearance above the cooking surface
- Combustible materials stored above cooking surfaces — the most common arrangement in informal kitchens — shall be relocated. The space above a cooking surface shall be non-combustible
- Grease accumulation on cooking surfaces and in flues above cooking areas is a primary fire source in community kitchens — cooking surfaces and flue connections shall be cleaned of grease accumulation at minimum weekly in high-use community kitchens

**Heating appliances:**

**Wood stoves and fireplaces:**

- Stove clearances: minimum 18 inches to any combustible wall, floor, or ceiling on sides and rear. Minimum 36 inches to combustible materials in front of the firebox opening
- Non-combustible floor protection: a non-combustible hearth pad extending minimum 18 inches in front of the firebox opening and 8 inches on sides — catches embers that fall from the firebox during loading
- Single-wall stovepipe clearance: minimum 18 inches to any combustible material — single-wall stovepipe runs very hot on the exterior surface and ignites combustibles that contact or closely approach it
- Double-wall insulated stovepipe: minimum 2 inches to combustible material — the insulating airspace reduces exterior surface temperature significantly

**Chimneys and flues:**

- The primary chimney fire ignition mechanism is creosote accumulation in the flue — creosote is a byproduct of incomplete combustion that deposits on flue walls and ignites when flue temperatures are high
- Chimneys shall be inspected and cleaned of creosote accumulation at minimum annually, or more frequently in high-use applications
- Signs of creosote accumulation: visible black deposits in the firebox, tarry odor from the chimney, reduced draft
- Chimney fires — combustion of accumulated creosote — burn at very high temperatures (over 2000°F) and can ignite the surrounding structure through conduction through the chimney walls. A chimney fire is a structure fire emergency requiring immediate response

**Chimney clearances:**

- Chimney shall extend minimum 2 feet above any roof surface within 10 feet horizontally — this height ensures that the hot gas column exiting the chimney is above the wind turbulence zone created by the roof, preventing downdrafts that push smoke and embers back into the structure and preventing sparks from landing on the roof surface
- Spark arrestors — a metal mesh cap over the chimney opening — shall be installed on all chimneys in Tier 2 and Tier 3. Spark arrestors prevent embers from exiting the chimney and landing on the roof or adjacent structures. Mesh size: 3/8 to 1/2 inch — fine enough to arrest significant embers, coarse enough not to restrict draft significantly

**Electrical ignition sources:**

In degraded infrastructure contexts, electrical systems are frequently improvised — salvaged wire, non-standard connections, overloaded circuits. These are among the most common ignition sources in informal settlements globally.

- Electrical connections shall be mechanically secure and insulated — exposed wire connections that can arc are the primary electrical fire ignition source
- Extension cords are temporary connections — they shall not be used as permanent wiring. Extension cords routed under rugs, through walls, or across traffic paths are both fire and shock hazards
- Overloaded circuits — connecting more load than the wire is rated to carry — cause the wire to heat and eventually ignite surrounding materials. The wire rating and the load shall be matched
- Community electrical systems shall include overcurrent protection — fuses or circuit breakers — that interrupt the circuit before wiring reaches ignition temperature. A fuse or breaker sized larger than the wire it protects provides no protection — the wire heats and ignites before the overcurrent device trips

**Fuel storage:**

**Liquid fuels — kerosene, gasoline, alcohol:**

- Liquid fuel storage shall be in sealed containers specifically designed for the fuel type — not in open containers, not in food containers, not in containers that previously held incompatible materials
- Fuel storage shall be outside of occupied structures in Tier 1 and Tier 2 — in a separate non-combustible structure or a dedicated fuel storage area minimum 15 feet from occupied structures
- In Tier 3: fuel storage quantities within occupied structures limited to 1 day's use — larger quantities in designated external storage
- Fueling of any appliance shall occur outside and with the appliance cool — fueling a hot appliance or fueling indoors with a spill risk is an immediate ignition source

**Rationale:** The creosote chimney fire standard is included with particular emphasis because chimney fires are the most common structural fire ignition source in communities relying on wood heating — and they are almost entirely preventable through regular cleaning. The physics of creosote deposition are straightforward: slow-burning fires with unseasoned wood produce more creosote than fast-burning fires with dry wood because incomplete combustion at lower temperatures produces more condensable tar compounds. A community that burns wet or green wood in slow-burning overnight fires is producing creosote at high rates and requires more frequent chimney cleaning than one burning dry wood in hot fires. The annual cleaning standard is a minimum — communities burning significant quantities of unseasoned wood shall clean more frequently.

The electrical improvisation section addresses the reality that in degraded infrastructure contexts, community members will wire their own structures with whatever materials are available — salvaged wire, automotive fuses, improvised connections. This is not inherently dangerous if done with understanding of the underlying principles. The three principles — mechanical security of connections, matching wire size to load, and overcurrent protection sized to the wire — are the minimum knowledge required for safe improvised electrical work. A community that understands these three principles will make better decisions when improvising electrical systems than one that has only been told not to improvise.

The extension cord permanent wiring prohibition addresses one of the most consistent fire hazards in informal residential construction globally — extension cords used as permanent wiring because permanent wiring materials are unavailable or expensive. Extension cords are designed for temporary use at light loads — they are not insulated or constructed for the continuous heavy loads of permanent wiring and their connections are not designed to remain secure under repeated thermal cycling. A community that cannot install permanent wiring shall use battery storage for lighting and minimal electrical loads rather than overloaded extension cord wiring for high-current applications.

## 1.8 Community Fire Response Infrastructure

**Standard:** In a degraded infrastructure scenario, professional fire department response cannot be assumed. The community's fire response capacity — water supply, access, organization, equipment, and the triage decisions that determine where limited resources are deployed — is the difference between a contained fire and a community-level catastrophe. This section addresses the infrastructure that enables community fire response, and the strategic thinking that makes limited response effective.

**Water supply:**

**The primary constraint:**

- Community fire response without adequate water supply is fundamentally limited — suppression of a structure fire requires sustained water application that exceeds what bucket brigades can provide for a fully involved structure
- A fully involved single-family structure requires approximately 500 gallons per minute for effective suppression — a flow rate that requires either municipal water infrastructure, dedicated fire water storage, or a water source with adequate pump capacity
- Communities without this capacity shall design their fire response strategy around what is achievable — controlling spread rather than suppressing the source fire in many scenarios

**Water supply options by community scale:**

**Community cisterns:**

- Underground or surface water storage dedicated to fire suppression
- Minimum useful capacity: 5,000 gallons for a small community cistern — provides approximately 10 minutes of suppression at meaningful flow rates
- Location: within 500 feet of the structures it serves, accessible by the community's pump equipment
- Construction: non-combustible, frost-protected in cold climates, covered to prevent evaporation and contamination
- A network of smaller cisterns distributed through the community is preferable to a single large cistern — it provides redundancy and reduces the maximum distance from cistern to fire

**Natural water sources:**

- Ponds, streams, and rivers within the community can serve as fire water sources with appropriate pump equipment
- Access: a hard surface or stable path from the water source to within pump suction distance (typically 20-25 feet) of the water surface, accessible year-round
- Dry season flow: natural sources that dry seasonally are unreliable as primary fire water supplies — assess year-round availability

**Rainwater collection:**

- Community cisterns fed by roof collection from large community buildings can maintain significant water reserves
- The fire water supply shall be separated from the potable water supply — fire water does not need to meet potable standards and maintaining it as a separate dedicated reserve ensures it is available when needed

**Pumping equipment:**

- A gasoline or diesel powered pump capable of moving 100-200 gallons per minute provides meaningful suppression capacity for exposures and incipient fires
- Pump maintenance: community fire pumps shall be started and run under load monthly — a pump that has not been run in 6 months will not start reliably in an emergency
- Fuel supply: fire pump fuel shall be stored in a dedicated reserve — not drawn from the community's general fuel supply during an emergency when that supply may also be depleted

**Access routes:**

**Vehicle access:**

- Where the community has vehicle-based fire response capability, access routes shall be maintained with minimum 12 feet width clearance and minimum 13 feet 6 inches vertical clearance
- Access routes shall be passable in all weather conditions — unpaved routes that become impassable in rain provide no access when fire risk from lightning and cooking fires is often highest

**Foot access:**

- Even without vehicle response, foot access to all structures through maintained paths allows personnel and equipment movement
- Dead-end paths with no through access create fire response problems — personnel advancing on a fire along a dead-end path have no escape route if the fire advances faster than expected
- Minimum path width for foot access with equipment: 6 feet

**Community fire response organization:**

This is the section where the triage reality must be addressed directly.

**The triage principle:**

- In a degraded infrastructure scenario with limited water, limited equipment, and limited personnel, not every fire can be fought and not every structure can be saved
- The decision about where to commit limited resources shall be made in advance through community planning — not in the moment under stress, with incomplete information, and with the emotional pressure of watching community members' homes burn
- Pre-planned triage decisions are made rationally. In-moment triage decisions are made under panic

**Triage framework — structure prioritization:**

**Priority 1 — Defend at all costs:**

- Structures whose loss would be catastrophic for the entire community: water treatment, food storage, medical facility, community power infrastructure
- Structures whose loss would trigger cascading failures: the building that houses the seed stock, the structure containing irreplaceable community records, the workshop housing the only metalworking capacity
- These structures shall be identified in advance, their locations known to all community fire response personnel, and their defense prioritized explicitly in the response plan

**Priority 2 — Defend if defensible:**

- Structures that can be saved with available resources — they are not yet involved, fire spread to them is not yet inevitable, and available water and personnel are sufficient to prevent ignition
- Characteristics of a defensible structure: non-combustible or fire-resistant construction, adequate spacing from the burning structure, access for water application, no combustible debris on roof or in gutters
- A structure is not defensible if: it is already involved in fire, its spacing from the burning structure is inadequate, its construction type means fire spread is already inevitable regardless of intervention, or defending it would require committing all resources and leaving other structures unprotected

**Priority 3 — Monitor and contain:**

- Structures that are already lost — fully involved or with fire spread inevitable — but whose burning creates spread risk to higher-priority structures
- Action: apply water to adjacent Priority 1 and 2 structures to prevent ignition, not to suppress the burning structure
- Commit to accepting the loss rather than committing resources to a losing battle

**Sacrificial firebreaks:**

- In a rapidly spreading community fire, creating firebreaks by demolishing structures in the fire's path may be the most effective containment strategy available — the same approach that stopped the Great Fire of London
- The decision to sacrifice a structure to create a firebreak is a community-level decision requiring leadership authorization — it shall not be made by individual response personnel
- Structures identified in advance as potential sacrificial firebreak points — based on their position relative to prevailing wind direction and their relationship to Priority 1 structures — allow this decision to be made rationally in pre-planning rather than in the moment

**Community fire response roles:**

**Fire safety officer:**

- Designated community member responsible for: maintaining the community fire response plan, conducting regular drills, maintaining equipment, and serving as incident commander during fire events
- The fire safety officer role requires training in basic fire behavior, water supply management, and incident command — not professional firefighter training but informed leadership
- A backup fire safety officer shall be trained and designated — the primary officer may be absent or injured during an event

**Response team:**

- Minimum 4 community members trained in basic fire response operations — pump operation, hose handling, exposure protection
- Response team members shall know the location of all Priority 1 structures, all water supply points, and all access routes
- Regular drills — minimum quarterly — maintain response readiness and identify gaps in planning

**Warning and communication:**

- A community fire warning system shall be established and known to all community members — a bell, horn, or other signal audible throughout the community that means fire emergency
- The warning signal shall be distinct from other community signals
- All community members shall know the response to the warning signal: the evacuation assembly point, who is responsible for accounting for community members, and where to go if they are not in the immediate fire area

**Early detection:**

- Early detection dramatically expands response options — a fire detected at ignition is manageable. A fire detected at flashover is not
- Community fire watch during high-risk conditions — dry weather, high wind, active community cooking and heating — shall be established as a community responsibility
- A designated person monitoring for smoke and fire during overnight high-risk periods provides early warning that no installed detection system can match in folk construction contexts without electrical infrastructure

**Rationale:** The triage framework is the most original contribution of this section and the one most directly informed by professional fire response experience. Standard fire codes do not address triage because they assume unlimited professional response — the professional fire service's obligation is to attempt to save every structure and every life. The Society operates under different assumptions — limited resources, limited response capacity, and the responsibility to make difficult decisions that maximize community survival even when that means accepting the loss of individual structures.

The sacrificial firebreak concept has direct historical precedent. The Great Fire of London was stopped when King Charles II ordered the demolition of structures in the fire's path to create firebreaks. The Chicago Fire was contained when the fire reached areas of different construction type that provided natural firebreaks. The Lahaina fire was not contained because the community had no firebreak planning and no capacity to create them. A community that has identified potential firebreak positions in advance — and has the difficult community conversation about which structures might be sacrificed to save others — is better positioned to make that decision effectively when the moment arrives.

The monthly pump testing requirement is included because pump failure on demand is the single most common community fire response failure in informal and rural settings globally. A pump that starts reliably during monthly tests and is maintained with fresh fuel and clean water in the pump casing will start when needed. A pump that has sat for 6 months with stale fuel and a dry primer will not start at 2am during a community fire emergency. The monthly test is a 15-minute commitment that determines whether the community's primary suppression resource is functional when needed.

## 1.9 Vegetation Management and Defensible Space

**Standard:** Vegetation is fuel. In communities where structures are surrounded by natural vegetation — grasses, shrubs, trees — the management of that vegetation determines whether a wildland fire approaching the community ignites community structures, and whether a community structure fire spreads into the surrounding landscape. Vegetation management is most critical in Tier 1 and Tier 2 settings where structures are embedded in or adjacent to natural vegetation.

**The ember transport mechanism:**

The primary mechanism by which wildland fires ignite community structures is not flame contact — it is ember transport. Burning vegetation produces firebrands — burning fragments — that are carried aloft by the fire's convective plume and transported downwind for distances of up to a mile or more. These embers land on combustible surfaces — roofs, decks, stored materials, dry vegetation — and ignite new fires ahead of the main fire front.

A community structure can be ignited by embers from a fire that never approaches closer than a quarter mile. The spaces between structures, the roof surfaces, the gutters, and the stored materials around structures are the primary ember landing zones. Defensible space and ember-resistant construction address this mechanism — not by stopping the fire but by removing the fuel that embers would ignite.

**Defensible space zones:**

**Zone 1 — Immediate zone (0-5 feet from structure):**

- This zone shall contain no combustible materials — no stored firewood, no mulch, no dry vegetation, no stored equipment or materials
- Non-combustible ground cover — gravel, stone, bare earth — or low-moisture plants that do not carry fire
- This zone is the most critical — embers that land in this zone are immediately adjacent to the structure. Non-combustible ground cover in this zone prevents ember-to-structure ignition even when vegetation elsewhere is burning

**Zone 2 — Lean, clean, and green (5-30 feet from structure):**

- Vegetation maintained in a condition that does not carry fire readily
- Grass mowed short — below 4 inches — and kept green or brown-cut during fire season
- Shrubs spaced so that fire cannot ladder from ground to tree canopy — minimum 10 feet between shrubs
- Tree canopy maintained so that branches do not touch the structure and lower branches are at least 6 feet above the ground
- No continuous run of combustible vegetation connecting to adjacent structures or to wildland vegetation beyond Zone 2

**Zone 3 — Reduced fuel (30-100 feet from structure, where applicable):**

- Natural vegetation maintained at reduced density compared to undisturbed conditions
- Dead and down material removed — accumulated dead branches, fallen logs, and deep leaf litter are the primary ground fire fuel in this zone
- The goal is not eliminating vegetation but reducing its continuity and density so that fire moves through this zone more slowly and at lower intensity

**Seasonal management:**

- Vegetation management is not a one-time activity — it requires seasonal maintenance as vegetation dries and accumulates
- Pre-fire season inspection: before the dry season or fire season for the community's climate, Zone 1 and Zone 2 shall be inspected and maintained
- Post-fire season inspection: after fire season, accumulated material shall be cleared before the next fire season begins

**Roof and gutter management:**

- Roof surfaces and gutters are ember collection points — dry leaves, pine needles, and debris accumulating in gutters and on roof surfaces are ignition points for ember-started fires
- Gutters shall be cleaned of combustible debris at minimum twice yearly — before and after major leaf fall seasons
- Roof surfaces shall be inspected and cleared of debris accumulation at minimum annually
- Metal gutter guards that prevent debris accumulation are a worthwhile permanent investment for structures in Tier 1 and Tier 2 settings

**Rationale:** The ember transport distance — up to a mile or more — is included because it defines the scope of the wildland fire threat in a way that spacing and construction standards alone cannot address. A community that has achieved excellent spacing and fire-resistant construction but has accumulated dry grass and debris in Zone 1 around its structures can still be destroyed by embers from a fire that never approaches the community directly. The defensible space standards address the mechanism that spacing and construction cannot — the ignition of structures by transported embers landing on combustible surfaces adjacent to or on the structure.

The Zone 1 non-combustible ground cover requirement is the most consistently validated defensible space standard in post-fire structure survival research. Studies of structure survival in California wildfire zones consistently find that the single most predictive factor for structure survival is the condition of the immediate zone — the 5 feet immediately surrounding the structure. Structures with non-combustible immediate zones survive at dramatically higher rates than those with combustible materials immediately adjacent, regardless of other construction characteristics. This finding is counterintuitive — it suggests that the first 5 feet matter more than construction type — but it reflects the ember ignition mechanism. An ember that lands on gravel does not ignite. An ember that lands on dry bark mulch or stored firewood immediately adjacent to a wood wall ignites the structure at its most vulnerable point.

## 1.10 Special Occupancies — Community Buildings and Dense Urban Standards

**Standard:** Community buildings — assembly spaces, markets, medical facilities, food production — and the dense urban core require fire safety standards above those applicable to residential structures because they concentrate people in spaces they did not choose and cannot fully evaluate for safety, and because their loss may be catastrophic for the entire community.

**Assembly occupancies — markets, meeting halls, community spaces:**

**Occupant load and egress:**

- Maximum occupant load for any assembly space shall be calculated based on egress capacity — not based on the space's physical capacity to hold people
- Egress capacity calculation: each 22 inches of exit width provides egress for approximately 60 persons per minute. A space with two 36-inch doors has egress capacity for approximately 200 persons at the 2-minute evacuation time considered acceptable for assembly occupancies
- The maximum occupant load shall be posted visibly in the space and enforced — exceeding the posted occupant load means people cannot egress safely in a fire

**Exit distribution:**

- Assembly spaces shall have exits distributed so that no occupant is more than 75 feet from an exit
- Exits shall be on opposite sides of the space — exits clustered on one side provide no benefit if fire blocks that side

**Exit marking:**

- Exits shall be clearly marked — signage, paint, or other permanent marking visible in smoky conditions at floor level
- In spaces without electrical lighting: phosphorescent or reflective exit marking at floor level provides guidance in smoke-filled low-visibility conditions

**Community kitchens and food production:**

**The fire risk:**

Commercial and community-scale cooking produces grease-laden vapors that accumulate in exhaust systems and above cooking surfaces. Grease fires are among the most dangerous structure fires — grease ignites at relatively low temperatures, burns intensely, and cannot be extinguished with water (water application to a grease fire causes explosive vaporization and fire spread). A community kitchen without grease management is a persistent high-ignition-risk space.

**Grease management standards:**

- Exhaust hoods above cooking surfaces shall extend minimum 6 inches beyond the cooking surface on all sides — capturing grease vapors before they reach the ceiling
- Exhaust ductwork above community cooking areas shall be cleaned of grease accumulation at minimum monthly in high-use kitchens
- Class K fire suppression capability — wet chemical suppression appropriate for grease fires — shall be available in community kitchens. In the absence of commercial suppression systems: a metal lid sized to cover the largest cooking vessel (to smother grease fires by excluding oxygen) and a supply of dry sand (for grease that has spread beyond the vessel)
- Never use water on a grease fire — this is community education that shall reach every person who cooks in community kitchens

**Dense urban core standards — Tier 3:**

**Construction requirements:**

- New construction in Tier 3 shall use masonry or earthen construction for exterior walls and party walls
- Roofs in Tier 3 shall use non-combustible roofing materials — metal, tile, or slate
- Combustible folk construction in Tier 3 requires community review and approval — the review shall assess the fire risk to neighbors and may require fire-resistant treatment of exposed surfaces as a condition of approval

**Party wall standards:**

- Party walls — walls shared between adjacent structures or between units in attached construction — shall be continuous from foundation to roof
- Party walls shall not be penetrated by combustible materials — wood beams, joists, or rafters that pass through a party wall carry fire from one unit to the next through the penetration
- Party walls shall extend above the roof surface by minimum 18 inches — a parapet wall that extends above the roof prevents fire from traveling across the roof surface from one structure to the adjacent structure

**Height limits by construction type:**

- Unreinforced masonry or earthen construction: maximum 3 stories — above this height, seismic and structural concerns intersect with fire concerns. Occupants above the third floor cannot safely egress by window and require reliable stair egress
- Light wood frame construction in Tier 3: maximum 2 stories — the fire spread rate through light framing and the egress limitations of upper floor occupants make taller light frame construction unacceptable at Tier 3 density
- Heavy timber construction: maximum 3 stories with appropriate fire separation

**Rationale:** The grease fire water prohibition is included as an explicit standard because it is among the most dangerous kitchen fire response errors possible — applying water to a grease fire converts a contained vessel fire into an explosive spread event that instantly involves the surrounding space. The physics: water applied to oil above its boiling point (approximately 600°F for cooking oils) instantly vaporizes, expanding to approximately 1700 times its liquid volume in milliseconds, carrying burning oil droplets in all directions. The resulting fireball can instantly ignite an entire kitchen. This outcome is so consistently catastrophic and so consistently the result of untrained response that community education specifically addressing this prohibition is warranted.

The parapet wall requirement for Tier 3 roofs addresses a specific and historically documented fire spread mechanism in dense urban construction — fire traveling across roof surfaces from one structure to adjacent structures. In attached or closely spaced construction, a fire that reaches the roof surface of one structure can spread along combustible roofing materials to adjacent structures faster than any response can follow. A parapet wall that extends 18 inches above the roof surface creates a physical barrier at the property line that interrupts this horizontal spread. The parapet was a standard feature of pre-20th century urban commercial construction precisely because its fire containment function was understood — it was lost as construction moved to non-combustible roofing materials and fire suppression was relied upon instead of passive barriers. In a degraded response capacity context, the passive parapet barrier is more reliable than suppression.
