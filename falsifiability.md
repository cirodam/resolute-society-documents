# The Resolute Society
## Theoretical Framework & Founding Hypotheses
*A Working Document — Founded July 4, 2026*

---

## Preamble

The Resolute Society is not built on faith in good intentions or the assumption that communities simply lack the will to thrive. It is built on a set of specific, falsifiable hypotheses about what has actually happened to American community life — what broke, why it broke, and what a local institution might do to address it.

These hypotheses draw on existing scholarship and on ground-level observation. They are offered not as settled conclusions but as working propositions to be tested against reality. The Society is, among other things, a long-form applied test of whether these hypotheses are correct and whether the proposed mechanisms address the right constraints.

If the hypotheses are wrong, the Society will fail in specific, informative ways. If they are right, the Society will succeed in ways that can be documented, transmitted, and replicated by other chapters.

---

## Hypothesis One: The Liquidity Constraint

**Claim**

Local communities possess latent productive capacity — skills, land, labor, relationships — that remains unmobilized because the medium of exchange required to activate it is artificially scarce relative to the community's actual productive potential.

**Mechanism**

Dollar scarcity is not a reflection of genuine scarcity of value or capacity. A community may contain people who need work done and people who can do it, land that could produce food and people who could grow it, knowledge that could be transmitted and people who need it — and yet none of these exchanges occur because no one has the dollars required to initiate them.

A complementary currency circulating locally, denominated in contribution rather than dollars, can unlock this latent capacity without requiring dollar injection from outside. When the medium of exchange is decoupled from the extractive dollar economy, exchanges that were previously impossible become possible.

**Historical Grounding**

This mechanism has documented historical precedent. The town of Wörgl, Austria in 1932 issued a local complementary currency during the Depression and maintained economic activity — employment, public works, local commerce — while surrounding dollar-denominated economies seized. The productive capacity was present throughout. The medium of exchange was the binding constraint.

Silvio Gesell's work on demurrage currency — currency that loses value when hoarded — addresses the velocity dimension of this problem: money that circulates faster activates more latent capacity per unit issued. The Society's kin currency incorporates demurrage for this reason.

**Key Sources**

- Silvio Gesell — *The Natural Economic Order*
- Thomas Greco — *The End of Money and the Future of Civilization*
- Bernard Lietaer — *The Future of Money*

**What Would Falsify This**

If kin currency fails to activate exchanges that dollar scarcity was preventing — if the currency circulates but no new productive activity results — then the liquidity constraint hypothesis was wrong or insufficient. The binding constraint may lie elsewhere.

## Hypothesis Two: Social Fabric Destruction

**Claim**

The relational substrate required for collective action — trust, reciprocity, associational habit, shared institutional memory — has been actively dismantled by market disembedding and the collapse of associational life. The capacity is not merely latent. In many communities it is genuinely gone and must be rebuilt before economic alternatives can function.

**Mechanism: The Disembedding**

Karl Polanyi's central insight is that markets do not simply replace non-market exchange — they actively disorganize the social relationships that made non-market exchange possible. When livelihood depends on market performance rather than community standing, the behaviors that sustain commons governance — reciprocity, long time horizons, tolerance for free riders in exchange for belonging — atrophy through disuse.

The consequence is not merely that people trade differently. It is that they relate differently. The market trains people out of the capacities required for the alternative.

**Mechanism: Bowling Alone**

Robert Putnam's research documented the collapse of associational life across every measurable dimension — civic organizations, church participation, informal socializing, political engagement, neighbor trust — accelerating sharply from the 1970s onward. The timing correlates with deindustrialization, suburbanization, and the destruction of workplace-based social solidarity.

What replaced the union hall, the parish, the neighborhood institution was largely nothing. The social capital required to organize collective action has to be reconstructed almost from scratch in many communities. This reconstruction is a prerequisite for the economic model, not a byproduct of it.

**Implication for the Society**

The Society's sequencing — practical work before currency, relationship before transaction, covenant before governance — is not merely a strategy for managing cynicism. It is an acknowledgment that the relational substrate must exist before the economic infrastructure can function. You cannot circulate a trust-based currency in a community that has lost the habit of trust.

**Key Sources**

- Karl Polanyi — *The Great Transformation*
- Robert Putnam — *Bowling Alone*
- Elinor Ostrom — *Governing the Commons*

**What Would Falsify This**

If chapters can achieve economic function — kin circulation, pantry operation, skills exchange — without a prior period of relationship-building, then the social fabric destruction hypothesis overstates the damage. The relational substrate may be more resilient than assumed.

## Hypothesis Three: Counterproductivity and Learned Helplessness

**Claim**

Institutions designed to serve people have, past a threshold of scale and complexity, begun actively disabling the capacities they were meant to supplement. Populations have lost practical skills, agency, and the habit of self-governance — not through personal failure but through institutional counterproductivity operating over generations.

**Mechanism: Illich's Counterproductivity**

Ivan Illich identified the point at which institutions designed to serve human needs begin to produce the opposite of their stated purpose. The healthcare system that makes people less able to manage their own health. The school system that makes people less curious and less capable of self-directed learning. The transportation system that consumes more time than it saves. The political system that makes people less capable of self-governance.

These are not failures of implementation. They are structural consequences of scale and specialization. When every function of daily life is assigned to a specialist institution, the human capacity to perform that function atrophies. The institution becomes necessary precisely because it has made itself necessary.

**Mechanism: Tainter's Complexity**

Joseph Tainter's research on societal collapse identifies increasing complexity as a response to problems that eventually produces diminishing and then negative returns. As institutions grow more complex to address problems, the overhead required to sustain them consumes the surplus they were meant to generate. Communities at the periphery of complex systems experience this as abandonment — the system is consuming resources to sustain itself rather than delivering services.

**Implication for the Society**

The Society is explicitly a counter-institution in this sense — designed to rebuild practical capacity and the habit of agency rather than to deliver services that create dependency. The master-journeyman-apprentice College system, the hands-on infrastructure divisions, the sortition governance — these are mechanisms for practicing capacity rather than receiving it.

Success is measured not by services delivered but by capabilities developed and transmitted.

**Key Sources**

- Ivan Illich — *Tools for Conviviality*; *Deschooling Society*
- Joseph Tainter — *The Collapse of Complex Societies*

**What Would Falsify This**

If members fail to develop genuine practical capacity through participation — if the Society becomes another service-delivery institution that people consume rather than inhabit — then the counterproductivity hypothesis identified the problem correctly but the Society replicated it rather than solved it.

## Hypothesis Four: The Illegitimacy Vacuum

**Claim**

Existing institutions have lost legitimacy faster than alternatives have emerged, creating a vacuum that is simultaneously dangerous and generative. The vacuum is dangerous because it is being filled by authoritarian and destructive alternatives. It is generative because it means demand for legitimate local institutions has never been higher.

**Evidence**

Trust in major American institutions — Congress, the presidency, the courts, the media, banks, organized religion — has been in structural, not cyclical, decline for decades. The decline persists regardless of which party holds power, suggesting the problem is systemic rather than partisan.

The near-universal contempt for politicians across the political spectrum is a reliable indicator. The specific villains differ by constituency. The underlying sentiment — *these people are self-serving and do not represent me* — is shared across divisions that agree on almost nothing else.

**The Opportunity**

The Society does not need to argue that existing institutions are failing. That conclusion is already widely held. It needs only to visibly do things those institutions demonstrably cannot — local food security, practical skills transmission, genuine self-governance, economic circulation that stays in the community.

The contrast does the recruiting work without requiring oppositional framing that might trigger defensive reactions. The Society is not against anything. It is building something.

**The Risk**

Illegitimacy perception at scale also produces dangerous responses — authoritarian movements, scapegoating, violence that fills governance gaps. The Society is a constructive answer to the same conditions that produce destructive ones. The destructive alternatives have advantages: they are emotionally satisfying in the short term and require no hard work of building.

The Society must be compelling enough and real enough that it becomes the answer people reach for rather than the others.

**Key Sources**

- Steven Levitsky & Daniel Ziblatt — *How Democracies Die*
- Francis Fukuyama — *Trust*
- Ground-level observation — Franklin County, Georgia and analogous communities

**What Would Falsify This**

If institutional trust recovers before the Society establishes itself — if the existing system reforms in ways that genuinely restore legitimacy — the urgency of the vacuum hypothesis diminishes. The Society remains valuable but loses its particular historical moment.

## Hypothesis Five: Scale Pathology

**Claim**

Governance and economic systems have exceeded the scale at which human beings can maintain meaningful relationship to them, producing dysfunction that no amount of reform at the existing scale can fix. The solution is not better large institutions but legitimate small ones operating at human scale.

**Mechanism**

Elinor Ostrom's research on commons governance identified specific scale conditions under which self-governance works. Beyond those boundaries — when participants cannot know each other, cannot monitor each other, cannot develop shared norms through repeated interaction — commons governance fails and either privatization or state control becomes necessary.

E.F. Schumacher's argument that "small is beautiful" is not aesthetic preference but structural claim: human institutions function well within scale boundaries that industrial civilization has systematically exceeded. The pathologies of large-scale organization are not bugs to be fixed but features of scale itself.

**Implication for the Society**

The federated chapter model is not merely an organizational convenience. It is a structural commitment to operating within the scale boundaries where self-governance actually works. Chapters are sized for genuine mutual knowledge. The General Assembly can contain people who know each other. Sortition draws from a pool where reputations are legible.

The federation allows scale through multiplication rather than growth. Each chapter remains human-sized. The network grows. This is the only form of scaling compatible with the governance model.

**Key Sources**

- Elinor Ostrom — *Governing the Commons*
- E.F. Schumacher — *Small is Beautiful*
- Aristotle — *Politics* (on the natural scale of the polis)

**What Would Falsify This**

If chapters prove too small to sustain the full range of Society functions — if the skills base, economic mass, or governance capacity requires larger scale to work — then the scale pathology hypothesis has set the boundaries too small. The chapter size or federation model would require revision.

## How the Hypotheses Interact

These five hypotheses are not independent. They describe a system of mutually reinforcing failures, which means the Society's interventions must address multiple constraints simultaneously rather than sequentially.

The liquidity constraint (H1) cannot be resolved by currency alone if the social fabric (H2) required to circulate trust-based currency has been destroyed. Both must be addressed in parallel, which is why the Society builds relationship before launching economic infrastructure.

The counterproductivity problem (H3) means that even successful economic function is insufficient if it creates dependency rather than capacity. Every Society intervention must ask: does this build capability in members or does it deliver a service that replaces capability?

The illegitimacy vacuum (H4) creates the window for the Society to establish itself, but the scale pathology hypothesis (H5) constrains how it can grow. It must multiply through federation rather than scale through growth, or it will replicate the pathologies it is trying to escape.

The Society is structured as if all five hypotheses are simultaneously true and mutually reinforcing — because they probably are.

---

## A Note on Method

These hypotheses are offered in the spirit of genuine inquiry rather than institutional justification. The Society is not built to confirm them. It is built to test them.

This means maintaining honest records of what works and what does not, why chapters succeed or fail, which constraints prove binding and which prove secondary. The theoretical framework is only useful if it remains open to revision by contact with reality.
