# What a Household Should Be Able to Do

A household is the unit between the person and the society — people sharing a home and resources who have taken on mutual responsibility for each other. A household may be a family, a chosen group, or a single person living alone. Size doesn't define it; shared responsibility does.

The society's divisions exist to provide what genuinely exceeds household capacity. The household's job is to handle everything that doesn't require that escalation. A household that offloads things it could manage itself is drawing on community resources that are better directed at members who genuinely cannot manage without them.

The standard here is a household that can absorb a disruption — a week, a month, a season — without becoming a burden on the society, and that is actively contributing rather than only drawing. The disruptions this standard is designed for are not exotic: a power outage, a supply chain gap, an illness, a job loss, an infrastructure failure. These happen. A household prepared for them is more resilient than one that has assumed nothing will go wrong.

---

## Food

- Maintain a food stock sufficient for weeks, not days — dry staples, preserved food, and a rotation system that keeps the stock current
- Grow a meaningful portion of their own vegetables — not every calorie, but a genuine contribution to the household diet
- Preserve food across seasons: canning, fermentation, drying, root storage
- Cook from scratch, from staples, without depending on packaged food or takeout as a baseline
- Know what they have and what they need — not vague awareness, but actual knowledge of the household's food position

## Water

- Store water sufficient for short-term disruption — enough for drinking and basic sanitation if supply is interrupted
- Know how to purify water from a non-treated source
- Know where the nearest water source is that they could reach on foot

## Energy and Heat

- Have non-electric alternatives for heat, light, and cooking
- Not be one grid outage away from a crisis — the household can function, at reduced capacity, without power
- Know their energy consumption and have thought through what they would cut first if supply became constrained

## Shelter and Maintenance

- Perform basic structural maintenance: stop a leak, patch a wall, replace a broken window, address minor damage before it becomes major damage
- Weatherize adequately for the local climate — the house can hold heat in winter and manage heat in summer without extreme energy input
- Know what to do in the likely emergencies for their location: flood, fire, severe storm, extended power outage, whatever the regional hazards are
- Know where they would go and how they would get there if they could not stay home

## Health

- Maintain a real first aid kit — stocked, organized, and known to every adult in the household
- Know each household member's medical needs, medications, and conditions
- Have medications stocked adequately for known chronic conditions — not one refill away from a gap
- Know how to respond to a medical emergency involving any member, including who to call and what to do before help arrives

## Textile and Material Maintenance

- Maintain the household's clothing stock through repair — not every garment professionally, but basic mending capacity to extend the life of what they have
- Know what the household needs before the need becomes a crisis — adequate cold-weather clothing before winter, not in January

## Information and Relationships

- Know their neighbors by name and have a reciprocal relationship — not friendship necessarily, but mutual awareness and the expectation of help in an emergency
- Have hard copies of important documents: identification, medical records, insurance, financial information
- Have a communication plan that does not depend on phones working — how members of the household reach each other if communication infrastructure is down
- Know what resources the society provides and when to ask for them — including not waiting until a situation is a crisis before asking

## Financial Position

- Have a buffer — not be one unexpected expense or missed paycheck away from inability to meet basic needs
- Know their actual financial position: income, obligations, and margin
- Understand the society's credit system and how to use it for exchange within the community

## Contribution

- Be actively contributing to the society, not only drawing from it — through labor, skills, food production, care, teaching, or other means
- Know what the household produces or provides and treat it as a community resource, not only a private one
