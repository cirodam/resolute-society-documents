# The Resolute Society
## Kin Currency Tax Treatment
### A Technical Framework Proposal for Private Letter Ruling Request
*Founding Document — July 4, 2026*

---

## Prefatory Note

This document is a technical proposal for the treatment of kin — the Resolute Society's internal community currency — under federal income tax law. It is not a claim that kin transactions are categorically non-taxable. It is a demonstration that existing rules, applied mechanically to kin's specific architecture, produce results that bear no relationship to the economic reality of what members actually receive — and a proposal for a framework that would produce proportionate, administrable results instead.

The Society is not opposed to taxation of genuine economic benefit. It is opposed to treatment that imposes dollar tax liabilities on instruments that cannot be converted to dollars, on members who have no dollar capacity to satisfy those liabilities, for transactions that the dollar economy never made available to them in the first place.

The Society seeks a private letter ruling confirming the proposed framework before significant transaction volume occurs. It operates transparently, maintains complete records available to any reviewing authority, and is prepared to modify the proposed framework based on IRS guidance. The goal is workable clarity, not blanket exemption.

## Section One: The Organization and the Problem It Addresses

### The Resolute Society

The Resolute Society is a cooperative mutual benefit organization operating under applicable state law. It serves communities experiencing structural dollar scarcity — communities where the dollar economy has withdrawn, where productive capacity sits idle because no medium of exchange exists to activate it, and where basic needs go unmet not because people lack the capacity to help each other but because they lack the dollars required to initiate exchange.

The Society's governance is structured on one member, one vote. It has no investors, no shareholders, no profit mechanism, and no surplus distribution. Every design decision reflects a single organizing principle: economic activity should serve life and meet genuine need, not generate surplus or accumulate wealth.

### The Problem

Dollar scarcity in the communities the Society serves is not a temporary condition. It is a structural outcome of deindustrialization, extraction, and the deliberate organization of local economies to export value rather than circulate it. A community may contain people who need work done and people who can do it, land that could produce food and people who could grow it, knowledge that could be transmitted and people who need it — and yet none of these exchanges occur because no one has the dollars required to initiate them.

The Society's response is a complementary currency — kin — that circulates locally and activates this latent productive capacity without requiring dollar injection from outside. Kin is not designed to replace dollars. It is designed to serve the domain dollars cannot reach.

Understanding this foundational purpose is essential to understanding kin's tax treatment. Kin does not exist because the Society wanted to create a tax-advantaged instrument. It exists because dollar scarcity was preventing community members from helping each other. The tax consequences of kin's architecture follow from design decisions made entirely for community economic reasons.

## Section Two: The Kin Instrument — Technical Description

### What Kin Is

Kin is a closed-loop, non-convertible mutual credit instrument denominated in member-backed productive capacity. It is internal accounting for covenant exchange between members of the Society's circuit.

Kin is not a dollar substitute. It is not a convertible virtual currency. It is not a security, a commodity, or a pre-issued fiat instrument. It is a record of contribution and a claim on community productive capacity that exists only within the covenant circuit and has no value or existence outside it.

### Mutual Credit Architecture

Kin operates on mutual credit principles. When two members transact — one contributes labor and receives a credit of 45 kin; the other receives that labor and incurs an obligation of 45 kin — the credit and obligation materialize simultaneously from the transaction itself. No pre-existing kin changed hands. The aggregate kin in the system reflects the distribution of contribution and obligation among members.

### The Daily Stipend

Every member receives a daily stipend of 12 kin — 4,380 kin annually — as a baseline entitlement of covenant membership. The stipend reflects the Society's commitment that membership itself provides a foundation of circuit participation regardless of individual contribution capacity. It is not wages. It is not compensation for specific services. It is a membership benefit tied to the covenant obligations inherent in belonging.

### Non-Convertibility

Kin cannot be converted to dollars through any Society mechanism. There is no redemption window, no cash-out function, no secondary market. A member who holds 400 kin holds a claim on community productive capacity — food, skills, repair, tools — accessible only through covenant membership and only within the circuit.

Non-convertibility is not a limitation imposed for tax purposes. It is the design feature that keeps productive capacity circulating within the community rather than being extracted into the dollar economy. A convertible kin would be extracted the moment it was earned, defeating the circuit's purpose entirely.

### Demurrage

Kin carries a holding cost — it loses value when hoarded. This demurrage mechanism, derived from Silvio Gesell's research on complementary currency, ensures that kin circulates rather than accumulates. A currency that penalizes hoarding is a currency that actively serves exchange rather than storage.

Demurrage has significant implications for income characterization. Kin received today is worth less tomorrow. A member who earns kin and does not spend it is not accumulating wealth — their kin balance is actively deteriorating. This is the structural opposite of income, which connotes accretion to net worth.

### Cost Pricing

All goods and services within the Society's circuit are priced at the cost of inputs plus labor valued at community-assessed contribution rates. No margin is captured. No surplus is generated. The measure of a successful transaction is whether a need was met, not whether a profit was realized.

Cost pricing means that kin redeems at cost value, not market value. A physician consultation available at $250 in the dollar market redeems at the cost of the physician's time as assessed by the community — a systematically lower figure. The economic benefit received by the member is real but substantially below the FMV that barter rule imputation would assign.

### The Marketplace and Dollar Reference Pricing

The Society operates a members-only marketplace where goods and services are listed with kin prices and dollar reference rates displayed alongside. The dollar reference rate is informational — it shows members what the equivalent dollar cost would be and demonstrates, over time, that kin maintains its purchasing power relative to inflating dollar prices. Transactions on the marketplace settle in kin. The dollar reference rate is not a payment option. It is a reference point.

### Capital Calls and the Dollar Ore Mechanism

The only point at which dollars formally interface with the circuit is through capital calls. When the Society's collective purchasing function needs to acquire goods from the external economy — bulk food purchases, equipment, supplies — it calls on members to contribute dollar currency, referred to as dollar ore, which is irrevocably converted to collective productive capacity at a commodity-indexed basket rate.

The basket rate — the only formal dollar-to-kin conversion rate the Society publishes — exists for this specific institutional purpose. It is not a standing exchange rate. It is not designed to value kin as income. It measures what dollar contributions are worth as collective purchasing power at the moment of a specific capital call event.

Dollar ore contributed through this mechanism cannot be withdrawn, returned, or redeemed for dollars. The one-way valve between the dollar economy and the kin circuit is absolute.

### What Kin Cannot Do

Kin cannot pay rent. It cannot pay utilities. It cannot pay taxes. It cannot satisfy any external obligation. It cannot be spent outside the covenant community. It cannot be saved without losing value to demurrage. It cannot be invested. It cannot be used as collateral. It cannot be transferred to heirs.

For most American households, the majority of consumption — housing, transportation, utilities, external food, taxes, debt service — occurs in categories kin cannot reach. Kin provides access to a specific domain of cost-priced community goods and services. It does not provide general purchasing power. It is less valuable than an equivalent dollar amount in every dimension relevant to the external economy.

## Section Three: Where Existing Rules Break Down

The IRS barter rules — primarily Rev. Rul. 79-24 — treat exchanges of services as two simultaneous taxable transactions, each valued at fair market value. Applied mechanically to kin, these rules produce results that bear no relationship to economic reality and that no administrable methodology can calculate.

### The Foundational Assumption That Fails

The barter rules rest on a substitution premise: the taxpayer received the economic equivalent of dollar income through a non-dollar medium. The paradigm case is a lawyer who takes a painting instead of cash — a market participant making a medium-of-exchange substitution while retaining the full economic benefit of a market transaction.

This substitution premise requires that a dollar transaction was possible and was bypassed. For the Society's membership — people whose dollar budgets are fully committed to survival obligations before any kin transaction is contemplated — no dollar transaction was bypassed. The yard mowing didn't happen in dollars before kin existed. The healthcare wasn't accessed before the physician joined the circuit. The food wasn't purchased through the dollar economy before the pantry opened. These transactions came into existence because kin existed. They were not rerouted from dollar channels. They were created in a domain dollars had structurally vacated.

The barter rules were not designed to reach this category of transaction. They were designed to prevent market participants from avoiding dollar taxation by substituting non-dollar mediums for transactions that would otherwise occur in dollars. When the dollar transaction was never possible — because the member's dollar budget was fully committed, because the dollar market for these services in these communities was not clearing, because these needs were going unmet before the circuit existed — the substitution premise fails and the barter rules' application produces a category error.

### The Currency Mismatch Problem

Tax liabilities must be paid in dollars. Kin cannot be converted to dollars. Any treatment that generates dollar tax liabilities on kin receipts creates an obligation the member has no mechanism to satisfy from the instrument received.

The barter rules presuppose that conversion to dollars is possible — the taxpayer can sell the painting, sell the stock, liquidate the barter goods and satisfy a dollar tax liability from the value received. This presupposition is load-bearing. Remove it and the mechanism by which non-dollar income becomes dollar-payable tax liability breaks down entirely.

A dollar-poor member who receives kin and incurs a dollar tax liability must satisfy that liability from their existing dollar budget — the same dollar budget that was already fully committed to survival obligations before the kin transaction occurred. The tax liability doesn't create new dollars. It competes with rent, utilities, and food for the same scarce dollar resources. The circuit gave the member access to cost-priced community goods. The IRS responded by increasing their dollar obligations. The net effect of circuit participation, under mechanical barter rule application, is negative in dollar terms.

### The Phantom Income Problem

Demurrage drives rapid kin circulation. The same kin unit changing hands multiple times generates multiple imputed income events on the same underlying value under the barter rules. A member who earns kin and spends it quickly — as demurrage incentivizes — generates transaction-by-transaction imputed income that bears no relationship to net economic benefit received.

Consider a circuit with 4,000 members and 60,000 kin per member — 240 million kin in potential circulation. With demurrage driving velocity, the same kin unit might represent five, ten, or twenty transactions annually. The barter rules impute income at each transaction. The aggregate imputed income across all members could be many multiples of the community's actual dollar income — not because wealth was created, but because the currency was designed to move fast.

This is taxation of velocity, not income. The same value circulating rapidly looks like a lot of income under transaction-by-transaction imputation. It is the same value moving. No net accretion occurred.

### The Proportionality Problem

A member with $30,000 in annual dollar income who participates actively in the circuit might have $80,000 in annual kin transaction volume at imputed FMV. The barter rules would characterize their total income as $110,000. Their actual dollar income remains $30,000. The tax liability generated by $110,000 of imputed income must be paid from $30,000 of actual dollars — dollars already fully committed before the kin transactions were contemplated.

The imputed income bears no relationship to dollar capacity to pay. The member is not wealthier by $80,000. They received cost-priced community goods and services that improved their material situation without adding to their dollar resources. Treating the improvement as dollar income when it generated no dollars and cannot be converted to dollars produces tax liability disproportionate to any reasonable measure of actual economic benefit.

### The FMV Methodology Problem

Fair market value imputation requires an ascertainable market rate. For kin the IRS has two possible sources:

**External comparable rates.** Yard mowing in the county costs $45. The IRS imputes $45 regardless of what currency was used. But this methodology ignores every structural feature that makes the kin transaction categorically different from the dollar market transaction it's being compared to. The dollar market transaction involves strangers with no covenant relationship, at market pricing, between parties with functioning dollar budgets. None of those conditions describe the kin transaction. The comparable is not comparable.

**The capital call basket rate.** The Society publishes a commodity-indexed conversion rate at capital call events. The IRS could use this to derive a kin-to-dollar rate. But this rate was designed to measure food purchasing power at the moment of specific institutional events. It is not a standing exchange rate. It is not designed for income valuation. Extrapolating it to value daily stipends and individual transactions on days when no capital call occurred has no methodological basis.

Neither source produces a defensible FMV for kin received in covenant community exchange. The external comparable ignores the transaction's character. The basket rate was designed for a different purpose and exists only at irregular intervals.

### The Double Counting Problem

Under the barter rules, both parties to an exchange have a taxable event. Jennifer pays kin and receives services — taxable on the services received. Bill receives kin and performs services — taxable on the kin received.

If the IRS taxes Bill on receiving kin, he establishes a basis in that kin. When Bill subsequently spends the kin on food from the pantry, his disposition of kin with established basis against cost-priced goods produces minimal or zero gain. The IRS has collected tax at receipt and zero at redemption — one tax event on the full imputed value.

If the IRS taxes Bill only at redemption — when he spends kin on cost-priced goods — the taxable value is cost, not market. The tax is proportionate to actual benefit received.

If the IRS taxes at both receipt and redemption without accounting for basis, it taxes the same value twice. The methodology must choose one event or properly account for basis across the other. Neither is simple. Both require tracking every kin transaction with its basket rate at time of receipt — an accounting burden no individual member can practically sustain.

### The Threshold Reality

The federal income tax's effective threshold — after standard deductions, child tax credits, and earned income tax credits — means that a significant portion of the Society's membership owes no federal income tax regardless of imputation methodology. Adding kin income to their returns changes the calculation without changing the outcome: zero liability remains zero liability until imputed income is large enough to overcome the effective threshold.

For these members, the compliance burden of tracking, valuing, and reporting kin transactions produces no revenue. The IRS would be imposing a complex reporting obligation on people with no tax liability for the purpose of confirming that they have no tax liability.

### The Character Question

The barter rules treat medium as determinative: you exchanged services, therefore you have barter income, therefore FMV is imputed. But medium is a proxy for character, not the thing itself. The tax code taxes income — a specific legal concept that requires more than the receipt of value.

Gifts have FMV. They are not income. Inheritances have FMV. They are not income. Household production has FMV — the IRS estimates it in the trillions annually. It is not income. SNAP benefits have FMV. They are not income. What distinguishes taxable receipts from non-taxable ones is not the existence of value but the character of the transaction that produced it.

Kin transactions in the Society's circuit are covenant exchanges between members who entered a mutual obligation relationship. They are not market transactions between strangers acting in commercial self-interest. The physician providing health consultations in the circuit is not billing a patient. She is honoring a covenant obligation to a neighbor who has no access to her services through the dollar economy. The carpenter repairing a roof is not operating a business. He is fulfilling a membership commitment to a community member whose dollar budget cannot accommodate a market rate.

The character of these transactions places them outside the commercial context the barter rules were designed to reach. Medium is kin. Character is covenant. The barter rules reach the former only when it reflects the latter. Here it does not.

## Section Four: Illustrative Cases

The following cases illustrate the range of membership the Society serves and demonstrate how existing rules and the proposed framework produce different results across that range.

### Case One: Jennifer — Dollar-Poor Member

**Profile.** $18,000 annual dollar income. Single parent, two children. Dollar budget fully committed to rent, utilities, food, phone, and transportation. No discretionary dollars.

**Kin participation.** Receives daily stipend — 4,380 kin annually. Contributes labor to the pantry and community maintenance — earns an additional 8,000 kin. Total annual kin: 12,380. Spends entirely on cost-priced food and household services. Net year-end kin position near zero due to demurrage. Cost-priced redemption value of goods and services received: approximately $4,200.

**Federal income tax liability on dollar income.** Zero. After standard deduction and child tax credit, $18,000 with two dependents produces no federal income tax liability.

**Mechanical barter rule result.** Imputed income of $10,000-15,000 at external comparable rates added to $18,000 dollar income. Total imputed income: $28,000-33,000. Tax liability on imputed income: $1,500-2,500. Payable only by redirecting dollars from rent, utilities, or food — survival obligations that were already consuming her entire dollar budget. The circuit gave Jennifer access to food and community services. The IRS responded by creating a dollar liability she can only satisfy by taking dollars from her electric bill.

**Proposed framework result.** Jennifer's dollar income falls below the federal poverty threshold for a family of three. Kin participation generates no taxable income. Zero additional liability. Zero reporting obligation.

### Case Two: Bill — Working Poor Member

**Profile.** $32,000 annual dollar income. Skilled laborer. Dollar budget largely committed — rent, car payment, utilities, groceries — with modest flexibility.

**Kin participation.** Active circuit contributor. Earns 20,000 kin annually through skilled labor — carpentry, repairs, yard work. Spends most on cost-priced food, healthcare consultations, and circuit services. Net year-end kin position near zero due to demurrage pressure. Cost-priced redemption value of goods and services received: approximately $7,000.

**Federal income tax liability on dollar income.** Approximately $1,800 after standard deduction.

**Mechanical barter rule result.** Imputed income of $18,000-25,000 at external comparable rates. Total imputed income: $50,000-57,000. Additional tax liability: $3,000-4,500. Bill transacted in kin because no dollar-paying market existed for his labor in this community at prevailing rates. The IRS is now demanding several thousand dollars from someone whose dollar budget had modest flexibility before the kin transactions and no flexibility after the imputed liability is added.

**Proposed framework result.** Bill's dollar income exceeds the poverty threshold. His kin participation generates a taxable event at redemption. Taxable value: cost-priced redemption value of $7,000. At his marginal rate — 12% — additional tax liability: approximately $840. Proportionate to actual economic benefit received. Payable from his dollar income without eliminating his dollar slack entirely.

### Case Three: Maria — Near-Threshold Member

**Profile.** $45,000 annual dollar income. Some dollar flexibility — managing but not comfortable.

**Kin participation.** Moderate contributor. Earns 8,000 kin annually through professional skills — bookkeeping, administrative support. Spends on circuit food, healthcare, community services. Cost-priced redemption value: approximately $3,000.

**Federal income tax liability on dollar income.** Approximately $3,200 after standard deduction.

**Mechanical barter rule result.** Imputed income of $7,000-10,000 at external comparable rates. Additional tax liability: $840-1,200. Payable but disproportionate — Maria received $3,000 of cost-priced goods and the IRS is imputing $7,000-10,000 of income on the same transactions.

**Proposed framework result.** Taxable event at redemption. Taxable value: cost-priced redemption value of $3,000. Additional tax liability: approximately $360. Proportionate to actual benefit received and fully payable from her dollar income.

### Case Four: Dr. Sarah — Wealthy Ideological Member

**Profile.** $180,000 annual dollar income. Physician. Joins for covenant and community reasons. Significant dollar flexibility.

**Kin participation.** High-volume contributor. Earns 40,000 kin annually through medical consultations, health education, and community healthcare. Spends on circuit goods and covenant obligations. Cost-priced redemption value of goods and services received: approximately $14,000.

**Federal income tax liability on dollar income.** Approximately $32,000 after standard deduction.

**Mechanical barter rule result.** Imputed income of $35,000-50,000 at physician consultation rates. Additional tax liability: $8,000-12,000. Dr. Sarah can pay this. But the imputed income substantially overstates what she actually received — $14,000 of cost-priced goods, not $35,000-50,000 of market-rate services.

**Proposed framework result.** Taxable event at redemption. Taxable value: cost-priced redemption value of $14,000. Additional tax liability: approximately $3,360 at her marginal rate. Meaningful, proportionate, and accurately reflects the economic benefit she actually received rather than a theoretical market equivalent of services delivered to people who had no dollar access to them.

### What the Cases Demonstrate Together

Cases One and Two show where mechanical application is genuinely confiscatory — creating dollar liabilities that can only be satisfied by taking dollars from survival obligations, for transactions that only existed because dollars were unavailable.

Case Three shows the transition zone — where some tax liability is appropriate and the proposed framework produces it proportionately.

Case Four shows that the proposed framework is not a blanket exemption. It produces real, meaningful tax liability for members with genuine dollar capacity — liability that accurately reflects the cost-priced value they actually received rather than the market value of services delivered to people who could never have accessed them at market rates.

## Section Five: The Proposed Framework

The Society proposes the following framework for IRS confirmation. It is designed to produce tax liability proportionate to actual economic benefit received, administrable by members without professional tax assistance, and consistent with the economic reality of what kin transactions represent.

### Foundation: Subchapter T Treatment

Kin credits are non-qualified written notices of allocation under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code. This is the natural home for kin within existing law — members are using their cooperative's internal accounting unit to record patronage activity. Subchapter T provides an established framework that does not require novel doctrine.

Non-qualified written notices of allocation are not taxable at issuance. The taxable event is deferred to redemption. This single principle resolves the phantom income problem — circulation velocity generates no tax events — and the currency mismatch problem — the tax event occurs when the member has actually received something usable, not when they received a non-convertible instrument they cannot deploy to pay the liability.

### Taxable Event: Redemption at Cost-Priced Value

When a member redeems kin for goods and services within the circuit, they have a taxable event equal to the cost-priced value of what they received. Not the FMV of those goods and services at external market rates. The cost at which the circuit provided them.

This is the correct measure of economic benefit. The member did not receive a physician consultation worth $250 on the dollar market. They received a physician consultation provided at community-assessed cost — a systematically lower figure that accurately reflects what the circuit actually delivered.

Valuing at cost rather than market eliminates the FMV methodology problem. The Society's accounting records contain the cost of every good and service delivered through the circuit. No external comparable is required. No basket rate extrapolation is needed. The number is in the records.

### Reporting: Net Annual Position

Members report net annual kin redemption value — the aggregate cost-priced value of goods and services received through the circuit during the tax year — rather than individual transaction-by-transaction reporting.

This eliminates the compliance burden of tracking individual transactions. Members receive an annual statement from the Society showing their total cost-priced redemption value for the year. They report that figure if it exceeds the de minimis threshold.

### De Minimis Threshold

Kin redemption producing cost-priced value below $600 annually generates no reporting obligation. This reflects the practical reality that members whose circuit participation is modest — primarily receiving the daily stipend and occasional services — receive economic benefit too small to warrant compliance costs.

### Poverty Threshold Floor

Members whose dollar income falls at or below the federal poverty threshold for their household size have no kin tax liability or reporting obligation regardless of kin participation volume. For these members — the primary population the circuit exists to serve — kin participation is meeting basic needs the dollar economy demonstrably failed to provide. Imposing any dollar tax liability on them for receiving cost-priced food and community services through a covenant institution creates obligations they cannot satisfy without redirecting dollars from survival obligations.

This floor is consistent with the tax code's existing recognition that below certain income levels, adding tax obligations produces results the system was designed to avoid. The earned income tax credit, the child tax credit, and the standard deduction all reflect this principle. The poverty threshold floor for kin taxation asks the IRS to apply the same principle consistently.

### Summary of Framework

| Member Profile | Kin Tax Event | Valuation | Reporting |
|----------------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| Below poverty threshold | None | N/A | None |
| Above poverty threshold, redemption below $600 | None | N/A | None |
| Above poverty threshold, redemption above $600 | At redemption | Cost-priced value | Annual net statement |

## Section Six: Why the Framework Produces the Right Results

### It taxes genuine economic benefit.

Members who receive real economic value through the circuit — cost-priced goods and services that improve their material situation — have a tax event proportionate to that value. The framework doesn't exempt wealthier members from all liability. It accurately measures what they received.

### It protects the members who need protecting.

Dollar-poor members whose circuit participation meets basic needs that the dollar economy failed to provide have no additional dollar obligations. The circuit improved their lives. The tax system doesn't take that improvement back.

### It is administrable.

Annual net cost-priced redemption value, reported from the Society's accounting records, is a number every member can understand and every tax preparer can handle. It requires no FMV methodology, no basket rate extrapolation, no transaction-by-transaction tracking, no demurrage adjustment calculations.

### It generates real revenue where real revenue exists.

Wealthier members participating at high volume — the physician, the attorney, the skilled professional — receive meaningful cost-priced economic benefit and pay meaningful tax on it. The framework is not a blanket exemption. It is a proportionate measurement.

### It is consistent with the tax code's foundational principles.

Income requires accretion to wealth. Cost-priced goods and services received by members with dollar capacity represent genuine accretion. Cost-priced goods and services received by members with no dollar capacity, in a non-convertible instrument that cannot satisfy external obligations, represent accretion to material wellbeing in a specific domain — but not accretion to wealth in the Haig-Simons sense that the income tax was designed to capture.

## Section Seven: The Request

The Society requests confirmation of the following:

One — kin credits constitute non-qualified written notices of allocation under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code and are not taxable to members at issuance.

Two — the taxable event for kin participation is redemption, not receipt, valued at the cost-priced value of goods and services received as reflected in the Society's accounting records.

Three — members report net annual cost-priced redemption value above a de minimis threshold of $600, based on annual statements provided by the Society.

Four — members whose dollar income falls at or below the federal poverty threshold for their household size have no kin tax liability or reporting obligation.

Five — the Society's daily stipend of 12 kin constitutes a non-qualified patronage allocation subject to the same treatment as transaction-based kin earnings under items one through four above.

The Society is prepared to modify any element of the proposed framework based on IRS guidance. It is not seeking blanket exemption. It is seeking a workable methodology that produces results proportionate to economic reality and administrable by the members it serves.

## Conclusion

Kin is a genuinely novel instrument. It does not fit cleanly into any existing tax category because it was not designed with tax categories in mind. It was designed to solve a community economic problem — the structural absence of a medium of exchange in dollar-scarce communities — and its architectural features reflect that purpose entirely.

The barter rules, applied mechanically, produce results that no reasonable interpretation of the income tax's foundational purposes can support: dollar tax liabilities on non-convertible instruments, imposed on dollar-poor members, for transactions that only existed because dollars were unavailable, collectible only by taking dollars from survival obligations.

The proposed framework does not ask the IRS to forgo significant revenue. The members most affected by mechanical barter rule application — those whose dollar incomes fall below the poverty threshold — have no federal income tax liability regardless of methodology. The revenue at stake is negligible. The human cost of mechanical application is not.

The Society asks the IRS to engage with kin's specific architecture, evaluate the proposed framework on its merits, and provide the clarity that will allow the Society to operate transparently and in full compliance with whatever treatment the IRS determines is appropriate.

The circuit exists to serve communities the dollar economy left behind. It creates exchange where none existed, meets needs that went unmet, and builds productive capacity where extraction left none. The tax system's treatment of that activity should reflect what it actually is — not what it superficially resembles.
