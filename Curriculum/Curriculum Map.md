# Curriculum Map

This document tracks what curriculum exists and what needs to be written. The Working Society's curriculum is not a school. It is the community's answer to two questions: what do members need to know to live well and participate fully, and what does the present moment specifically require that ordinary life no longer provides?

Those two questions are different. The first produces a curriculum of enduring practical knowledge — how to grow food, build structures, care for the sick, reason clearly. The second produces something more specific to this moment: a curriculum of restoration. The attention economy damaged things in us that affect everything else. A person who cannot sustain attention, who forms beliefs based on social pressure rather than evidence, who has lost the capacity to be genuinely present with another person — that person cannot learn effectively, cannot govern well, cannot participate in community life the way mutual obligation requires. The formation courses address this damage before it quietly undermines everything built on top of it.

These two dimensions of the curriculum operate in parallel. Members need both, and neither waits for the other to be complete.

---

## Course Types

Three types of curriculum documents exist in this project. The distinction matters because they serve different functions and require different delivery.

**Formation courses** address what the attention economy, political polarization, and the collapse of common epistemic ground have done to the capacities people need for everything else. They are taught in small groups with a trained guide, running 90 minutes per session over 12–14 structured sessions, then continuing as ongoing practice groups. The content is experiential, not informational — reading about attention is not the same as rebuilding attentional capacity. These courses cannot be self-paced or delivered by text alone. They require a room, a guide, and other people. They are listed by course number to indicate the recommended sequence. Format metadata is embedded in each course document.

**Training programs** develop specific knowledge and skill to a level of functional competence. Foundational Literacy, Foundational Numeracy, and the Community Health Worker program are training programs. They have defined learning objectives, structured progression, assessment criteria, and identifiable completion points. They can accommodate a range of delivery contexts — tutoring, cohort, self-guided with support — depending on the program. They are appropriate for members who need a specific capability rather than general formation.

**Skill curricula** transmit practical knowledge of the physical world — agriculture, construction, animal care, food systems, tool use, emergency response. This category is almost entirely unwritten. The knowledge being mapped here is the content of what the Division of Education's apprenticeship and College structure ultimately carries. Not every skill needs a written curriculum — much of it is properly transmitted through doing alongside someone who knows. But some skills, particularly those with safety implications or knowledge that is actively disappearing from the population, warrant documented curricula that can be referenced, taught, and updated. Where hazard education already exists for a given domain, the skill curriculum in that domain assumes it.

## Division of Responsibility

The Division of Education is responsible for curriculum across the Society — its design, delivery, and ongoing development. But the Division of Education does not own the expertise in every domain. For most practical and specialized curricula, delivery is a joint function: the Division of Education provides the pedagogical form; the relevant substantive Division provides the content authority and the people who actually know the thing being taught.

The Division of Food is the expert on food production. The Division of Water on water systems. The Division of Health on medicine and care. The Division of Housing on construction. The Judicial Division on conflict and harm resolution. The Division of Sanitation on waste systems and public health infrastructure. The Division of the Watch on safety and emergency response. Where a curriculum falls within a Division's domain, that Division is a co-author of the curriculum and a co-deliverer of the instruction — not a subject consulted at a distance, but a genuine partner in what is taught and how.

This arrangement has a practical consequence: curriculum gaps in any domain are a shared problem between Education and the relevant Division. The Division of Food cannot treat soil health education as Education's problem to solve. The Division of Health cannot treat emergency response training as something Education will eventually get to. The obligation to build curriculum in a domain belongs to the Division that owns that domain, with Education as the institutional partner that turns expertise into transmissible knowledge.

Formation courses — those that develop cognitive, epistemic, and interior capacities — belong to the Division of Education alone. There is no substantive Division that owns the domain of attention or practical reasoning. These are Education's native territory.

## Foundational Skills

**Status: Literacy and numeracy programs exist; civic and technological literacy absent.**

Foundational literacy and numeracy are prior to everything else in the curriculum. A member who cannot read with fluency and comprehension cannot engage fully with the Society's printed materials, regulations, or governance documents. A member without functional numeracy cannot participate meaningfully in resource decisions, agricultural planning, or health data interpretation. These programs serve members who need them, without assumption about why — the causes of educational deficit are as varied as the people who carry them, and the programs treat each learner accordingly.

| Program | Status |
|---|---|
| Foundational Literacy | Exists |
| Foundational Numeracy | Exists |
| Technological Literacy | **Needed — High** |
| Civic Literacy | **Needed — Medium** |

**Technological Literacy** — The Technology article in governance requires that every member understand how attention-capture technologies work. The Division of Education carries the standing obligation to provide this education. It does not yet exist as a curriculum. The course that needs to be written is not a digital detox program and not a technical manual — it is an explanation of the specific mechanisms: variable reward schedules, engagement optimization, algorithmic curation, outrage amplification, and their documented effects on cognition, belief formation, and social behavior. The goal is that every member can name what is being done to their interior life and why, which is the prerequisite for any genuine defense of it.

**Civic Literacy** — The basic knowledge of how governance works — not at the Society level, which is covered elsewhere, but at the level of the wider political structures members inhabit and interact with. How federal, state, and local government actually functions. What rights members actually hold. How to navigate the legal and administrative systems that affect community life. This is not political education — it is practical knowledge of the terrain.

## Health Knowledge

**Status: CHW training program exists; general health education and emergency response absent.**

The Community Health Worker program trains specific members for a specific role. What does not yet exist is the general health knowledge the Society's members broadly need — not at CHW depth, but enough to understand their own bodies, recognize when something requires attention, and participate as informed principals in their own care. The CHW module on the body as a system (Module 2) and on social determinants (Module 6) are written for CHWs but contain knowledge that belongs in the general curriculum.

| Program | Status |
|---|---|
| Community Health Worker | Exists |
| Understanding Your Body | **Needed — High** |
| Emergency Response and First Aid | **Needed — Critical** |
| Mental Health Literacy | **Needed — High** |
| End of Life and Grief | **Needed — Medium** |

**Emergency Response and First Aid** — A community without guaranteed ambulance access within a clinically meaningful timeframe cannot treat first aid as something members will figure out when needed. CPR, hemorrhage control, airway management, burns, fractures, shock recognition, anaphylaxis, and the specific rural emergency scenarios the CHW curriculum addresses (AMI at distance from definitive care, stroke, drowning, agricultural trauma) need to be known by a broader population than just designated CHWs. This is the knowledge that determines whether the minutes before professional help arrives are productive or passive.

**Understanding Your Body** — The CHW curriculum teaches physiology at depth because CHWs need to understand mechanism. The general population curriculum version covers the same systems at a level appropriate for informed self-care: what's normal, what's a warning sign, how the major chronic diseases develop and what can be done about them at the lifestyle level. The social determinants content belongs here too — members who understand why economic precarity is literally physiological understand something important about their own bodies and their community's health.

**Mental Health Literacy** — Distinct from the CHW mental health module, which trains someone to support others. What the general curriculum needs is the capacity to recognize mental health conditions in oneself and people one is close to, to understand what they are and what they aren't, and to know when to ask for help and from whom. The specific presentation in this population (Module 7 covers this for CHWs) — depression that looks like irritability, trauma that looks like hostility, grief that looks like decline — is worth knowing broadly, not just among designated health workers.

## Practical Craft

**Status: Largely absent. This is the largest gap in the curriculum.**

The Society's foundational commitment to capacity — the opposite of atrophy — requires that members can do the things the Divisions are organized around. The Division of Food is not a food provider; it is the community organized around food production, and its success is measured by what the community can grow, preserve, and provide for itself. That framing requires members who actually know how. The same is true for every obligated Division. The skill curricula in this section are the curricular expression of that commitment.

Where hazard education already exists in the regulatory framework (fermentation microbiology, pressure vessel safety, butchering), the skill curriculum in that domain assumes it and does not duplicate it.

**Soil and Garden Fundamentals** — Everything else in food production rests on soil health. A community that does not understand soil structure, organic matter, nitrogen cycling, pH, and what plants actually need cannot produce food reliably at community scale. This is the foundational practical curriculum — analogous to Foundational Literacy in the academic track.

**Foraging and Wild Food Identification** — Wild food is a legitimate and historically significant food source in this region. It is also the area where ignorance is most dangerous — *Amanita phalloides* looks edible, poison hemlock looks like wild carrot, and misidentification kills. The curriculum must include both positive identification of species the community harvests and negative identification of the dangerous lookalikes. Complementary to the foraging safety regulation in the regulatory framework; the regulation governs distribution standards, the curriculum builds identification competence.

## Governance and Civic Participation

**Status: No dedicated curriculum exists. Governance is documented; the skills to use it are not taught.**

The governance documents describe what the Society does. This section addresses what members need to know and be able to do to participate in it meaningfully.

| Curriculum | Status |
|---|---|
| How the Society Governs Itself | **Needed — Critical** |
| Facilitation and Meeting Practice | **Needed — High** |
| Conflict and Conciliation | **Needed — High** |
| Writing Petitions and Proposals | **Needed — Medium** |

**How the Society Governs Itself** — Every new member needs an orientation to the actual governance structures: what the General Assembly is and does, how sortition works, how petitions function, what the referendum cycle looks like, what the Maintenance Committee does, and what the Divisions are responsible for. This is not the Formation Course on civic and democratic life — it is a practical orientation to this specific Society's structures. It should be required early in provisional membership, before the oath.

**Facilitation and Meeting Practice** — The General Assembly's deliberative requirements — the steel man, the dissent, the written rationale — require members who know how to facilitate and participate in structured deliberation. Running a meeting well is a learnable skill, and the Society's governance depends on it being broadly held rather than concentrated in whoever has the most confidence.

**Conflict and Conciliation** — The Judicial Division's orientation (Module 3 of its governance article) articulates the principles. What most members need is not that depth — it is the practical capacity to raise a concern, navigate a disagreement, and participate in a restorative process as either a person who has been harmed or a person who has caused harm. This curriculum builds those capacities before members need them in a crisis.

## Priority Summary

**Formation and foundational — write next:**
1. Technological Literacy (formation prerequisite)
2. How the Society Governs Itself (member orientation)
3. Emergency Response and First Aid
4. Soil and Garden Fundamentals

The first three address things the existing courses and governance structures assume members can do but don't teach. The fourth addresses a life-safety gap. The fifth and sixth begin the practical curriculum that the Division commitments require.

**Practical craft — write before community operations begin at scale:**
1. Water Testing and Treatment
2. Basic Carpentry and Framing
3. Food Preservation — Canning, Fermentation, Drying
4. Animal Husbandry
5. Foraging and Wild Food Identification

The practical craft curricula cannot wait indefinitely. A Society that guarantees food and water security to its members needs members who can participate in producing it. These are the five that are most foundational to everything else in the practical domain.
