## Purpose

There are moments in history that test the character of a nation, that demand more than ordinary times prepare any person for. We believe we are living in such a moment. This curriculum is an act of educational justice, not a training program.

## 0. Why This Exists

- Short — possibly the most important section in the document
- Sets tone: this is not a credentialing exercise; it is a community choosing to carry knowledge together
- States plainly what the present moment requires and why this program is a response to it
- Makes clear that the CHW is not a substitute for a failed medical system but a parallel institution built on different premises

## 1. The Role and Its Limits

**What a CHW is**
- A trusted community member who has chosen to carry more health knowledge than the average member
- The person who notices, who stays, who helps someone understand what's happening and take the next right step

**What a CHW is not**
- Not a doctor, nurse, or diagnosis machine
- Not a treatment provider or a substitute for clinical care

**The practical limits**
- Cannot: diagnose, prescribe, treat clinically
- Can: observe, assess, educate, support, refer, follow up

**Why limits matter**
- Recognizing the boundary and escalating appropriately saves lives
- Acting beyond competence causes harm
- Knowing where your knowledge ends is as important as knowing what it contains

## 2. The Body as a System

*Goal: every system taught as a problem the body is solving, not as anatomy to memorize. Ends with integration and homeostasis.*

### Cardiovascular
- The delivery problem: every cell needs oxygen, fuel, and waste removal continuously; diffusion is insufficient at scale
- Heart as pump: fist-sized muscle, ~100,000 contractions/day; right side (to lungs) vs left side (to body)
- Circulation as closed loop: arteries (oxygenated, high pressure) → capillaries (exchange site) → veins (return, low pressure)
- Blood pressure: systolic (pump contracting) vs diastolic (pump resting); what the numbers mean and why they matter
- Electrical system: SA node as pacemaker; what irregular rhythm looks like and means; what the pulse tells you beyond rate
- Coronary arteries: the heart's own blood supply separate from chambers; why this matters for heart attack mechanism

### Respiratory
- The gas exchange problem: cells need oxygen and produce CO₂ continuously
- Mechanics: diaphragm as primary muscle; rib cage expansion; negative pressure drawing air in; passive exhale at rest
- Airways as branching tree: trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli; why surface area (tennis court) matters
- Alveolus as functional unit: thin membrane, capillary network, partial pressure gradient exchange
- Protective mechanisms: cilia, mucus, cough reflex, sneeze; what happens when overwhelmed or damaged
- Breathing rate and depth as clinical information: what changes in rate indicate; labored vs effortless

### Renal
- The waste removal problem: metabolism produces toxins; body must remove while conserving useful substances
- Kidney as sophisticated filter: not a sieve — actively reabsorbs what body needs; nephron, glomerulus, tubule
- Scale: entire blood volume filtered every ~30 minutes; 180L filtrate/day, ~1.5L excreted
- Urine as information: color, volume, frequency, odor, blood — all clinically meaningful
- Kidney-blood pressure feedback loop: kidney regulates BP; high BP damages kidneys; damaged kidneys raise BP
- Electrolytes: sodium, potassium, calcium, phosphorus; why precise balance matters; what disruption means

### Endocrine
- The coordination problem: body needs long-range signaling across organs over longer time scales than nerves handle
- Hormones as chemical messengers: the body's own signaling molecules; target organ concept
- Key players and what they do:
  - Pancreas: insulin and glucagon (blood glucose regulation — explain fully as foundation for diabetes understanding)
  - Thyroid: metabolic rate
  - Adrenal glands: cortisol and adrenaline (stress response — foundational for Module 3 and Module 6)
  - Sex hormones; growth hormone
- Negative feedback loops: hormone's own effect suppresses further production; the mechanism of hormonal homeostasis
- Insulin resistance: explain fully here — cellular receptor resistance, pancreatic compensation, eventual failure; foundational for Module 3

### Nervous System
- The integration problem: rapid coordination, sensation, movement, information integration
- Central (brain, spinal cord) vs peripheral nervous system
- Neuron, electrical signal, synapse, chemical neurotransmission — foundational for understanding psychiatric medications and substance use
- Autonomic nervous system: sympathetic (fight/flight) vs parasympathetic (rest/digest); balance as health marker
- Chronic sympathetic activation → chronic disease: introduce the link here so Module 3 can fully develop it
- Brain regions functionally:
  - Brainstem: automatic functions (breathing, heart rate, consciousness)
  - Limbic system: emotional processing, memory, threat assessment
  - Prefrontal cortex: executive function — most impaired by chronic stress, poor sleep, economic precarity
- Pain: acute (signal) vs chronic (different phenomenon requiring different understanding)

### Musculoskeletal
- Skeleton: structure, protection, mineral reservoir; axial vs appendicular as organizing concepts
- Joints: types, what each allows, vulnerability; knee as detailed example; spine as structural marvel and pathology site
- Muscle: major groups in functional terms; opposing pairs; effects of aging, disuse, injury
- Connective tissue: tendons, ligaments, cartilage; why they heal slowly; why cartilage damage is consequential; sprain vs strain
- Bone as living tissue: continuous remodeling; responds to stress; loses density with age, disuse, nutritional deficiency; osteoporosis trajectory and why it matters for this population

### Immune System
- The defense problem: environment full of threats to bodily integrity
- Layers: skin/mucous membranes → innate response (rapid, nonspecific) → adaptive response (slower, specific, learns and remembers)
- Inflammation as process not pathology: acute inflammation = active repair (redness, heat, swelling, pain are signs of work being done)
- Chronic inflammation: distinct and damaging; low-grade sustained activation running in background
- Immune system and chronic disease: chronic inflammation as common thread in cardiovascular disease, diabetes, depression, cancer, Alzheimer's
- Fever as information: mechanism, what it's doing, when concerning, when body is doing its job; specific thresholds for different populations

### Digestive System
- The nutrient extraction problem
- Gut as tube with specialized regions: mouth (enzymatic digestion begins) → stomach (acid, mechanical) → small intestine (primary absorption, villi surface area) → large intestine (water reabsorption, microbiome)
- Liver as metabolic hub: bile production, central processing of gut absorption, drug metabolism, toxin processing, protein synthesis, glucose storage; why liver health matters across systems
- Microbiome: gut bacterial community affects immune function, mood, metabolic health; what disrupts and supports it
- Digestion as parasympathetic function: chronic stress directly impairs digestion through neurological mechanism

### Integration
- Body as one interconnected system, not separate chapters
- Concrete example: poorly controlled diabetes affecting cardiovascular, renal, nervous, immune systems simultaneously
- Homeostasis as the unifying concept: body's continuous effort to maintain stable internal conditions
- Disease = homeostasis failing or overwhelmed; treatment = supporting the body's own mechanisms or replacing what they can no longer do
- Close with honest wonder: this works as well as it does, for as long as it does, without conscious effort — that is remarkable

## 3. How the Body Fails

*Two parts: common mechanisms (upstream processes feeding multiple disease pathways — taught once, applied everywhere); major pathways (each traced from earliest upstream conditions through established disease to late complications using case narratives).*

### Part One: Common Mechanisms

**Inflammation**
- Builds on Module 2: the reader already understands acute inflammation as healing response
- Chronic inflammation: what happens when the acute response never fully resolves
  - Persistent low-grade immune activation; not dramatically symptomatic
  - Causes: visceral fat (metabolically active, continuously secretes inflammatory signals), processed diet, chronic stress via cortisol, sleep deprivation, smoking, chronic infections, environmental toxins
- What chronic inflammation does over time:
  - Damages blood vessel walls → stiff, plaque-prone
  - Disrupts insulin signaling → insulin resistance
  - Damages kidney filtering apparatus
  - Activates pain pathways → chronic pain
  - Interferes with neurotransmitters → depression
  - Accelerates cellular aging
- The cluster insight: not five separate diseases but one process expressing across five systems simultaneously — why the conditions cluster

**Insulin Resistance**
- Builds on Module 2 insulin/glucose explanation
- Normal: blood glucose rises → pancreas releases insulin → cells take up glucose → glucose normalizes
- Resistance: cells stop responding normally; pancreas compensates with more insulin; eventually fails
- Prediabetes → Type 2 diabetes trajectory
- Causes: visceral fat, chronic inflammation, physical inactivity, sleep deprivation, chronic stress — interrelated and compounding
- Timeline: damage accumulates years before glucose becomes abnormal enough for diagnosis; the diagnosis is not the beginning of the disease

**The Cortisol Pathway**
- Acute stress response: normal, adaptive, resolves
- Chronic activation: same system, lower intensity, sustained duration
- What chronically elevated cortisol does:
  - Raises blood pressure persistently
  - Promotes insulin resistance
  - Promotes visceral fat accumulation (body stores energy centrally for anticipated continued threat)
  - Disrupts sleep architecture
  - Impairs immune regulation → promotes chronic inflammation
  - Impairs prefrontal cortex function
  - Disrupts neurotransmitter systems → depression and anxiety
- Economic precarity as chronic stressor: the direct mechanistic account of how economic conditions change the body's biology — not a social justice argument dressed in physiological language

**Cellular Aging and Damage Accumulation**
- Cells replicate to replace damaged/worn cells; copying process accumulates errors
- Telomeres shorten with each replication; when too short, cell enters senescence
- Senescent cells secrete inflammatory signals → more chronic inflammation
- Chronic stress, chronic inflammation, oxidative damage all accelerate telomere shortening
- The practical implication: chronic stressors don't just cause disease — they accelerate biological aging measurably
- The reversibility point: sleep, movement, nutrition, stress reduction measurably slow and partially reverse cellular aging — intervention points are real

### Part Two: Major Pathways

*Structure for each pathway:* case narrative opening → mechanism traced through stages, pausing to explain what's producing what → clinical hooks woven in (what CHW would observe/ask) → intervention points named explicitly at each stage → late complications described honestly

**The Cardiovascular Pathway**
- Character: late 30s-40s, borderline BP, physically demanding job, poor sleep from financial stress, drinks to unwind, family history of early MI
- Stage 1 (silent): BP at 135/88; endothelial damage beginning; LDL lodging in vessel walls; early plaques forming; no symptoms
- Stage 2 (established): BP 145/92; plaques larger, some calcified, some soft/vulnerable; narrowed coronary vessels; exertional shortness of breath; early renal effects; medication prescribed but adherence poor
- Acute event: stressful moment → sympathetic spike → BP spike → vulnerable plaque ruptures → clot → MI
- MI as endpoint of 20-30 year process with multiple visible warning signs
- Intervention points: early BP monitoring + lifestyle + sleep/stress/alcohol; established HTN = medication adherence + CHW relationship; complication stage = recognizing shortness of breath and decreased exercise tolerance as signals

**The Metabolic Pathway**
- Character: mid-40s woman, abdominal weight, family history, sedentary, constant fatigue, sugar cravings, prior borderline glucose with no follow-up
- Stage 1: established insulin resistance; pancreas compensating; normal glucose at cost of hyperinsulinemia; visceral fat secreting inflammatory cytokines; fatigue has a mechanism (cells insulin resistant → energy-depleted despite elevated glucose)
- Stage 2: diabetes diagnosis; glycation damage to small vessel proteins beginning; peripheral neuropathy developing
- Foot complications in depth: neuropathy removes warning system → unnoticed blisters → infection → impaired healing → pathway to amputation
- Intervention points: prediabetes is reversible with lifestyle; medication adherence + dietary support; foot care as CHW skill at every visit

**The Respiratory Pathway**
- Character: 50s man, 30-year smoker (quit 5 years ago), morning cough (normalized), worsening exertional breathlessness, active physical life now declining
- COPD mechanism: cilia damaged/destroyed; alveolar walls ruptured and merged (emphysema, less surface area); airways inflamed with excess mucus (chronic bronchitis)
- Key concept: damage largely irreversible but progression not inevitable — stopping smoking stops accelerated damage
- Progression: right heart failure (cor pulmonale) from increased pulmonary resistance; barrel chest hyperinflation
- Acute exacerbations: infections, air quality events, cold air can precipitate rapid deterioration
- Intervention points: smoking cessation at any stage; pulmonary rehabilitation; vaccination; medication adherence; air quality awareness (wood smoke, agricultural burning, cold air)

**The Renal Pathway**
- Continues metabolic pathway character, 10 years further: diabetes 15 years, inconsistent control; hypertension also present
- Mechanism: hypertension (mechanical stress) + hyperglycemia (glycation) both damaging glomeruli
- The insidious quality: kidneys have enormous reserve; can lose >50% function before symptoms appear; damage accumulates silently
- Early warning signs visible in labs: protein in urine, elevated creatinine — CHW's alert system when person shows lab results
- Late stage: waste accumulation, fluid imbalance, anemia (kidney produces erythropoietin), cardiovascular risk acceleration
- Intervention points: BP control (most important), glucose control, NSAID avoidance — NSAIDs widely used for chronic pain in this population and genuinely harmful in compromised kidneys

**The Depression Pathway**
- Character: late 40s man, plant closure 3 years ago, identity around physical skilled work, lesser job now, increased drinking, poor sleep, withdrawal, wouldn't use the word "depressed"
- Mechanism: chronic stress of job loss + economic precarity + loss of purpose → sustained HPA activation → hippocampal neurogenesis impairment, serotonin/dopamine dysregulation, neuroinflammation
- Depression as predictable physiological consequence of sustained stress and loss, not character failing
- Real-world presentation: irritability, withdrawal, alcohol ("taking the edge off"), physical complaints (fatigue, chronic pain, sleep disruption), decreased engagement — not sadness
- Bidirectional disease relationships: depression worsens every chronic disease; poor self-care and nonadherence are mechanisms
- Intervention points: sleep, physical activity, social connection, purposeful work, alcohol reduction, addressing underlying stressors; accessible interventions have real evidence — these are not consolation prizes for absent clinical resources

### Integration
- The clustered patient: 58-year-old with hypertension, T2DM, early CKD, chronic pain, depression, alcohol misuse — one process, multiple expressions
- Every intervention addressing underlying mechanisms affects all five expressions simultaneously
- Lifestyle and social interventions hit the trunk; medication hits one branch
- Emotional close: this will be recognizable to the reader — in themselves, in their family, in their community; understanding the mechanism is the beginning of the capacity to interrupt it

## 4. Seeing What's There

*Four parts: vital signs; systematic observation; home visit as assessment tool; documentation.*

### Part One: Vital Signs

**Blood Pressure (Manual)**
- Equipment: aneroid sphygmomanometer; importance of cuff size (too small = falsely elevated, too large = falsely low); how to determine correct size; need for multiple sizes
- Stethoscope: bell vs diaphragm side; placement
- Patient preparation and why it matters:
  - Seated quietly 5 minutes; no caffeine, exercise, smoking in prior 30 minutes
  - Feet flat, back supported, arm at heart level
  - Explain the mechanism each violation produces — not arbitrary rules
- Procedure:
  - Locate brachial artery by palpation (inside elbow crease)
  - Cuff placement: lower edge ~1 inch above crease, artery marker aligned
  - Palpate radial pulse; inflate until pulse disappears (estimates systolic, prevents under-inflation); inflate 20-30 mmHg beyond
  - Place stethoscope; release slowly (~2 mmHg/second)
- Korotkoff sounds: what produces them; first tap = systolic; disappearance = diastolic; five phases briefly, clinically important ones in detail
- Common errors and what each produces:
  - Releasing too fast: falsely low systolic, falsely high diastolic
  - Loose cuff: falsely high
  - Ambient noise: missed sounds
  - Re-inflating without full deflation: venous congestion = falsely high
  - Auscultatory gap: silence between phases that can be mistaken for diastolic if under-inflated; missing it = dangerously false low systolic
- Interpretation: categories (normal, elevated, Stage 1 HTN, Stage 2 HTN, hypertensive crisis); trend > any single reading; what gradually increasing BP means; what previously controlled BP becoming elevated means
- White coat effect and masked hypertension: why clinic and home readings differ and why that matters
- When to act immediately:
  - >180/120 with symptoms (headache, vision changes, chest pain, shortness of breath) = hypertensive crisis = emergency
  - >180/120 without symptoms = urgent medical attention within hours

**Pulse**
- Rate, rhythm, quality — all three matter
- Rate: normal range; expected (exertion, fever, anxiety, pain) vs concerning (unexplained at rest, new onset, accompanied by other symptoms) tachycardia; bradycardia
- Rhythm: regular vs irregular; what AFib feels like on palpation; document irregularity even without characterizing it further
- Quality: strong/weak, bounding/thready; what thready pulse indicates about perfusion; shock pulse (rapid and weak); bounding pulse (fever, hyperthyroidism)
- Where to palpate: radial (standard), carotid (stronger signal), femoral, dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial (diabetic peripheral circulation assessment)
- Capillary refill: press nail bed until blanches, release, count seconds to color return; normal <2 seconds; prolonged = impaired peripheral perfusion

**Respiratory Rate**
- Most neglected vital sign; most sensitive early deterioration indicator
- Why it changes when observed: count without telling the person; standard method — fingers on wrist as if still counting pulse, observe chest rise and fall, count full minute or 30 seconds × 2
- Normal range; outside normal:
  - Tachypnea: early sign of respiratory compromise, sepsis, metabolic acidosis, pain, anxiety
  - Kussmaul breathing: deep, labored, rapid — DKA pattern
  - Bradypnea: abnormally slow, what it suggests
- Quality as important as rate: labored vs effortless; use of accessory muscles (neck, intercostals); nasal flaring; pursed-lip breathing in COPD (self-taught, tells you disease severity)
- Sounds audible without stethoscope: wheezing (narrowed airways), stridor (upper airway obstruction), gurgling (upper airway fluid) — what each means and requires

**Temperature**
- Oral, axillary, temporal: differences in normal values and reliability
- Fever as information: mechanism (inhibits bacterial/viral replication, activates immune function); when suppression appropriate vs monitoring
- Threshold concerns:
  - Infants <3 months: low threshold for emergency response
  - Elderly: fever often blunted or absent even in serious infection — presence more significant when it occurs
  - Fever + stiff neck, rash, or altered mental status: specific concern
- Hypothermia: presentation, rural elderly risk in inadequately heated homes, cardiac arrhythmia concern

**Pulse Oximetry**
- Device and correct use; what affects reading: nail polish, poor perfusion, cold fingers — and workarounds
- What SpO₂ measures: percentage of hemoglobin molecules carrying oxygen
- What it doesn't measure: work of breathing, CO₂ levels, adequacy of tissue perfusion
- Normal range; thresholds: <95% warrants attention, <90% emergency
- Baseline matters: COPD patients may run lower — knowing their baseline is clinically essential
- SpO₂ vs respiratory rate: SpO₂ can be maintained until person is working extremely hard; it's a late indicator in some presentations; respiratory rate rises earlier; use both together

### Part Two: Systematic Observation

**General Appearance**
- Well vs unwell; does appearance match stated condition; type of distress (acute pain ≠ chronic pain ≠ respiratory distress ≠ anxiety)
- Level of consciousness: AVPU (Alert, Voice, Pain, Unresponsive) — rapid rough assessment anyone can apply
- Skin color:
  - Pallor: anemia or poor perfusion
  - Cyanosis: blue-gray lips/fingernails = low O₂ saturation; visible before pulse oximeter alarms
  - Jaundice: yellow tinge of elevated bilirubin; visible first in whites of eyes
  - Flushing: fever, hypertensive urgency, alcohol intoxication
  - Mottling: blotchy purple-gray = seriously impaired circulation
- Skin turgor (hydration): pinch back of hand or forearm; tenting (delayed return) = dehydration; less reliable in elderly
- Edema: location (ankle/lower leg = heart failure or venous insufficiency); pitting vs non-pitting (press 5 seconds, release, check for indentation); bilateral vs unilateral significance
- Breathing quality visible before you measure: breathing hard at rest, can't finish sentence, accessory muscles
- Gait and movement: guarding, change since last visit
- Affect and engagement: eye contact, normal responsiveness, flatness or withdrawal, agitation

**The Hands**
- Nail beds: color, capillary refill, clubbing (associated with chronic respiratory/cardiac disease), splinter hemorrhages (endocarditis)
- Tremor types and what each suggests: resting (Parkinson's), intention (cerebellar), alcohol withdrawal (medically significant, potentially dangerous)
- Grip strength: rough functional assessment and sarcopenia marker in older members
- Skin: work calluses, wounds and healing, diabetic hand changes (skin, reduced sensation, healing problems)

**The Face**
- Eyes: whites for jaundice and conjunctival pallor (anemia); pupil size and symmetry — unequal pupils are a neurological emergency
- Drooping eyelid or facial asymmetry: stroke
- Mouth/throat (penlight): mucous membrane color and moisture (hydration), oral pathology, dental infection, thrush, lesions, throat redness/swelling/exudate
- Facial symmetry: subtle asymmetry of early facial nerve involvement vs full droop of established stroke

**The Abdomen**
- Visual inspection before palpation: distension shape (central vs diffuse) — possible ascites, bowel obstruction, organomegaly
- CHW is not doing clinical abdominal exam — but can observe and ask:
  - Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, pain characteristics (location, quality, onset, aggravating/relieving factors)
  - Liver region: note if person winces at right lower rib pressure → prompt follow-up, not self-diagnosis

**The Feet (Diabetic Members)**
- Non-negotiable: remove shoes and socks every visit
- Look at entire foot: soles, between toes, around nails — blisters, calluses, wounds, color changes, swelling
- Feel: temperature differences between feet
- Ask: sensation, symmetry of feeling
- Monofilament test / CHW equivalent without monofilament: touch multiple locations, ask if felt and if symmetrical
- Nails: fungal infection (thickened, discolored); ingrown nails in neuropathy = wound risk
- Between toes: interdigital moisture, maceration, fungal infection
- Why this matters stated plainly: diabetic foot ulcers are the leading cause of non-traumatic lower extremity amputation; most start with something small the person couldn't feel; the CHW who looks at feet every visit is doing something no clinic does reliably

### Part Three: The Home Visit as Assessment Tool

**Medication Assessment**
- Ask to see all medications — frame as care, not surveillance
- Are they actually being taken: check fill dates and remaining pills against prescription schedule
- Multiple prescribers: potential interactions
- Medications that shouldn't be combined with diagnoses present:
  - NSAIDs + kidney disease; beta-blocker + asthma; sedative + COPD
- Medications that have run out and not been refilled: high-value finding — identify why (cost, transportation, side effects, feeling fine, belief medication isn't working, fear)
- OTC medications as part of picture: NSAIDs most important — ubiquitous, genuinely harmful in kidney disease, taken without clinical oversight

**Food Environment**
- What's actually in the kitchen — information, not judgment
- Is there food; what kind; degree of processing
- Diabetic-specific: concentrated sweets, fruit juice consumed as water, high-glycemic staples as primary carbohydrate
- Food insecurity as clinical finding: disrupts glucose control in diabetics, elevates cortisol, impairs medication adherence when meds should be taken with food

**Physical Environment**
- Temperature: heat risk (summer) and cold risk (winter) — especially elderly without AC or heating
- Fall hazards: sliding rugs, cords, poor lighting, bathrooms without grab bars — injury prevention the clinic doesn't perform
- Mold: visible, smell, chronic respiratory symptoms in household possibly connected to indoor air quality
- Smoking in home: for COPD member especially — secondhand smoke as direct disease driver; conversation needed carefully and without judgment
- General organization as functional status indicator: previously maintained home becoming disorganized, dirty, unopened mail = visible functional decline requiring attention

**Functional Status Assessment**
- ADLs (basic self-care): bathing, dressing, eating, toileting, transferring, continence
- IADLs (independent living functions): medications, finances, transportation, household tasks, shopping, cooking, phone
- CHW approach: narrative questions not formal checklist
  - "Tell me about your day yesterday" / "How did you get to your last appointment" / "Have you been able to keep up with your bills"
- Changes from baseline are the key finding: used to cook, garden, walk to mailbox, drive — and now doesn't; each has specific causes warranting investigation

### Part Four: Documentation

*Covered more fully in Module 10; basics belong here because inseparable from assessment skills.*

- SOAP note simplified: Subjective (what person tells you), Objective (what you observe/measure), Assessment (what you think is happening), Plan (what you'll do)
- CHW version of Assessment section: trend + observation without requiring diagnosis (e.g., "BP trending up over three visits, person reports headaches, medication unfilled six weeks — action warranted")
- Always document:
  - Date and time
  - Vital signs with method
  - Medications reviewed; changes noted
  - New symptoms or concerns
  - Functional status compared to last visit
  - Follow-up plan
- Longitudinal value: single visit note = modestly useful; twelve months of notes showing trend = powerful clinical information no clinic encounter produces; continuity is the distinctive CHW value

### Cohort Requirements
- Session 1: vital signs workshop — all practice on all; BP, pulse, respiratory rate, temperature, pulse oximetry; feedback on technique; common errors identified and corrected
- Session 2: systematic observation practice — case scenarios; learners run through observation framework and document findings
- Session 3: simulated home visit — scenario set up with planted findings; conduct visit, document, debrief

## 5. Talking to People

*Goal: not technique deployment but the quality of presence that makes technique work. MI as the framework.*

### Framing
- What you are actually doing: creating conditions for the person to hear something true about their situation, connect it to what they care about, and find their own motivation to move
- Not: delivering information to a passive recipient; persuading; fixing
- Information delivery model fails consistently — people change when they find their own reasons
- This principle is what makes MI make sense — state it before introducing the technique

### Part One: Foundations

**Listening**
- Listening to respond vs listening to understand: different cognitive activities; deliberate practice required
- What listening to understand looks like: fully present, not formulating response, tracking content and delivery — hesitations, changes in volume, what's circled without landing
- Tracking the gap: what's said vs what's felt; confident description of regimen with hesitation when asked about last few weeks
- Eye contact in this cultural context: direct sustained gaze from peer = attention and respect; same from authority figure = assessment and judgment — everything surrounding the eye contact makes the difference
- Physical positioning: sitting not standing; same level; no desk between; body language of a visitor not an inspector
- Silence: hardest skill; instinct to fill; practice sitting longer than feels comfortable; the person will often say the most important thing into the silence

**Reflective Listening**
- Not paraphrasing or summarizing — making a guess about the meaning underneath and offering it as a statement
- Statement not question: a question puts person in position of confirming or denying; a reflection invites deepening
- Types:
  - Simple: restating content at similar level
  - Amplified: slightly more intensity, allows person to correct down
  - Double-sided: both sides of ambivalence — "on one hand... on the other hand..." — person feels heard rather than pushed
- The righting reflex: instinct to correct, advise, warn, disagree — the enemy of reflective listening; shift to advice = person becomes passive recipient; suppress this

**Open Questions**
- Closed: limited answer set; produce yes/no; may not be honest; don't advance understanding
- Open: invite the person to tell you something; "how have you been managing your medications lately," "tell me what a typical day looks like," "what's been going on since I was here"
- Best open questions invite narrative: person tells you what's actually important to them, not what they think you want to hear

**Affirmations**
- Not praise or cheerleading — specific recognition of actual strengths and efforts
- Why specificity matters: "you're doing great" is hollow; named specific effort lands differently
- Clinical purpose: build self-efficacy, which research consistently shows predicts behavior change better than knowledge or intention

**Summaries**
- Periodically pull together what you've heard and offer it back
- Functions: demonstrates listening; creates checkpoints for correction; tracks what's covered; creates natural transition points
- After a summary: "what feels most important to address today" — returns agency to person

### Part Two: Motivational Interviewing in Practice

*Four processes as map, not script — real conversations don't follow clean sequences.*

**Engaging**
- Foundation everything else rests on; cannot do motivational work until person trusts you're genuinely there for them
- Takes as long as it takes; Working Society CHW advantage: existing community relationship
- What engaging looks like: showing up when you said; doing what you said; no hidden agenda; interested in whole life not just medical status
- The self-reliance challenge: reframe as collaborative ("I'd like to hear what you know about this"), not helping; find ways for person to contribute rather than only receive

**Focusing**
- Finding direction from person's priorities, not CHW's clinical agenda
- Agenda-setting conversation: transparent offer + genuine centering of person's priorities
- Questions: "what matters most to you right now," "what would you most want to be different in six months"
- Clinical and personal interweave: grandchildren, not feeling tired, not ending up like their father — more useful motivationally than the diagnosis

**Evoking**
- Most counterintuitive: not providing reasons for change — eliciting them
- Person's own articulated reasons become theirs; your reasons remain yours
- Change talk types to recognize and follow: Desire (want), Ability (can), Reason (because), Need (have to), Commitment (going to)
- Evoking change talk: importance ruler (1-10), confidence ruler (1-10), "why not lower" question — forces person to articulate their own reasons
- Sustain talk: normal, expected; don't argue with it, don't ignore it; double-sided reflection is the tool

**Planning**
- Only when person has found motivation and is ready to move
- Specificity is everything: "eat better" is not a plan; "stop buying soda at Dollar General" is a plan
- Small steps principle: what is achievable in real circumstances, not what is optimal; small success builds self-efficacy for next step
- Anticipating obstacles: practical preparation, not pessimism; person who thought through the obstacle navigates it when it appears
- Follow-through and accountability: CHW will be back; knowing someone will ask = real accountability; failure = information not verdict

### Part Three: Rural Working Class Georgia

**Self-Reliance**
- A value with deep historical roots — people who survived on their own ingenuity when institutions failed them; masculinity and the equation of need with weakness; rational distrust of help-with-strings
- Treating it as an obstacle to overcome will fail; working with it as a genuine value will succeed
- In practice: neighbor helping neighbor framing; asking for person's expertise; finding contribution opportunities not just receiving opportunities

**Privacy and Non-Disclosure**
- Privacy as dignity and control in a world of limited power — not hiding something
- Earning disclosure gradually, never demanding it
- Never sharing what you've been told; never raising prior disclosures in front of family without permission; treating privacy as serious obligation not information-gathering barrier

**Masculine Identity**
- Men who defined themselves through physical capability and productive work have the hardest time with the CHW relationship
- Strategies: instrumental framing (blood pressure measurement not wellness check); engage what they know and are proud of first; connect to what they care about (work capability, family, not ending up like father)
- Entering depression from the side: sleep, not enjoying things, energy — without using the word depression

**Distrust of Institutions**
- Rational and earned — should be treated as such, not defensively countered
- Antidote: consistency over time; honoring stated limits; willingness to say "I don't know" and "the system failed you"
- Trust built slowly, lost quickly; timeline longer than most programs expect; Working Society design creates conditions for the required timeline

**Religion and Folk Health Beliefs**
- Faith is central to many people's understanding of illness and healing — dismissing or working around it loses the person
- Engaging it respectfully: taking care of the body as consistent with values person already holds; acknowledging faith without endorsing or undermining
- Folk health beliefs: find genuine overlap with what you know; work from overlap not disagreement; goal is not to correct the wrong belief

### Part Four: Difficult Conversations

**The Person Not Following Their Treatment Plan**
- Get curious not explanatory: "what has been making it hard" not "are you taking your medication"
- Nonadherence always has a specific cause: cost, side effects, transportation, not understanding why when feeling fine, belief medication isn't working, fear of what the condition means
- Each cause has a specific response; Module 3 pathophysiology pays off here — the person who understands what uncontrolled BP does to kidneys over decades is different from one who just knows their BP is high

**The Person in Mental Health Crisis**
- Presence more than solutions
- Asking about suicidal ideation directly: research is consistent — asking does not increase risk, often reduces it by breaking isolation
- Exact language required: "Are you thinking about suicide?" / "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" — not indirect softened versions
- If yes: stay present, don't leave the person alone, get support; know the escalation pathway for this context

**The Person Who Doesn't Want Help**
- Don't push; leave the door open without forcing it
- "I understand. I'm not going anywhere and if anything changes I'm here."
- Maintain relationship at whatever level person accepts; the person who refused today may call in a year; the CHW who respected the refusal and maintained the relationship is the one they'll call

**Delivering Hard Information**
- Ask permission: "can I share something I've been noticing"
- State observation specifically and without catastrophizing
- Give time to respond before continuing
- Connect to something they care about
- Identify next step together
- What not to do: soften past the point of landing; overwhelm with everything at once; present information and move on without processing space

### Cohort Requirements
- Most extensive mandatory requirement in the curriculum — skills cannot be learned from text
- Role plays must be genuinely challenging: defensive nonadherence, deflection with humor, unexpected tears, strong righting-reflex provocation
- Debrief as important as the role play: feelings during pushback; when righting reflex fired; what happened when silence was held
- Self-awareness as clinical skill: knowing your triggers, discomfort areas, tendencies under pressure is a safety requirement
- Pairs practice integrated into community life: BP measurement with conversation, home visit practice

## 6. Social Determinants of Health

### Framing
- Central finding of social epidemiology: birthplace, income, work, education, neighborhood safety, social relationships predict health outcomes more reliably than medical care, genetics, or almost anything else measurable
- Not a political argument — one of the most replicated findings in public health; holds after controlling for health behaviors and access to care
- Clinical knowledge without context is systematically incomplete
- The CHW who treats hypertension without understanding chronic economic stress is addressing the downstream effect while the upstream cause continues operating

### Part One: Mechanisms

**Economic Precarity as Physiological Condition**
- Builds directly on Module 3 cortisol pathway
- Chronic economic insecurity = sustained HPA axis activation — not acute crisis but persistent background condition of not having enough
- Research: salivary cortisol studies show elevated baseline in economically precarious people vs secure controls; persistent modest elevation not dramatic
- Whitehall studies (Britain): health gradient tracked precisely with employment grade all the way up hierarchy; not just poor vs not-poor; mechanism was chronic stress activation from lower control, lower status, higher demand
- Franklin County population: not middle rungs of hierarchy; bottom of an economy that has systematically divested from rural communities for fifty years

**The Cognitive Bandwidth Tax**
- Mullainathan and Shafir scarcity research
- Chronic financial precarity: portion of cognitive capacity continuously occupied with calculations of not enough — this processing is not voluntary
- Evidence: ~13 IQ point reduction under financial stress priming; Indian sugar cane farmers show dramatically worse cognitive performance pre-harvest vs post-harvest (same people, same environment)
- CHW implication: non-follow-through is often the cognitive tax of chronic scarcity, not character or intelligence; plan may have been entirely sincere but cognitive bandwidth to execute it was genuinely unavailable
- Reducing economic stress — even partially — is a health intervention; Working Society kin economy and medication cost assistance are examples

**Social Isolation as Biological Threat**
- 26-29% increased mortality risk from social isolation; comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes/day; larger than obesity effect
- Meta-analysis of 148 studies, 300,000+ participants
- Mechanisms: threat response activation (isolation registered as threat by brain evolved in social groups); immune dysregulation (elevated inflammatory markers, impaired infection response); health behavior disruption
- Gene expression research: chronic loneliness produces characteristic pattern — upregulation of inflammatory/threat genes, downregulation of antiviral/antibody genes
- Franklin County context: churches at full strength, civic organizations, local employers providing decades-long peer relationships — all weakened over the same period chronic disease burden has risen

**The Food Environment**
- Dollar store phenomenon: Dollar General, Dollar Tree, Family Dollar expanding aggressively into rural communities where supermarkets have closed
- Product mix: predominantly ultra-processed, shelf-stable, calorie-dense, nutritionally poor; fresh produce absent or minimal
- Documented public health problem: high dollar store density + low supermarket access = measurably worse dietary patterns and metabolic outcomes
- Key frame: people are making best choices available in an impoverished food environment — not making poor choices
- CHW implication: dietary counseling ignoring actual food environment is victim-blaming dressed as health education

**Transportation as Hidden Determinant**
- Urban public health assumes transportation; rural Georgia reality makes it one of the most important determinants
- What no reliable transportation means: medications not picked up when copay due and no ride to pharmacy; appointments not kept; specialist referrals expiring; emergencies managed at home; diet determined by walking distance
- Assessment item: "do you have reliable transportation / how did you get to your last appointment / is there anything you've been unable to do because of transportation"
- Working Society transit service as genuine health intervention

**Housing Quality**
- Rural Franklin County housing stock: older manufactured housing, older frame construction, deferred maintenance
- Specific health hazards: lead paint (pre-1978), mold (roof leaks, foundation moisture, inadequate ventilation), temperature extremes, combustion appliances without ventilation, pest infestation, well water near failing septic
- Health outcomes: lead exposure (neurodevelopmental in children), mold (respiratory disease), temperature extremes (cardiovascular stress in elderly)
- Home visit as simultaneous housing assessment: temperature feel, smell, wall/ceiling condition, water appearance

**Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)**
- ACE score (abuse, neglect, household dysfunction): dose-response linear relationship with every major adult chronic disease category
- Mechanism: early adversity programs stress response system in ways persisting across lifespan (same HPA axis pathways)
- Population baseline in Franklin County: economic precarity, substance use in household, domestic violence, parental mental illness, incarceration — heavily concentrated here
- CHW implication: the 55-year-old with hypertension, diabetes, depression, and chronic pain is carrying the accumulated physiological effects of their entire life history; the clinical picture is the biography made flesh

### Part Two: Franklin County Specifically

**Economic History**
- Manufacturing jobs leaving; agricultural consolidation (fewer operations, fewer people employed); retail consolidation into distant big-box; local hospital as major employer lost
- Not background color — direct cause of the health outcomes seen in members
- Community didn't develop a "culture of poor health behaviors"; the economic conditions that supported health were systematically withdrawn

**Current Employment Picture**
- Available work: poultry processing, construction, agricultural labor, healthcare work
- What it pays; specific injury and health patterns for each sector; instability; absence of employer health insurance at meaningful levels

**Healthcare Landscape**
- Honest and specific: distance to nearest hospital, ED, psychiatric services
- Primary care access reality: appointment availability, cost, what insurance covers
- Mental health inaccessibility; specialty care distance; medication costs; pharmacy availability and hours

**Community Assets**
- UGA Extension; agricultural knowledge and relationships; churches as infrastructure; informal mutual aid (neighbors checking on neighbors, families sharing food and labor)
- Working Society is not building on empty ground — building on existing strengths, relationships, and informal practices

### Part Three: What the CHW Does

**Seeing the Whole Person in Context**
- Blood pressure reading interpreted differently when you know about current eviction threat
- Nonadherence interpreted differently when pharmacy is 35 minutes away without reliable transportation
- Dietary pattern interpreted differently when nearest fresh produce is 25-minute drive
- This seeing changes the assessment

**Addressing What's Addressable**
- Some structural conditions are not changeable at CHW level; trying to fix the unfixable leads to burnout
- Within fixed structural conditions there are specific problems with specific solutions: medication cost, transportation barrier, food access, isolation
- These are responses to individual expressions of structural problems — genuinely valuable without being solutions to the structure itself

**Documenting Social Determinants**
- Transportation barriers, food security concerns, housing observations, social isolation — clinically relevant, document them
- Individual record + organizational aggregate: what the membership actually needs informs organizational priorities and resource allocation

**Advocacy Within the Organization**
- Recurring transportation barrier across multiple households → case for transit service prioritization
- Recurring medication cost barrier → case for medication assistance program
- Recurring mental health gap → case for internal mental health support capacity
- CHW is the organization's most direct source of ground-level intelligence; it belongs in governance

## 7. Mental Health and Substance Use

### Framing
- Reframe from the start: mental health in this population is not primarily individual brain chemistry gone wrong
- It is the predictable human response to conditions that would strain any person: chronic economic precarity, social isolation, physical illness, loss of meaningful work and purpose, ACEs, accumulated weight of a life made harder than it needed to be
- Psychiatric conditions are real; suffering is genuine; physiological changes are measurable
- But understanding the causes — largely social and structural — changes how you see the person and what you understand yourself to be doing
- The person isn't broken; their circumstances have exceeded their resources

### Part One: Neuroscience Foundation

**Stress Response System**
- Builds on Module 2 (nervous system) and Module 3 (cortisol pathway)
- Structural brain changes from chronic stress — visible on imaging:
  - Hippocampal volume reduction: impaired memory and threat contextualization
  - Prefrontal cortex impairment: executive function, planning, emotional regulation, impulse control
  - Amygdala hyperreactivity: threat detection firing too easily
- Functional consequences misread as character failing: impulsivity, difficulty planning ahead, strong reactions to minor provocation, difficulty maintaining commitments — these are the neurological consequences of chronic threat activation

**Neurotransmitters and Mood**
- Monoamine systems: serotonin (mood, impulse control, sleep, appetite), dopamine (motivation, reward, anticipation), norepinephrine (alertness, energy, stress response)
- Chronic stress dysregulates all three: depression is sustained downregulation; anxiety is sustained upregulation of threat-response components
- Why antidepressants work: SSRIs (serotonin), SNRIs (serotonin + norepinephrine), bupropion (dopamine + norepinephrine) — CHW doesn't prescribe but understanding mechanism is useful
- Substance use connection: every major substance acts on these same systems; self-medication as pharmacologically rational — remove the moral frame, substitute the mechanistic one

**The Window of Tolerance**
- Zone of arousal within which a person can function well — think clearly, connect with others, regulate emotions, respond flexibly
- Below (hypoarousal): shut down, numb, dissociated; Above (hyperarousal): overwhelmed, reactive, flooded
- Trauma narrows the window; ordinary stressors push outside it — reactions that look disproportionate are often appropriate to the window, not the stimulus
- CHW role: help person stay within their window through pace, conditions, quality of presence
- Co-regulation: regulated nervous system of CHW has direct physiological effect on dysregulated nervous system of person present — your calm is genuinely, measurably contagious

### Part Two: The Conditions

**Depression**
- Clinical criteria vs real-world presentation in this population:
  - Real presentation: irritability more than sadness (especially men); withdrawal described practically not emotionally; physical complaints (fatigue, chronic pain, headaches) worked up without medical explanation; alcohol described as "taking the edge off"; decreased engagement described in practical terms
- How to ask: concrete terms not clinical vocabulary ("how have you been sleeping," "do you still go fishing on weekends," "have you been drinking more than usual," "how's your energy")
- Severity spectrum: mild (lifestyle + connection) → moderate (medication may significantly help, function impaired) → severe (significantly disabled, self-harm risk elevated)
- Duration dimension: situational (loss or stressor, resolves as person adapts) vs persistent depressive disorder (long-running, person considers it "just how I am") vs major depressive episode (more severe, more episodic, more clearly biological) — these respond differently

**Anxiety**
- Often co-occurs with depression; may be dominant complaint while depression is underlying
- Spectrum: normal adaptive → generalized anxiety disorder (pervasive, uncontrollable worry, "can't turn off my brain") → panic disorder (episodic intense physical fear experiences)
- Panic attack specifics: rapid heart rate, chest tightness, shortness of breath, dizziness, sense of impending doom; person convinced of cardiac event; symptoms are real and distressing
- First episode with cardiac risk factors: emergency evaluation warranted; person with known panic disorder having recognized episode: calm presence + grounding + reassurance
- Somatic presentation: headaches, GI distress, muscle tension, fatigue without medical explanation; nobody asked about the anxiety

**Post-Traumatic Stress**
- PTSD requires specific traumatic event; complex/chronic trauma from ACEs, economic precarity, illness, accumulated losses doesn't require single identifiable event
- Real-world presentation: hypervigilance (persistent sense of threat, always scanning), intrusive thoughts (not necessarily dramatic flashbacks), avoidance, emotional numbing, sleep disturbance
- Physical connection: hypervigilance = sustained sympathetic activation = all downstream physiological effects of chronic stress activation
- CHW role: trauma-informed presence not trauma processing; building trust more slowly and demonstrating reliability more consistently; learning to notice when trauma is being triggered in an interaction

**Substance Use**

*Framing: self-medication of dysregulated systems*

- Major categories in this population:
  - Alcohol: most common, most normalized, harm invisible for years
  - Opioids: prescription-to-dependence transition common; person may not identify as having a substance use problem because it started medically
  - Methamphetamine: present in rural Georgia; bidirectional mental health effects (stimulant use, crash, psychosis with heavy chronic use)
  - Cannabis: lower harm profile; heavy chronic use has real effects on motivation and cognitive function; can worsen psychotic disorders

*Alcohol in depth:*
- Stages: hazardous use → harmful use (consequences but control) → dependence (withdrawal produces symptoms, compulsive not chosen) → severe dependence (physiologically necessary, withdrawal medically dangerous)
- Alcohol withdrawal: unlike opioid withdrawal (miserable but not typically dangerous), can produce seizures and delirium tremens (potentially fatal — severe autonomic instability, hallucinations, seizures); heavy daily drinker who suddenly stops = risk of medical emergency
- Harm reduction frame: abstinence not always realistic near-term goal; naloxone for opioid users regardless of intent to stop; not driving after drinking; not mixing dangerously; reducing toward less harm even if not zero
- CAGE screening (natural in conversation): Cut down, Annoyed (others criticizing), Guilty, Eye-opener (morning drink); 2+ = concerning

**Psychosis**
- Positive symptoms: hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thinking; Negative symptoms: flat affect, withdrawal, reduced motivation, poverty of speech
- CHW role: don't challenge psychotic content (arguing doesn't help; can escalate); don't collude; maintain calm; facilitate psychiatric care access
- Distinguishing from delirium: acute confusional state from medical illness is a medical emergency; person oriented yesterday who is confused and hallucinating today = medical evaluation
- Methamphetamine psychosis: clinically indistinguishable from schizophrenia while using and for a period after; factor substance use history into any psychotic presentation

**Grief and Loss**
- Not psychiatric diagnosis; pervasive in this population: death of spouses/siblings/lifelong friends, loss of physical capability, loss of meaningful work and identity, loss of community that used to exist
- Normal grief: moves; acute intensity decreases gradually and irregularly; person maintains connections and function
- Complicated grief: stuck; intensity doesn't decrease; function severely impaired months or years after loss; CHW should know markers and local resources
- CHW role with grief primarily: presence and witness; allow talking about who was lost; don't rush to resolution; don't fill silences with reassurance; ask about the person who died as a person

### Part Three: Recognition in Context

**The Household Pattern**
- When everyone in a household is struggling, look for shared cause before attributing individual pathology
- Household organized around severe depression or substance use has a recognizable relational pattern affecting everyone
- Seeing household as unit: home visit enables what no clinical encounter can

**The Somatic Presentation**
- Mental health conditions in this population present through the body more than through the psyche
- Depression → fatigue, unexplained pain, GI disturbance, headaches; Anxiety → chest tightness, palpitations, dizziness; Trauma → chronic pain, sleep disturbance, hyperreactivity to physical sensations
- The CHW has temporal and contextual information no clinic has: headaches started after job loss; fatigue got worse after spouse's cancer diagnosis; GI symptoms appear during family conflict

**Functional Change as Key Indicator**
- More reliable than any specific symptom
- Garden untended, Working Society events stopped, engagement flat, household maintenance declining, driving stopped without apparent physical reason
- "Do they seem like themselves" — departure from baseline is the clinical finding; longitudinal CHW relationship provides the baseline

### Part Four: What the CHW Does

**Presence as Intervention**
- For a population with thin formal mental health resources, the CHW relationship is often not a bridge to treatment — it often is the treatment
- Research on mental health recovery: quality of human connection is consistently the most powerful factor — more than specific technique
- Qualities required: consistency; non-judgment; genuine curiosity; tolerance for pain without needing to resolve it immediately; appropriate limits

**The Naming Conversation**
- "What you're describing sounds like what happens to people carrying what you've been carrying"
- Accurate naming removes shame without adding pathology label
- Person who understands their experience as comprehensible response to comprehensible conditions has more agency to address it

**Connecting to the Community**
- Garden, communal kitchen, skills sessions — purposeful activity with others
- Depression makes first step feel impossible; CHW is the bridge: "come with me once, you don't have to stay long, I'll be there"
- Follow-through essential: CHW who said "come with me" and wasn't there has done damage; CHW who was there has built something

**Practical Problem Solving**
- Underrated intervention: solving a concrete problem that has been overwhelming restores sense of agency
- Practical problem often directly contributing to mental health picture — removing it reduces the load

**Motivational Interviewing Applied**
- Movement for depression; harm reduction conversations for alcohol; medication adherence for psychiatric medications
- Righting reflex is strongest in mental health conversations; MI discipline is most essential here

**Recognizing the Ceiling**
- Active suicidal ideation with plan and means: beyond CHW alone
- Acute psychosis requiring medical stabilization: medical
- Severe alcohol withdrawal with seizure risk: medical emergency
- CHW role in these situations: stay present, maintain safety as much as possible, facilitate access to whatever care exists even when inadequate
- Know the least-bad escalation pathway specifically for Franklin County

### Part Five: Suicide and Self-Harm

**Epidemiology**
- Rural suicide rates significantly higher than urban and rising: higher firearm access (more lethal means), geographic isolation, higher risk factor rates, cultural barriers to help-seeking
- Highest-risk demographic: middle-aged and older white men — highest-risk in the US; least likely to seek help; most likely to use lethal means; significant portion of Working Society membership

**Asking Directly**
- Research is consistent and strong: asking does not increase risk; often decreases it by breaking isolation and communicating attention
- Required language: "Are you thinking about suicide?" / "Are you thinking about ending your life?" — not indirect softened versions that allow answering the wrong question
- If yes or maybe: stay present; listen; ask about specifics (plan, means); passive ideation ≠ active ideation with specific plan and means — both warrant attention, active with plan and means warrants immediate response

**The Means Conversation**
- Means restriction: one of the most evidence-based suicide prevention interventions
- Primary means in rural communities: firearms
- Framing required: not gun control — practical safety during a hard time: "is there someone who can hold onto the firearms while you're going through this — not permanently, not a statement, just right now"
- CHW with relationship and cultural familiarity can have this conversation more effectively than professional responders who lack both

**After a Disclosure**
- Responsibility doesn't end when the visit ends: follow up; check in genuinely
- Disclosure was an act of trust requiring continued presence to honor
- Document carefully with attention to privacy; communicate with program oversight

### Part Six: Self-Care for CHWs

**Vicarious Trauma**
- Predictable consequence of sustained empathic presence with people carrying significant trauma, grief, and mental health burden
- Signs: intrusive thoughts about people you work with; difficulty leaving work behind; hypervigilance spilling into personal life; emotional numbing; worldview shift toward seeing world as predominantly dangerous and suffering; disrupted sleep
- Distinct from burnout: burnout is depletion; vicarious trauma is a change in nervous system and worldview from sustained exposure

**Peer Supervision**
- Most important protective factor: regular structured time with other CHWs to process what they're carrying, share difficult cases, maintain perspective
- Not therapy; not case management review — space to say "this one is hard" and have someone who understands hear it
- Working Society structure supports this naturally

**Personal Practices**
- Same interventions CHW recommends for members apply to CHW: sleep, movement, social connection outside CHW role, meaningful non-service activity
- Transition rituals: something that marks the end of CHW work and the return to personal life — physical transition, brief written reflection; what matters is that it exists and is practiced consistently

**When More Support Is Needed**
- Normal to need more at certain points: when a member's situation is close to the CHW's own history; when multiple difficult situations occur simultaneously; when vicarious trauma or burnout signs aren't resolving
- Working Society's responsibility: create conditions where asking for support is normal and available, not stigmatized

## 8. Navigating What Actually Exists

### Framing
- Official resource map ≠ actual accessible resource map for this population
- Operating from official map: referrals that go nowhere, generated paperwork, lost credibility with members
- This module is the real map

### Part One: The Formal System

**Primary Care**
- What exists: specific providers, locations, hours, accepted insurance, actual appointment availability
- Honest picture: physician pipeline into rural practice has essentially collapsed (debt, lifestyle, payment structures favoring procedural over cognitive work); mid-level providers and FQHCs filling some gap
- Appointment reality: weeks to months out for new patients
- Cost reality: copays represent genuine barriers; choosing between copay and groceries is a rational choice that looks like noncompliance from the clinical side

**Emergency Department**
- De facto primary care for uninsured/underserved; worst setting for chronic disease management and mental health support
- What ED is good for: acute emergencies, urgent diagnostic workups
- What it's not good for: chronic disease management, mental health crisis, primary care substitution
- Financial consequences: billing practices, charity care programs and how to access them, medical debt as bankruptcy cause

**Medicaid and the Coverage Landscape**
- Georgia Medicaid expansion history: partial, late; significant coverage gap for working poor (earn too much for traditional Medicaid, too little to afford marketplace insurance — specific to non-expansion states)
- What Medicaid covers in Georgia: specific limitations, prior authorization requirements, provider network constraints in rural areas
- Marketplace plans: subsidies, navigator program, where to find navigators in Northeast Georgia
- Key programs to know: PeachState Health Management (dominant Medicaid MCO in region); pharmaceutical manufacturer patient assistance programs for most commonly prescribed medications; GoodRx and similar for uninsured cash-pay

**Mental Health Resources — the Honest Picture**
- State plainly: outpatient mental health in Franklin County is extremely limited; wait for psychiatric appointment measured in months; cost without insurance prohibitive
- Community Service Board (public mental health system): specific CSB serving Franklin County, location, services, honest capacity and wait time assessment
- Crisis resources: 988 Lifeline (genuinely useful for some, real limitations including wait times and welfare check responses that can escalate); specific local crisis response; mobile crisis teams and their actual availability
- Inpatient psychiatric beds: scarcity; Georgia 1013 process (involuntary commitment); people in genuine psychiatric crisis may wait in EDs for days

**Social Services**
- DFCS: SNAP, TANF, Medicaid eligibility; application process and barriers; specific Franklin County office
- SNAP: who qualifies, actual benefit level vs food costs, application and recertification requirements for someone working irregular hours without reliable internet
- Housing assistance: years-long waiting lists; Housing Choice Voucher reality; emergency rental assistance programs
- Utility assistance: LIHEAP timing in Georgia; utility company programs; specific contacts

### Part Two: Real Accessible Resources

**UGA Cooperative Extension**
- Likely the most useful formal resource; significantly underutilized
- Franklin County office in Carnesville: Master Gardener support, water testing (low cost), food preservation education including canning safety, agricultural assistance, SNAP-Ed nutrition education
- Water testing process in specific terms: container, collection method, turnaround time, how to read results
- Organizational relationship worth cultivating — Extension agent has knowledge, relationships, and access complementary to Working Society mission

**Free Clinics and Charitable Care**
- Specific free clinics within reasonable distance: hours, eligibility, capacity, wait times
- RAM (Remote Area Medical) clinics: schedule and locations for this region; services provided (typically dental, vision, basic medical); what they don't provide; how to find next scheduled event
- Charitable pharmacy programs: NeedyMeds, RxAssist, manufacturer programs; application requirements, documentation, turnaround times

**FQHCs**
- What an FQHC is: federally funded community health center, sliding fee scale regardless of ability to pay
- Nearest FQHCs to Franklin County; actual accessibility given transportation reality

**Faith Communities**
- Specific congregations with food pantries, emergency assistance funds, informal mutual aid — not in any official directory
- Who to call, what they can provide, eligibility expectations
- Approach thoughtfully: use practical resources without requiring ideological alignment; some will be natural allies, others will have views creating friction

**Informal Networks**
- Mutual aid existing outside any organization: neighbors checking on each other, families sharing food and labor
- Hyperlocal knowledge: who has a truck, who does repairs, who checks on elderly people on a particular road, who can provide meals for a week
- Accumulates through relationship and presence over time; three-year member knows things no newcomer can know

### Part Three: The Working Society as Primary Resource

*For many members, the Working Society is not a supplement to the formal resource landscape — it is the primary resource landscape.*

**Health Resources**
- CHW program: how members access CHW support; how CHWs coordinate; physician oversight interface
- Medical equipment library: shared BP cuffs, glucometers, pulse oximeters
- Health knowledge commons: disease pages, quick references, resource guides from this curriculum

**Food Resources**
- Community garden: what it produces, when, how members access it
- Communal kitchen: what it provides, hours, how members participate
- Bulk purchasing: available items, ordering process, kin currency payment
- Food preservation: canning equipment, knowledge holders, workshops

**Economic Resources**
- Kin currency: how it works, what it's used for, how to earn and spend
- Commons fund: what it covers, access process, requesting support
- Practical assistance: application help, appointment accompaniment, transportation coordination

**Social Resources**
- Events, working groups, skills sessions, garden gatherings as connection infrastructure
- CHW role: not "here's a program" but "here's a specific person who will welcome you" — making human introductions, not referrals

### Part Four: Navigation as Skill

**Barrier Assessment**
- Before any referral: transportation, cost, scheduling vs work/caregiving, documentation requirements, institutional trust
- A referral not accounting for these barriers is passing the problem, not solving it

**The Warm Handoff**
- Giving a phone number vs making the connection actually happen
- Making the call together; accompanying to the appointment; CHW time used this way is a legitimate and valuable use

**Following Up**
- Referral is complete when person has received the service — not when referral was made
- Converts referral list into actual navigation; maintains the credibility on which future recommendations rest

**Maintaining Resource Knowledge**
- Programs end, funding lapses, contacts change; resource knowledge from two years ago may not reflect current terrain
- Living resource directory maintained collectively by all CHWs

**When Nothing Exists**
- Document the gap: individual problems aggregate into organizational roadmap
- Be honest with the member: "what you need doesn't really exist in accessible form right now; here's what I can do, here's what the community can do"
- Honesty maintains trust; false referrals fail twice — once by the system and once by the person who sent them toward something that wasn't there

### Part Five: Advocacy

**Identifying Patterns**
- Recurring gap across multiple households → case for organizational response
- Transportation barrier → transit service prioritization; medication cost → assistance program; mental health gap → internal capacity rather than continued referral to inaccessible resources

**Organizational Advocacy**
- CHW is organization's ground-level intelligence source; patterns belong in governance and decision-making

**External Advocacy**
- Considered carefully given non-partisan institutional positioning
- Naming facts (closed hospital, nonexistent services, rationed prescriptions) is not partisan — it is description; the Society can name it

## 9. Death and Palliative Care

### What This Module Must Accomplish
- Normalize death as topic — not morbidly, but as every culture before modern medicine understood it: a part of life people participated in rather than outsourced to institutions
- Give the CHW a genuine framework for when the medical system is unavailable, unreachable, or unable to change the outcome
- Address palliative care — serious illness with comfort and quality of life as primary goals; most underserved area in rural medicine
- Address legal and practical realities of home death in Georgia
- Address grief as appropriate human response, not pathology to manage

### Framing
- Death is not a medical failure — it is the end of every life
- Medical system has done extraordinary things to push it further away; it has also produced a culture that cannot look at death, be present with it, or find meaning in it
- For most of human history this knowledge was common; it has been lost not because death became less common but because it moved into institutions
- This module is a partial restoration of that knowledge

### Part One: Understanding the Dying Process

**Physiology of Dying**
- Common pathways: cardiovascular failure, respiratory failure, neurological failure, organ failure following catastrophic illness or injury
- All share a recognizable final territory; understanding the pathway reduces fear of witnessing it

*Signs in final days and hours:*
- Decreased oral intake: natural, not to be corrected; body redirecting resources away from digestion; forcing food/fluids increases discomfort without supporting life
- Increased sleep and decreased responsiveness: person leaves gradually; hearing often last sense to go — family members can still speak to the person when unresponsive
- Cheyne-Stokes respiration: irregular breathing with pauses; neurological phenomenon, not struggle; distressing to witness but typically not reflecting the person's distress
- Death rattle: secretions in throat person can no longer clear; typically not conscious distress; repositioning and mouth care can reduce it
- Mottling: blotchy purple pattern on legs and feet as circulation centralizes; hands and feet cool while torso remains warm; typically precedes death by hours to ~2 days
- Moment of death: cessation of breathing, cessation of heartbeat; how to observe and confirm; what to do (and not rush to do) afterward

**AMI Scenario — Specific and Honest**
- The math problem: 40-minute ambulance; 60+ minutes to nearest cardiac catheterization lab; 90-minute damage window for major MI; the arithmetic sometimes doesn't work
- What the CHW can actually do:
  - Call 911 immediately regardless — the calculation might be wrong; early arrival of any care > no care; don't let the arithmetic paralyze the first step
  - Aspirin: 325mg chewed (not swallowed), immediately, if not allergic and no obvious contraindication; explain why it's in the household kit
  - Position of comfort: semi-reclined, not flat
  - Oxygen: beneficial if SpO₂ <94%; not clearly necessary above 94%; most households won't have it
  - Calm presence: real physiological intervention; person in MI + fear = sympathetic activation increasing myocardial oxygen demand; CHW's regulated nervous system is a genuine intervention
  - Hold the hand; talk to the person; if losing consciousness, tell them they're not alone and the people who love them are there
- The honest part nobody says: some situations will not be survived before help arrives; the CHW must be able to recognize this and make the transition from supporting survival to supporting a good death — not giving up; recognizing when the goal has shifted
- Supporting the family through watching someone they love die: tell them it's okay to stay close, to speak, to touch; the CHW as calm witnessing presence is what the family needs

**Stroke — Same Honest Treatment**
- Recognition: FAST + additional signs
- Call 911 immediately; describe clearly: suspected stroke, exact symptom onset time, distance to hospital
- tPA window: 4.5 hours from symptom onset for ischemic stroke; knowing whether that window is achievable given transport time matters and should be communicated to dispatch
- Hemorrhagic stroke: tPA contraindicated; surgical intervention; shorter window; more specialized requirements
- Position: recovery position if unconscious (on side, airway clear); nothing by mouth (swallowing may be impaired, aspiration risk); monitoring until help arrives or situation resolves

### Part Two: Palliative Care

**What Palliative Care Is**
- Not synonymous with hospice; not giving up; not stopping curative treatment
- Approach to serious illness prioritizing comfort, quality of life, alignment with person's values — coexists with whatever medical treatment is being pursued
- Rural context: formal palliative care services nearly absent outside hospice; CHW can provide the human dimensions — regular presence, attention to comfort, values conversations, family caregiver support

**Symptom Management**

*Pain:*
- Most important symptom; most often undertreated in this population
- WHO analgesic ladder: non-opioid → opioid by severity
- What's accessible without prescription; how to use it effectively; when to advocate urgently for better management
- Non-pharmacological contributions: positioning, heat/cold, gentle massage, distraction, relaxation — real in moderate pain; complements medication in severe pain

*Dyspnea (shortness of breath):*
- One of the most distressing symptoms; interventions available without oxygen equipment
- Upright positioning (reduces work of breathing); fan directed at face (sensation of moving air reduces subjective breathlessness — consistent, real, mechanism not fully understood)
- Calm presence: dyspnea triggers panic which worsens dyspnea; co-regulation is a real intervention

*Nausea:*
- Small amounts of fluid if tolerated; cool cloths; positioning; avoiding strong smells; OTC options; when to advocate for prescription antiemetics

*Constipation:*
- Nearly universal with opioid medications; stimulant laxatives (not bulk-forming) for opioid-induced constipation

*Mouth Care:*
- Critical in final stages when person can no longer manage own oral hygiene and dehydration dries mucous membranes
- Techniques: mouth swabs, lip moisturizer, positioning to prevent secretion pooling
- Family members can do this with guidance

*Skin Care and Pressure Injury Prevention:*
- Bedbound patient who cannot reposition will develop pressure injuries at bony prominences — painful, potentially serious
- Reposition every 2 hours if possible; protective padding; skin assessment at each turn
- Family caregivers can do this with instruction

**Goals of Care Conversation**
- What does this person actually want — not the family's wants, not the medical default, not what you do when no one has asked
- CHW often best positioned: longitudinal relationship vs episodic clinical encounter; not entangled in family's own needs and fears
- Questions to open the conversation: "what matters most to you right now"; "what are you most afraid of"; "what does a good day look like for you at this point"; "if you couldn't speak for yourself, what would you want the people around you to know about what you'd want"
- These require the Module 5 relational foundation and Module 7 capacity to sit with difficulty

**Advance Directives in Georgia**
- Georgia Advance Directive for Health Care: combined living will (treatment preferences) + healthcare agent designation; free form from State Bar of Georgia; 2 witnesses required, no notary; know where to find it and how to help complete it
- MOLST (Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment — Georgia's POLST equivalent): medical order signed by physician/APP; translates wishes into specific orders emergency responders and hospital staff are required to follow
- Advance directive vs MOLST: directive guides decisions; MOLST is an order — for someone with serious illness and clear preferences, MOLST is operationally more powerful
- DNR: what CPR actually is and what outcomes it actually produces in serious illness (television portrayal is not accurate); honest information is required for genuine informed choice; this conversation happens if person has the information and the CHW can provide it in ways rushed clinical encounters often can't

**Hospice**
- What it is: Medicare/Medicaid benefit for terminal diagnosis + prognosis of 6 months or less; provides nursing, aide, social work, chaplaincy, terminal-diagnosis medications, and medical equipment at no cost to patient
- Access barriers in rural Georgia: agencies operate in Franklin County; geographic reality means lower visit frequency
- Six-month prognosis requirement and referral timing reality: physicians refer far too late; people who enroll earlier have better symptom management, better family support, less chaotic dying — and in some studies live slightly longer; earlier is better
- CHW role: recognize eligibility; facilitate conversation; reframe "giving up" misconception

**Supporting Family Caregivers**
- Caregiver is also a patient: specific chronic stress with specific health consequences; own health maintenance often collapses; social connections narrow; sleep disrupted; identity outside caregiver role erodes
- Attend to the caregiver at every visit — not just caregiving tasks but how they themselves are doing
- Respite (even brief periods of relief from caregiving): one of the most important interventions; Working Society membership can provide it
- Anticipatory grief: grief before the death; watching someone diminish incrementally before they're gone; real phenomenon caregiver often can't name or doesn't feel entitled to; naming it reduces isolation around it

### Part Three: After Death

**Immediate Practical Reality**
- Expected death (hospice patient, known terminal illness with POLST/DNR): call hospice nurse if enrolled; no urgency to move the body; there is time
- Unexpected death: Georgia coroner notification required; know local coroner contact and when notification is required
- Death certificate: who can sign (attending physician, coroner, or medical examiner depending on circumstances); information required; timeframes; why it matters (financial accounts, estate, benefits)
- Funeral homes: what happens when called; simple and affordable options are legitimate (direct cremation, graveside service, green burial available in Georgia); Working Society's potential role in meaningful and affordable death rituals

**Being Present at Death**
- Knowledge that was common and isn't — people were present at deaths throughout human history; medicalization removed most people from the experience
- What presence looks like: quiet, calm; no urgency to call anyone or do anything in the immediate moments; allow time with the person before practical processes begin — an hour or two belongs to the family
- What helps family: tell them it's okay to stay close, to touch, to speak; the person can hear until the end; CHW as witness not coordinator in this moment

### Part Four: Grief

**The Reality of Grief**
- Not Kübler-Ross stages: never intended as universal linear sequence; their reduction to checklist has done harm by suggesting grief should proceed in order and people not following it are doing it wrong
- What grief actually is: disordered, non-linear, profoundly physical whole-body reorganization around an absence; affects sleep, appetite, concentration, immune function, cardiovascular health
- Timelines: first year is acute phase; second year often harder (acute support has withdrawn, reality has become more permanent); years 3-5 involve integration into genuinely changed life rather than restoration to what was
- This population: multiple compounding losses (spouse, capability, work identity, community that no longer exists); cumulative grief never adequately processed

**What Grief Needs**
- Witness, not intervention
- Someone who can hear about the loss — about who they lost, about what is missing — without becoming uncomfortable, rushing toward resolution, or offering silver linings (silver linings manage the witness's discomfort, not the grieving person's)
- Ask about the person who died as a person: "tell me about her"; "what was he like"; "what do you miss most" — honors the loss by honoring the person who was lost
- Return to the subject across multiple visits: not checking on progress but genuine ongoing interest in who is missing

**Complicated Grief**
- Markers: intense longing not diminishing over time; difficulty accepting reality of death; bitterness that doesn't soften; sense that life is meaningless; withdrawal from all connections; inability to function many months or years after loss
- Resource picture in Franklin County: thin; hospice bereavement services available for hospice families for up to one year; grief support groups where they exist; formal options otherwise are the Module 8 mental health landscape
- Working Society as primary resource: community that knows this person, includes them in its life, gives them reasons to be present

### Closing Note
- The other modules close with competence — here's what you now know, here's what you can do
- Module 9 closes differently: the CHW who has completed this module is prepared not to prevent death but to be present with it
- The families who are accompanied through a death by someone who knows what is happening — who can name it, stay present without flinching, tell them what to do and what not to do — carry the experience differently than families left alone with something they didn't understand
- The person who dies surrounded by people who can be genuinely present dies better than the person who dies in chaos or far from the people they love
- Being able to provide that is one of the most profoundly human things the Working Society's CHWs will do
