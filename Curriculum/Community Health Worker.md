# Community Health Worker

**Format:** Cohort, approximately 8–12 members plus one coordinator
**Session length:** 90–120 minutes
**Frequency:** Weekly
**Duration:** Structured phase of approximately 10 modules, then supervised practice
**Setting:** In person; cohort sessions supplemented by supervised home visits and skills practice

---

## Why This Program Exists

The formal healthcare system in rural America is failing the people who need it most. Primary care is scarce, specialist care is distant, mental health services are nearly inaccessible, and the social conditions that drive most chronic disease — economic precarity, isolation, poor food environments, inadequate housing — are invisible to a system organized around episodic clinical encounters. The CHW program is not a workaround for that failure. It is a different model: health knowledge distributed broadly through the community, held by trusted members who show up in people's lives consistently, who know the context, and who understand that the blood pressure reading and the food environment and the chronic stress are part of the same picture.

The Resolute Society does not outsource health to a Division. The Division of Health, in partnership with the Division of Education, is the community organized around health knowledge — and the CHW is the primary expression of that in members' daily lives.

---

## What This Program Is

Training for community members to serve as trusted health supporters for their fellow members. Not clinical training — CHWs do not diagnose, prescribe, or treat. They observe, assess, educate, support, refer, and follow up. Within those functions they have enormous value, particularly in a rural population with thin formal resources and strong reasons to distrust institutions.

The program combines classroom learning with supervised practice. Knowing the content is necessary but not sufficient — the skills that matter most (taking blood pressure, conducting a home visit, having a motivational interviewing conversation) can only be built through doing.

---

## The Coordinator's Role

The coordinator is not a lecturer. They are a more experienced practitioner who holds the structure, introduces content, facilitates cohort discussion, and supervises practice. They bring their own experience with the work — they are not outside it.

The cohort is as important as the curriculum. Members learn from each other's practice, debrief together, and hold each other accountable over time. The coordinator protects and develops that dynamic.

## Modules

### Module 0 — Why This Exists

Short. Possibly the most important module in the program. Establishes what the CHW is and is not. Names the present moment honestly: the healthcare system is failing this population, and this program is a community choosing to take responsibility for its own health rather than waiting for a system that isn't coming. Sets the tone for everything that follows.

### Module 1 — The Role and Its Limits

What a CHW does: observe, assess, educate, support, refer, follow up. What a CHW does not do: diagnose, prescribe, treat clinically. Why knowing the boundary is as important as knowing the content — a CHW who acts beyond their competence causes harm. The relationship as the primary instrument.

### Module 2 — The Body as a System

The major body systems taught not as anatomy to memorize but as problems the body solves. Cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, endocrine, nervous, musculoskeletal, immune, digestive. Each system introduced through its function, its failure modes, and its relevance to what the CHW will actually see. Closes with homeostasis as the unifying concept — disease is homeostasis failing.

### Module 3 — How the Body Fails

The common mechanisms that feed multiple disease pathways: chronic inflammation, insulin resistance, the cortisol pathway, cellular aging. Each taught once, applied across the specific disease trajectories that follow — cardiovascular, metabolic, respiratory, renal, depression. Case narrative structure throughout: a recognizable person traced from earliest upstream conditions through established disease to late complications, with intervention points named explicitly at each stage.

### Module 4 — Seeing What's There

The observational and measurement skills of the CHW. Vital signs: blood pressure (manual), pulse, respiratory rate, temperature, pulse oximetry. Systematic observation: general appearance, skin, hands, face, abdomen, the diabetic foot exam. The home visit as an assessment tool: medication review, food environment, physical environment, functional status. Documentation: the SOAP note simplified, longitudinal value of visit records.

Cohort requirement: skills workshop where all members practice on each other, with feedback on technique.

### Module 5 — Talking to People

What the CHW is actually doing in conversation: creating conditions for the person to find their own motivation, not delivering information to a passive recipient. Motivational interviewing as the framework — the four processes (engaging, focusing, evoking, planning) as a map, not a script. The specific textures of this population: self-reliance, privacy, masculine identity, institutional distrust, religion and folk health beliefs. Difficult conversations: the person not following their treatment plan, the person in mental health crisis, the person who doesn't want help.

Cohort requirement: role play with genuine debrief — what did it feel like, when did the righting reflex fire, what happened in the silence.

### Module 6 — Social Determinants of Health

The central finding of social epidemiology: where you're born, your income, your work, your social connections predict health outcomes more reliably than access to medical care. The mechanisms in plain language: economic precarity as physiological condition, the cognitive bandwidth tax of chronic scarcity, social isolation as biological threat, the food environment, transportation, housing quality, adverse childhood experiences. Franklin County specifically — economic history, current employment, healthcare landscape, community assets. What the CHW can and cannot address, and how to distinguish between them.

### Module 7 — Mental Health and Substance Use

The reframe: mental health conditions in this population are largely the predictable human response to conditions that would strain any person, not primarily individual brain chemistry gone wrong. The neuroscience foundation. The conditions the CHW will actually encounter — depression, anxiety, post-traumatic stress, substance use, psychosis, grief — with attention to how each presents in this population specifically. What the CHW does: presence as intervention, the naming conversation, connecting to community, practical problem solving, recognizing the ceiling. Suicide and self-harm in depth — asking directly, the means conversation, after a disclosure. Self-care for CHWs: vicarious trauma, peer supervision, knowing when you need more support.

### Module 8 — Navigating What Actually Exists

The real resource map, not the official one. What formal resources exist in Franklin County and what they actually deliver. What is genuinely accessible and what isn't. The Resolute Society as primary resource for many members. Navigation as a skill: barrier assessment before any referral, the warm handoff, following up until the referral results in actual care, maintaining resource knowledge as it changes, being honest when nothing adequate exists.

### Module 9 — Death and Palliative Care

Restoring knowledge that was common before medicine moved death into institutions. The physiology of dying — what is actually happening in the body and what the family needs to know. The specific rural emergency scenarios: what to do when help is far away and someone is having a heart attack or stroke. Palliative care as an approach — symptom management, the goals of care conversation, advance directives in Georgia, hospice. After death: the practical reality, being present at the moment of death, what families need in those hours. Grief — not as pathology but as the appropriate human response to loss, what it needs from the CHW, when it becomes complicated.

### Module 10 — Documentation and Continuity

The CHW's records as a clinical asset. What to document, how, and why continuity across visits is the distinctive value the CHW provides that no clinic encounter can replicate. The Resolute Society's health knowledge commons — how CHW documentation feeds into the shared resource the Division of Health maintains. Handoff when a CHW relationship needs to transfer.

## What Success Looks Like

A CHW who completes the program and enters supervised practice should be able to:

- Take a manual blood pressure accurately and interpret the trend over time
- Conduct a home visit that assesses medication adherence, food environment, physical environment, and functional status
- Have a genuine motivational interviewing conversation without triggering the righting reflex
- Recognize the major chronic disease presentations in this population and know the intervention points
- Ask directly about suicidal ideation and respond appropriately to the answer
- Navigate the real resource landscape honestly — warm handoffs, follow-up, and honest acknowledgment when nothing adequate exists
- Be present with a dying person and their family without flinching
- Maintain the longitudinal documentation that makes the CHW relationship clinically valuable over time

The CHW is not a substitute for a healthcare system. They are something the healthcare system cannot be: a trusted member of the community who shows up consistently, understands the context, and stays.
