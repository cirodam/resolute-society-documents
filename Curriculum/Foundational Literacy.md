# Foundational Literacy

A tutor-delivered program for adult learners. Delivered one-on-one, one to two sessions per week, approximately 45–60 minutes per session.

## Why This Program Exists

Approximately 130 million adults in the United States — more than half the adult population — read below a sixth-grade level. One in five reads at or below a third-grade level. These are not primarily people who never attended school. They are people the school system passed through without teaching. They sat in classrooms, received grades, and emerged without the foundational skills those classrooms were supposed to provide. The system's failure was quiet and gradual, distributed across years and institutions, and the people it failed were left holding the consequences alone.

The effects are not abstract. Adults who cannot read reliably cannot navigate medical information, legal documents, employment paperwork, or civic life without help from others — which means without dependency on others. Literacy is infrastructure. Its absence constrains everything built on top of it.

The Resolute Society does not treat this as someone else's problem to solve. Members who carry literacy gaps carry them into every part of community life — governance, health, the Compendium, the Society's own materials. This program is how the Society takes responsibility for that directly, through the one-on-one relationship that the research consistently shows works when institutional instruction did not.

## Part One: For the Tutor

### Who This Learner Is

An adult who cannot read, or who reads far below the level daily life requires, has spent years developing workarounds. They memorize, ask others, avoid situations that expose the gap, and present competence they don't feel. By the time they sit down with a tutor, they have usually carried shame about this for a long time — often since childhood, often in connection with experiences of school that did not go well.

This shapes everything about how tutoring must work. The learner is not a slow child. They are an adult with a full life, real knowledge, and genuine skills who happens not to have this one thing. The tutor's job is to make the learning feel consistent with the dignity they already have, not like a remediation of a defect.

### What Makes This Different from Teaching Children

Adults bring motivation that children often don't — they have a reason they're here, and that reason is usually immediate and practical. Use it. The content layer throughout the program should connect to what actually matters to the learner: the forms they need to fill out, the labels they want to read, the messages they want to write, the Society materials they want to engage with. Invented examples are weaker than real ones.

Adults also bring more frustration tolerance and more accumulated fear. Progress may feel slow to them even when it isn't, because they have been failed before and expect to be failed again. The tutor's job is to make progress visible — concrete, specific, undeniable — so the learner can hold onto evidence of what is actually happening.

### The Tutoring Relationship

This is not school. There are no grades, no wrong answers that go on a record, no other students watching. The tutor and learner are two people working together on something the learner wants. The tutor's authority comes from knowing the path, not from institutional position.

Establish this explicitly in the first session and return to it when the dynamic drifts. If the learner apologizes for struggling, redirect: struggling is what learning looks like.

Pace follows the learner, not the curriculum. The session count ranges in each unit are estimates, not targets. A learner who needs twelve sessions on a unit that typically takes six is not behind — they are learning at their pace, and rushing produces the worst possible outcome: a fragile foundation the rest of the program is built on.

### Session Structure

Sessions are 45–60 minutes. Longer is usually counterproductive — adult attention and working memory under the stress of difficult learning tire quickly.

A session has a rhythm: open with something the learner already knows and can do successfully (two to three minutes); introduce or practice new material (twenty to thirty minutes); close with something again in the learner's competence (five to ten minutes). Starting and ending in competence is not a formality — it regulates the emotional arc of the session. The learner should leave feeling capable, even when the session was hard.

Between sessions, brief practice matters more than long practice. Ten minutes three times before the next session outperforms an hour the night before.

### When Something Isn't Working

If a learner is not making progress on a unit after the upper end of its session range, stop and assess before continuing. Common causes: the foundational skill from a prior unit wasn't actually solid; the learner has an unidentified vision problem; the learner has a phonological processing difference that requires a different approach to phonics instruction. The answer is almost never to repeat the same instruction more slowly or loudly.

If a learner mentions or shows signs of a vision or hearing problem that hasn't been addressed, name it directly and connect them to the Division of Health. Uncorrected vision is the single most common non-instructional cause of reading difficulty in adults.

## Part Two: The Program

The program has two stages. Stage One builds the decoding foundation — the ability to turn written symbols into sounds and words. Stage Two builds fluency and comprehension — the ability to read with enough ease that meaning comes through.

Most learners need Stage One before Stage Two is accessible. Some learners have a partial foundation and can enter at a later unit. The baseline assessment in Part Three identifies the entry point.

### Stage One: Foundation

**Unit 0 — Orientation (2–3 sessions)**

No reading yet. The purpose is to build rapport, establish the rhythm, and learn the learner.

The tutor listens. What does this person do, care about, talk about? What words keep coming up? What do they want to be able to read or write? That material becomes the content layer for the rest of the program — the examples, the sentences, the words.

Also establish the contract: this is not school, there is no wrong, struggling is expected and normal, the pace is theirs.

**Unit 1 — Phonemic Awareness (4–6 sessions)**

Entirely oral — no print yet. The goal is a single conceptual move: spoken words are made of separate sounds that can be heard, counted, and manipulated. Without this, phonics instruction has no substrate.

Activities: rhyme recognition, syllable clapping, isolating the first sound in a word, isolating the last sound, blending separate sounds into a word. Keep sessions short on this material — 15–20 minutes of phonemic work, then shift to conversation or something else. Phonemic awareness work is mentally effortful in a way that doesn't feel like effort until fatigue sets in.

If the learner struggles here, slow down. This unit is the foundation for everything that follows.

**Unit 2 — Letters and Their Sounds (6–8 sessions)**

Introduce the most consistent consonants first: m, s, t, f, n, b, p, h, d. Then short vowels: a, i, o, then e and u. Avoid c, g, x, q early — they are inconsistent or redundant.

Letter formation is integrated from day one. The learner says the sound, writes the letter, reads it back. This tri-modal approach (sound, hand, eye) builds more durable connections than visual recognition alone.

The goal is sounds, not the alphabet. The learner who can recite the alphabet but cannot produce the sound for each letter has not completed this unit.

**Unit 3 — CVC Words (8–10 sessions)**

Consonant-vowel-consonant words: sat, fin, hop, bed, mud. Blending (reading) and segmenting (spelling) are taught as mirror skills — they are the same process run in opposite directions.

Vocabulary for practice is drawn from the learner's life where possible. Tool names, plant names, animal names, family members' names — whatever connects. First sentences are simple: *The dog sat. Sam ran.* Resolute Society context enters naturally here.

**Unit 4 — Blends, Digraphs, and Short Vowel Variants (8–10 sessions)**

Consonant blends: bl, st, tr, dr, sp, and others. Digraphs: ch, sh, th, wh — two letters, one sound. Words that combine these with CVC patterns: shed, chip, trap, blend.

First multi-sentence texts appear here — three to five sentences on a topic the learner cares about. The learner writes one sentence from dictation and one of their own per session.

**Unit 5 — Long Vowel Patterns (8–10 sessions)**

Silent-e first: make, pine, hope, cute. Then vowel teams: rain, boat, feet, day. This is where many learners hit a wall — the patterns are real but less consistent than what came before.

Slow down here. Sorting activities work well: given a set of words, the learner sorts them by vowel pattern before decoding them. The category recognition supports the decoding rather than the other way around.

**Unit 6 — High-Frequency Irregular Words (woven throughout; formalized here)**

Words that cannot be fully decoded: the, said, was, have, they, come, some, where, there, of, to, do. These are learned as wholes — recognized on sight.

Introduce a few per unit beginning at Unit 3. Unit 6 formalizes them: flash card review, dictation practice, reading them in context. The learner should be able to read and write the thirty to forty most common irregular words by Stage One completion.

**Stage One Gateway Assessment**

Tutor reads a short passage aloud with the learner, then asks the learner to read a similar passage alone. Learner writes two sentences — one dictated, one of their own.

The tutor records observations on a simple rubric: decoding accuracy, word-by-word vs. phrase-level reading, confidence, writing legibility and spelling. Decision: ready for Stage Two, needs reinforcement in specific units, or needs a different instructional approach entirely.

### Stage Two: Fluency and Comprehension

**Unit 7 — Multisyllabic Words (8–10 sessions)**

The syllable types in plain terms: open syllables (ba-, go-), closed syllables (-cat, -sit), silent-e syllables (-make), vowel team syllables (-boat), r-controlled syllables (-farm, -her).

The chunking strategy: cover part of a long word, decode one chunk at a time, then blend the chunks. Practice words: together, children, important, garden, problem, remember. These are real words the learner will encounter in actual reading.

**Unit 8 — Prefixes, Suffixes, and Roots (6–8 sessions)**

Common prefixes: un-, re-, pre-, dis-. Common suffixes: -ing, -ed, -tion, -ness, -ful, -less, -er. Morphology as a meaning tool, not just a decoding tool. If the learner knows what *work* means, *rework* and *workless* and *worker* follow immediately.

This unit significantly expands accessible vocabulary without requiring new decoding learning.

**Unit 9 — Fluency Practice (6–8 sessions)**

Repeated reading of meaningful passages — the same text read aloud three times across two sessions. The goal is automaticity: when decoding no longer requires conscious effort, comprehension can happen in the space that frees up.

Texts are drawn from Resolute Society materials, practical documents, local news, or whatever the learner most wants to be able to read. The content matters — motivation to read the text affects how much effort the learner brings.

**Unit 10 — Comprehension Strategies (8–10 sessions)**

Four strategies, taught explicitly and practiced on real texts:

- *Main idea and detail:* what is this about, and what supports it
- *Sequence:* what happened first, then, then
- *Cause and effect:* what caused what
- *Inference:* what the text implies but doesn't say directly

The learner keeps a simple reading log — not a school journal, just a record: what they read, one thing they noticed or one question it raised.

**Unit 11 — Writing Paragraphs (6–8 sessions)**

Topic sentence, two or three supporting sentences, closing sentence. Starts with transcription and modification: here is a paragraph, change it to be about your own experience. Moves to independent paragraph writing on prompts tied to real life and Resolute Society context.

The learner who completes this unit can read a simple document with comprehension, write a short coherent message, and engage with the Society's printed materials. That is the completion threshold.

**Completion**

A ceremony is not required. One is appropriate. The learner has done something real and hard that they may have believed for a long time they could not do.

## Part Three: Assessment and Materials

### Baseline Assessment

Conducted in Unit 0, informally. The tutor listens to the learner talk and notes vocabulary range and complexity. If the learner is willing, the tutor asks them to read a short familiar text aloud and write their name and a word or two. The goal is to identify the entry point — not to produce a score.

The baseline identifies: Does the learner know letter-sound correspondences? Can they blend? Do they have sight word vocabulary? Are they beginning readers or non-readers? The entry point follows from these observations. A learner with partial decoding skills may enter at Unit 3 or 4. A learner with no print knowledge starts at Unit 1.

### Session Records

The tutor keeps brief notes after each session: what was covered, what the learner did well, where they struggled, what to return to next session. These notes are not formal documentation — they are the tutor's working memory across sessions. A tutor who starts each session cold loses continuity; the notes prevent this.

### Materials

The program does not depend on purchased materials. What the tutor needs:

- Index cards for letter and word work
- A notebook the learner keeps and owns
- Simple decodable texts at each stage — short passages the tutor writes or selects, built from the phonics patterns the learner has covered
- Real documents drawn from the learner's life: forms, labels, messages, Society materials

The learner's notebook travels with them between sessions. It contains the words they've learned, the sentences they've written, the things they want to be able to read. It is evidence of accumulation — visible proof that something is being built.
