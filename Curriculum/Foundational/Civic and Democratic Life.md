# Civic and Democratic Life

**Format:** Small group, approximately 6 members plus one guide
**Session length:** 90 minutes
**Frequency:** Weekly
**Duration:** Structured phase of approximately 12 sessions, then ongoing practice through participation in community governance
**Setting:** In person, no devices in the room
**Prerequisite:** Living Well in Difficult Times is strongly recommended — the interior stability that course builds is the substrate for engaging honestly with collective decisions under genuine disagreement. Practical Epistemology is also valuable here.
**Note:** This course is most useful when members are actively participating in some form of shared governance — Society meetings, community decisions, collective planning. The course reflects on real governance happening in real time.

---

## What This Course Is

A formation course in what self-governance actually requires of the people who participate in it. Not a civics class. Not an orientation to any particular governance structure. A course in the interior and relational capacities that genuine collective decision-making demands: the capacity to deliberate honestly rather than advocate strategically, to dissent constructively rather than destructively, to hold authority accountable without becoming destructive, and to participate in collective decisions without either deferring to whoever seems most confident or turning every disagreement into a conflict.

Most people living in the United States have almost no experience of genuine self-governance. They have voted in elections managed by institutions they did not build, on questions they did not set, through processes they did not design, producing outcomes they cannot trace to their own participation. That is not self-governance. It is ratification performed at irregular intervals. Meanwhile, the communities, neighborhoods, associations, and workplaces where self-governance is actually possible — where people could genuinely govern their shared life together — mostly don't practice it either. Decisions are made by whoever has the most status, the loudest voice, or the most persistence. Disagreement is managed rather than used. People disengage rather than dissent.

The result is a population that has absorbed the idea of self-governance without developing the capacities it requires. This course is about developing those capacities — not in theory but in practice, through the actual experience of trying to govern something together and examining honestly what that requires.

---

## The Guide's Role

Be a genuine participant in this course, not a teacher above it. You are also a person who must govern with others. Your job is to model the capacities being built — honest deliberation, genuine dissent, willingness to be wrong in public — not to instruct others in them from a position of having mastered them.

The hardest thing this course asks of the guide: genuine neutrality on contested questions while being rigorous about process. If you have positions — and you will — the job is to model how someone with positions participates in a process that doesn't guarantee those positions will prevail, and does so without either suppressing the positions or weaponizing the process.

Name the difficulty honestly when it arises. The capacities being built here are hard. Members will fail at them during the course. That is the material.

---

## A Note on What This Course Is Not

This course does not teach members what positions to hold on contested questions. It does not argue for any political ideology or governance model. It builds capacities — the ability to deliberate well, to dissent constructively, to hold authority accountable — that are valuable regardless of what conclusions they are used to reach.

The guide holds this line. If the course is being used by members or by the guide to advocate for positions on contested questions — even good positions — it has failed.

## Phase 1: What Self-Governance Actually Is

**Sessions 1–3 — Goal: Build a shared honest account of what self-governance requires and what stands in the way**

### Session 1 — The Experience We Don't Have

Opening question: have you ever governed anything? Not voted in — governed. Made a decision you were genuinely responsible for, together with others who were equally responsible, through a process you participated in designing, with real consequences you had to live with.

Let that sit. Most people, when they think carefully, find the answer is closer to no than they expected.

The experience most people have had called "democratic participation" is not self-governance. Voting in elections you had no role in designing is not self-governance. Being consulted before a decision is made by someone else is not self-governance. Attending meetings where outcomes are effectively predetermined is not self-governance. These can be valuable, but they are not the same thing as genuine collective decision-making by people who bear real responsibility for the outcome.

The communities where self-governance is actually possible — where the people affected by a decision are also the people who make it — exist, but they are not common. A neighborhood association that actually decides how its shared resources are used. A cooperative whose members genuinely set its direction. A community organization that is governed by the people it serves. These work when they work because the people involved have capacities most people never develop: the capacity to deliberate honestly with people who disagree, to be genuinely changed by what they hear, to dissent without destroying, to hold each other accountable without cruelty.

This course is about developing those capacities. Not as a preparation for some future governance structure — as a practice for the actual shared life this community is building now.

No practice assigned beyond one instruction: between now and the next session, observe one instance of a group trying to make a collective decision. Not to evaluate it — to notice what is actually happening. How are people talking to each other? Who is being heard? What is being avoided? Where is the process working and where isn't it?

### Session 2 — How Groups Decide, and How They Go Wrong

The specific failure modes of collective decision-making — the ones that appear in every community that tries to govern itself, regardless of its values or intentions.

**Deference to confidence.** Groups systematically overweight the most confident voice. The person who speaks first, speaks loudest, or speaks without visible uncertainty gains disproportionate influence in unstructured deliberation. This is not a character flaw in those who defer — it is a predictable feature of groups under uncertainty. When no one knows the right answer, confidence functions as a substitute for correctness. The result is that collective wisdom — the reason to make decisions together in the first place — gets replaced by the judgment of whoever is most comfortable being assertive.

**Faction.** The corruption of disagreement into teams. Genuine disagreement is about the question. Faction is about the camps. When people begin evaluating arguments by who made them rather than whether they're sound, deliberation has ended and coalition management has begun. Faction is self-reinforcing: once it is present, crossing factional lines becomes a social cost, and the group loses access to honest individual judgment.

**Conflict avoidance.** The opposite failure: groups that suppress disagreement to maintain social peace. Decisions reached through conflict avoidance look like consensus but aren't — the objections went unspoken, the doubts were managed, and the decision carries hidden fracture lines that surface later. The people most likely to practice conflict avoidance are often the most invested in the community's harmony, which makes it harder to name.

**Groupthink.** The pressure toward unanimity that makes groups dumber than their individual members. When belonging to the group becomes more important than being honest about what you think, the group loses access to minority judgments that might be right. This is most dangerous when the group is under stress and most tempting to the people who care most about the group's coherence.

**The planning fallacy in collective form.** Groups are systematically optimistic about their own plans — they underestimate difficulty, overestimate capacity, and discount what could go wrong. This is worse in groups than in individuals because optimistic projections feel more credible when others agree.

Group discussion: which of these does this group already show signs of? Be honest. The guide goes first.

**Practice:** In any group setting this week, notice once when one of these failure modes appears. Write it down. What would have been different if it had been named?

### Session 3 — What Genuine Self-Governance Asks

The session examines what genuine collective decision-making — as opposed to the various simulations of it — actually requires of its participants.

It requires that people enter decisions genuinely open to changing their minds. Not as a performance of open-mindedness, but as an actual epistemological stance: I don't know the right answer yet, and I am willing to end this conversation holding a different view than I came in with. Without this, group deliberation is just sequential advocacy with a vote at the end.

It requires that people be able to disagree with each other without treating the disagreement as a personal attack or a threat to the community. Disagreement is the mechanism through which a community approaches better answers. Suppressing it doesn't produce consensus — it produces unresolved conflict that surfaces later in more destructive forms.

It requires that people be willing to hold and exercise authority — to make decisions that bind others, to take responsibility for how those decisions turn out, to be accountable for the judgment calls they made. Most people are more comfortable as members of a group that decides than as persons who must decide. Genuine self-governance requires both.

It requires that people be able to raise concerns without requiring the community to validate them. A person who can only raise a concern if they're confident it will be well received is not really raising concerns — they are seeking affirmation. Genuine community accountability requires the willingness to say something the group doesn't want to hear.

Group discussion: which of these feels most foreign? Most difficult? Where is each member least prepared?

## Phase 2: The Capacities

**Sessions 4–9 — Goal: Build and practice the specific capacities that genuine self-governance requires**

### Session 4 — Deliberation

Deliberation is not discussion. Discussion is the exchange of views. Deliberation is the collective examination of a question in search of the wisest available response — a process that requires participants to hold their own position lightly enough to be genuinely moved by what they hear.

Most group conversation is not deliberation. It is sequential advocacy: each person argues for a position they arrived at before the conversation began, waits for others to finish, and argues again. The positions may soften slightly through the exchange but they rarely change substantially. What looks like deliberation is often just negotiation among people who have already decided.

Genuine deliberation requires something harder: entering the conversation willing to end it holding a different view than you started with. Not as performance — as an actual openness. This is related to what Interior Capacity and Practical Epistemology build. It is the public, collective version of those interior practices.

Concepts introduced: the difference between advocacy and inquiry as orientations to group discussion. How to contribute to collective thinking rather than to advance a position. What a question that opens deliberation looks like versus a question that closes it.

Structured exercise: the group takes a real question facing the community — something genuinely open, not a question that has already been answered — and deliberates on it explicitly, with the guide naming in real time when the group shifts from deliberation to advocacy. Debrief: what changed in the quality of thinking when the group was genuinely deliberating?

**Practice:** In one group conversation this week, notice the moment when you stop being curious about the question and start defending a position. What triggered the shift?

### Session 5 — The Steel Man

Before reaching a collective decision, the strongest case against that decision should be heard and genuinely understood — not as a procedural requirement, but because no group should decide against a position it hasn't honestly engaged with.

This is the discipline of steel-manning: the genuine effort to construct and hold the strongest possible case against your own position. Not to defeat it — to understand it fully before deciding. The steel man that meets the standard is the version that the people who hold the opposing view would recognize as a fair representation of their strongest case, stated by someone who actually disagrees with it.

This is genuinely hard. It requires temporarily inhabiting a position you oppose well enough to argue for it honestly. It requires distinguishing between the weakest version of an opposing view — the version easiest to dismiss — and the strongest version, which is the one that honest people actually hold. Most people are much better at the former than the latter. Most public discourse actively rewards the former.

The practical obstacle: steel-manning feels like losing. If you state the opposing case well, you have done their work for them. People with strong positions will resist it. The guide should name this resistance honestly rather than demanding it be overcome through will.

Structured exercise: pairs. Each member takes a position they actually hold. Their partner constructs the steel man — the strongest case against that position. The member who holds the position evaluates whether the steel man is genuine. Debrief: where did the steel man fall short? What was being avoided?

**Practice:** Before the next session, steel man one position you hold strongly. Write it out. Bring it.

### Session 6 — Dissent

Dissent is not complaint. It is not obstruction. It is not sore losing. Dissent — genuine dissent — is the honest record of a minority judgment that the majority may have missed something, offered for the community's consideration now and over time.

Communities that handle dissent well make better decisions. Not because the dissenters are always right — because minority judgments that are heard and engaged with improve collective thinking in ways that unexamined consensus does not. The community that can hear "I think we're making a mistake, and here's why" and engage with it honestly is a community that is less likely to compound its errors.

What genuine dissent requires of the person who offers it: clarity about what specifically they believe was missed or wrongly weighted. Not "this is a bad decision" but "this decision doesn't adequately account for X, and here is why X matters." Not performance of opposition but honest testimony about what the decision costs. And the willingness to offer that testimony even when the community has decided against you — which requires exactly the interior stability that Interior Capacity builds.

What dissent requires of the community that receives it: the capacity to hear it without treating the dissenter as disloyal. Communities that have invested in a decision often experience dissent as an attack on the community itself. This instinct is understandable and almost always wrong. The dissenter who offers honest testimony about what a decision costs is doing the community a service, not causing harm.

Structured exercise: the group makes a practice decision — something real but not high-stakes. One member is designated the dissenter. They must offer a genuine dissent — specific, honest, not performative — even if they privately agree with the decision. Debrief: what did it require to dissent well? What did it require to receive dissent well?

**Practice:** This week, in any group setting, offer one genuine dissent or objection to something the group has decided or is moving toward. Not to obstruct — to ensure the community's thinking is honest. Notice how it lands and what it required of you.

### Session 7 — The Governed Who Must Govern

In genuine self-governance, there is no permanent distinction between the governed and those who govern. The same people occupy both roles at different times. The member who this year participated in a decision is next year responsible for making one.

This rotation — being governed and governing in turns — is the structural feature that makes self-governance different from every other governance arrangement. It is also what makes it demanding. The person who must govern is not someone with specialized training, particular wisdom, or exceptional gifts. They are an ordinary member of the community, asked to exercise judgment that will bind others, with real consequences, under genuine uncertainty.

What this asks: the capacity to hold authority without becoming attached to it. To make decisions when the right answer isn't clear. To be accountable for judgment calls that turned out to be wrong. To govern in a way that would make sense to the people who will live with the results, not just the people who are watching now.

And what it asks of those who are currently governed: the capacity to allow authority to be exercised without constant interference, and to hold it accountable without destroying it. A community that second-guesses every decision its governing members make cannot be governed. A community that never challenges any decision is governed rather than self-governing. The balance is a practice, not a formula.

Group discussion: what would it require of you to govern well? What would you bring? What would be hardest? What would you need from the people not currently governing?

**Practice:** Write one page as if you had just been asked to make a real decision for this community — something that would affect everyone, with no obviously correct answer. What process would you use? Who would you need to hear from? What would you need to know before deciding?

### Session 8 — Raising a Concern

Genuine self-governance requires that members can raise concerns effectively — not just feel them, not just complain about them, but translate them into communication that the community can actually hear and act on.

This is harder than it sounds. Most of the ways people raise concerns are not effective. Expressing a concern as a grievance positions the community as having wronged you and puts them on the defensive. Raising a concern about a person rather than about a situation makes it personal. Raising a concern without a clear account of what is actually wrong leaves the community uncertain about what to address. Raising a concern in a way that requires immediate agreement doesn't open a conversation — it demands a verdict.

A concern that can be heard does three things: it names what is specifically wrong with enough precision that others can understand it, it accounts for what those who created the situation might have been thinking (which demonstrates that the concern isn't just unfamiliarity or disagreement), and it opens a question rather than dictating an answer. "Something needs to be fixed" is not a concern — it is a demand. "I believe X is happening, for reasons I understand, and I think it's worth the community examining whether Y might address it" is a concern the community can work with.

What makes this hard: concerns often arise when something has gone wrong and the person raising them is hurt, angry, or frustrated. Translating those feelings into legible communication requires exactly the interior capacities Interior Capacity builds — emotional literacy, the capacity to act from your own ground rather than from the urgency of the moment.

Structured exercise: members identify a real concern — something about the community's life that they believe deserves attention. They draft it as a communication to the community. The group reads each draft and responds: what in this communication makes it easy or hard to hear? What would they need to understand that is missing?

**Practice:** Revise your concern draft based on the group's response. Bring the revision to the next session.

### Session 9 — Accountability

Communities that cannot hold themselves accountable repeat their mistakes. The question is not whether to hold people and institutions accountable — it is how to do so in ways that are honest, proportionate, and that preserve the community's capacity to function.

Accountability requires first the capacity to distinguish between something that is failing and something with which you merely disagree. These are genuinely different. A governing body that is making decisions you oppose through a legitimate process, with genuine deliberation, is not failing — it is governing, and you are being governed. A governing body that is ignoring concerns, skipping deliberative requirements, or concentrating decisions in a few voices is failing. The distinction is not always obvious, and making it requires exactly the epistemic and deliberative capacities this course is building.

Accountability also requires proportionality. The failure of a single decision is not the same as the failure of an institution. A pattern of failures is different from an error. A structural problem is different from a lapse. Communities that treat every mistake as a crisis, or every lapse as evidence of fundamental failure, cannot govern themselves — no person or body can hold authority under those conditions.

What accountability requires of the community: the capacity to name failure without destroying the institution that failed. This is the hardest thing. Communities that cannot hold themselves accountable tend to handle it in one of two ways: they tolerate failures indefinitely rather than confront them, or they respond to failure by destroying the person or institution responsible. Neither produces a community that can improve. The practice of genuine accountability — honest naming of what went wrong, real consequence proportionate to the failure, genuine repair — is a community capacity built through repeated use, not through crisis management.

Group discussion: what does accountability look like when it's done well? The group draws on examples from their own experience — not necessarily governance, but anywhere in their lives where someone or something was held accountable in a way that was fair and effective. What made it possible?

**Practice:** Identify one place in the community's current life where you believe accountability is needed — a place where something is not working as it should. Write about it: what is specifically wrong, what accountability would look like, and what it would require to raise it well.

## Phase 3: The Hard Cases

**Sessions 10–11 — Goal: Practice the capacities on genuinely difficult governance questions**

### Session 10 — Genuine Value Conflict

Some collective decisions are not problems. They are conflicts between genuine goods — between the liberty of members and the obligations they bear to each other, between the welfare of present members and the claims of those not yet born, between the integrity of a living system and the immediate need of the community that depends on it.

These conflicts do not have correct answers. The goods in tension are genuinely distinct and cannot be derived from each other. Liberty is not a more particular expression of security. The welfare of the living cannot simply outweigh the claims of the unborn, or the other way around. People who assert that they have found the master value from which all genuine goods can be derived have not solved the problem of moral complexity — they have decided to stop seeing it.

What this means for collective decision-making: when a community faces a genuine value conflict, the most important thing it can do is name the conflict honestly — to say aloud what goods are in tension, and what cannot be preserved regardless of what is decided. A decision made without acknowledging what it costs is a decision the community cannot honestly evaluate later. And the people who dissent from that decision are not simply wrong — they may be honoring a real good that the majority has decided to trade against. Their testimony about what was sacrificed is evidence, not losing.

This provision does not make decisions less necessary. Communities must act under genuine moral tension. It makes the community honest about what it is doing — and capable of learning, over time, whether the trades it made were worth their cost.

Structured exercise: the group works through a real governance question that involves genuine value conflict — one where people in the group actually hold different goods at stake. The goal is not consensus but honest characterization: what are the genuine goods in tension? What cannot be preserved regardless of what is decided? If a decision must be made, what does it cost?

### Session 11 — When the Community Gets It Wrong

Communities make mistakes. Every community that governs itself will make decisions that turn out to be wrong — harmful in ways not anticipated, based on information that was incomplete, informed by values that didn't hold up when tested.

The question is not whether this happens. The question is whether the community can acknowledge it when it does. Communities that cannot acknowledge their own mistakes repeat them. The mechanism is simple: acknowledging the mistake requires acknowledging the process that produced it, which implicates the people who participated in that process, which the community finds it easier to avoid. The result is that decisions that went wrong get defended long after the evidence against them is clear.

What it takes to acknowledge collective error: the capacity to say "we were wrong about that" without it meaning "we cannot be trusted." These are different claims. Every community that governs for long enough will be wrong about something. The communities that earn trust are not the ones that never err — they are the ones that can look honestly at their errors and show what they learned.

This is the civic version of what Practical Epistemology builds — the ability to update a belief under evidence, applied to the community's collective judgments rather than to individual ones. It requires the same thing: the capacity to hold a position as provisional, to treat disconfirming evidence as information rather than as attack, and to update genuinely rather than cosmetically.

Group discussion: what would it take for this community to acknowledge that it had gotten something important wrong? What would make that possible? What would make it impossible?

## Phase 4: Integration

**Session 12 — Becoming the Sovereign**

The word sovereignty, applied to a community that genuinely governs itself, is not metaphor. The members are the source of governing authority. Not as an occasional check on leaders they chose — as the people in whom authority actually resides, exercised directly in decisions and delegated temporarily to those they ask to govern on their behalf.

This means every member carries a share of responsibility for how the community is governed. Not as consumers of governance but as its source. The member who disengages from collective decisions has not escaped responsibility — they have decided to let others bear it. The member who raises no concerns has decided to let whatever is drifting continue to drift.

This is not a burden to be distributed evenly regardless of capacity. It is a responsibility calibrated to what each member can actually do — to show up when something matters, to raise a concern when something is wrong, to govern when asked, to hold others accountable when they fail and accept accountability when you do. The community's ability to govern itself well is exactly as strong as the members' willingness to participate honestly in that project.

The session closes with honest reckoning: what does each member now understand about what they are being asked to be? Not what they are asked to believe or support — what they are being asked to be. A person who can deliberate honestly. Who can dissent constructively and receive dissent graciously. Who can hold authority accountable without destroying it. Who can govern when asked without having sought the role. Who can acknowledge when the community was wrong and help it see why.

These are not extraordinary political capacities. They are what self-governance requires of ordinary people. This community is built on the belief that ordinary people can do this. This course is about making that belief true.

## Ongoing Practice

No terminus — woven into participation in community governance.

This course continues as the quality of engagement in the community's actual governance life. Members bring what they've built here to the meetings, decisions, and disagreements that constitute the community's shared life. When they are asked to lead, they govern as this course has prepared them to. When they dissent, they dissent honestly. When the community errs, they help it see why.

The guide may convene the group periodically to reflect on how the community's governance is actually going — not as a course but as a check-in. Moments when governance is under strain are not failures to manage. They are the actual experience of self-governance, and they require exactly what this course builds.

## Running Themes Throughout

**Capacity not performance.** Governance is not theater. The deliberative practices this course builds are not rituals to be performed correctly. They are the actual cognitive and relational work of governing well. The course consistently returns to whether the work is happening rather than whether the form is being maintained.

**Process not outcome.** A member who governs well is not a member who always prevails. It is a member who brings genuine deliberation, honest dissent, and principled judgment to every question — and whose participation is worth something regardless of whether the decision went their way.

**Ordinary not exceptional.** Genuine self-governance is designed for ordinary people, not for those who are unusually wise or unusually prepared. The course builds the capacities of ordinary members, not the skills of specialists.

**Collective not individual.** Self-governance is a community capacity, not an individual one. A single member who can deliberate well cannot do much in a group that cannot. The capacity is built in community because it can only be exercised in community.

---

## What Success Looks Like

A member who completes the structured phase should be able to:

- Enter a collective decision with genuine openness — curious about the question rather than defending a position
- Construct a genuine steel man of a view they oppose, sufficient that those who hold it would recognize it as fair
- Offer a dissent that is specific, honest, and not performative — and receive dissent from others without experiencing it as an attack
- Distinguish between a governing body that is failing and one with which they merely disagree
- Raise a concern in a way that is legible, specific, and directed at a question rather than at people
- Name a genuine value conflict as such, without reaching for a false resolution
- Govern when asked — bring their actual judgment to bear on real questions with real consequences
- Acknowledge when the community was wrong and help it see what went wrong without destroying the community's capacity to function

None of these are reserved for the politically experienced or the intellectually exceptional. They are what self-governance requires of its participants. This course is about becoming one of them.
