# Foundational Numeracy

A tutor-delivered program for adult learners. Delivered one-on-one, one to two sessions per week, approximately 45–60 minutes per session.

## Why This Program Exists

Adult numeracy gaps in the United States are as widespread as adult literacy gaps and far less discussed. Studies consistently find that the majority of American adults struggle to perform basic quantitative tasks — interpreting a chart, calculating a percentage, understanding a medicine dose, reading a utility bill. These are not failures of intelligence. They are the accumulated result of math education that moved too fast, that prioritized procedure over understanding, and that left large portions of the population behind while producing the appearance of coverage.

The consequences are practical and immediate. Adults who cannot work with numbers reliably are vulnerable to financial exploitation, excluded from informed participation in health decisions, and dependent on others for tasks that directly affect their own lives. In a community organized around self-governance and shared practical work, numerical incapacity creates real limits on what members can do and decide.

This program addresses that directly. It meets the learner where they are, builds understanding before procedure, and grounds every concept in the practical contexts that actually matter — growing food, managing resources, caring for health, participating in the Society's economic life. The goal is not mathematical fluency for its own sake. It is the practical numerical competence that genuine participation requires.

## Part One: For the Tutor

### Who This Learner Is

An adult who struggles with numbers has usually found ways around it — estimating, asking others, avoiding situations that require calculation, carrying anxiety about math that has been present since school. They may be entirely capable in practical domains that involve quantity (measuring by eye, managing household resources, negotiating prices) while being unable to perform the same operations symbolically on paper. That gap between practical and symbolic competence is the terrain this program works in.

Adult numeracy learners often believe they are not "math people" — a belief usually installed by early school experiences and reinforced every time a numerical task produced failure. The tutor's job is to work against that belief not by arguing with it but by creating consistent small successes that accumulate into evidence the learner can hold.

### What Makes This Different from Teaching Children

Adults have more prior experience with quantities than children do. That experience is the program's greatest asset. A learner who has been scaling recipes, calculating change, measuring lumber, or managing a household budget has more numerical intuition than they realize — they have just never connected it to symbolic notation.

The tutor's approach throughout is to make that connection explicit: what you already do is math, and what we are learning is the language for it. Every abstract concept in this program has a practical grounding the learner already understands or can quickly develop through physical examples. The concept comes from the real example, not the other way around.

### The Tutoring Relationship

What applies in the Literacy program applies here: this is not school, pace follows the learner, struggling is normal, and progress must be made visible. Shame around numeracy is at least as common as shame around literacy, and the tutor handles it the same way — by keeping the work dignified, practical, and grounded in the learner's existing competence.

One additional consideration: adults who have had bad experiences with math often tense up when calculation is presented as a test. Keep the tone exploratory. *What do you think?* and *how would you figure that out?* are better entry points than *what is the answer?*

### Session Structure

Sessions are 45–60 minutes. The same rhythm as the Literacy program: open with something the learner already knows, work the new material, close in competence. Physical objects — coins, seeds, measuring cups, blocks, a tape measure — are useful throughout and should be available at sessions. Abstract numerical concepts become more accessible when they have physical referents.

Between sessions, brief practical tasks work better than worksheet practice. *Before next session, notice what you're measuring or counting this week.* The goal is to activate the learner's existing numeracy so the session can build on it.

### When Something Isn't Working

The most common cause of persistent difficulty in a numeracy unit is a gap in a prior unit that was not fully consolidated. Place value confusion underlies most addition and subtraction difficulty. Fraction intuition underlies most ratio and percentage difficulty. When a learner is stuck, go back before going forward.

If a learner has significant difficulty despite solid foundational work, consider whether there is a processing difference at play. Dyscalculia is real and less commonly recognized than dyslexia. The response is not to stop — it is to adjust: more physical objects, more time, different representations of the same concept.

## Part Two: The Program

The program has two tracks. The Foundation Track builds number sense and the four operations. The Numeracy Track applies them to practical domains: measurement, money, percentages, ratios, data. Most learners work through the Foundation Track before the Numeracy Track, though learners who already have solid arithmetic skills can enter the Numeracy Track directly.

The baseline assessment in Part Three identifies the entry point.

### Foundation Track

**Pre-Numeracy (as needed, no fixed session count)**

For learners who need it: counting with physical objects, one-to-one correspondence, recognizing and writing numerals 0–9, comparing quantities (more, less, same). Assess quickly — most adult learners have this. If they do, skip to N1.

**Unit N1 — Number Sense and Place Value (6–8 sessions)**

What numbers actually represent. The ones, tens, hundreds structure — not as a rule but as a way of packaging quantities. Reading and writing numbers to 1,000. Comparing magnitudes: which is larger, 304 or 340? Where does 750 sit between 700 and 800?

Worked through physical quantities throughout: weights on a scale, counts of objects, measurements. The learner who understands place value physically before they encounter it symbolically builds a sturdier foundation.

Introduce the number line as a tool here and use it throughout the program.

**Unit N2 — Addition and Subtraction (6–8 sessions)**

Concept before algorithm. What does adding mean — combining quantities. What does subtracting mean — finding the difference, or taking away. When do you use which operation.

Mental strategies before written procedures: rounding to convenient numbers, breaking a number apart, counting up from the smaller number. Written procedures (carrying, borrowing) come after the mental strategies are solid — otherwise learners follow steps without understanding what they're doing and cannot recover when they make an error.

Practical contexts throughout: adjusting a recipe, calculating change, figuring out how much material is left after a cut.

**Unit N3 — Multiplication and Division (8–10 sessions)**

Multiplication as repeated addition — not times tables as rote memorization. Division as equal sharing or equal grouping. The intuition before the procedure.

Example problems drawn from real contexts: how many fence posts for 60 feet at one every 8 feet? How many jars does a batch of 24 servings fill if each jar holds 3? How many people does a 5-pound bag of rice feed at a half-pound per person? The learner who can set up the problem correctly and understands what operation answers it has learned what this unit is for.

Multiplication tables are useful once the concept is solid. Memorizing them before the concept is understood produces fragile knowledge.

**Unit N4 — Fractions (8–10 sessions)**

What a fraction means: a part of a whole, or a part of a group. Start with halves, thirds, and quarters — the fractions that appear in daily life. Equivalence: one half equals two quarters equals four eighths, and understanding why, not just stating it.

Measuring cups and a tape measure belong on the table during this unit. The fractions on those tools are the fractions in this unit. Practical contexts: recipe scaling, dividing a plot of land, splitting resources among members.

Comparing fractions (which is larger, 2/3 or 3/4?) is taught through reasoning rather than procedure — find a common way to look at them, not a rule to apply mechanically.

**Unit N5 — Decimals (6–8 sessions)**

Place value extended past the decimal point. The connection to fractions is explicit and central: 0.5 is one half, 0.25 is one quarter, 0.1 is one tenth. The learner who understands fractions can understand decimals as a different notation for the same idea.

Reading decimals on scales, thermometers, and price tags. Comparing and ordering: which is larger, 0.75 or 0.8? (A common error — the learner who doesn't connect decimals to fractions often reads more digits as meaning more value.)

**Unit N6 — Measurement (6–8 sessions)**

Length, area, volume, weight, temperature. Reading a ruler and tape measure. Standard and metric — enough of each to function in both.

Area as a concept: why multiplying two dimensions gives a quantity of a different kind. Practical: how much soil to fill a raised bed, how much paint for a wall, how to read a medicine dose. The measurement unit is what makes quantity meaningful in context — this unit develops the habit of attending to units, not just numbers.

### Numeracy Track

**Unit N7 — Percentages (6–8 sessions)**

Percent means per hundred. The explicit connection: 50% is one half is 0.5 — all three are the same quantity in different notation. Calculating simple percentages: what is 20% of 80? How much is a 15% tip? What does a 10% yield increase mean?

Reading percentage information in health contexts (test results, risk statistics) and agriculture contexts (germination rates, nutrient concentrations). Percentages are everywhere; learners who can't read them are locked out of a large portion of the information that affects their lives.

**Unit N8 — Ratios and Proportional Reasoning (8–10 sessions)**

Scaling up and down. Mixing ratios: fertilizer, mortar, compost. Recipe scaling for a crowd. Unit rates: cost per pound, yield per acre, dose per kilogram. This is the numeracy of practical skilled work — the unit that most directly enables participation in the Society's productive life.

The central skill is recognizing when two quantities are in a proportional relationship and using that relationship to find an unknown. Taught through problems the learner will actually encounter.

**Unit N9 — Time and Scheduling (4–6 sessions)**

Reading analog and digital clocks. Calendars: days, weeks, months, seasonal cycles. Calculating durations and intervals: how long from 9:15 to 11:40? How many days between planting and harvest? Planning backwards from a deadline: if the event is on the 20th and preparation takes three days, when do we start?

Seasonal and planting schedules are practical contexts for this unit. Time is the measurement domain adults most often manage by feel; this unit makes the feel explicit and calculable.

**Unit N10 — Money and Basic Finance (6–8 sessions)**

Making change. Budgeting: income, fixed expenses, variable expenses, what remains. Calculating unit prices and comparing value: which is a better deal, 12 ounces for $2.40 or 16 ounces for $3.04? Understanding simple interest in plain terms.

Reading a basic bill, statement, or invoice. The kin currency system and how it works numerically. The goal is functional financial literacy — enough to manage the learner's own resources, participate in the Society's economy, and not be exploited by opaque pricing or billing.

**Unit N11 — Data and Estimation (6–8 sessions)**

Reading tables, bar charts, pie charts — what they show and what they hide. What an average means and when it misleads (a community where three people have a lot and seven have a little has a misleading average). Range, typical value, outlier — concepts before vocabulary.

Estimation as a checking tool: before calculating, what should the answer be in the right range? Rounding strategies for mental math. Interpreting health data (what does "normal range" mean on a lab result?), yield records, and community resource figures.

The learner who completes this unit can engage with numerical information in practical contexts — read a chart, question a statistic, check whether an answer makes sense. That is the completion threshold.

**Completion**

A ceremony is not required. One is appropriate. The learner has worked through something that has followed them for a long time and found they could do it.

## Part Three: Assessment and Materials

### Baseline Assessment

Conducted informally in the first session. The tutor asks the learner to walk through a few practical scenarios: making change for a purchase, reading a number off a scale, dividing something in half. The goal is to find where the learner's practical competence is and where the symbolic gap appears.

The tutor also asks directly: what do you most want to be able to do with numbers that you can't do now? That answer shapes which parts of the Numeracy Track matter most to this learner, and in what order.

Entry point: a learner who can reliably add and subtract whole numbers enters at N3 or later. A learner who is comfortable through fractions enters the Numeracy Track. A learner who needs place value enters at N1.

### Session Records

Brief notes after each session: what was covered, what the learner handled well, where they got stuck, what to return to. The tutor's notes maintain continuity across sessions and allow progress to be made visible to the learner over time.

### Materials

The program does not depend on purchased materials. What the tutor needs:

- Physical objects for the Foundation Track: coins, dried beans or seeds, measuring cups and spoons, a ruler and tape measure, a kitchen scale if available
- A notebook the learner keeps and owns — their work, their examples, their own number line
- Real documents: grocery receipts, utility bills, seed packets with planting ratios, medicine labels with dose instructions, Society resource records
- A calculator — introduced in the Numeracy Track for checking work, not as a substitute for understanding

The physical objects matter. A learner who can line up beans into groups of eight before they can multiply eight times a number is learning multiplication; they are not cheating. The objects are the bridge between the practical competence the learner already has and the symbolic notation they are building.
