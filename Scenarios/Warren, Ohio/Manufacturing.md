# Warren, Ohio — Manufacturing

## What Was Here

Warren was not incidentally an industrial city. It was built on two pillars that, at their peak, employed more people than currently live in many American towns.

**Packard Electric** was founded in Warren in 1890 — originally making light bulbs, then pivoting to automotive wiring as the car industry emerged. General Motors acquired it in 1932. By the 1980s, GM's Packard Electric division was the dominant producer of wire harnesses and electrical components in the industry, with a sprawling campus along North River Road and Larchmont Avenue. At peak, it employed **13,000 hourly workers** in Warren alone. In 1995 it became Delphi Automotive Systems; Delphi filed Chapter 11 in 2005, emerged from bankruptcy in 2009, and has been shedding workers ever since. Current membership in IUE-CWA Local 717: approximately **660 employees** spread across four plants. The former 800,000-square-foot main manufacturing building is now used as a fireworks warehouse by B.J. Alan Co.

**Trumbull Steel** opened on the west side of Warren in 1913, employing 7,000–9,000 workers at its height. It became part of Republic Steel in 1928 and continued under various owners until its final closure in **2012**. The mill was demolished in 2014, the blast furnace in 2017. In 1982, as the closures were beginning, unionists marched with the chant: *Warren is a union town. We won't let you tear it down.* They lost.

Trumbull County lost **62.5% of its manufacturing jobs between 1990 and 2016**. That is not a decline. That is a demolition.

---

## What Remains

Manufacturing is not gone. It is present at roughly one-sixth its former scale, and what remains is different in character.

**By the numbers:** 1,846 people in Warren work in manufacturing (13.8% of employment). Trumbull County has 198 manufacturers employing approximately 10,700 workers as of 2019 data. The Youngstown-Boardman-Warren metro area employs 26,600 manufacturing workers total. There are 558 open manufacturing jobs posted within a 20-mile radius of Warren.

**What those jobs look like:**

- **Delphi (now Aptiv)** — still making wire harnesses and electrical components in Warren, reduced to ~660 union workers from 13,000. The craft knowledge is still there; the scale is not.
- **Thomas Steel Strip** — steel processing, one of the remaining metal operations in the city.
- **Uniserv LLC** (Brookfield, near Warren) — slits steel and metal coil to specification for manufacturers of guardrails, tubing, solar panel framing, seat belt clips. 105 employees. Processes about 20 tons of steel per hour across four slitting lines.
- **Soft Touch Furniture** (Girard) — 32 workers, hand-crafted commercial furniture (Panera Bread, Firebirds). Two CNC wood-cutting machines, one fabric cutter. Small but skilled.
- **Transglobal** (Champion Township) — makes drop-down doors for Rivian and other EV manufacturers. $5 million new plant, 90 jobs projected.
- **West Warren Industrial Park** — new multimillion-dollar development for mid-sized manufacturing, warehouse, and R&D. First building complete, 50 acres eyed for expansion.

The regional picture is more active. Just southwest of Warren:
- **Ultium Cells** (Lordstown) — LG/GM joint venture battery cell plant, the region's largest single employer with 2,000+ workers, launched August 2022.
- **Foxconn** (Lordstown) — former GM plant (6.2 million square feet) being converted to EV manufacturing.

## What's Coming

The most significant incoming investment is **Kimberly-Clark**: an $800 million manufacturing facility on a former steel mill site near Pine Avenue in the Howland/Warren township area. 491 full-time jobs. $49.1 million in projected annual payroll. Under active construction as of May 2025 — 350 tradespeople on site including operators, plumbers, pipefitters, laborers, iron workers, roofers, electricians, sheet metal workers, bricklayers, painters, carpenters, and cement finishers. A $160 million distribution center addition is also proposed, adding 65 more jobs. Kimberly-Clark makes tissue paper and personal care products — not a craft industry, but real employment at real wages on real former industrial land.

**Graphite One** has signed a 50-year lease on the former Warren Depot site for a graphite mining and manufacturing operation targeting EV batteries. Projected 160 jobs.

## The Skills Picture

The collapse of the mills did not erase the people who worked in them or their children. The trades knowledge is still in Warren: welding, machining, electrical work, fabrication, pipefitting, mechanical repair, steel processing. It is in the hands of people in their 40s, 50s, and 60s — the generation that either caught the tail end of the industrial economy or was trained by people who did. USW Local 1375 and IUE-CWA Local 717 still maintain membership. The Mahoning-Trumbull AFL-CIO Labor Council still exists.

This is the competence tradition the telos names: knowledge earned through practice, transmitted across generations, belonging to the community. In Warren it is genuinely present. What has collapsed is not the knowledge but the institutional structure — the employer, the union hall, the economic base — that once organized and sustained it.

## What This Means for the Society

Warren is not a post-manufacturing city in the sense of having forgotten how to make things. It is a post-industrial city in the sense that the institutions built around making things have collapsed, leaving the skills dispersed and underemployed.

A Society in Warren does not need to import manufacturing knowledge. It is already there, in members and potential members who know how to work metal, wire electrical systems, fabricate and repair, and operate machinery. The Machining and Metallurgy master plan is not aspirational in this context — it is a recovery of capacity that already exists in the community, needing only a structure to organize and transmit it.

The incoming EV economy (Ultium, Foxconn, Transglobal, Kimberly-Clark) will provide employment, but it is employment structured by corporations, not community. The jobs are real; the dependency is also real. Every worker at the Kimberly-Clark plant is one corporate decision away from the same situation as the Delphi workers. The Society's interest is not in competing with those employers but in building the complementary capacity — the skills, tools, and relationships — that remain when any particular employer leaves.

The former steel mill site that Kimberly-Clark is now building on was vacant for over a decade. There will be other sites. The question is who controls what gets built on them, and toward what end.
