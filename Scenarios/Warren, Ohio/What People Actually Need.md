# Warren, Ohio — What People Actually Need

The go-to answer has always been jobs. It is the wrong answer — not because jobs don't matter, but because it treats people as labor and consumers whose flourishing is downstream of economic conditions rather than as ends in themselves. Warren has already run the jobs experiment. Packard Electric had 13,000 of them. Republic Steel had 7,000–9,000. The jobs left. The lesson Warren has already lived is that jobs held by corporate employers are not community assets — they are corporate assets that happened to be located in your city until they weren't. Kimberly-Clark will bring 491. Every person in Warren old enough to remember 1982 knows exactly what that promise is worth over a long enough timeline.

The jobs answer is also, at its core, a postponement. It says: wait for the right economic conditions, and then flourishing will follow. Warren has been waiting since 1977.

---

## What the Actual People Need

Looking at the specific people documented in this scenario:

**The working-class man in his 50s with a machinist's knowledge and a house worth $90,000** doesn't primarily lack a job. He lacks a community that recognizes his competence as real and worth transmitting. He lacks institutions that treat him as a person with knowledge rather than a labor unit whose value depreciates. He lacks honest accounting of what happened to his city — who made the decisions, who benefited, who paid. He has been given partisan rage as a substitute for that accounting, and it hasn't satisfied him because it wasn't designed to.

**The Black single mother raising children on Medicaid and a home health aide's wages** doesn't primarily lack a job — she likely has one, possibly two. She lacks time. She lacks people who will show up consistently. She lacks food security that doesn't require navigating assistance bureaucracies. She lacks housing that is sound and warm. She lacks the experience of her children being genuinely known and valued by a community that intends to be around. She has mutual aid traditions doing real work right now, invisibly, without recognition or institutional support.

**The 45.9% of children chronically absent from school** are not absent because they lack ambition. They are absent because their households are in crisis, because school hasn't been demonstrated to lead somewhere real, because no adult has made their presence feel consequential. What they need is to be known. To have consistent adults in their lives. To experience learning as something that connects to their actual situation rather than a credential acquisition program for an economy that doesn't exist where they live.

**The veteran, isolated, competent, on VA healthcare** doesn't lack a job description. He lacks community. He lacks people who are genuinely accountable to him.

**The elderly woman who remembers what Warren was** doesn't lack economic activity. She lacks people who understand that her memory is irreplaceable and will be lost when she dies.

---

## What They Actually Need

**To be known.** Genuinely, by people who are accountable to them and will still be there next year. The atomization — 48.4% non-family households, 40.9% female-headed — is not a statistic. It is the felt experience of going through difficulty without anyone who is actually obligated to you.

**To have their competence recognized and transmitted.** The machining knowledge, the mutual aid knowledge, the knowledge of how to get through a hard winter, the memory of what this city was — all of it is real, all of it is at risk of dying with the people who carry it, and none of it has an institution currently treating it as worth preserving.

**Real agency over their immediate world.** Not political representation — Warren votes and it doesn't change much. Actual agency: over their food, their housing, their children's formation, their community's governance. The capacity to make real decisions that have real consequences and to live with those consequences together.

**Physical security that doesn't depend on corporate or government permission.** Food that isn't contingent on SNAP not being cut. Housing that isn't contingent on a landlord's decision. Healthcare that doesn't require a Medicaid bureaucracy. These things can be built. They are harder to take away than a corporate employer.

**Honest reckoning with what happened.** Not grievance — reckoning. The difference is that grievance has an enemy and demands retribution; reckoning has a history and demands acknowledgment. What happened to Warren happened because of specific decisions by specific people for specific reasons, and no one has ever given Warren's residents a clear, honest account of that history and what it means. Partisan politics has offered two versions of the story, both designed to redirect anger toward electoral ends rather than genuine understanding. The capacity to name what happened, to grieve it honestly, and to build forward from it — that is something people need and something the Society can help create.

**Something that cannot be taken away.** This is the deepest need. Every institution Warren trusted — the steel companies, the union, the Democratic Party, the federal employment programs — has either left, failed, or been captured. What people need is something whose continuity is not contingent on a corporate decision or a federal budget cycle. A community that owns its space, produces some of its own food, governs itself, and transmits its own knowledge is not immune to pressure — but it is much harder to simply close down than a factory.

---

## The Guiding Principle

The people of Warren are ends, not means. They are not labor to be deployed or consumers to be served or a demographic to be mobilized. They are persons whose flourishing is the point — not a byproduct of economic conditions, not a downstream consequence of the right policy environment, not something that will arrive when the right employer decides to locate here.

Flourishing is built directly, in the texture of daily community life. It is built by people who know each other, work alongside each other, govern themselves honestly, and transmit what they know to the people coming after them. That can begin before Kimberly-Clark opens its doors. It can continue if Kimberly-Clark leaves.

That is what the Society is for. Warren is a place where that argument is testable in the most demanding possible conditions.
