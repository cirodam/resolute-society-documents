# Culture — Phase 2: Performance, Celebration, and Memory

## What Changes

Phase 1 built the habit of being together. Phase 2 expands what the community does with that togetherness. The relationships are established enough that more ambitious cultural work becomes possible — work that requires trust, preparation, and the willingness to be seen by people you know.

---

## Plays and Performance

A play performed by members for members is a different thing from a film watched together. It requires members to learn something, rehearse it, and stand in front of the community and do it — which is an act of vulnerability that only a community with real trust can ask for and receive well.

The Society's plays don't need a stage or a budget. They need a script, members willing to perform, and a room full of members willing to watch. What they produce — beyond the performance itself — is the experience of one part of the community making something for the whole community to receive. That is a different kind of social bond than eating together or watching the same film.

The material can be anything. Existing plays. Something adapted from a film the community has watched together. Something written by a member about Warren — which is the direction the culture lane moves toward in Phase 3. The first play doesn't have to be original. It has to happen.

Music belongs here as well. A member who plays an instrument performing for other members at a community meal. A group of members who sing together. The tradition of the Black church includes music as a communal act rather than a performance for an audience — the community participates rather than watches. Bringing that practice into Society gatherings, if members want to, honors something that has always been central to the community's life.

## Seasonal Celebrations

Regular occasions matter. Seasonal ones anchor the year. A Society that marks the harvest, the new year, the first warm weekend of spring with a gathering gives its members a recurring experience of being part of something larger than their individual lives — the thing that religious communities have always done well and that secular communities often fail to replace.

In Warren, the agricultural and seasonal calendar is real — the Society has members doing actual agricultural work, and the harvest is a genuine occasion worth marking. A fall gathering that celebrates the season's gleaning, the jars on the shelf, the gardens that produced — that is both a cultural event and an acknowledgment of real work the community has done together.

These celebrations are not performances or programs. They are the community being together with a specific occasion as the reason. The meal is part of it. Music if members bring it. Recognition of work done and people present.

## Oral History

Warren has elders who remember the city at its peak — the Packard Electric cafeteria at lunch hour, what the union hall was like before the vote, what their block looked like in 1965, how they felt when the mill announced its closure. This memory is not recorded anywhere. It lives in the people who lived it, and it will die with them.

The Society creates occasions for that memory to be spoken and heard. Not a formal documentary project, not an institutional archive, though those things may come in Phase 3. In Phase 2, it is simpler: a community meal where an elder is invited to speak about what she remembers, and the members listen. A conversation recorded on a phone and kept. A member whose role is to sit with elders and ask them questions and write down what they say.

The oral history project serves the culture lane's goal directly: it builds social capital between generations, it gives younger members a relationship to the city's history they didn't have before, and it gives elders the experience of being received as carriers of knowledge worth preserving. In a community where the old have often been left alone and the young have often been left without roots, that exchange is significant.

## A Community Library

Books, films, music — shared resources that members can borrow. Not a branch of the public library system, which already exists and serves its function. A curated collection of things that matter to this community: books about labor history, deindustrialization, mutual aid, Black history in the Midwest; films that name experiences Warren's members recognize; music that belongs to the traditions the community carries.

The library is a small physical space within the Society's rooms — a few shelves, an organized collection, a simple checkout system through the ledger. A member who builds and curates it earns credits. A member who donates a book or a film that belongs in it is contributing to something that will outlast the individual contribution.

The library connects to the book club and to the education lane's formation work in Phase 3. The books on the shelf are the books the book club might read, the films are the films the movie night might screen. The collection reflects what the Society thinks is worth knowing and worth feeling.

## Events Across Both Neighborhoods

The two-store model serves the Society's founding well — rooting each store in its own neighborhood, building trust within communities before asking trust across them. But the culture lane is where the communities begin to come together.

A community meal that draws members from both stores. A play performed at one store, then the other. A seasonal celebration held in neutral space. The culture lane doesn't force integration — it creates occasions where it can happen naturally, at a pace that reflects actual relationship rather than programmatic intention.

The member from the Southeast Side who finds herself laughing at the same moment as the member from the North End, at the same film, in the same room — that moment is not a diversity initiative. It is just two people who are now, in a small way, less strangers than they were.
