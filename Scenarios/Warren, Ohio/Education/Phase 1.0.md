# Education — Phase 1: Tutor Program

## What It Is

The tutor program is the Society's first educational offering and the one that requires the least of everything — no space beyond a table to sit at, no curriculum, no schedule, no capital. One member knows something. Another member wants to learn it. The ledger pays the tutor in credits. That is the whole structure.

This is where the education lane starts because it can start the moment the Society has two members, and because it is the lowest-shame path into learning that exists. Asking one person for specific help with a specific thing is a different act than enrolling in a class. You are not announcing a general deficit. You are asking someone you know for something you need. In a community where a significant portion of members carry wounds from the education system's failures, that distinction is not small.

---

## What Gets Tutored

Anything a member needs that another member can provide.

A member who needs help reading through a lease agreement sits with a member who can read it well. A member who wants to understand why her power bill jumped in January sits with a member who can walk through it line by line. A member who wants to learn how to change his oil before the formal vehicle maintenance course runs sits with the shade-tree mechanic on his street. A member who has never cooked dry beans sits with the woman who has been doing it her whole life.

The subject doesn't have to be on a list. If a member has a need and another member has the knowledge, the tutor program is the mechanism that compensates the exchange and makes it a community act rather than a personal favor.

---

## What the Tutor Program Discovers

The tutor program is how the Society learns what it has before it has to design anything.

Which members will teach? Who do people trust enough to ask? What do members actually need help with — not what the Society assumes they need, but what they're willing to ask for? Where are the real knowledge gaps and where are the real depths? The organic pattern of tutor matches, visible through the ledger, tells the Society what its educational work should prioritize.

The member who tutors well — who people seek out, who explains things clearly, who creates conditions where the person being helped doesn't feel diminished — is the candidate to teach a Phase 2 class. The tutor program surfaces that capacity before any formal course exists to surface it.

---

## The Relationship to Phase 1.1 and 1.2

Many of the tutoring relationships that develop in Phase 1 are about reading and math — the things most immediately useful, the things most people won't ask for in a group. The tutor program creates the relationship in which a member becomes willing to acknowledge a literacy or numeracy gap, because the person they're acknowledging it to is someone they already trust.

Phase 1.1 and Phase 1.2 — Foundational Literacy and Foundational Numeracy — formalize what the tutor program has already started. The member who has been working one-on-one with a tutor on reading is better positioned to join a small literacy group than a member walking in cold. The tutor program is the on-ramp to the structured curriculum.

---

## What This Requires

A member who knows something. A member who wants to learn it. Somewhere to sit — the store table, a kitchen, a porch. The ledger to compensate the tutor's time.

Nothing else.
