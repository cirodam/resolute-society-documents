# Education — Phase 2: Classroom Space

## What Changes

The tutor program and the foundational courses in Phase 1 can run in whatever space the Society has — the store table, a back room, a member's living room. Phase 2 is when the Society has a dedicated space for organized instruction: a room or rooms set up for it, with the equipment and layout that structured teaching requires.

This is not a large capital step. A classroom in Warren does not mean a building designed for education. It means a room the Society controls, with enough chairs, a surface to write on, and the ability to arrange the space for a group. In a city with cheap real estate and a store that already exists, this is achievable early. What matters is that the space is dedicated — not the store floor that has to be rearranged every time, but a room that is set up for learning and used for it consistently.

---

## What the Space Enables

A dedicated classroom changes what the education lane can do.

Courses that require demonstration — home repair, vehicle maintenance, cooking, food preservation — need space appropriate to the work. Teaching someone to seal a pipe joint is different from describing it. A classroom adjacent to a workspace, or a community kitchen, or a driveway where a car can be pulled in — these configurations become possible once the Society has physical infrastructure that can be arranged for them.

Courses with larger groups become manageable. The tutoring relationships and small literacy groups of Phase 1 work at four to six people. A practical skills course can run ten or fifteen members productively if the space allows it. The classroom expands the Society's instructional capacity without requiring more teachers — it lets each teacher reach more people per session.

Consistent scheduling becomes meaningful. When a room exists and is known to members as the place where courses happen, the course calendar has an anchor. Members can commit to attending something that meets in a specific place at a specific time. That predictability is what makes a curriculum rather than a series of one-off events.

---

## What Runs Here

The sub-phases document the specific courses that the classroom enables:

**Phase 2.1 — Practical Skills.** The hands-on curriculum: home repair, vehicle maintenance, food preparation, food preservation, gardening. These require demonstration and practice, benefit from dedicated space, and are where the trades knowledge the Society's membership holds gets formally transmitted.

**Phase 2.2 — Systems Navigation.** The applied knowledge curriculum: benefits enrollment, tenant rights, healthcare navigation, the credit economy. Less physically hands-on than practical skills, but requiring structured instruction and real documents to work through.

---

## Knowledge Transmission

The classroom is also where the Society organizes what its elders know. The tutor program surfaced informal knowledge exchange. The classroom makes that exchange deliberate and recorded.

The machinist who teaches basic metalworking, the retired nurse who teaches medication literacy, the former union steward who teaches how to run a meeting, the elder farmer who teaches what grows in Trumbull County soil — these are courses. They run in the classroom. They are paid through the ledger. The Society maintains a record of what was taught and what was learned.

Some knowledge is best transmitted through doing rather than through a classroom session — the apprenticeship relationship that pairs a younger member with a skilled elder belongs to Phase 1's tutor structure as much as Phase 2's classroom. But the classroom is where that knowledge gets formalized: the course that follows the apprenticeship, the documentation of what the apprentice learned, the curriculum that can be taught to the next person after the elder is gone.

---

## Children

The dedicated space is where children in the community have the most consistent presence. A room that is regularly used for learning, staffed by adults who take learning seriously, is a place children can come to — for homework help, to sit in on a course, to be part of what's happening.

The Society does not build a program for children. It builds a community that includes children in its life, and the classroom is where that inclusion is most visible and most practiced.
