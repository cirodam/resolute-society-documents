# Education — Phase 3: Formation

## What Changes

Phases 1 and 2 built the practical foundation: skills that work, literacy and numeracy that function, knowledge transmission that preserves what the community holds. Phase 3 is where the Society's educational work becomes what it was always intended to be — not a workforce development program, not a GED prep course, not a skills training operation, but a genuine formation project.

Formation is not a supplement to the practical work. It is what gives the practical work meaning and direction. A member who has learned home repair, taken the literacy course, and participated in the Society's governance for several years has the foundation from which formation work becomes possible — and from which it becomes something that person can transmit to the next person who arrives.

---

## The Formation Curriculum

The Society's full curriculum has been available in some form since founding. What changes in Phase 3 is that it runs consistently, that members engage with it systematically rather than occasionally, and that it becomes a recognized part of what membership in the Society means.

**Practical Epistemology.** How do we know what we know? What is the difference between opinion, evidence, and genuine knowledge? How do we evaluate sources, assess arguments, and recognize when we're being misled? In a media environment designed to exploit cognitive vulnerabilities, these are not abstract philosophical questions. They are survival skills. This course is taught not as academic philosophy but as applied reasoning practice — using real texts, real claims, real decisions members face.

**Practical Reasoning.** The deliberative skills that make governance work: how to think through a complex decision, how to weigh competing values, how to disagree without destroying a relationship, how to change your mind in public without losing face. This is the curriculum that makes the General Assembly something more than a meeting where the loudest person wins.

**Interior Capacity.** What the telos calls the inward dimension: the capacity to be honest with yourself about your own motivations, to recognize your own moral failures without being destroyed by them, to sustain commitment through difficulty. Not therapy. Not self-help. The kind of honest self-examination that serious communities have always required of their members.

These courses don't graduate you. They are entered and re-entered at different stages of life, with different things at stake. A member who takes Practical Epistemology at 25, during the first year of the Society, is a different student than the same member at 45, after twenty years of practice. The curriculum is not depleted by use.

## Intergenerational Learning

By Phase 3, the Society has members who have been part of it for a decade or more. They have history with each other. They carry the Society's institutional memory. They have made mistakes and learned from them — and some of what they learned is not written anywhere.

The transmission of that knowledge — not just skills, but judgment, the kind that comes only from sustained practice — is an educational task the Society has to actively create conditions for. Older members who have developed genuine wisdom about how the Society works, how community operates, and what the telos demands in practice are a resource the younger members need. The mentorship relationships that pair experienced members with newer ones, that pair the person who has been through something difficult with the person who is about to face it, are the most important educational relationships the Society can sustain.

The Society's practice is not to formalize this into a program. It is to recognize it as part of what the community is for — and to pay people, in credits, for the time they spend in genuine mentorship.

---

## Young People Becoming Members

The children who have grown up around the Society — the teenager who ran deliveries, who sat in on cooking courses, who helped with the garden — are now adults. Some of them want to become members. They arrive with a formation that most newcomers don't have: they have watched how the community works, they know what the telos is not just as words but as practice, they have seen the mistakes and seen them reckoned with.

These are the Society's second generation. How they are received, how seriously their formation is taken, and whether the Society is genuinely better for their membership than it would have been — that is the test of whether the formation work produced anything real.

---

## Connections to External Education

Kent State University's Trumbull Campus is in Warren. It is a two-year campus that has historically served the working-class population of the region. ASPIRE and Trumbull Career & Technical Center provide adult education. These institutions are not the Society, and the Society is not a substitute for them.

What Phase 3 may open is a genuine conversation about how the Society's formation work and the credentials of the formal education system can coexist and reinforce each other rather than being entirely separate tracks. A Society member who has completed the Foundational Literacy curriculum, engaged with the formation courses, and demonstrated the kind of sustained reasoning and civic commitment the Society develops has built something real — even if the credential economy doesn't recognize it.

The Society does not credential. It does not issue transcripts or diplomas. But it can advocate for members, help them access formal education opportunities that build on what the Society has developed, and maintain relationships with educational institutions that take the Society's formation work seriously.

---

## What Education Has Built

By the end of Phase 3, the Society's educational work has produced something that didn't exist in Warren before:

A community in which adults learn continuously, across the full span of their lives, in relationship with other people who take knowledge seriously. A community in which the practical skills that keep households functional are taught and transmitted. In which the literacy gaps the school system created are being addressed without shame. In which the knowledge Warren's elders carry — about the mills, the union, the food, the land — is preserved rather than buried. In which young people grow up surrounded by adults who are genuinely curious, genuinely accountable, and genuinely committed to each other's formation.

That is not a school. It is something more durable and more rare: a community that treats education as a practice it engages in together, not something that happens to children in a building and is then finished.
