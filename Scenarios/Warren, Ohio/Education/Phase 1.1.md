# Education — Phase 1.1: Foundational Literacy

## What It Is

Foundational literacy is the capacity to read and understand the texts that govern everyday life. Not academic reading. Not literary analysis. The texts Warren's members actually encounter: utility bills, lease agreements, medication instructions, food labels, SNAP renewal forms, a letter from a landlord, a school notice about a child, a prescription from a doctor.

This is where the Society's literacy work begins — not with a reading level on a standardized test but with the specific documents that determine whether a member can navigate their own life without needing to ask someone else to read for them.

---

## Why It Comes Here

Literacy is not Phase 1 because it requires something the tutor program does not: trust that is organized rather than individual. A member can ask one person they know for reading help without announcing anything publicly. Joining a literacy group — even a small one — is a different act. It means being in a room with several other people and acknowledging, implicitly, that reading is hard.

That acknowledgment becomes possible once the Society has established itself as a place where members are genuinely known to each other, where the tutor relationships have built enough trust that people are not afraid to be seen struggling with something. The tutoring relationships of Phase 1 are often the on-ramp. The member who has been working one-on-one with a tutor is better positioned to join a literacy group than a member walking in cold.

Phase 1.1 runs as soon as the trust is there and a member can teach it — which may be early in the Society's life for some members, and later for others.

---

## How It Runs

Small groups — four to six people at most. Adult literacy work requires trust, and trust requires proximity. A group small enough that everyone in it is known to everyone else is a group small enough that vulnerability is manageable.

The tone is not remedial. The framing is not deficit. The framing is: there are things you need to be able to do, here is how we learn them together. The teacher is a member who knows how to teach — someone who emerged from the tutor program as someone others trust and learn well from. The ledger pays them in credits for their time.

Sessions work through real texts: the HEAP application form, the PIPP Plus enrollment paperwork, a typical residential lease, a medication information sheet, the Society's own governance documents. Not invented exercises. The actual things. The member who leaves a session able to read their utility bill has acquired something that changes their practical life immediately.

---

## What Literacy Unlocks

The most direct payoff is practical: members who can read their own documents don't have to rely on others to read for them, don't miss deadlines because they didn't understand a notice, don't sign agreements they can't evaluate. In a city where navigating government programs, landlord-tenant relationships, and healthcare is already difficult, the inability to read fluently compounds every other challenge.

The less obvious payoff is participatory: the Society's own governance requires reading — the agenda, the proposed resolutions, the governance documents. A member who struggles to read the agenda is disadvantaged in the assembly not through any formal exclusion but through a practical one. Foundational literacy is part of what makes democratic participation real rather than nominal.

Phase 3 — the formation curriculum — requires sustained engagement with texts. It builds on what Phase 1.1 builds.
