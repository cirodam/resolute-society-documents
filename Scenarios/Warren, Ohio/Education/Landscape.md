# Warren, Ohio — Education Landscape

## The School District

Warren City School District serves 4,717 students across 6 schools, grades PK–12. It is the public school system for the city proper.

**Performance (2024–2025 Ohio Report Card):**
- Overall rating: **2.5 out of 5 stars**
- Math proficiency: **29%** — state average is 55%
- Reading proficiency: **39%** — state average is 60%
- High school math proficiency: **14%**
- High school reading proficiency: **39%**
- Graduation rate: approximately **83–89%**, improving from a lower baseline
- Chronic absenteeism: **45.9%** — the highest rate in Trumbull County

**Finance:**
- Total revenue: $102.5 million ($21,886 per student)
- Federal sources: **27%**
- State sources: **56%**
- Local sources: **18%**

The district spends more per student than many wealthier districts, not because Warren is prosperous but because federal Title I funding and state equity formulas compensate for a local tax base that has almost entirely collapsed. 82% of the district's revenue comes from outside the city. The schools are not an expression of the community's wealth or priorities — they are a federal and state program operating in a place whose economy can no longer fund its own children's education.

---

## Chronic Absenteeism

The 45.9% chronic absenteeism rate requires attention. Ohio defines chronic absenteeism as missing 10% or more of the school year — 18 or more days. Nearly half the children in Warren's schools meet that threshold.

This is not primarily a discipline or motivation problem. Chronic absenteeism at this scale is a structural condition: children whose parents are working multiple jobs and cannot arrange consistent transportation; children dealing with housing instability; children with untreated health problems; children in households directly affected by the opioid crisis; children who have absorbed, accurately, that school attendance has not reliably led anywhere for the adults around them.

The district is aware of the problem and has launched attendance campaigns. The fact that a campaign is necessary — and that the rate is nearly half the student body — reflects conditions the school cannot fix from inside the school.

## Adult Educational Attainment

- **High school diploma or higher: 85.2%**
- **Bachelor's degree or higher: 15.7%** — the national average is 35.7%

Warren's adult population is overwhelmingly non-credentialed by the metrics the contemporary economy uses. The people who have bachelor's degrees have largely left — to Columbus, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, or beyond. What remains is a community whose educational credentials are thin by national comparison but whose practical knowledge is not.

The diploma figure (85.2%) requires context. Many of the adults holding those diplomas graduated from the same Warren City School District now producing 29% math proficiency and 45.9% absenteeism. The credential exists; the functional preparation it represents is variable.

## Adult Literacy

Specific literacy data for Trumbull County is not cleanly available, but the picture can be reasonably inferred. National data consistently shows that roughly 1 in 5 American adults reads below a 5th-grade level. In counties with concentrated poverty and historically weak school performance, that rate is higher. The practical picture: a meaningful share of Warren's adult population has significant difficulty with extended reading, complex documents, and mathematical reasoning beyond basic arithmetic. This is not a moral failing — it is the predictable output of decades of underfunded schooling compounded by economic conditions that made the payoff of educational investment invisible.

**Adult education infrastructure that exists:**
- **ASPIRE program** — Ohio's adult education system, operating in Trumbull County through OhioMeansJobs. Basic literacy, GED preparation, numeracy, and workplace readiness. Free to participants.
- **Trumbull Career & Technical Center** — adult education and workforce training
- **OhioMeansJobs Trumbull County** — workforce training, education navigation, GED testing

These programs exist and serve real people. They are designed primarily around credential acquisition and workforce entry rather than the kind of full formation the Society is concerned with.

## What This Means for the Society

The educational picture in Warren is not a background condition — it is the foreground.

A Society with members drawn from Warren's population will include people with significant gaps in formal literacy and numeracy. The Foundational Literacy and Foundational Numeracy curricula are not remedial supplements for edge cases. They are central to what the Society's educational work means in this specific place.

The 45.9% absenteeism rate tells you something beyond school performance: there are nearly 2,200 children spending substantial time outside school, in households under severe stress, without structured learning. A Society with physical space, consistent adult presence, and genuine educational capacity is positioned to reach some of those children — not as a school replacement, but as the kind of place where learning happens because the community treats it as important.

The adult literacy infrastructure is narrowly focused on workforce outcomes. The Society's curriculum is focused on something the workforce economy does not measure: the capacity to think clearly, read the world honestly, govern oneself, and participate fully in a deliberating community. That is a different project, and there is no existing institution in Warren doing it.

The school district is doing what it can with what it has in the conditions it operates in. The Society is not a school district alternative. It is the structure in which the formation the school system was supposed to provide — and largely hasn't — can continue across the full span of a person's life.
