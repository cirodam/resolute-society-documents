# Education — Phase 1.2: Foundational Numeracy

## What It Is

Foundational numeracy is the math of daily life. Not algebra, not abstract arithmetic. The calculations that govern whether a household survives to the end of the month: budgeting, understanding interest, measuring for repair work, scaling a recipe, reading a utility bill, knowing whether a bulk purchase is actually cheaper per unit than the retail price.

In Warren, numeracy gaps destroy household finances in specific, predictable ways. Payday lending is common. The math behind a $15 fee on a $100 loan, rolled over four times, is not complicated — but it is opaque to someone who hasn't done it, and the people who extend those loans have no interest in making it clear. A member who understands that calculation is a member who avoids a product that extracts hundreds of dollars a year from households that can't afford it.

---

## How It Runs

Same structure as Phase 1.1: small groups, real materials, a teacher drawn from the membership who is compensated through the ledger.

The materials are the actual calculations Warren's members face. A utility bill from First Energy — what each line means, what drove the total up from last month, what a normal winter baseline looks like. A budget worksheet built around Warren's median income and typical household expenses. A payday loan disclosure form worked through in detail. A bulk price comparison between the Society's store and Dollar General for the same items. These are not constructed examples. They are the math that is already in members' lives.

Numeracy runs alongside literacy — the skills interact. Reading a utility bill requires both. So does understanding a lease with a late fee clause. The two courses reinforce each other, and members who take both acquire a combined practical capacity that neither course produces alone.

---

## Where Numeracy Shows Up

**The store.** The member who can calculate cost-per-unit makes better purchasing decisions. The member who understands what demurrage means for her credit balance manages her credits differently. The numeracy course and the store are in direct dialogue.

**The housing lane.** Measuring for repairs. Calculating material quantities. Understanding a heating bill and knowing what drove it. The housing triage process surfaces numbers — a furnace efficiency rating, a square footage for insulation — that a numerically fluent member can evaluate.

**The credit economy.** The Society's credit system has rules — the demurrage rate, the individual balance ceiling, how capital calls work. These are not complicated, but they require arithmetic to grasp. A member who understands the math of the credit economy participates in it differently than one who takes it on faith.

**Governance.** The General Assembly votes on budgets, capital calls, and treasury allocations. A member who can follow the numbers in a proposed capital call resolution is a more capable participant in that vote than one who cannot.

---

## The Shame Question

Numeracy gaps carry the same shame as literacy gaps, for the same reason: the school system failed to produce these capacities and then made people feel the failure was their own. The same care that Phase 1.1 takes around shame applies here.

The framing is not "you don't know math." The framing is: the math you need for your actual life is specific and learnable, and here it is. Nobody needs to perform the part of their life where this gap has cost them. They need to leave the room able to do the calculation.
