# Housing — Phase 2: Systematic Weatherization and Tenant Support

## What Changes

Phase 1 housing work was responsive — a member surfaces a problem, the Society addresses it. Phase 2 becomes more systematic. The Society has enough members with enough housing knowledge to run programs rather than just respond to crises. The triage database from Phase 1 tells the Society what it's dealing with across the membership. The repair relationships are established. The energy assistance enrollments are underway. Now the work scales.

---

## Weatherization at Scale

The Phase 1 weatherization work was household by household, responsive to what triage revealed. Phase 2 runs a systematic weatherization program — targeting a cohort of member households each season, working through the priority list that accumulated in Phase 1, and bringing the Society's combined repair knowledge to bear on the most vulnerable housing situations.

A weatherization run in Warren in early fall might mean eight households in a season: installing attic insulation in three houses, replacing storm windows in two, sealing basement rim joists in four, adding door sweeps across all of them. Members with carpentry and insulation skills earn credits. Materials — blown insulation, weatherstripping, rigid foam — are purchased through capital calls or the dollar treasury. The federal Weatherization Assistance Program covers some households; the Society's own labor and capital covers the gap.

The payoff is measurable. A house that loses 40% less heat in January is a member who doesn't face a utility shutoff in February. In a city with Ohio winters and a housing stock built in 1940, this is not marginal. It is the difference between a stable household and one in periodic crisis.

## Property Tax Assistance

Warren's elderly homeowners face a specific and underappreciated financial threat: property taxes on homes they have owned for decades, calculated against assessed values that don't reflect income. Ohio's **Homestead Exemption** reduces property taxes for homeowners 65 and over or permanently disabled — a meaningful relief program that many eligible residents in Warren are not enrolled in.

Phase 2 makes systematic Homestead Exemption enrollment part of the Society's housing navigation work. A member with the knowledge to walk another member through the application earns credits for the time. For an elderly homeowner on Social Security, the Homestead Exemption is worth several hundred dollars a year — money that stays in the household rather than leaving as a tax payment on a $90,000 house the person has lived in for forty years.

The same navigation approach applies to other property tax relief programs in Ohio and to any programs that reduce the fixed costs of homeownership for members on fixed incomes.

## Tenant Rights and Renter Support

A significant portion of Warren's members are renters. A significant portion of Warren's rental housing is maintained poorly, managed by landlords who have limited obligation to their tenants and know it. The power imbalance in Warren's rental market is stark: renters are numerous, poor, and often unaware of what Ohio landlord-tenant law actually requires.

The Society does not practice law and does not provide legal advice. But it can teach:

**Tenant rights education.** What Ohio law requires of landlords — habitability standards, notice requirements, repair obligations, retaliation prohibitions. What a renter's options are when a landlord fails to make legally required repairs. How to document conditions in writing. How to withhold rent appropriately under Ohio's repair-and-deduct provision. What constitutes illegal eviction. When to call Community Legal Aid.

**Documentation support.** Helping members document housing conditions — photographs, written records, dated communications with landlords — in the format that will be useful if the situation escalates to a legal dispute. This is practical knowledge that changes the power dynamic in a rental relationship even before any formal action is taken.

**Connection to Community Legal Aid.** The Society maintains an active relationship with Community Legal Aid in Youngstown, knows the intake process, and helps members access it when their housing situation has legal dimensions the Society cannot address on its own. The point of connection matters — many members who have legal options do not pursue them because navigating an intake process alone is one more difficult thing in a life full of difficult things.

## More Complex Repair Work

The repair capacity that developed in Phase 1 has deepened. Members who were learning trades alongside experienced practitioners have now done real work on real houses. Younger members who apprenticed with the retiring electrician or the laid-off plumber are now capable of a broader range of jobs. The knowledge that was at risk of dying with the older generation is being transmitted.

Phase 2 repair work takes on projects that Phase 1 could not: a furnace replacement, a plumbing re-route, a structural repair on a sagging porch that needed professional assessment before anyone touched it. The Society's relationship with the Community Action Agency and with licensed contractors in Warren means that work requiring permits or specialized knowledge goes to the right people — while the Society's members handle the surrounding work, the cleanup, the follow-through.

An important development in Phase 2: members who have learned repair skills through the housing lane begin teaching formal courses in the education lane. The practical knowledge becomes curriculum. The apprenticeship relationship that happened informally in Phase 1 becomes a structured course in Phase 2. The cycle of knowledge transmission closes.

## Relationship with TNP

The Trumbull Neighborhood Partnership has done more sustained physical housing work in Warren than any other organization. By Phase 2, the Society has been present in Warren long enough to have a real relationship with TNP — not a formal partnership necessarily, but a mutual awareness of what each organization does and where they can work together.

TNP's focus is vacant and blighted property. The Society's focus is occupied housing and member welfare. These are complementary rather than competing interests. A TNP renovation that returns a vacant house to occupancy on a block where Society members live is a neighborhood improvement that serves everyone. The Society's weatherization work on occupied housing is something TNP's program doesn't cover.

The relationship is tended by members who understand what TNP does and who take it seriously as a genuine partner in the long work of stabilizing Warren's neighborhoods.
