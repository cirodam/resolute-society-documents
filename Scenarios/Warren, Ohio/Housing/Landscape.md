# Warren, Ohio — Housing Landscape

## The Stock

Warren's housing stock is old, cheap, and in variable condition. The city was built for 73,000 people and now holds 38,912. That contraction is legible in the housing supply: 19,012 units for 16,871 households, a vacancy gap that reflects decades of population loss and a sustained demolition program.

The Trumbull Neighborhood Partnership has spent a decade doing the hardest physical work — demolishing approximately 1,200 blighted properties, renovating 600 occupied homes. Roughly 400 vacant structures remain. The demolition program has removed the most dangerous properties; what's left is a mix of stable stock, slowly deteriorating occupied housing, and a residual of vacant buildings that have survived because they are not yet at the demolition threshold or because ownership is complicated.

Median home value: approximately $90,000 — roughly one-quarter of the national median. Median gross rent: $805/month. These numbers make Warren an unusually affordable place to own property, which is simultaneously an asset and a symptom.

---

## Who Owns and Who Rents

Homeownership rate: 56.1% — slightly higher than the national average, which is notable given the poverty profile. Many of Warren's homeowners acquired their homes in an era when factory wages made ownership accessible. The homes are paid off or nearly so, but the property tax, maintenance, and utility costs of ownership remain — on incomes that have not grown at the pace of anything.

Racial disparity in homeownership is significant. White households account for nearly 80% of homeowners but only 60% of renters. Black and Hispanic households are 40% of renters but only 20% of owners. This is not accidental. It is the product of the same exclusions — redlining, discriminatory lending, neighborhood steering — that shaped residential patterns in every Rust Belt city. The dispossessed renter population is disproportionately Black and disproportionately poor.

## The Condition Problem

The people who own homes in Warren often cannot afford to maintain them. The people who rent often have no leverage to demand that landlords do it instead. The result is a city full of occupied housing that is slowly deteriorating around the people living in it.

A 1920s or 1940s-era house — which describes a large portion of Warren's stock — has specific vulnerabilities: aging electrical panels (often knob-and-tube or early aluminum wiring), cast iron or galvanized plumbing, insufficient insulation, and heating systems of varying vintage and reliability. These are not catastrophic failures in most cases. They are slow degradation that becomes acute when deferred maintenance compounds over years into decades.

The specific hazards that winter creates are serious. Ohio winters are cold. An aging house with poor insulation, drafty windows, and a struggling furnace is a health hazard as much as a housing problem. Pipe bursts are common in houses without insulated crawl spaces and exterior walls. Heating system failures in mid-January are emergencies.

## What Is Absent

- Any community-owned housing institution
- Any organization providing systematic maintenance support for owner-occupied households
- Any mechanism for helping renters assert their rights without navigating a legal system alone

The Trumbull Neighborhood Partnership does real work but focuses on demolition and renovation of vacant and blighted properties. Community Action Agency administers weatherization assistance with real waitlists. Community Legal Aid serves Trumbull County but cannot reach everyone who needs it. The gap between what these institutions can do and what the housing stock's condition requires is large.

---

## What This Means for the Society

Cheap real estate is a genuine asset. A Society seeking physical space for stores, kitchens, and meeting space will find it here at prices unavailable in most American cities. The same real estate market that reflects fifty years of disinvestment opens possibilities that would otherwise require large capital.

The housing lane's work, particularly in the early phases, is not about construction or acquisition. It is about helping members who already have homes keep those homes safe, warm, and functional — through the labor the Society can mobilize, the programs it can help members access, and the knowledge it can provide about what a house needs and why.
