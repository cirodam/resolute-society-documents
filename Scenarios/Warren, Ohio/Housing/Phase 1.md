# Housing — Phase 1: Triage, Repair, and Assistance

## What This Phase Does

Phase 1 housing work is practical and immediate. It does not require a building, a large budget, or a formal division. It requires trained members, a working ledger, and knowledge of the resources that already exist. The goal is to stabilize the housing situations of the people who join the Society, and to do that without requiring them to demonstrate need or accept charity.

---

## Household Triage

The foundation of everything else. A trained member — earning credits for the work — walks through each member's home and produces a simple, honest picture of what's there.

What triage looks at: the heating system and its condition; plumbing for slow leaks, drainage problems, and pipe vulnerability in winter; electrical for obvious hazards — overloaded circuits, exposed wiring, outdated panels; the roof and whether water is getting in; moisture and mold, particularly in basements and bathrooms; smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors and whether they work; draft points where heat is escaping; pest problems; and any structural issues visible to a careful non-specialist eye.

What triage produces: a short prioritized list. Urgent — address now because it is a safety hazard or will cause serious damage if ignored. Before winter — practical urgency given Ohio's winters. When resources allow — real issues that don't require immediate action. The list gives both the member and the Society a clear picture of what the situation actually is, which is often different from what the member assumed.

Triage is the prerequisite for everything else in the housing domain. You cannot prioritize repair without knowing what needs repairing. You cannot identify housing insecurity without walking into the house.

## Household Repair

The trades knowledge exists in Warren — electricians, carpenters, plumbers, people who understand HVAC and roofing and structural basics. Much of it is underemployed or retired. The ledger makes it available to members.

The repair work the Society coordinates in Phase 1 is the kind that keeps houses safe and functional: fixing the wiring hazard before it causes a fire, repairing the porch before ice makes it dangerous, patching the roof before the next rain does more damage inside, fixing the slow leak that is quietly destroying a floor, insulating the pipes before a cold snap bursts them. None of this is glamorous. All of it matters.

The member with the skill earns credits. The member whose house gets fixed spends credits. The work stays inside the community. The trades knowledge that exists in Warren — often held by people in their 50s and 60s, often in danger of dying with them — gets used and, over time, transmitted to younger members learning alongside.

The Society does not do work that requires licensed professionals in contexts where licensing matters for safety or liability. Where a job needs a licensed electrician or a permitted structural repair, the Society helps the member navigate that rather than substituting untrained labor.

## Identifying Housing Insecurity

Housing insecurity takes more forms than eviction. A member who cannot pay this month's rent is housing insecure. So is the member whose landlord has ignored a broken furnace for two months, the member doubled up in a relative's house because they have nowhere else to go, the member living in a structurally unsafe building they cannot afford to leave, the member who owns their home but is one repair bill away from losing it.

The Society's triage process is one way housing insecurity surfaces. But it also surfaces through relationships — when a member mentions in passing that they're behind on rent, or when a Community Health Worker visit reveals a household in crisis. The Society is not a surveillance system. It is a community in which people are actually known to each other, which means housing problems become visible in the normal course of community life rather than only at the point of crisis.

When a member is housing insecure, the Society's first response is to understand the specific situation and connect them to whatever help exists — internal, external, or both.

## Power Bills and Energy Costs

Heating costs are a serious budget pressure for low-income households in Warren. Ohio winters are cold. The housing stock is old and often poorly insulated. Utility shutoffs are a real risk for members who cannot keep up with bills.

**Programs that exist and that many eligible members are not accessing:**

*HEAP — Home Energy Assistance Program.* Ohio's primary utility assistance program. Eligible for households up to 175% of the federal poverty level. Given Warren's poverty profile, a substantial portion of members will qualify. The Society helps members apply, navigate the process, and access the benefit.

*PIPP Plus — Percentage of Income Payment Plan.* An Ohio utility program that caps monthly utility payments at a percentage of household income for low-income customers. Customers on PIPP Plus cannot be shut off as long as they make their capped payments. Many eligible households in Warren are not enrolled.

*Federal Weatherization Assistance Program.* Free energy efficiency improvements — insulation, air sealing, heating system tune-ups — for income-eligible households. Administered locally through the Community Action Agency of Trumbull County. Waitlists can be long, but the benefit is significant.

**What the Society does directly:**

Weatherization through the ledger. Door draft sealing, window film insulation kits, pipe insulation wrap, attic hatch insulation — these are low-cost materials and straightforward labor that members can do for each other, paid in credits. The triage process identifies where heat is escaping. The repair work addresses it. The combination of program enrollment and practical weatherization can meaningfully reduce what members spend on heating.

## Relocation Help

Sometimes a member needs to move. Eviction, unsafe conditions, a landlord who won't repair, a living situation that has become untenable. Moving is logistically hard and expensive, and it falls disproportionately on people who already have the least capacity to manage it.

The Society helps with the physical work — packing, moving, cleaning — paid in credits through the ledger. It helps with finding new housing through members who know the rental market in Warren. It helps with the deposit and first-month problem through knowledge of programs that provide emergency rental assistance. It helps the member understand their rights as a tenant if they are being pushed out in ways that are not legally clean.

Relocation help is not a standalone service. It is what the Society does for a member in a housing crisis, drawing on the relationships, the ledger, and the knowledge of external resources that the housing work has built.

## External Resources

The Society does not operate alone in the housing domain. Existing institutions do real work in Warren and the Society's role is often to help members access them rather than to duplicate them.

**Trumbull Neighborhood Partnership** has spent a decade doing physical housing work in Warren — demolition of blighted properties, renovation of occupied housing. They are the most active housing organization in the city and a potential relationship for the Society to cultivate.

**Community Action Agency of Trumbull County** administers weatherization assistance and other low-income household programs. Knowing their intake process and eligibility requirements is practical knowledge for any member doing housing navigation.

**Community Legal Aid (Youngstown)** provides free legal services for housing matters including eviction defense, landlord-tenant disputes, and foreclosure. Many members facing housing crises have legal options they don't know exist.

**HEAP and PIPP Plus** enrollment through the Ohio Department of Development and local utility companies.
