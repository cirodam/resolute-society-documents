# Food — Phase 1: The Community Store

## What It Is

The community store is a buying club with a physical location. It purchases goods in bulk at wholesale and makes them available to members through the credit economy. It is the founding institution of the Warren Society.

The pitch is concrete: we buy rice, toilet paper, laundry detergent, and chicken in bulk at wholesale prices, and you pay significantly less than Dollar General. That offer lands for the machinist, the single mother on SNAP, the elderly woman on a fixed income, the veteran. The community forms around the store before it has to form around an idea.

---

## What the Store Provides

Ordered by maximum benefit:

**1. Household staples — toilet paper, laundry detergent, dish soap, cleaning supplies, trash bags**

These are the highest-priority items the store can carry. They cannot be purchased with SNAP, which means members are currently buying them with cash at Dollar General's retail markup — one of the most expensive ways to acquire them. A case of toilet paper bought at wholesale costs a fraction of the per-roll price at Dollar General. Applied across laundry detergent, dish soap, and cleaning supplies, the annual cash savings for a household are significant and immediate. Every member needs these things every month. The savings begin with the first purchase.

**2. Bulk dry staples — rice, beans, lentils, oats, flour, cornmeal, pasta**

The foundation of the cheapest nutritious diet that exists. These are SNAP-eligible, storable for months or years, and bought at per-unit prices that are dramatically lower in bulk than at retail. A 50-pound bag of rice from a food service wholesaler costs roughly what four small retail bags cost at Dollar General. Dry beans are among the cheapest complete proteins available. Oats, flour, and cornmeal are the base of home cooking across both the Black and working-class white food traditions in Warren. These items reduce food cost substantially while improving nutrition compared to the processed shelf goods most accessible in the inner neighborhoods.

**3. Delivery to disabled and elderly members**

Not a product — a service. Members who cannot reach the store receive deliveries. The member making the delivery earns credits. This function transforms the store's reach from a location into a network. The elderly woman on Social Security who cannot drive to the east side Walmart receives her bulk staples and household goods at her door. The disabled member who has difficulty leaving the house is served without requiring them to ask for charity — they are members with credits, purchasing goods the community provides. Delivery is probably the highest per-person benefit the store produces for the most vulnerable members of the community.

**4. Protein in bulk — eggs, chicken, canned fish, ground beef**

Nutritionally critical and purchased in quantity by every household. Eggs bought by the flat (30 eggs) cost significantly less per egg than retail. Chicken leg quarters in case quantities are among the cheapest animal proteins available anywhere and are the cut most families in Warren are already buying. Canned tuna and salmon by the case. Ground beef in bulk. The savings here are real, the nutrition value is high, and these are items members are already spending money on every month.

**5. Personal care — feminine hygiene products, shampoo, toothpaste, diapers and wipes**

Cash-only purchases — not SNAP-eligible — at retail markup from Dollar General. Warren's population is 40.9% female-headed households, which means a large share of members are women managing these costs on tight budgets. Feminine hygiene products specifically represent a recurring cash expense that has no substitute. Diapers and wipes for families with young children. Shampoo and toothpaste in bulk quantities. The per-unit savings are significant and the items are needed continuously.

**6. Cooking essentials — oil, salt, sugar, vinegar, baking powder, spices**

The inputs that make the bulk dry staples into actual food. These are SNAP-eligible and bought in quantities from a restaurant supplier at a fraction of the retail cost. A large container of vegetable oil, a bag of salt, bulk spices — these are things every household replaces regularly. Bought wholesale and available through the store, they reduce the cost of home cooking and make the dry staples genuinely usable.

**7. Frozen and canned vegetables**

SNAP-eligible and significantly better nutritionally than the processed shelf food that dominates Dollar General's inventory. Frozen vegetables bought in case quantities and stored in the Society's chest freezer. Canned tomatoes — diced, crushed, paste — are a cooking staple that goes into almost everything and is dramatically cheaper per case than per can at retail.

**8. Over-the-counter medicines and health basics — aspirin, ibuprofen, cold medicine, bandages, antacids**

Not SNAP-eligible, purchased with cash, needed by every household on a recurring basis. Bought in bulk from a wholesale supplier and available at the store, they reduce cash expenditure on healthcare basics.

**9. Fresh produce — seasonal, from Trumbull County farms**

The highest nutritional value the store can offer and the item most completely absent from Dollar General's inventory. Fresh vegetables and fruit require more frequent restocking and more careful inventory management than shelf-stable goods, which is why they come later rather than first. But Trumbull County has agricultural land and farms. A relationship between the Society and local farms — purchasing seasonal produce directly — is achievable once the store is operationally stable.

**10. Seeds and growing supplies**

The connection between the buying club and the Society's longer-term agricultural work. Seed packets, basic growing supplies, eventually seedlings. The store becomes one node in a food production system rather than purely a purchasing operation.

## How the Store Works

**The credit economy.** The store prices its goods in credits. Members earn credits through labor and through the monthly allowance that flows from the treasury to every member in good standing. Credits spent at the store return to the treasury.

**Capital calls.** Stocking the store requires dollars. When the Society needs to fund a bulk order — from Restaurant Depot in Youngstown, from a local farm, from a food service wholesaler — it opens a capital call: a specific, governance-authorized, time-limited window in which members may contribute dollars in exchange for credits disbursed from the treasury. The call closes when the declared amount is raised or the deadline passes.

**SNAP.** Most food items the store carries are SNAP-eligible. Members using SNAP contribute their benefits toward the buying pool, paying their share with their EBT card. Federal food benefits circulate within the community rather than at Walmart.

**Delivery.** Members who cannot come to the store receive deliveries from the store. The member doing the delivery earns credits.

---

## What the Store Is Not

The store is not a food pantry. Members are not recipients of charity; they are members purchasing goods with credits they have earned. Credits received through the allowance are earned — they are the community honoring its obligation to a member who cannot reasonably contribute labor right now. That is a different thing from charity, and the distinction matters.

---

## Physical Requirements

**Dry storage.** A cool, dry space for staples, household goods, canned goods, and personal care items. The volume of dry goods a store serving 30–50 members carries is manageable in a modest commercial space.

**A chest freezer.** The single most important piece of capital equipment for the store in the early phase. One chest freezer handles frozen protein and frozen vegetables for the membership. Cost: a few hundred dollars.

**A walk-in cooler (later).** Fresh produce requires refrigeration. This is a more significant capital investment that comes after the store is operationally stable and the farm relationships are in place.

**Proximity to members.** Each store should be walkable or bikeable for the members it primarily serves.
