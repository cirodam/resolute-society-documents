# Food — Phase 3.1: Grain Milling

## Why Milling

The store has carried bulk flour and cornmeal since Phase 1 — bought from a food service wholesaler, adequate for cooking courses and everyday baking. Milling is an enhancement on that foundation that improves what members eat, reduces long-term storage costs, and adds a processing capacity that connects the food lane to the Agriculture lane.

The case is straightforward: whole grains store for years or decades without degrading. Flour and cornmeal, once milled, go rancid within weeks to months as the germ oils oxidize. The Society that stores whole wheat berries and whole dried corn in sealed containers is holding a stable, long-lived food supply. The Society that stores flour is managing a perishable. Milling on demand inverts that — you keep the grain, mill what you need, and the rest waits indefinitely.

Fresh-milled flour is also noticeably better to cook with. The oils that go rancid in stored flour are, before oxidation, flavor and nutrition. Fresh cornmeal tastes like corn. Fresh whole wheat flour makes bread that commercial flour can't approximate. These are not trivial distinctions for a community building food traditions worth keeping.

---

## What Gets Milled

**Wheat berries → flour.** Hard red or hard white winter wheat — Ohio is wheat country, and Trumbull County farms grow it — milled into whole wheat flour for bread, biscuits, and the flour-based cooking that is central to both the Black and working-class white food traditions in Warren. Hard red winter wheat stores for twenty-plus years in proper conditions.

**Dried corn → cornmeal.** Cornmeal is the base of cornbread, grits, polenta, and a dozen other staples already being cooked in the Society's kitchen. Open-pollinated field corn dried and milled fresh produces a product qualitatively different from the commodity cornmeal in a paper bag. As the Agriculture lane develops, the Society's own dried corn comes through the mill.

**Oats → oat flour and rolled oats.** A grain mill with the right attachments can process oat groats into oat flour or, with a flaker attachment, rolled oats. Oats are already in the store as a staple. Whole oat groats store much longer than rolled oats, which go stale.

---

## The Equipment

A good stone burr grain mill — countertop, hand-crank or electric — costs $300–600 new and is available used for less. It produces flour in the volume a household kitchen or small-scale cooking operation needs. For the Society's kitchen, milling in batches before cooking sessions is practical at this scale.

A larger electric stone mill — the kind used in small commercial bakeries — costs more but produces larger volumes faster. This becomes relevant when the Society's kitchen is regularly baking for community meals and the meal delivery program. It is not required at the start of Phase 3.1.

The equipment is purchased through the Society's capital infrastructure — modest cost relative to the walk-in cooler or commercial kitchen equipment already in place.

---

## The Connection to Agriculture

The grain mill is where the food lane and the agriculture lane most directly meet. A Society that mills its own grain and a Society that eventually grows or sources grain directly are two parts of the same arc. As the Agriculture lane develops farm purchasing relationships and potentially access to land, the grain that comes off those fields has somewhere to go: the mill, the kitchen, the store.

This is also where seed saving enters the food lane. Open-pollinated grain varieties — the same varieties the Society saves seed from — are the varieties worth milling. The seed library in the Agriculture lane and the grain supply for the mill are, eventually, the same stock.

---

## What Milling Adds

Milling is not a transformation of the food lane's character. The store is still the store, the kitchen is still the kitchen. What milling adds is: better product, longer storage, lower long-term cost for bulk grain, and a processing step that brings the Society meaningfully closer to handling grain from field to table.

The member who mills the grain, bakes the cornbread, serves it at the community meal — and who knows that the corn came from a Trumbull County field — is inside a food relationship that didn't exist when the Society started. That is the accumulation the food lane is building toward.
