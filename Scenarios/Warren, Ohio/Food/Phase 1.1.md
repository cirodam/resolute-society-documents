# Food — Phase 1.2: Gleaning

## What It Is

Gleaning is the first sourcing practice the store adds beyond wholesale purchasing. It requires no capital, no formal agreements, and no infrastructure beyond members willing to show up with containers and transportation. It begins as soon as the store is operational and someone knows a farm well enough to ask.

---

## Field Gleaning

Trumbull County has agricultural land surrounding Warren. After harvest, significant quantities of produce remain in fields — not worth the cost of mechanical harvest to the farmer, but perfectly edible. Tomatoes left on the vine after the primary pick. Sweet corn past peak eating date. Potatoes and squash left in the ground when the labor cost of collection exceeds the commodity return. Farmers are typically glad to let gleaners take what remains — it costs them nothing and saves them the bother of discing it under.

A Society with twenty members can organize a gleaning party, come home with hundreds of pounds of produce, and stock the store with fresh food it could not carry through wholesale purchasing alone. The cost is labor, which the ledger pays in credits to every member who shows up.

The agricultural lane's landscape document describes the farms around Warren in more detail. The gleaning relationship is the Society's first relationship with those farms — lower friction than a purchasing agreement, requiring nothing but showing up and being reliable about it. First relationships lead to other things.

---

## Grocery Store Surplus

The Giant Eagle on East Market Street discards food every day — past sell-by date, overstock, cosmetically imperfect produce that doesn't move. A relationship with store management that routes this surplus to the Society rather than the dumpster is achievable through a direct conversation and costs nothing.

Second Harvest Food Bank, which already operates through 160 pantries in the three-county area, is another potential source. The Society can make food bank distributions available through the credit economy rather than as charity distributions — the same food, but arriving in the hands of members as something they are purchasing rather than something they are receiving.

---

## What Gleaning Forces

The most useful thing gleaning does is force preservation. A hundred pounds of tomatoes gleaned in August must be processed within days or they're lost. That urgency produces a canning session — which is a practical skills course, which teaches members something they will use for the rest of their lives.

The gleaning party that ends with members canning together in someone's kitchen is simultaneously a food lane operation, an education lane operation, and a community-building event. The store gets stocked. Skills get transmitted. Credits are earned by everyone who shows up. The relationships that form over a day of hard collective work are different from the relationships formed across a store counter.

This is the pattern that runs through the Society's food work: one activity produces multiple things at once. Gleaning and preserving together are more than the sum of their parts.

---

## What Gleaning Is Not

Gleaning is not the Society's food strategy. It is an enhancement to the store — a sourcing practice that adds fresh produce and processed goods the wholesale operation cannot provide, at no cost beyond labor. Members still shop at the store for staples. The store still purchases in bulk from Restaurant Depot and food service wholesalers. Gleaning fills in what purchased inventory cannot: seasonally abundant fresh food from the land immediately surrounding the city.

The arc from gleaning toward organized preservation toward farm partnerships toward eventual food production is the arc of the food lane across its phases. Gleaning is where the Society first touches the agricultural land around Warren. Everything that comes later in that relationship starts here.
