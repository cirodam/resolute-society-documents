# Food — Phase 3: Processing and Preservation

## What Changes

Phase 2 built the kitchen as a care institution — producing meals for members who cannot cook, enabling community meals, teaching cooking. Phase 3 turns the kitchen into a processing facility during harvest season and builds the cold storage and preservation infrastructure that carries the Society's food supply through winter.

The shift is one of volume and season. The kitchen that produces 30 meals a week in February becomes the kitchen that puts up 500 jars of tomatoes in September. The chest freezers that handled the store's protein inventory expand into a cold storage system that holds the season's gleaned and purchased produce. The Society that was buying its canned goods from Restaurant Depot now produces its own.

---

## Preservation at Scale

Household-scale preservation — a canning session in someone's kitchen after a gleaning party — was the right entry point in Phase 1.2. Phase 3 is where it becomes an operation.

**Canning.** The kitchen's commercial range can run multiple pressure canners or large water-bath setups simultaneously. A full Saturday in September can produce hundreds of jars of canned tomatoes, salsa, pickles, or beans. The labor is credited through the ledger. The jars go into the Society's stored inventory and are available through the store across the winter at credit prices. A jar of tomatoes the community put up in September, available in January — sourced from a Trumbull County field, processed by member labor — is not the same thing as a can from a warehouse in New Jersey.

**Fermentation.** Lacto-fermentation at volume: large-format crocks of sauerkraut, kimchi, pickles, hot sauce. Fermented goods have long shelf lives without refrigeration, are nutritionally dense, and require only produce, salt, and time. A fifty-pound crock of sauerkraut started in October is ready by Thanksgiving and lasts through spring. The fermentation operation requires almost no capital beyond the crocks and a cool storage space.

**Drying.** The kitchen's oven or a bank of food dehydrators processes herbs, mushrooms, peppers, and fruit into shelf-stable form. Dried goods have the longest storage life of any preservation method and take up the least space. The herb garden and the gleaned produce that can't be canned or frozen gets dried.

**Freezing.** The Society's freezer capacity has grown by Phase 3 — multiple chest freezers or a dedicated commercial freezer unit — with organized inventory management. Blanched and frozen vegetables, processed meat, and pre-cooked meals the kitchen produces in quantity are managed as a collective resource, tracked through the ledger.

---

## Cold Storage

Fresh produce at scale requires more than chest freezers. Phase 3 acquires a walk-in cooler — the capital investment deferred in Phase 1 until the Society's treasury and operational capacity could support it.

A used walk-in cooler unit, purchased through a restaurant closure or equipment dealer and installed in the store or kitchen space, changes what the Society can source and hold. Root vegetables — potatoes, carrots, beets, turnips, winter squash — store for months in a properly maintained cooler. The fall gleaning haul that in earlier phases had to be processed immediately now has somewhere to live while processing catches up.

The walk-in cooler is funded through capital call. It is maintained by members and managed as community infrastructure — tracked inventory, rotation protocols, temperature monitoring by members who know what they're looking at. A member checking the cooler temperature twice a day earns credits for that work.

---

## Farm Supply for Processing

The preservation operation requires volume that gleaning alone cannot provide. Phase 3 builds the direct farm purchasing relationships that supply the processing line.

A direct purchase arrangement with a Trumbull County vegetable farm might look like: the Society commits to buying 500 pounds of roma tomatoes at an agreed price, picked on a specified date, for the canning operation. The farm has a guaranteed buyer it can plan around. The Society gets a committed supply at a price below retail. Payment is in dollars — funded through capital call or the dollar treasury — because the farm has its own obligations to meet. The relationship is not commercial in the extractive sense: it is a durable arrangement between two institutions with overlapping interests in the agricultural future of the region.

These purchasing relationships are the precursors to deeper agricultural partnerships — the land lease conversations, the farm succession conversations that belong to the Agriculture lane's later phases. By the time the Society is having those conversations, it has been a reliable buyer for several years. That track record is the foundation.

---

## What the Society Holds

By the end of Phase 3, the Society's food infrastructure spans the full arc from sourcing to storage to distribution:

The store carries wholesale-purchased staples, farm-purchased produce, and Society-processed preserved goods. The kitchen produces meals for homebound members and feeds community gatherings. The cooler holds the season's harvest. The shelves hold jars the community put up. The freezers hold what the community processed.

A meaningful portion of what members eat has passed through the Society's hands — gleaned, purchased, processed, stored, and distributed within the community's own relationships. The supply chain that was entirely corporate when the Society founded now has a community institution inside it that the community owns and operates.

That is not self-sufficiency. It is sovereignty in the sense that matters: the capacity to do for yourself what you were entirely dependent on others to do, and to expand that capacity over time.
