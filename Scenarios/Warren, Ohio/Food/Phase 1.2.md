# Food — Phase 1.2: Store Delivery

## What It Is

Store delivery extends the buying club from a location into a network. A member places an order in credits. Another member fills it from the store's inventory and brings it to their door. The receiving member spends credits. The delivering member earns them. The store serves members who cannot reach it — and in Warren, a significant number of members cannot.

---

## Who It Reaches

**Elderly members** who no longer drive and whose mobility has declined. The east-side Walmart was never accessible on foot; the Society's store is walkable for some members and not for others. An 80-year-old woman on Social Security who lives alone on the southeast side is not going to figure out a ride to the store every week. Delivery means she is a full member of the buying club — ordering what she needs, spending her allowance credits, receiving her goods at her door — rather than a theoretical beneficiary who in practice cannot participate.

**Disabled members** who face physical barriers to leaving the house consistently. Same logic.

**Members without vehicles** in a city where car access is uneven and the store may not be on a bus route. Warren's inner neighborhoods are car-dependent by design, and 6.6% unemployment in a 32% poverty city means a portion of members lack reliable transportation.

**Time-constrained members** — the single mother working retail or home health who cannot get to the store during operating hours. This is the population the scenario most risks losing: she would benefit from the store, she has credits through the allowance, but she cannot show up. Delivery solves this. An order placed in the evening is waiting on her doorstep the next morning.

---

## How It Works

The operation is simple. The store maintains a standing order window — members submit what they need, in what quantity, to the store's coordinator. Orders are batched by neighborhood and assigned to delivery members who are compensated through the ledger per delivery or per route. Routes are efficient: a single member delivering to five addresses on the southeast side on a Saturday morning earns credits for a few hours of work and extends the store's reach to five households who otherwise wouldn't be served.

Members on a standing delivery schedule — the elderly woman, the disabled member, the member who has been receiving weekly delivery for months — are the most important deliveries. Consistency matters. The member who shows up every Saturday becomes a known presence. That is not incidental to the delivery function. It is the delivery function.

---

## The Relationship

Delivery is not just logistics. The member who drops off groceries to an elderly woman alone in a house is also the person who notices if she seems worse than last week, if something seems off, if she mentions that she hasn't been sleeping. The delivery visit and the check-in visit are the same visit. The credit the delivering member earns is for carrying boxes. The community value they produce includes something harder to quantify.

This is where the food lane and the healthcare lane meet at the most basic level. The delivery member is not a Community Health Worker. They are not assessing or diagnosing anything. They are a neighbor who shows up, which is the thing the healthcare lane identifies as its most foundational intervention — before any training, before any program.

---

## What Delivery Requires

A coordinator who manages the order queue and assigns routes. Delivery members willing to run routes on a schedule. A ledger that tracks and compensates the work. The store's existing inventory.

No additional infrastructure. No vehicle beyond what members already have. The store absorbs delivery into its operations from the beginning, not as an add-on after the store is established but as part of what the store does from day one.
