# Warren, Ohio — Food Landscape

## The Geography

Full-service grocery stores in Warren are concentrated along the eastern and northeastern fringe of the city, in the suburban commercial corridor:

- **Walmart Supercenter** — 2015 Walmart Drive NE (northeast edge)
- **Giant Eagle** — 8202 East Market Street (east side strip mall)
- **ALDI** — east side
- **Cortland Walmart** — just outside city limits to the east

These are not neighborhood stores. They are car-dependent destinations. If you have a reliable vehicle, this is where you do your real grocery shopping. If you don't — and in a city with 32% poverty, a large single-mother population, significant elderly residents, and 6.6% unemployment, a substantial portion of people don't — you are largely cut off from them.

What fills the gap in the residential interior is Dollar General. At least six or seven locations are scattered across Warren's inner neighborhoods on the major arterials:

- Mahoning Ave NW — multiple locations
- Tod Ave SW
- South Street SE
- Elm Road NE
- East Market Street

Dollar General sells heavily processed, shelf-stable items at per-unit prices that are often worse than Walmart, with almost no fresh produce, no real meat, and no capacity to feed a family a nutritious diet. For residents of the inner west and south sides without reliable car access, this is where supplementary food comes from — and for some, the primary option.

This is the standard Rust Belt food geography: full-service stores retreat to the suburban fringe where car-owning customers are concentrated; the urban core gets dollar stores.

## The Assistance Layer

A large portion of Warren's food system runs on government benefits and charity.

**SNAP.** Given Warren's 32% poverty rate and 40% Medicaid enrollment, SNAP participation is very high. Across Trumbull, Mahoning, and Columbiana counties combined, 91,000 people are enrolled in SNAP — roughly a third of the three-county population. Warren skews much poorer than the county average, so the city's rate is likely higher. SNAP dollars are spent almost entirely at Walmart and Dollar General, which accept EBT. Federal food assistance flows directly into corporate revenue.

**Second Harvest Food Bank of the Mahoning Valley** (Youngstown-based) covers Trumbull County through a network of 160 food pantries across the three-county area. 470,000 pantry visits in 2024. 11 million pounds of food distributed annually. 89,000 people food insecure in the region, including 25,700 children.

**St. Vincent de Paul** operates the Nick Parilla Dining Hall on Niles Road: daily meals Monday through Saturday, curbside carryout, no questions asked. Monthly food box distributions on the last Friday of each month.

**CSFP** (Commodity Supplemental Food Program): federal monthly food boxes for low-income elderly. Given that 18.5% of Warren is over 65 and the poverty rate is what it is, this is not a marginal program.

## The Informal Layer

The Black church network and extended family structures sustain informal food sharing that no database captures: church dinners, neighbors with gardens, family members who cook and distribute. This is real mutual aid that has operated continuously and without institutional recognition. It is the closest thing Warren has to a food commons.

---

## What Is Absent

- Any meaningful food production within the city
- Any cooperative or community-owned food infrastructure
- Any food system that is not corporate supply chain, federal benefits, or charity

The 160 food pantries in the three-county network represent a large charitable infrastructure doing real work under real strain. Adding one more pantry to that network is not what a Society is for.

---

## What This Means for the Society

The food problem in Warren is structural. People are not food-insecure because food doesn't exist nearby — Walmart is accessible to anyone with a car and SNAP covers basic purchasing for those who qualify. The problem is the nature of what's available, the terms on which it's available, and the complete dependency on systems the community neither controls nor influences.

Every dollar spent at Walmart on food leaves Warren. Every SNAP benefit redeemed at Dollar General transfers federal money to a corporation that has no obligation to this city. Every food pantry visit is a charitable transaction rather than a community one — something received rather than something built.

The Society's interest is not in emergency food distribution. It is in food sovereignty over time: the capacity of the community to feed itself from what it grows, raises, preserves, and distributes within its own relationships. That is a long-horizon project. In Warren, it starts with whatever land is available, whatever growing knowledge exists among members, and whatever kitchen and storage infrastructure the Society can build or acquire.

The interim is honest: for the foreseeable future, members of a Warren Society will shop at Walmart and use SNAP like everyone else. The question is whether, five years in, they are also eating food grown in the city and preserved in a Society root cellar — and whether that capacity is expanding rather than contracting.
