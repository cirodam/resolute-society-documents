# Warren, Ohio — The Founding Picture

## What the Society Is

Warren has been the object of interventions for fifty years. Urban renewal. Federal programs. Nonprofit initiatives. Economic development plans. City council resolutions. None of it reversed the trajectory, because all of it was done to Warren rather than by Warren. The people making the decisions were not the people whose lives depended on the decisions being right. The money came from outside, the priorities were set outside, and when the grant ended or the initiative lost momentum, it left.

The Society is not an intervention. It is an instrument through which the people of Warren act on their own behalf. It does not require a government charter, a foundation grant, or a city council vote to begin. It does not answer to a board of directors in another city or a funder with different priorities. It exists as long as its members choose to sustain it, and it does what its members decide to do.

This is its primary advantage, and it is not a small one. The legitimacy of the Society comes from the only source that produces durable legitimacy: the people themselves, acting together, in their own name, for their own purposes. That legitimacy cannot be defunded. It cannot be restructured by a new mayor. It cannot have its mission redirected by a change in foundation priorities. It is the community's own institution, and it remains so as long as the community keeps it.

This is also why the Society is not another social service. Social services are things done for people who qualify. The Society is something done by people who choose to be part of it. The member who earns credits fixing a neighbor's furnace is not a volunteer in a program. The member who takes the literacy course is not a client in a system. They are members of a community that takes their agency seriously — which is different from anything Warren has been offered in a long time.

The cross-racial dimension of this matters in Warren specifically. The Black community on the Southeast Side and the white working-class community on the North End have been failed by the same institutions in different but parallel ways — different in form, parallel in the experience of being managed and left behind. The Society does not ask them to agree on everything before building something together. It offers both communities the same thing: an instrument of their own, accountable to them, built by their own hands and sustained by their own commitment. That shared ground is not cultural or historical. It is the shared experience of being done waiting.

## What the Ledger Does

Warren already has what Warren needs.

The machinist whose skills have no outlet since the plant closed. The grandmother who can put up forty quarts of tomatoes in an afternoon and has been doing it alone. The mechanic who fixes his neighbors' cars as a favor because there's no structure for it to be anything else. The nurse's aide who knows how to read a medication label and watches elderly patients struggle with theirs. The elder who remembers what the land around this city grows and what it needs. The organizer who knows how to run a meeting so the quiet people get heard.

This productive capacity has always been in Warren. The commercial economy extracted it — paid wages for it and sent the value to shareholders in another state. When the plants left, the extraction stopped but the capacity remained, scattered across the city's households, unconnected, untranslated into anything the community could use.

The Society's ledger is the mechanism that changes this. When the machinist fixes the transit shop equipment and earns credits, those credits don't leave Warren. They get spent at the community store, which funds the next bulk order, which feeds the elderly member who cannot cook for herself, whose delivery is run by a member who earns credits for it, who spends them on the literacy course, where a member earns credits for teaching. The capacity that was latent and isolated begins to move. It compounds. It stays.

The Society is the beating heart that keeps this moving — the regular pulse of community meals, governance, store operations, courses, check-ins that ensures the ledger doesn't go still. The ledger is the lifeblood. The Society is what keeps it circulating.

The goal is not growth in the economic sense. It is not revenue, not scale, not a balance sheet that impresses anyone outside Warren. The goal is that the productive capacity of the people living in Warren works solely for the benefit of the people living in Warren — that what this community has to give, this community receives.

That has not been true here for a long time. The Society is the structure that makes it true.

## Resilience

The current arrangement — Warren households depending on supply chains that stretch from overseas factories through container ports through national distribution centers to a store on the east fringe of a shrinking city — is not a permanent feature of reality. It is a temporary condition produced by cheap shipping, cheap fuel, global wage arbitrage, and the political stability that makes all of it function. Any serious disruption to any of those conditions ripples directly into the household.

COVID made this visible in ways that couldn't be dismissed. Baby formula vanished because production was concentrated in two factories. Semiconductor shortages idled auto plants because a single supply chain node failed. The supply chains that appear maximally efficient in stable conditions are maximally brittle when conditions change — because efficiency and resilience are opposites. The efficient system eliminates redundancy. The resilient system is built on it.

Warren should produce what it sensibly can for itself. Not autarky — the community imports what it must and produces what it can. But a deliberate, lane-by-lane expansion of what is made and grown and maintained locally, prioritizing the goods and capacities most critical to member welfare and most exposed to supply chain risk.

The Society is building that redundancy. The bulk stored grains in the store that outlast commercial flour by decades. The preserved harvest in jars on the shelf. The grain mill that processes what the agricultural lane grows. The mechanic who keeps vehicles running without waiting on parts from a disrupted supply chain. The Community Health Worker who monitors a diabetic member without requiring a hospital appointment. The washing machine built in Warren and repaired by someone in Warren with skills that exist in Warren.

Each of these is a point at which the community's dependency on an external supply chain is reduced. None of them individually is decisive. Together, accumulated over years, they represent a community that is in a fundamentally different position when the supply chains that serve it today are disrupted — not if, but when.

This is not only a practical argument. It is a statement about what a community owes its members across time. The Society that spends thirty years building local productive capacity is preparing the people of Warren for the world that is coming. The community that spends those years in total dependency on systems it does not control and cannot influence will face that world without the tools to navigate it.

The store is the beginning. The capacity it grows into is the point.

## The Entry Point

The founding institution of a Resolute Society in Warren is a community store — a buying club with a physical location that purchases goods in bulk and makes them available to members through the credit economy.

This is the right starting point for several reasons that converge.

It offers something immediately legible. You do not have to explain the telos or the governance structure to get someone through the door. The pitch is concrete: we buy rice, toilet paper, laundry detergent, and chicken in bulk at wholesale prices, and you pay significantly less than Dollar General. That offer lands for the machinist, the single mother on SNAP, the elderly woman on a fixed income, the veteran. All of them want this. The community forms around the store before it has to form around an idea.

It is inclusive from the first day of membership. New members receive the monthly allowance from the treasury, which gives them purchasing power at the store before they have contributed a single hour of labor. The store is accessible to the disabled member, the elderly member, the member working two jobs — not as a charity but as a membership benefit they have standing to claim. The covenant is expressed in something they can walk in and use.

It is a physical anchor. In a city with cheap real estate, a dedicated space is achievable earlier than almost anywhere else. Once the Society has a location, everything else has somewhere to happen — courses in the back, the community health worker based there, deliveries to elderly and disabled members originating from it, the tool library in a corner. People come in for goods and find community. The space is the condition for everything else.

It makes the Society visible to the city. Not as an idea, but as a place you can walk into. A place that is there next month and the month after. In a city that has watched institutions leave for fifty years, that continuity is itself the message.

## Two Stores, Not One

Warren has distinct neighborhoods with distinct demographics and distinct histories. The Southeast Side — where SESCA meets, where the Black neighborhood associations are organized, where the mutual aid traditions of the Great Migration communities have persisted — is a different place from the Northwest Side or the North End, where the white working-class population is concentrated.

A single store will feel like it belongs to one community. It will draw members from the neighborhood it sits in more readily than from the neighborhoods it doesn't. Asking the elderly Black woman from the Southeast Side to come to the Northwest store, or the laid-off machinist from the North End to come to a store in SESCA territory, is asking people to cross lines that are real even when they're informal.

Two stores — one rooted in the Southeast Side, one in the Northwest or North End — serve the whole city without asking anyone to travel to someone else's institution. They share the same ledger. Members can shop at either. The buying club operates as a single purchasing operation, stocking both locations from the same bulk orders. But each store is embedded in its own neighborhood, run by members who live there, known by the people who walk past it.

The founding energy for each store may come from different places. The Southeast Side store likely grows from the existing mutual aid networks — the neighborhood associations, the Black church community, the informal food sharing that already operates there. The Northwest or North End store likely draws from the working-class trades community, the union tradition, the people who know how to fix things. Both are Resolute Societies operating under the same governance. Neither is subordinate to the other.

## How It Works

**The credit economy.** The store prices its goods in credits. Members earn credits through labor — deliveries, repairs, teaching courses, community health work — and through the monthly allowance that flows from the treasury to every member in good standing. Credits spent at the store return to the treasury and are available for future allowances and treasury payments for community work.

**Capital calls.** Stocking the store requires dollars. When the Society needs to fund a bulk order — from Restaurant Depot in Youngstown, from a local farm, from a food service wholesaler — it opens a capital call: a specific, governance-authorized, time-limited window in which members may contribute dollars in exchange for credits disbursed from the treasury. The call closes when the declared amount is raised or the deadline passes. This is how the store gets stocked without requiring anyone to personally advance money on behalf of the community.

**SNAP.** Most food items the store carries are SNAP-eligible. Members using SNAP contribute their benefits toward the buying pool, paying their share with their EBT card. The Society coordinates the order; individual members pay their portion. This is legal and keeps federal food benefits circulating within the community rather than at Walmart.

**Delivery.** Members who cannot come to the store — the elderly, the disabled, those without transportation — receive deliveries from the store. The member doing the delivery earns credits. The delivery cost is paid in credits by the receiving member or, for members without sufficient credits, covered by the treasury as a basic care obligation. The store's reach is not limited to people who can get there.

## What Grows From It

The store is the founding institution, not the final one. What it produces — beyond cheaper goods — is a membership that knows each other, a ledger with a history of real transactions, a physical space that belongs to the community, and a demonstrated track record that membership is worth something tangible.

From that base, everything else becomes possible: courses that run in the space, a Community Health Worker program that serves members and their families, a housing mutual aid network that puts the trades knowledge in the community to work, gardens on vacant lots that stock the store with local produce, eventually a kitchen that prepares meals for members who cannot cook for themselves.

None of that requires explaining the telos at the first meeting. It requires showing up, running a store that works, and letting the community form around something real.
