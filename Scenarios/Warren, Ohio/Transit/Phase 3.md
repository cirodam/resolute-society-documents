# Transit — Phase 3: Vehicle Sourcing and Society Fleet

## What Changes

Phases 1 and 2 extended the life of vehicles members already have and organized rides among members who have functioning cars. Phase 3 addresses the member who needs a vehicle and doesn't have one — and builds the Society's own vehicle capacity to serve its cross-lane operations.

---

## Vehicle Sourcing for Members

Warren's used vehicle market reflects its poverty profile: cheap cars, often with deferred maintenance histories, sold by private sellers and small lots to buyers who need transportation and can't afford to be selective. A member who needs a car and has limited dollars often ends up with the vehicle most likely to become next month's crisis.

The Society's transit mechanics, by Phase 3, have the knowledge and the shop to evaluate used vehicles before a member commits to buying one. A pre-purchase inspection by Society mechanics — compensated through the ledger — tells a member whether the $3,000 car is worth $3,000 or is already carrying $4,000 in deferred repairs. That inspection is worth real money: it prevents the purchase that becomes a crisis, and it makes the member a better buyer in a market where sellers have information advantages.

Beyond inspection, the Society may develop a more active role in vehicle sourcing. Police auctions, estate sales, and insurance salvage produce vehicles that are mechanically repairable but cosmetically damaged or non-running — exactly the kind of vehicle the Society's shop can bring back to service at a cost in credits rather than dollars. A car purchased for $500 at auction, repaired through the ledger, and made available to a member who needs transportation is a different outcome than a member taking out a high-interest auto loan on a vehicle from a buy-here-pay-here lot.

---

## The Society Vehicle

The food lane's delivery operation, the housing lane's repair crews, the healthcare lane's CHW visits, the transit lane's coordination work — all of these require vehicles. In Phase 1 and Phase 2, those vehicles are members' personal cars, compensated through the ledger for their use. Phase 3 is where the Society acquires a vehicle of its own.

A used cargo van or large SUV — maintained by the Society's own mechanics — serves the delivery operations, the moving and relocation help the housing lane provides, and the coordination function that gets members to appointments. It is available to any Society operation that needs it, scheduled through the ledger, maintained in the Society's shop.

The Society vehicle is not a perk. It is infrastructure. The same way the walk-in cooler is food infrastructure and the shop lift is mechanical infrastructure, a Society vehicle is transit infrastructure — a shared asset that makes every other lane's work easier and extends the Society's reach into parts of member life that individual member vehicles cannot reliably serve.

---

## Long-Term Mechanical Capacity

By Phase 3, the Society's mechanics have been doing this work for years. Some of them have developed capabilities that go beyond automotive repair into the broader mechanical competence that Warren's manufacturing history produced: welding, fabrication, small engine repair, equipment maintenance.

The manufacturing landscape document describes what remains in Warren — Delphi/Aptiv workers, Thomas Steel Strip, the incoming Kimberly-Clark and Graphite One operations. These are facilities that require maintenance workers with real mechanical skills. The Society's transit division, by developing and compensating mechanical skill through the ledger over years, produces members whose competence is visible and whose reliability is demonstrated. That matters for employment, for the Society's relationship with the industrial employers remaining in Warren, and for the community's long-term capacity to maintain its own infrastructure.

The mechanics division that started as oil changes in a driveway is, by Phase 3, a real institution: a shop, a vehicle, members whose skills have deepened through years of work, and a contribution to the community's mechanical knowledge that outlasts any individual member's tenure.
