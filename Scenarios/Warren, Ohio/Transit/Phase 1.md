# Transit — Phase 1: Vehicle Repair Through the Ledger

## What It Is

Phase 1 transit work is the same structure as Phase 1 housing repair: the mechanical knowledge that exists in the Society's membership becomes available to members through the credit economy. A member with a car that needs work spends credits. The member who does the work earns them. The repair that would have cost $400 at a shop happens in a driveway, paid in the community's currency.

This requires no dedicated space, no equipment beyond what members already own, and no organizational infrastructure beyond the ledger. It starts the moment the Society has a member who can work on cars and another member whose car needs working on.

---

## What the Membership Has

Warren's manufacturing history left the city with a deep concentration of mechanical knowledge. The machinist who spent twenty years at Packard Electric knows how engines work. The former Delphi worker understands automotive electrical systems. The shade-tree mechanic who has been fixing his own vehicles and his neighbors' for thirty years knows how to diagnose by sound and feel. This knowledge is underemployed. It is not being compensated. The ledger changes that.

The vehicle maintenance course in the education lane's Phase 2.1 teaches basic self-maintenance to members who want it. The transit lane's Phase 1 is something different: it is skilled mechanics doing real repair work for members who don't have the skill or the time to do it themselves, compensated through the credit economy for work that genuinely reduces household financial risk.

---

## What Gets Done

The prioritization follows the same logic as the store's inventory: what produces the most benefit, addressed first.

**Inspection readiness.** Ohio requires annual vehicle inspections. A car that fails inspection cannot be legally driven, which means a member loses access to their vehicle — and often their job — until it passes. Many failures are preventable: worn wiper blades, a burned-out tail light, brake pads below the minimum threshold, an exhaust issue. A pre-inspection check by a Society mechanic, paid in credits, catches these before the inspection rather than after.

**Routine maintenance that members defer.** Oil changes. Air filters. Tire rotation. The maintenance that costs $50–80 at a shop and gets skipped when cash is short, then costs $3,000 when the engine seizes. Doing this work through the ledger removes the cash barrier and keeps vehicles running.

**Brake jobs.** The single most safety-critical repair that falls within what a competent non-specialist can do with the right tools. Bad brakes are dangerous and common on aging vehicles with deferred maintenance.

**Basic diagnostics.** Reading a check engine light with an OBD scanner ($30 tool) tells you whether the warning is urgent or can wait. A member who doesn't know what the light means may keep driving a car that's about to fail catastrophically, or may stop driving a car that's fine. Diagnosis removes that uncertainty.

**Battery and electrical.** Battery replacement, alternator diagnosis, starter testing. These are the repairs that produce the stranded-car call — the member who can't get to work because the car won't start. Quick and manageable for a member who knows what they're doing.

---

## The Financial Logic

A brake job at a shop in Warren runs $200–350 per axle. Done through the ledger, it costs the receiving member credits that came from their allowance or their own labor contributions — not dollars they don't have. The member doing the repair earns credits that circulate back into the store or the kitchen or other Society services.

No dollars leave the community. The repair that would have triggered a cascading financial crisis instead happens within the community's economy. That is the ledger doing exactly what it is supposed to do.

---

## What Phase 1 Cannot Do

Phase 1 vehicle repair is constrained by what can be done without a lift, a proper shop, and professional-grade equipment. Work done in a driveway or parking area has real limits: engine or transmission work that requires the vehicle to be raised safely, repairs that require specialized tools, emissions-related work. Phase 1 catches the high-frequency, high-impact repairs that keep vehicles running day to day. Phase 2's dedicated shop handles what Phase 1 cannot.
