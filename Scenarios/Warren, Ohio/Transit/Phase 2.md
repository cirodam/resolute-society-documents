# Transit — Phase 2: Transportation Coordination and a Dedicated Shop

## What Changes

Phase 2 addresses two things in parallel: the member who has a car but needs more serious mechanical work than a driveway can handle, and the member who doesn't have a functioning car at all. A dedicated shop space expands what the mechanics can do. A transportation coordination network gives members without reliable vehicles access to the mobility the Society's other operations assume.

---

## The Shop

A lift changes what repairs are possible. Work that requires raising the vehicle safely — undercarriage inspection, exhaust repair, suspension work, transmission access — cannot be done without one. A two-post lift installed in a bay the Society controls, combined with basic shop equipment, takes the transit division from shade-tree mechanics to a functioning repair operation.

In Warren, a commercial bay is not expensive to rent or acquire. The Society identifies space — adjacent to one of the stores, in a commercial building the housing lane's Phase 3 might help acquire, or through a separate arrangement — and equips it with what the mechanics need to work safely and effectively.

The shop runs on the same ledger model as Phase 1. Members bring their vehicles. Society mechanics do the work. Credits move. What changes is the range of work the division can handle: engine work, transmission diagnosis, full brake system rebuilds, suspension repair, serious electrical troubleshooting. The financial protection this provides is larger — these are the $1,000–3,000 repairs that, done through the ledger, don't happen in dollars at all.

The shop also becomes the space where the vehicle maintenance education that began in Phase 2.1 of the education lane gets real equipment behind it. Teaching someone to do a brake job is better done in a bay with a lift than in a driveway on jack stands.

## Transportation Coordination

The member without a functioning vehicle faces a different problem than the member whose vehicle needs repair. Coordination is what serves her.

**Ride-sharing among members.** Members traveling similar routes to similar destinations — shifts at Mercy Health, appointments at the Medicaid office, the grocery run to the east side — can coordinate. The ledger creates the mechanism: the member providing the ride earns credits, the member receiving it spends them. This is not a formal dispatch system. It is organized ride-sharing within a community that already knows each other, made compensated and reliable through the ledger.

**Medical and appointment transportation.** Getting to a medical appointment without a car in Warren requires either a family member with a car and flexibility, a taxi or rideshare that costs $20–40 each way, or a transit route that may not reach the destination. Many members miss appointments for this reason, which compounds the healthcare problems the healthcare lane is working to address. A Society member who can provide a ride to a medical appointment, compensated through the ledger, closes a gap the healthcare system cannot close on its own.

**Cross-lane coordination.** The food lane's delivery operation already requires vehicles. The housing lane's repair crews move between member households. The healthcare lane's check-in visits and CHW work require getting to members. The transit lane's coordination infrastructure serves all of these — the same network that gets a member to a medical appointment is the network that coordinates the delivery route and the housing repair crew.

## Insurance and Registration Assistance

Maintaining legal vehicle operation in Ohio requires insurance and current registration. Both are real cost burdens for low-income households — insurance premiums in particular can run $150–200/month for a member with a modest vehicle and a thin credit history.

The Society does not pay members' insurance bills. What it does is provide the systems navigation knowledge — what coverage is legally required versus what dealers push, what programs exist for low-income drivers, how to shop coverage effectively — that helps members carry the minimum they need without overpaying. This is the same systems navigation logic as HEAP enrollment: knowledge that is worth money to members who don't currently have it.

---

## What Coordination Does for the Society's Reach

Transportation coordination is one answer to the time-constrained member problem. The single mother who can't get to the store has the store come to her through delivery. But she also can't get to a medical appointment at 2pm on a Tuesday because she has no car and can't afford an Uber. The coordination network means another member — someone whose schedule has a window, who lives nearby, who earns credits for the trip — can take her.

This is the Society functioning as an institution that reduces the practical cost of being poor in a car-dependent city. Not by solving the transit infrastructure problem — that is not within reach — but by organizing the resources the community already has into a network that members can rely on.
