# Agriculture — Phase 1: Community Member Support

## What It Is

Phase 1 agriculture starts with the members themselves. Before the Society has any relationship with the farms surrounding Warren, before it has access to vacant lots at scale, it supports members who want to grow food — in their backyards, on their porches in containers, in whatever patch of ground they have or can access. The barrier to entry is as low as a five-gallon bucket on a porch and a packet of seeds from the store.

This is not a farming program. It is the Society treating food production as a capacity worth developing in its members from the beginning, at whatever scale those members can manage, and building the collective knowledge base that makes larger agricultural work possible later.

---

## Seeds and Starting Supplies

The community store carries seeds and basic growing supplies from Phase 1. This is the Society's first agricultural intervention: making it possible for members to grow something without having to go to a garden center they may not have transportation to reach, at a price point that doesn't require a capital outlay.

Seeds available through the store should prioritize high-yield, easy-to-grow varieties well-suited to Ohio's climate and Trumbull County's conditions: tomatoes, cucumbers, zucchini, green beans, kale, collards, Swiss chard, lettuce, radishes, herbs. These are things that produce meaningfully in a small space with modest effort from a beginning grower.

Starting supplies — seed starting mix, basic hand tools, small pots for starting seeds indoors before the last frost — give members what they need to begin before they have access to outdoor space.

---

## Garden Knowledge

The knowledge of how to grow food in Trumbull County specifically — what varieties perform, when to start seeds indoors, when the last frost is reliable, what the soil needs, what pests to watch for — exists in the Society's membership or close to it. The gardening course in the education lane's Phase 2.1 is the formal transmission mechanism. But garden knowledge also moves through the tutor program, through members working plots side by side, through the elder who has been growing tomatoes in this specific soil for thirty years showing someone how she does it.

Members who know how to grow things in this place earn credits for teaching what they know. The Society accumulates that knowledge and makes it available — not as a manual, but through the relationships in which it actually travels.

---

## Community Garden Plots

Warren has roughly 400 vacant properties, most of them cleared lots from demolished structures. Phase 1 is where the Society begins accessing some of this land for community garden use — through informal arrangements with property owners, through the city's vacant land programs, through relationships with the Trumbull Neighborhood Partnership.

A community garden plot doesn't require a long-term land agreement or significant capital. It requires cleared land, basic soil preparation, water access, and members willing to tend it. Members who maintain plots earn credits for their labor. The produce flows to the community store or kitchen.

Plots are distributed across both store neighborhoods rather than concentrated in one location. A garden associated with the southeast side store is tended primarily by members from that community; same on the north end. The work and the harvest are local to the neighborhood where both happen.

## The Seed Library

Seed saving — selecting seeds from open-pollinated varieties, drying them, storing them — begins in Phase 1 as a small practice and grows into an institution over time.

Members who save seeds from their best plants contribute to a Society seed library: an expanding collection of varieties that have been grown in Trumbull County by Society members, selected over multiple seasons for what performs here. A seed library is not a large infrastructure. It is envelopes in a box, properly labeled, stored cool and dry. But it represents the beginning of the Society's independence from the commercial seed supply and the beginning of a body of knowledge about what grows in this specific place.

---

## What Phase 1 Builds

By the end of Phase 1, some portion of the Society's members are growing food — in backyards, on porches, on community garden plots. The Society has a seed library with varieties it knows. The collective knowledge of how to grow in Trumbull County is circulating through the membership. And the members who have been growing for a season or two are the people who will be capable of the larger agricultural work Phase 2 opens up.

Phase 1 agriculture is not food sovereignty. It is the Society treating food production as a normal thing that members do — like cooking from scratch, like fixing their own houses — rather than something that requires a special program or a farm to exist first.
