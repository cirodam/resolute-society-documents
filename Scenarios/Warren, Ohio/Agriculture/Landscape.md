# Warren, Ohio — Agriculture Landscape

## The Land

Trumbull County is not an agricultural county in the way the flat corn-and-soy counties of western Ohio are. It sits at the edge of the industrial Mahoning River valley, where urban deindustrialization meets a rural hinterland that has been quietly farming without anyone in the city paying much attention to it.

The geography splits roughly along the river valley. Warren, Niles, and the adjacent industrial corridor occupy the lowland. As you move north, east, and west out of the valley, the land opens into gently rolling glaciated terrain — till plains and lake plain soils deposited by the retreating Wisconsin ice sheet. These are silt loams and clay loams, heavy and well-structured, with reasonable drainage where the land has been worked. They are not the deep black bottomland of central Illinois, but they are genuinely productive agricultural soils with over a century of cultivation behind them.

The county has roughly 1,000–1,200 farms on approximately 100,000–130,000 acres of agricultural land — a significant portion of the county's total area. Most of this land is within 20–30 miles of Warren. The farms that surround the city are not a distant abstraction. They are present, accessible, and largely invisible to the people living inside the city limits.

Ohio's northeast sits in growing zone 5b/6a, with some Lake Erie influence moderating temperature extremes. The frost-free growing season runs roughly late April through October. Adequate rainfall — 38–40 inches annually — means irrigation is not generally required for field crops or vegetable production. What the region lacks is not growing capacity. It lacks the relationships and institutions to connect what grows here to the people who live here.

## What Gets Grown

Field corn and soybeans dominate, as they do across most of non-Appalachian Ohio. These are commodity crops grown at industrial scale and sold to grain elevators along the county's rural roads, where they enter a commodity chain that leads to ethanol plants, hog confinements, and export terminals — not to the households of Warren. The corn and soybeans grown in Trumbull County do not become food in Trumbull County.

**Hay and forage** occupy a significant portion of county farmland, primarily in support of cattle and dairy operations. Trumbull County has some remaining dairy farms and beef cattle operations, though both have declined over the past thirty years as the economics of small-scale livestock have deteriorated.

**Vegetable production** exists but is scattered and small. There are market gardeners operating in the county — farms producing tomatoes, sweet corn, peppers, beans, cucumbers, squash, melons, and greens for direct sale. These are not large operations. Many are family farms of 5–50 acres growing vegetables as a primary or supplementary enterprise. Their distribution channels are farmers markets, roadside stands, restaurant relationships, and small CSA programs. They are the farms the Society has the most immediate reason to know.

**Orchards and small fruit** appear in scattered locations — apple orchards, berry patches, the kind of perennial production that requires years of establishment and benefits from patient long-term management.

**Specialty production** of various kinds exists in a county of this agricultural diversity: eggs, honey, herbs, flowers, specialty livestock. These operations are small, often run by people who farm as a secondary livelihood or who have moved toward specialty markets because commodity prices don't pencil out for small acreages.

## Who Farms

The most important demographic fact about Trumbull County's farmers is their age. Ohio's average farm operator is in his late 50s. Trumbull County likely matches or exceeds that average. What this means in practice is that a significant portion of the county's farmland is being worked by people who will not be farming in fifteen years — and a substantial portion of that land has no identified successor.

The farming demographic in this county is overwhelmingly white, predominantly male, and reflects a generation that came into farming in the 1970s–1980s when land was cheaper and commodity prices supported more operations. Their children, for the most part, did not stay in farming — they left for the same credential economy that everyone else's children moved toward. The farm is often the parents' home and primary asset but not the next generation's livelihood.

These are not wealthy people. The farm may be paid off and the land may be worth something, but farm income from a 200-acre operation in a soft commodity market is modest. The people farming Trumbull County land are working-class in every practical sense — they have equipment debt, variable income tied to weather and prices they don't control, and no particular expectation that the next generation will carry on what they built.

## How Farms Sell

**Commodity farmers** — the corn and soybean growers — sell to grain elevators. The crop leaves. They have no relationship with the people who ultimately eat what their land produces, because that relationship is mediated by an industrial food chain that doesn't go anywhere near Warren's neighborhoods.

**Market gardeners and small producers** sell through:
- Farmers markets (the nearest significant market is in Cortland or the broader Youngstown area; Warren does not have a robust weekly market)
- Roadside stands
- Restaurant accounts with regional buyers
- Small CSAs with subscriber bases in the area's professional-class households

The latter distribution pattern is significant. The small vegetable farmers in Trumbull County who sell directly are already selling direct — they know how to do it. But their buyers are predominantly the higher-income households in the county, not the working-class neighborhoods of Warren's interior. The price point of a CSA subscription is accessible to someone in the professional class; it is not accessible to a household on SNAP and a $37,000 median income.

**Surplus and gleaning.** After harvest, field produce remains. Tomatoes left on the vine after the primary harvest. Sweet corn past the best eating date. Potatoes and squash left in the ground because the labor cost of mechanical collection exceeds the commodity value. Seconds and culls from vegetable operations. This surplus is the closest thing to a functioning connection between Trumbull County farms and Warren households — and it is currently almost entirely uncaptured.

## The City-Farm Disconnect

Warren sits in the middle of agricultural land it has almost no institutional relationship with. The food that comes into the city comes from Walmart's supply chain, which sources nationally and internationally. The federal food benefits spent by Warren's households flow to national corporate retailers. The farms twenty miles from the city could, in principle, supply a significant portion of the fresh food Warren's residents eat — and functionally, they do not supply any of it.

This disconnect is not primarily geographic. The distance is not the problem. It is relational and institutional: there is no market structure, no purchasing relationship, no ongoing connection between what the farms produce and what the city needs. The commodity system connects Trumbull County's corn to a grain elevator in Iowa. Nothing connects its tomatoes to the people on Tod Avenue.

The farmers who might sell to Warren's households are not opposed to it. They are simply not organized toward it, and there is no institution on the city side that creates the purchasing relationship, handles the logistics, or provides the volume and consistency that makes direct sales viable for a farm operation.

## What This Means for the Society

The Society's agriculture lane begins with this gap and asks how it closes.

The immediate answer is gleaning — the entry point already visible in the food lane's Phase 1 work. Gleaning does not require a purchasing relationship, a formal agreement, or money. It requires showing up, with enough people, at the right time, with containers and transportation. It is the lowest-friction way to begin building a presence on Trumbull County farms.

But gleaning is a starting point, not an end state. The farms around Warren have something the Society needs — production capacity, land, soil knowledge accumulated over generations — and the Society has something some of those farms need: a committed buyer, a reliable relationship, and potentially a long-term successor for land that has no identified heir.

The agricultural lane is the slowest lane. The food production arc runs from gleaning to direct purchasing to leased land to potentially owned land — a trajectory measured in years and decades, not months. But it is also the lane with the most profound long-term stakes. A Society that, fifteen years in, is growing meaningful quantities of its own food on Trumbull County land it works and possibly owns has achieved something that cannot be taken away, that does not depend on supply chains or corporate retailers, and that produces knowledge and competence that belongs permanently to the community.

The land is there. The relationships don't exist yet. Building them is the work.
