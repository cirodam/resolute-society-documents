# Agriculture — Phase 2: Local Farm Support

## What Changes

Phase 1 agriculture was internal — seeds, plots, member gardens, the seed library. Phase 2 is the Society turning outward toward the farms that surround Warren and asking what the relationship between the community and that land can be.

The agricultural landscape document describes what exists: Trumbull County farms within 20–30 miles of Warren, dominated by commodity corn and soy, with a smaller layer of vegetable operations and aging farmer demographics facing succession problems. The city and the farms have almost no institutional relationship. Phase 2 is where that starts to change.

---

## Formalizing the Gleaning Relationship

The gleaning that began in the food lane's Phase 1.1 — showing up at farms after harvest, building initial relationships with farmers willing to let the Society glean — becomes something more deliberate in Phase 2.

The Society maintains an active map of Trumbull County farms: what they grow, when they harvest, which farmers have welcomed gleaners, what the contact relationship looks like. Gleaning is scheduled around the agricultural calendar rather than improvised. Members who coordinate gleaning operations are compensated through the ledger. A farmer who let the Society glean once becomes a farmer the Society calls in July to ask about the tomato harvest and whether they'll need help again.

Reliability is the asset being built. A farmer who can count on the Society to show up when they say, take what they said they'd take, and leave the field as they found it, will call the Society before he calls anyone else when he has surplus. That accumulated trust is what opens the doors Phase 2 needs to open.

---

## Direct Purchasing Arrangements

Some farmers in Trumbull County sell directly and already know how to do it. They sell at farmers markets, to restaurants, through small CSA programs. What they don't have is a reliable institutional buyer in Warren's inner neighborhoods — because no institution in Warren's inner neighborhoods has been buying from them.

The Society becomes that buyer. A direct purchasing arrangement might begin simply: the Society commits to buying a specific quantity of a specific crop at an agreed price before the season begins. The farm has a guaranteed buyer it can plan around. The Society has a committed supply at below-retail prices. Payment is in dollars from the treasury or a capital call.

These arrangements start small — a hundred pounds of tomatoes, a case of winter squash — and grow as the Society's kitchen and processing operations give it the capacity to absorb larger quantities. By Phase 2, the kitchen is running and the processing operation is building, which means the Society can take produce in volumes a household cannot use and turn it into stored goods. That capacity makes the Society a more valuable purchasing partner to a farm than a restaurant or individual CSA subscriber.

---

## Supporting Farmers Directly

The farms around Warren are largely operated by people in their 50s and 60s with limited succession plans. Many of them face practical challenges the Society is positioned to help with.

**Labor.** Harvest labor is a genuine constraint for small farms. Members who are available for day labor during harvest seasons can fill a gap that's become harder to fill as farm populations have aged and rural communities have thinned. The ledger pays Society members for farm labor in credits. The farmer pays the Society in produce or dollars. Both get something they need.

**Knowledge preservation.** The farmer who has been growing vegetables in Trumbull County soil for forty years knows things about this specific land that no extension publication captures — which fields drain well, what pest pressure looks like in a wet spring, which varieties outperform their catalog descriptions in this microclimate. That knowledge is at risk when a farmer retires with no successor. The Society creates occasions for that knowledge to move — through the education lane's knowledge transmission work, through members who work alongside farmers and absorb what they observe.

---

## The Succession Conversation

The most significant long-term opportunity in Phase 2 is one that has to be approached with patience and honesty: the farm succession conversation.

Some portion of Trumbull County's aging farmers will reach retirement without an identified heir interested in taking over their operation. The land doesn't disappear — it gets sold, often to larger commodity operators who absorb it into a bigger acreage. The market garden that fed the Youngstown area CSA subscribers gets converted to corn. The institutional knowledge dies.

The Society is not in a position to acquire farmland in Phase 2. What it is in a position to do is be present in the farming community long enough, and trustworthy enough, that when a farmer starts thinking about what happens to her land when she's done, the Society is part of the conversation. That positioning takes years of showing up — as gleaners, as buyers, as harvest labor, as people who take the farm seriously and treat the farmer with respect.

The conversation that belongs to Phase 3 of the agriculture lane — about leased land, about acquisition, about the Society working land under its own direction — begins as a relationship in Phase 2.

---

## What Phase 2 Builds

By the end of Phase 2, the Society has a real presence in the agricultural landscape surrounding Warren. Farmers know who it is and what it does. Several direct purchasing arrangements are in place, supplying the kitchen and processing operation. A small number of deeper relationships exist with farmers who are beginning to think of the Society as a genuine long-term partner.

The city that had no relationship with its surrounding farmland has an institution that does. That is not a small thing, and it did not require a farm to produce.
