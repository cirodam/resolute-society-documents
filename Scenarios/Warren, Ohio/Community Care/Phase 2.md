# Community Care — Phase 2: Coordination and Systems Navigation

## What Changes

Phase 1 community care was relational — checking on members, running errands, connecting needs to lanes. Phase 2 organizes that work and adds the systems knowledge that gets members with disabilities connected to formal support they're entitled to but may not be receiving.

Warren's disabled residents are often underenrolled in the programs designed to serve them. The PASSPORT program exists and covers real services; many eligible residents are not on it. SSI and SSDI are federal disability income programs that eligible residents sometimes don't claim — the application process is long, denial rates are high on first application, and navigating an appeal without help is difficult. Ohio's disability services system has entry points that require knowing where to look and how to persist. The community care lane's Phase 2 is where the Society develops the knowledge to navigate this on behalf of its members.

---

## Benefits Navigation for Disabled Members

**SSI and SSDI.** Supplemental Security Income and Social Security Disability Insurance are federal income programs for people with qualifying disabilities. In a city with Warren's poverty and disability profile, a substantial number of residents may qualify who are not currently enrolled — either because they don't know they qualify, because a prior application was denied and they didn't appeal, or because the application process is daunting enough that they gave up. A Society member with the knowledge to walk a disabled member through the application process, or to help them appeal a denial, earns credits for that work and potentially changes that member's financial situation permanently.

**PASSPORT and Medicaid waiver enrollment.** Ohio's home care waiver program provides significant services — personal care assistance, homemaker help, adult day services — for members who qualify. The enrollment process requires a functional assessment and income verification. A member who knows the process, knows the contact at the Area Agency on Aging for Trumbull County, and can sit with a disabled member through the application is providing something worth real money in real services.

**Ohio's disability services system.** County Boards of Developmental Disabilities, county mental health and addiction services, vocational rehabilitation for members with disabilities who want to work — these systems exist and serve real people and are navigated with difficulty by the people who need them most. The community care lane develops members who know how to move through these systems on behalf of the members they serve.

## Integration with Other Lanes

By Phase 2, the community care lane is functioning as the coordinator across all lanes for members with significant needs. The disabled member's situation is not a community care problem alone — it involves housing (triage and accessible modifications), food (store and kitchen delivery), transit (medical appointments, coordination), and healthcare (monitoring, CHW visits). Community care is the lane that knows the member's full situation and ensures the other lanes are actually serving her.

This requires a care coordinator function — a member who holds the picture of several members' needs, maintains the relationships with them, and routes tasks to the appropriate lanes. This is skilled work. It earns credits commensurate with that skill. Over time, it is the work from which the Society's Community Health Workers emerge — the Phase 3 of the healthcare lane begins with the members who have been doing Phase 2 community care.

## Respite for Family Caregivers

The most common care arrangement for disabled people in Warren is a family member — often a daughter or granddaughter — doing it alone, without compensation, without breaks, often while also working. These caregivers are at serious risk of burnout, and when they burn out, the person they're caring for is in crisis.

The Society provides respite: a member who sits with the disabled person for a few hours so the caregiver can leave the house, go to the store, sleep, do anything other than care. Compensated through the ledger. Scheduled reliably rather than depending on whoever can be asked this week.

Respite is one of the highest-value services the Society can provide to this population precisely because it is the thing that formal systems provide worst. Medicaid waiver programs have waitlists. The church can organize occasional help. What the Society offers is reliable, scheduled, credited care that the caregiver can count on — which is what makes it possible for a family caregiver to continue.

## Accessible Modifications

As the housing lane develops its repair capacity, the community care lane begins identifying housing modifications that make disabled members' homes safer and more functional: grab bars in bathrooms, ramps where there are steps, lever handles instead of doorknobs, better lighting in hallways. These are not major construction projects — most are within the competence of a careful member with basic carpentry skills, paid in credits.

The triage that the housing lane performs on every member's home is the basis. The community care lane flags accessibility needs in that triage. The housing lane addresses them. The coordination between the two lanes is what turns the triage into a comprehensive picture of what a disabled member's home needs to actually serve them.
