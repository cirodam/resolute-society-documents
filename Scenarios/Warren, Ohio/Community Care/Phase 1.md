# Community Care — Phase 1: Identification and Basic Assistance

## Why This Comes Early

Community care doesn't wait for infrastructure. It doesn't require a store or a kitchen or a shop. It requires members who know each other and a ledger that compensates the work of caring. Phase 1 begins the moment the Society has enough members to know that some of them need more than the buying club can provide on its own.

The founding question for the community care lane is simple: who among our members cannot fully care for themselves, and what do they need? Answering it is the work of Phase 1.

---

## Identification

The Society cannot help members it doesn't know about. Identification is the first task — not through a formal intake process, not by requiring members to announce their disability or need, but through the relationships that form in the course of normal Society life.

The member who comes to the store but seems to be struggling. The member whose house the housing team triages and finds in worse condition than expected. The member who receives the allowance but has never appeared at a community event. The member whose name comes up in conversation because a neighbor is worried about them. These are the signals.

The community care lane maintains a simple, confidential record of members with identified needs — not a medical record, not a case file, but a community record: who needs checking on, how often, what specific assistance they've indicated they need or that others have observed they need. This record is held with care and shared only with members doing care work directly.

Identification also extends outward — to people who are not yet members but who are known to members. The elderly neighbor who lives alone and seems to be declining. The family on the block where things seem hard. The Society does not require membership before caring about someone. The community care lane may be how some people first encounter the Society — not through the store but through a knock on the door.

---

## Basic Assistance

What disabled and elderly members in Warren most immediately need is practical help with the tasks of daily life that their disability makes difficult or impossible. This is not complex. It is the kind of help a good neighbor provides — organized, compensated, and reliable rather than sporadic and dependent on whoever happens to be available.

**Household tasks.** Cleaning, laundry, dishes — the maintenance of a home that accumulates into a crisis when a person cannot manage it. A member who earns credits for spending two hours helping an elderly woman with her laundry is doing community care work. The ledger makes that sustainable rather than an act of charity that depends on the goodwill of whoever is asked.

**Errand running.** Picking up prescriptions. Getting to the post office. Banking tasks. The small errands that healthy adults take for granted and that become significant barriers when mobility is limited or there's no transportation. The transit lane's coordination network handles larger trips; community care handles the daily errands.

**Personal check-ins.** Showing up. Sitting with someone. Noticing how they seem compared to last week. This is the healthcare lane's Phase 1 function expressed as a community care practice: the regular visit that confirms a member is managing, that surfaces problems before they become crises, and that provides the human contact that isolated members often lack entirely.

**Connection to other lanes.** The community care member doing a check-in is the same person who places the delivery order with the food lane, flags a housing issue for the housing team, and calls the transit coordinator for a ride to a medical appointment. The community care lane is the connective tissue that routes each member's needs to the lane capable of meeting them.

---

## The Allowance Connection

Members who cannot reasonably contribute labor receive the allowance. For many members with significant disabilities, the allowance is their primary credit income — the community honoring its obligation to members who are not in a position to work. The community care lane ensures that the allowance is actually reaching those members, that they know how to spend it, and that the Society's services (store delivery, kitchen delivery, transit coordination) are accessible to them in practice and not just in principle.

A member with a severe physical disability who receives the allowance but cannot navigate the store ordering process, cannot use transit coordination because no one has told her about it, and has never received a kitchen delivery because no one identified her as needing it — that member is a member in name only. Community care is how the Society closes the gap between what it offers and what its most vulnerable members actually receive.

## Children in Community Life

The Society does not run a daycare. What it does is create a community where children are simply present — at the store, at community meals, at the Saturday gleaning party — and where their presence is normal rather than a problem to be managed.

Warren has a large single-mother population carrying the full weight of both earning and caregiving. The thing that locks her out of full participation in the Society is not complexity or unwillingness. It is that her children are always with her and most institutions are not built to accommodate that. The Society is.

The specific practice: members who are good with children and available during Society activities earn credits for the informal attention they give to kids whose parents are occupied. Not a drop-off facility. Not a scheduled program. Just an adult who knows the kids by name keeping half an eye on them while their mother is in the General Assembly or the cooking course or a one-on-one conversation with the housing triage member. This is what neighbors do. The ledger makes it reliable rather than dependent on whoever happens to be around.

Children who grow up in Society space — known by adults outside their immediate family, present while real work gets done, included rather than sidelined — are receiving something the scenario cannot manufacture through programming. A child who knows the elderly woman whose groceries she helps carry, who watches the mechanic explain an engine to her mother, who sits at the table while adults argue honestly about a governance decision, is being formed by community life in a way no curriculum reaches.

The community care lane holds this practice. No licensing. No ratios. No intake forms. Just a community that includes its children and compensates the adults who make that inclusion reliable.

---

## What This Requires

Members willing to do care work — the check-in visits, the errand running, the household help. Compensation through the ledger for their time. A simple, maintained record of who needs what. A coordinator who ensures coverage and catches members whose needs aren't being met.

No capital. No building. No professional credentials. The community care lane starts with two things the Society already has: members who care about each other and a ledger that can compensate the work of caring.
