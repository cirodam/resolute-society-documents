# Warren, Ohio — Who Lives Here

Warren is a city built for 73,000 people now holding 38,912. The people here are the ones who didn't leave, or couldn't, or actively chose not to. Understanding who they are is the prerequisite for understanding what a Society in Warren would actually be, rather than what one might imagine it to be from a distance.

---

## The Numbers

**Population:** 38,912, down from a 1970 peak of ~63,500 and a mid-century peak near 73,000. The city has lost 8.6% of its population in the last decade alone — faster than the county or the state — and the trajectory has not reversed. This is a city that has been contracting for sixty years.

**Geography:** 16 square miles. Density of roughly 2,400 people per square mile — not sprawling, but not dense either. A city scaled for walking and neighborhoods, now hollowed enough that vacancy is visible on most blocks.

---

## Race and Ethnicity

- White (non-Hispanic): **62.7%** — approximately 24,400 people
- Black / African American: **27%** — approximately 10,500 people
- Two or more races: **4.7%**
- Hispanic: **3.4%** — approximately 1,300 people
- Asian: **0.6%**
- Foreign-born: **1.3%**

Warren is an almost entirely American-born city. The foreign-born share is a fraction of the national average. This is not a city of recent immigration. The white population descends largely from the European immigrant mill workers — Italian, Slovak, Polish, Eastern European — who arrived for the steel industry in the early twentieth century. The Black population largely descends from Great Migration families who came for the same mills, two and three generations ago, and stayed.

---

## The Economic Floor

- **Median household income: $37,887** — roughly half of Ohio's median ($71,389) and less than half the national figure
- **Per capita income: $23,094**
- **Poverty rate: 31.9–34.4%** — more than double the national rate of 12.5%, and increasing while county and state rates have declined
- **Unemployment: 6.6%** as of April 2026, versus Ohio's 3.1% — more than double the state rate
- **Cost of living index: 88.5** — slightly below the national average, which is the primary thing making any of this survivable

Poverty breaks down sharply by race. The Black poverty rate is **48.3%** — nearly half the Black population of Warren lives below the poverty line. White poverty is 24.8%, still nearly double the national rate. Hispanic poverty is 16.9%, suggesting either more recent economic integration or selection effects among those who came.

The most important single statistic in the dataset: **40.3% of Warren residents are on Medicaid** — the means-tested healthcare program for the very poor. Not Medicare. Medicaid. Four in ten residents qualify. More people in this city are on Medicaid than have employer-sponsored health insurance (25.5%). That figure tells you more about the economic floor of Warren than any other number.

---

## What People Do

Top employment sectors by number of workers:

- Retail trade: **15%** (2,002 people)
- Healthcare and social assistance: **14.6%** (1,953 people)
- Manufacturing: **13.8%** (1,846 people)
- Food services: **13.3%**

The city that once put 7,000–9,000 people into a single steel mill now employs its largest sector in retail. Manufacturing still exists — mostly automotive suppliers downstream of the broader regional economy — but nothing proportionate to the infrastructure and population that once depended on it. Healthcare employment is large in part because poverty generates healthcare need: hospitals, home health aides, social services. 74.6% of workers drive alone to work, average commute 23 minutes. Most people are not working within walking distance of where they live.

---

## Household Structure

- Total households: **16,871**, average 2.2 persons
- Married-couple households: **26.8%**
- Female-headed households, no spouse present: **40.9%**
- Non-family households (individuals alone, or with non-relatives): **48.4%**

The 40.9% figure for female-headed households means that a very large share of children in Warren are being raised by a single mother, in poverty, attending schools performing at roughly half the state average. The 48.4% non-family household figure means nearly half of Warren's households are a single person living alone, or people sharing space who are not family to one another. The telos names atomization as the defining crisis of this moment. In Warren, that diagnosis is legible in the census.

---

## Housing

- Total housing units: **19,012** for 16,871 households — significant vacancy even after the Trumbull Neighborhood Partnership demolished approximately 1,200 vacant properties and renovated 600 over the last decade; roughly 400 vacant buildings remain
- Homeownership rate: **56.1%**
- Median home value: **~$90,000** — approximately one-quarter of the national median
- Median gross rent: **$805/month**

Racial disparity in homeownership is significant. White households account for nearly 80% of homeowners but only 60% of renters. Black and Hispanic households are 40% of renters but only 20% of owners. Cheap real estate is a genuine asset for an organization seeking physical space. The dispossessed renters in deteriorating housing stock are the population that asset could serve.

---

## Education

- High school diploma or higher: **85.2%**
- Bachelor's degree or higher: **15.7%** — the national average is 35.7%; Warren is less than half
- Warren City School District math proficiency: **29%** (state average: 55%)
- Warren City School District reading proficiency: **34%** (state average: 60%)
- Graduation rate: approximately 83–89%, improving from a lower baseline

The credentialed class has largely left. The people who remain are predominantly people without four-year degrees — which does not mean they lack knowledge or skill, but it does mean the credential economy has not served them and is not designed to. The school system produces young people who will either stay (underprepared by its own metrics) or leave (and largely not return). 28% of the school district's revenue comes from federal sources, which reflects a local tax base so eroded it cannot fund its own children's education.

---

## Veterans

**8.2% of adults — 2,493 people.** Working-class veterans, a significant portion from the post-9/11 era. Many on VA healthcare. People with real training, real discipline, real technical and organizational skills, and frequently real trauma. This is not a marginal population.

---

## Health

- Insured: **92.9%**
  - Medicaid: 40.3%
  - Employer coverage: 25.5%
  - Medicare: 14.7%
  - Non-group (private purchase): 10.2%
  - Uninsured: 7.1%

Ohio's opioid addiction rate is nearly twice the national average. Trumbull County and the Mahoning Valley were among the hardest-hit areas in the state during the overdose crisis. No Warren-specific overdose figure is readily available, but the regional pattern and poverty profile make clear that virtually everyone in this city knows someone directly affected. The drug crisis is not background noise here — it is woven into the fabric of families, and it shapes what care, mutual obligation, and accountability look like in practice.

## The Political Arc

Trumbull County voted Democratic in every presidential election from 1972 through 2012. Obama won the county by 22 points in 2012. Trump won it in 2016 — a 30-point swing, the first Republican presidential victory in the county in 44 years. Between 1990 and 2016, Trumbull County lost **62.5% of its manufacturing jobs**. The political shift is not mysterious; it is the arithmetic of deindustrialization and institutional abandonment made visible at the ballot. The county has continued moving Republican through 2024. The party that was the union party is no longer perceived as the working-class party by much of the working class here.

This matters for the Society not because the Society takes political positions, but because the political shift is a symptom of the deeper condition the Society is built to address: the collapse of the institutions — union halls, Democratic ward organizations, community anchors — that once structured working-class civic life. The anger that produced the political shift is real, legitimate in its origins, and currently unhoused. It is not the Society's to capture. But it is the Society's to understand.

## The Community Fabric That Remains

**Neighborhood associations:** North End, North West, Southeast Side (SESCA, meeting at White Hall Meeting House), Kenmore, Roosevelt Area, Historic Perkins Homestead, Community Concerned Citizens II. Small organizations, monthly meetings, volunteer leadership. They exist and represent real place attachment, but they are not robust institutions.

**Community development organizations:** Trumbull Neighborhood Partnership has done sustained physical work — blight demolition, housing renovation — and represents real institutional capacity. Warren Neighborhood Initiative, led by D'André Bowers, is a Black-led community and economic development organization working on neighborhood revitalization.

**Faith communities:** The Catholic Diocese of Youngstown maintains parishes in Warren. The Black church network — Second Baptist, New Freedom Missionary Baptist (which hosts the community PTA), and others — retains institutional weight that most secular civic institutions no longer have. The Black church has been the connective tissue of mutual aid and community organization in Warren in a way that the labor movement and political parties once were more broadly.

**Labor:** The Mahoning-Trumbull AFL-CIO Labor Council and USW Local 1375 still exist. They are institutions carrying a tradition whose economic base has largely disappeared, but they carry genuine organizational memory and a culture of collective action that persists even where the jobs do not.

## Who the People Are

The statistics above describe a city, but a Society is built with persons. Several distinct populations compose Warren's remaining residents, each bringing different things and facing different pressures.

**Working-class whites who stayed.** Descendants of the mill worker families. Many in their 40s–60s. Trades knowledge, mechanical competence, place attachment. Often homeowners by a thin margin. Increasingly Republican, not from ideology but from the felt experience of abandonment by the institutions that were supposed to represent them. Skeptical of every institution, including new ones. Hard to reach but potentially very loyal when trust is established.

**Black families — multi-generational.** Great Migration descendants, two and three generations in Warren. Facing the harshest economic conditions in the city: nearly half in poverty, disproportionately renting, disproportionately female-headed households. But also carrying the most intact mutual aid traditions. The churches, the neighborhood associations with Black women at the helm, the informal networks of care that have held families together through conditions that would have destroyed communities without them. This population has the most experience doing what a Society is supposed to do — and the least reason to trust a new institution led by people who don't share their circumstances.

**Single mothers.** A population that cuts across race. The 40.9% female-headed household figure is not abstract. These are women raising children, often multiple children, often alone, in poverty, on Medicaid, working retail or home health. They carry the heaviest practical burdens in the city and have the least slack for anything that demands time without immediate return. A Society that cannot demonstrate concrete value to this population within a short horizon will not retain them.

**Veterans.** Dispersed through the city. Trained, often skilled, often isolated. The VA is their healthcare institution and often their primary social anchor. Some are doing well; many are not. A Society offering genuine competence-building, genuine community, and genuine purpose would be among the few institutions outside the VA capable of reaching this population.

**Young people who stayed.** Perhaps the most consequential population for a founding. The ones who chose to stay — or who returned after leaving and coming back — are people who have some version of a case for this place. They are the potential founding energy. They are also scarce: the brain drain is real, and the people with degrees left.

**The elderly.** 18.5% of the population is over 65. Many of them the last living memory of what Warren was at its height — people who remember the mills, the union halls, the block associations, the neighbor who brought food when someone was sick. They carry knowledge that is not written down anywhere. They are also often isolated, increasingly dependent, and invisible to most of what passes for civic life.

---

## What This Means

Warren is not a city waiting to be saved. It is a city full of people who have been managing their own survival for decades with whatever they had. The Society does not arrive to rescue them. It arrives, if it arrives at all, as an offer: here is a structure for doing together what you have been doing in fragments, here is a frame for the knowledge and competence you already carry, here is a community that takes seriously what you already know about what community requires.

Whether that offer lands depends entirely on whether the Society is actually that — rather than another institution that comes into a distressed place with plans, extracts what it needs (legitimacy, labor, stories), and leaves things roughly as it found them.

That is the test. Warren has seen enough of the alternative to recognize it quickly.
