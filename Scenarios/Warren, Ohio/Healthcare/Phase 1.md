# Healthcare — Phase 1: Knowing Each Other

## What This Phase Does

Phase 1 healthcare work requires no credentials, no clinical training, and almost no capital. It requires members who are genuinely present in each other's lives — which is what the Society, through the store and the courses and the repair work, is building anyway. The healthcare contribution in Phase 1 is what happens when a community actually knows its members well enough to notice.

---

## Before Any Formal Training

The most immediate healthcare contribution the Society makes is not a program. It is people knowing each other well enough to notice.

The diabetic neighbor who lives alone — does someone check on her? The elderly member with hypertension who hasn't been at the store this week — does anyone know to call? The person in early recovery from opioid addiction — does someone know to show up on the hard days? The new mother who seems to be disappearing into herself — does anyone see it?

This is the baseline. A community in which members are genuinely known to each other is already doing health work that the healthcare system cannot do. The ER cannot check on the diabetic neighbor. The Medicaid managed care plan cannot notice that someone seems worse this month. The Society can, because its members are present in each other's lives in a way that institutions are not.

Organizing this attention intentionally — who is checking on whom, who knows what about each member's health situation, who to call if something seems wrong — takes what would happen informally in a healthy community and makes it reliable. It costs nothing. It requires no credentials. It begins the moment members know each other well enough to notice.

## Health Education

Understanding health is a practical skill, and it is one the Society can teach through the same course model as any other practical skill.

**Chronic disease literacy.** Diabetes affects a significant portion of Warren's population. Teaching members what blood sugar means, how to recognize hypoglycemia, how diet affects glucose levels, what to watch for in a diabetic family member or neighbor — this is immediately applicable knowledge that saves lives. The same applies to hypertension: what blood pressure numbers mean, what makes them go up, when a reading is dangerous. Heart disease risk factors. The chronic conditions most present in this community.

**Medication literacy.** What common medications do and how they work. How to take medications correctly — with food, at the same time each day, not skipping doses. What side effects to watch for. What happens when someone stops taking medication they need. How to read a prescription label. This matters particularly for elderly members managing multiple prescriptions and for family members helping them.

**Navigating the healthcare system.** What to bring to a doctor's appointment. How to describe symptoms clearly. What questions to ask. How to understand what a doctor has said. When something warrants an ER visit versus a call to a nurse line versus waiting for a scheduled appointment. How to use Medicaid effectively. These are navigational skills that significantly affect the quality of care members receive from the healthcare system that already exists.

**Mental health basics.** Recognizing depression, anxiety, and acute distress — in yourself and in people around you. Understanding that mental health is health, not weakness or failure. Knowing when to seek help and where. In a community shaped by decades of economic decline, poverty, and the opioid crisis, mental health literacy is not optional enrichment. It is essential.

**Recognizing emergencies.** Stroke symptoms. Heart attack symptoms. Overdose. Diabetic crisis. Severe hypertensive event. Knowing what you're looking at and when to call 911 without hesitation.

## Harm Reduction

Warren has lived through the opioid crisis at close range. Virtually every member will know someone who has been affected — a family member, a neighbor, a friend.

Naloxone (Narcan) reverses opioid overdoses. It is available without a prescription in Ohio. Training members to carry it and use it is one of the highest-impact health interventions the Society can make, requires minimal training, costs very little, and saves lives. A Society member who knows how to respond to an overdose and has naloxone on hand is a resource the community currently lacks in the inner neighborhoods.

Harm reduction more broadly — meeting people where they are rather than where they should be — is the right posture for a community navigating addiction, recovery, and relapse. The Society does not moralize about drug use. It keeps people alive and maintains relationships with people in active use so that when they are ready for support, the support is already there.

## Basic Monitoring Tools

A blood pressure cuff costs $30. A blood glucose monitor costs $20. A pulse oximeter is $10. A thermometer is $5.

These tools, held collectively by the Society or carried by members who know how to use them, create a basic capacity to monitor health without requiring a clinic visit for every check. The member who visits the elderly diabetic neighbor once a week can check her blood glucose and her blood pressure and know whether something has changed. That check is a meaningful health intervention. It is also an act of care — the kind of specific, reliable attentiveness to a real person that the telos names as one of the eight capacities a Society is built to develop.

Teaching members how to use these tools, what the numbers mean, and when a reading warrants escalation is a practical skills course. It earns credits for the teacher and creates a distributed monitoring capacity across the membership.

## The Connection to Other Work

The housing work, the food work, and the education work are all health interventions.

A member whose house is warm because the Society helped them enroll in HEAP and weatherize their drafts has fewer respiratory infections this winter. A member eating from the community store instead of Dollar General is getting better nutrition, which affects their diabetes, their blood pressure, and their cognitive function. A member who can read their medication label because of the literacy course takes their medication correctly. These are not separate programs. They are the same community doing its work across domains, and the health benefits accumulate.
