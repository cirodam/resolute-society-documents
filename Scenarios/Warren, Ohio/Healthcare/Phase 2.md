# Healthcare — Phase 2: Systematic Monitoring and Recovery Support

## What Changes

Phase 1 healthcare work was grounded in presence and attention — knowing members well enough to notice, and having basic tools to act on what was noticed. Phase 2 organizes that work. The informal checking-on-each-other becomes a deliberate monitoring network. Health education becomes a running curriculum rather than one-off courses. Recovery support becomes a sustained practice rather than individual acts of friendship.

---

## A Monitoring Network

By Phase 2, the Society has members who have been checking on neighbors and monitoring blood pressure and glucose for long enough to know what they're doing and who needs what. This informal practice now gets organized.

The Society maintains a simple, confidential registry of members managing chronic conditions — with member consent and understanding. Not a medical record. A community record: the diabetic members who benefit from weekly glucose checks, the hypertensive members who should have their blood pressure monitored, the members on oxygen who need someone to check that the supply is adequate. Who is checking on whom, and how often.

Members doing this monitoring earn credits. The work is real — it takes time, it requires skill, and it produces genuine health outcomes. A member with poorly controlled diabetes who gets a weekly glucose check and a conversation about what she ate this week is receiving a meaningful health intervention. That conversation is not a clinical encounter. It is a neighbor doing something real, paid in credits for doing it.

The monitoring network is not a substitute for clinical care. It is the structure that catches problems early enough to send someone to clinical care before the situation becomes a crisis — and that provides follow-through after the clinical encounter, making sure the discharge instructions were understood and the medications were filled.

## Health Education Curriculum

The health education courses that began in Phase 1 run consistently in Phase 2. They are part of the Society's course schedule, not one-time events.

**Chronic disease management** runs as an ongoing series: not a single introductory course but a curriculum members can return to, deepening their knowledge and updating it as their own health situations evolve. A member managing diabetes for two years needs different education than one newly diagnosed. The curriculum responds to what members actually need.

**Mental health first aid.** A structured training in recognizing and responding to mental health crises — panic attacks, acute depression, suicidal ideation, psychotic episodes. Mental Health First Aid is a real curriculum with trained facilitators. It is eight hours. It produces members who can respond usefully rather than helplessly when another member is in crisis. In a community shaped by poverty, loss, and the opioid epidemic, this training is not optional.

**Recovery and wellness.** A regular space for members in recovery — from opioids, alcohol, or other substances — to gather with people who know them. Not a formal treatment group. Not AA. A Society gathering that holds space for the reality that recovery is long, that relapse happens, that people in recovery need community and continuity, not judgment and distance. The members who facilitate this space earn credits. The members who participate are held by community rather than having to navigate recovery in isolation.

## Systematic Recovery Support

The opioid crisis has not receded from Trumbull County. Members of the Society include people in various stages of active use, early recovery, and long-term recovery. The Society's posture is consistent across all of them: membership is not contingent on sobriety, and presence is maintained through relapse and difficulty.

What changes in Phase 2 is that recovery support becomes organized. Specific members develop particular expertise — through experience, through training, through the CHW curriculum that is being prepared — in accompanying people through addiction and recovery. They are available. They are known. When a member's sibling is in crisis at 2 a.m., there is someone to call who will come.

Naloxone distribution, which began in Phase 1 as individual member training, is now widespread across the membership. The Society may establish a supply arrangement with a local harm reduction organization or pharmacy that keeps naloxone available at the store.

---

## Healthcare Navigation

Phase 2 produces members who are genuinely skilled at navigating the healthcare system on behalf of other members. Not as legal advocates or case managers — the Society is not a social services agency. But as people who have accompanied enough members to enough appointments to know how the system works, what questions to ask, and how to get a member from a problem they have described to care that actually addresses it.

This navigation work earns credits. A member who spends three hours helping another member get a Medicaid referral to a specialist, understands the process well enough to prevent the runaround, and follows up to make sure the appointment was kept has done something of real value. The ledger recognizes it.

The healthcare navigation work produces members who are, in practice, beginning to do Community Health Worker work without the formal training. They are the people from whom the Phase 3 CHW cohort will be selected.
