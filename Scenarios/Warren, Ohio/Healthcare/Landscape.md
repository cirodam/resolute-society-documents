# Warren, Ohio — Healthcare Landscape

## The Situation

40.3% of Warren's residents are on Medicaid. That single figure describes a healthcare landscape unlike what most policy discussions assume. This is not a population with employer-sponsored insurance and a primary care physician they see annually. It is a population navigating a system designed for people with more stability, more time, and more institutional fluency than most Warren residents have.

Chronic disease — diabetes, hypertension, heart disease — is endemic. These conditions are the predictable output of decades of economic stress, Dollar General diets, poor housing conditions, and healthcare access gaps that mean a condition that should be caught early becomes advanced before anyone addresses it. The relationship between poverty and chronic disease is not mysterious; it is causative.

The opioid crisis has run through Trumbull County families for over a decade. Ohio's opioid addiction rate is nearly twice the national average. Trumbull County and the Mahoning Valley were among the hardest-hit areas in the state during the overdose crisis. In Warren's neighborhoods, virtually everyone knows someone directly affected — a family member, a neighbor, a friend. The crisis is not background noise. It is woven into the fabric of families, and it shapes what care, mutual obligation, and accountability mean in practice.

7% of Warren's population is uninsured despite Ohio's Medicaid expansion. 25.5% have employer-sponsored insurance. 14.7% are on Medicare. The uninsured percentage is real people who fall through the coverage gaps — workers whose employers don't offer coverage, people who are between jobs, people who don't know they qualify for something. Among a 40,000-person city, 7% uninsured is close to 2,800 people.

## What the Healthcare System Provides

The regional healthcare infrastructure includes Mercy Health — St. Joseph Warren Hospital, the primary acute care facility in the city, and various specialty and primary care practices affiliated with the regional health systems. Federally Qualified Health Centers provide sliding-scale primary care for uninsured and Medicaid patients. The VA provides healthcare for Warren's 2,493 veterans.

What this system cannot provide: the sustained, consistent relationship between a person and a knowledgeable presence in their life that prevents chronic disease from advancing unchecked. A primary care appointment every six months — if the patient can get one and keep it — does not substitute for someone who checks on the diabetic neighbor every week, notices when her blood pressure seems high, asks about her medications, and sits with her through the appointment she needs to make.

The gap between feeling bad and getting appropriate care is wide in Warren. Navigating the Medicaid system, getting a referral, understanding a diagnosis, following through on treatment — these are tasks that require institutional fluency and personal advocacy that many members of this community do not have and cannot easily acquire alone.

---

## What Is Absent

- Any community institution that provides systematic health monitoring for people managing chronic disease outside of clinical settings
- Any organized harm reduction presence in the inner neighborhoods
- Any structure that helps people navigate the healthcare system from inside a relationship of trust

The healthcare system does what healthcare systems do. What it does not do — what it structurally cannot do — is know people. The Society can.
