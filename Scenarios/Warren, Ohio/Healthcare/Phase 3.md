# Healthcare — Phase 3: Community Health Workers

## What Changes

Phase 3 is where the informal healthcare work the Society has been doing since founding gets formally trained and institutionalized. The members who have been monitoring chronic disease, navigating the healthcare system, supporting recovery, and teaching health education for years are ready for the Community Health Worker curriculum. By the end of Phase 3, the Society has a functioning CHW program — not as a separate professional service, but as a formalized expression of what the community has already been doing.

---

## The CHW Program

The Society's Community Health Worker curriculum trains members to provide systematic health navigation for other members: helping them manage chronic conditions, connecting them to care, monitoring health status, providing education, and serving as the consistent presence in someone's health life that the healthcare system does not provide.

CHW training in Ohio follows a competency-based model. The curriculum covers health education methods, communication skills, service coordination, advocacy, community outreach, and the specific health conditions prevalent in the community being served. It requires real commitment from the members who undertake it — this is not a one-weekend course. It is a sustained training that produces genuine capacity.

The CHWs who emerge from Phase 3 training are not auxiliary healthcare providers. They are members who have developed particular expertise in health navigation and care coordination, compensated through the ledger for their time, and embedded in the community they serve in a way that no outside professional can be. They know the members. They have been present for years. The trust is already there.

---

## Who Becomes a CHW

CHW candidates in Warren are not selected from the general membership based on credentials or prior healthcare experience. They are identified through the work that has already happened in Phases 1 and 2: the members who showed up consistently for the monitoring visits, who became skilled at navigating the Medicaid system, who people trusted when they were in crisis, who demonstrated through practice that they are the ones who want to go deeper into health navigation and care.

This matters for Warren specifically. The formal healthcare system is credentialed and licensed in ways that exclude most of Warren's population from anything beyond frontline labor roles. The CHW program is explicitly designed to train people from within the communities they serve. It is one of the few healthcare pathways that doesn't require a college degree as an entry ticket. Warren's CHWs are Warren people — and that is precisely what makes the program work.

## What CHWs Do

**Individual chronic disease support.** Each CHW carries a manageable caseload of members managing chronic conditions. They visit, they check in, they monitor. They help members understand their conditions and their treatments. They accompany members to appointments when needed and follow up afterward.

**Care coordination.** When a member has a complex health situation involving multiple providers, the CHW helps connect the pieces. The primary care doctor and the specialist are not communicating well — the CHW helps. The discharge plan from the hospital assumes resources the member doesn't have — the CHW helps navigate to alternatives.

**Preventive education.** Not one-off health courses but sustained, personalized health education delivered in relationship. The CHW who knows a member well knows which health behaviors are most important to support and what that specific person responds to.

**Crisis support.** When a member is in acute mental health crisis, when someone overdoses, when a household is destabilized by a medical emergency — the CHW is available. Not as a clinician, but as a skilled, present community member who knows what to do and who to call.

**Relationship with health systems.** The CHW program is most powerful when it operates in relationship with the healthcare institutions that provide clinical care. Some health systems actively partner with community CHW programs, recognizing that the community-embedded work they cannot do is precisely what CHWs do. A relationship with Mercy Health's community health programs, or with a Federally Qualified Health Center in Trumbull County, turns the Society's CHWs into a bridge between the clinical system and the community it serves.

## What the Society Has Built

At the end of Phase 3, the Society's healthcare lane looks like this:

Members know each other well enough to notice when something is wrong. The monitoring network covers members with chronic disease. Naloxone is widely distributed and members know how to use it. Recovery support is organized and sustained. A cohort of trained CHWs provides systematic health navigation and care coordination for the membership.

None of this is a clinic. None of it replaces the healthcare system. What it is: the structure that closes the gap between feeling bad and getting care, that catches problems early rather than in crisis, and that treats health as a community responsibility rather than an individual burden.

In a city where 40% of residents are on Medicaid, where chronic disease runs through families, and where the opioid crisis has not finished its work, that structure is not peripheral. It is the difference between a community that survives its circumstances and one that is ground down by them.
