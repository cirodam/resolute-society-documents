# Gary, Indiana — The Economy

## Numbers

- **Population:** ~67,000
- **Median household income:** $36,153 — roughly half the Indiana median
- **Poverty rate:** 32% — more than double the national rate
- **Homeownership rate:** 49.1%
- **Median home value:** $94,700
- **To afford a two-bedroom apartment:** $50,160/year required; median income falls $14,000 short
- **Unemployment:** chronically above state and national averages

---

## What People Do

The three largest employment sectors in Gary are healthcare and social assistance (4,528 workers), manufacturing (2,776), and retail trade (2,747). The pattern is familiar from Warren and every other deindustrialized city in the Rust Belt: the city that once put tens of thousands of people into a single industry now employs its largest sector in healthcare — which is largely there because poverty and its consequences generate medical need. Retail is Dollar General and Walmart. Manufacturing is a shadow of what it was.

The wages these sectors pay do not close the gap. Healthcare employment in Gary skews toward home health aides, nursing assistants, and social service workers — essential work, chronically underpaid, disproportionately done by Black women. Retail pays near minimum wage. The factory jobs that remain pay better than service work but employ a fraction of what the mills once did.

---

## US Steel Gary Works: The Plant That Stays and Doesn't

The Gary Works is still there. It is the largest integrated steel mill in North America, visible from most of the city, still operating. It employed over 30,000 workers at its peak in the early 1970s. It employs 2,246 today. That number — 2,246 — is the whole story of Gary's economy in three digits. The plant that the city was built to serve, that shaped every neighborhood and institution and demographic pattern in the city, now employs fewer people than a mid-sized hospital.

The plant's continued presence is its own complexity. It is not a ruin. It is not gone. It sits on the lakefront, still producing, still polluting, still employing a small and shrinking workforce under contracts that have been fought over for decades. Gary residents have a complicated relationship with it: the mill took from this community for a century, and its absence would remove the last remnant of the industrial identity on which the city was built.

## The Housing Economy: Vacancy as the Defining Condition

Gary is the size of San Francisco. It holds 67,000 people.

An estimated one in four housing units in the city is vacant. In the 46402 ZIP code — the most severely affected — 34% of all homes stand empty, making it one of the most concentrated areas of housing vacancy in the United States. Entire blocks have been bulldozed. Others stand with windows broken, porches collapsed, trees growing through rooflines. The city's cleanup capacity cannot keep pace with the volume of blight complaints filed each year.

The inverse of this is that real estate in Gary is extraordinarily cheap. Median home value is $94,700. Habitable properties in functioning neighborhoods can be acquired for prices that would be impossible in any city with a tighter market. This is a genuine material asset for any organization seeking physical space — the Society could establish multiple hubs, acquire land for gardens and production, build a physical footprint that would require millions of dollars elsewhere and requires a fraction of that in Gary.

The risk is the condition of the stock. Cheap acquisition costs are offset by rehabilitation costs, environmental remediation, and the ongoing challenge of maintaining properties in a city whose basic infrastructure — water, sewer, roads — is itself under chronic strain.

## The Chicago Wage Shadow

Many Gary residents work in Chicago or in the Cook County suburbs. The commute is real — about 45 minutes by car, or longer by the South Shore Line, the commuter rail that connects Gary to downtown Chicago. The wages earned in Chicago largely circulate elsewhere: in gas, in suburban retail, in the municipalities where employers are located. What Gary's workforce produces in economic value accrues to Cook County and not to Gary.

This is not an accident. The metropolitan economic geography of the Chicago region was shaped by decades of deliberate exclusion — redlining, discriminatory lending, highway placement that isolated Gary from the regional economy rather than connecting it. Gary workers participate in the Chicago labor market from a position of structural disadvantage: they bear the commute costs, they have fewer employer options within the city itself, and the wages available locally are far below what Chicago offers for comparable work.

## The Informal Economy

No dataset captures the informal economy of Gary, but it is significant. Informal childcare networks allow single mothers to work. Informal labor exchanges — the neighbor who fixes cars, the woman who does hair, the man who cleans gutters — circulate value without entering the formal economy. Church-based food sharing, mutual lending, and crisis support function as a parallel welfare system that the formal safety net does not provide.

This informal economy is the productive capacity the Society would most directly tap. It is already present. It already works. It is uncompensated, unrecognized, and entirely dependent on the relationships that sustain it. The Society's credit system is the mechanism for making this visible and reciprocal — for honoring what the community already does for itself.

---

## What an Extraction Economy Leaves Behind

Gary's economy was designed to extract. US Steel built the city to house labor for its mill, paid wages sufficient to reproduce that labor, and captured the surplus value. When the extraction was no longer profitable at that scale, the company reduced its footprint. It did not restore what it had taken from the land, the air, the water, or the community. It left the infrastructure, the vacancy, the contamination, and the people — and moved on.

What remains is a community with genuine productive capacity — skills, knowledge, mutual aid traditions, the ability to care for one another — and no institutional structure that honors or channels that capacity for the community's own benefit. The formal economy offers low wages for service work or the commute. The informal economy is real but invisible and fragile. The gap between what Gary's people have to give and what the existing economy allows them to receive is the specific opening the Society is built to occupy.
