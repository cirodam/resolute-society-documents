# Gary, Indiana — Social Capital

## What Social Capital Is and Why It Matters Here

Social capital is the connective tissue of a community — the relationships, institutions, norms of reciprocity, and networks of trust through which people get things done together. It is what the Society requires to exist and what the Society, at its best, produces more of. Understanding the social capital landscape of Gary is understanding what the Society would be entering, what it would be building on, and what it would need to earn.

Social capital comes in three forms. *Bonding capital* is the strong ties within groups — family, church congregation, neighborhood. *Bridging capital* is the ties across different groups — the relationships that connect different parts of a community to each other. *Linking capital* is the ties between communities and institutions that hold power — government, employers, funders. Gary has significant bonding capital in specific communities, limited bridging capital across the city, and very weak linking capital after generations of institutional abandonment. Understanding that distribution shapes everything about how the Society operates here.

## The Spine: The Black Church

The most durable institution in Gary — across all the plant closures, the fiscal crises, the state takeovers, the violence, the population collapse — is the Black church. Not any single congregation but the network of congregations, pastors, and church-based organizations that have held the community's social and spiritual life together through every crisis the city has faced.

The Black church in Gary does what it has always done in Black communities across America: it buries the dead and names the loss, it feeds people when other systems fail, it provides the institutional home for organizing when other organizing structures disappear, it transmits moral formation and community identity across generations, and it offers the specific kind of presence — unconditional, weekly, physically located — that most secular institutions cannot sustain. During the worst years of Gary's violence, pastors were doing funeral after funeral. They did not leave.

This is the first thing a Society in Gary needs to understand: the church is already doing much of what the Society is built to do, with deeper roots and more established trust than the Society will have for years. The relationship between the Society and the church community is not the Society offering something the church lacks. It is the Society offering a structure — the ledger, the credit system, the governance — that could channel and honor what the church community already does.

## Community Development Organizations

Gary has a layer of community development organizations that have persisted through the city's decline and done real, sustained work:

**Gary Neighborhood Services** has operated for more than forty years — longer than most of Gary's recent population has been alive. It represents the kind of organizational continuity that is itself a form of social capital: an institution that has been there, that the community can name and find and return to.

**Gary East Side Community Development Corporation**, founded in 2017, focuses on workforce development and housing — the two material conditions the economy section identified as most in need. Its specific geographic focus on the East Side reflects the reality that Gary's social capital is often neighborhood-scaled, rooted in specific blocks and corridors rather than the city as a whole.

**Gary Community Partnership** works on neighborhood revitalization through direct project implementation — a model oriented toward demonstrated, tangible results rather than advocacy alone.

**Sojourner Truth House** serves homeless women and children — the population at the intersection of poverty, domestic violence, and housing instability. Its existence represents the community's recognition that this need exists and its decision to meet it rather than wait for the state.

These organizations are not a complete social infrastructure. They are islands of sustained capacity in a landscape of significant institutional thinness. They are also potential partners and bridges for a Society — organizations that know the community, have existing relationships, and have already built some of the trust the Society will need to earn.

## Capital B Gary: A New Institutional Form

Among the most significant recent developments in Gary's civic life is Capital B Gary, a nonprofit local news organization covering Gary from the perspective of its residents. It has done sustained, high-quality reporting on the environmental health crisis, the school takeover, the housing situation, and the community's responses to each. In a media landscape where local journalism has collapsed across most of the country, Gary has an institution specifically dedicated to giving its community accurate information about itself.

This matters for social capital because civic information infrastructure is a precondition for civic action. A community that doesn't know what's happening to it cannot organize around it. Capital B Gary is not just journalism — it is a form of community capacity that makes other forms of organizing possible. A Society in Gary should be reading it, supporting it, and treating it as a natural ally in the work of building an informed and engaged membership.

## What the Union Hall Was and What Replaced It

The United Steelworkers local was once a significant social institution in Gary, not only an economic one. The union hall was where men gathered, where disputes were negotiated, where political organizing happened, where a cross-cutting civic identity — not purely racial, not purely ethnic, not purely employer-defined — was built through collective action. The union was imperfect in Gary, as everywhere: Black workers faced discrimination within the union as well as without. But at its best it offered something Gary has not had since: a secular civic institution with genuine power, accountable to its members, organized around shared economic interest across some of the city's divisions.

The union halls are largely gone. What replaced them is uneven. The church filled some of the void. Informal networks filled more. But the specific function the union served — organizing working-age men around collective civic action, providing a structure for mutual obligation outside the family and the congregation — has not been replaced by any comparable institution. This is a genuine gap that a Society is specifically designed to fill.

## The Trust Deficit

Gary has been managed, intervened upon, studied, and left behind by outside institutions for decades. Federal programs arrived and departed. Nonprofits came with plans and left when the grant ended. The state took over the schools and returned them seven years later without solving the underlying problems. Developers have proposed projects that never broke ground. The pattern is consistent enough that Gary residents have a well-developed and entirely rational skepticism toward new institutions, especially those that arrive with plans for the community rather than from it.

This skepticism is not cynicism. It is accurate pattern recognition. An institution that arrives in Gary and expects trust because it means well has not understood what Gary has experienced. Trust in Gary is earned through demonstrated staying — through being there next month and the month after, through delivering on the specific concrete things promised, through remaining accountable to the people it serves when accountability is inconvenient.

The Society's structural advantage in this environment is significant. It requires no grant to continue. It answers to no board of directors in another city. It exists as long as its members choose to sustain it. These are not rhetorical points — they are the specific features that distinguish the Society from the institutional pattern Gary has learned to distrust.

## The Bonding/Bridging Problem

Gary's social capital is strong within communities and weak across them. The church congregation is a tight, trusting network. The block has relationships. The extended family has deep bonds. But the ties that connect these strong local networks to each other — and to the broader civic life of the city — are thinner than they need to be for Gary to act with collective power.

This is partly a legacy of the physical destruction of shared civic space: the department stores and movie theaters where different parts of the city intersected are gone. It is partly a legacy of violence concentrating in specific corridors and teaching people to stay within familiar geography. It is partly the collapse of the institutions — the union, the political machine, the integrated workplace — that used to create bridging ties as a byproduct of their function.

A Society in Gary does not solve this problem by declaring itself a bridging institution. It solves it slowly, through the shared ledger that connects transactions across neighborhoods, through governance that draws members from across the city by sortition, through the community meal that puts people in the same room. The bridge is built in practice, not announced in founding documents.

## What the Society Enters

Gary's social capital landscape is not empty. It is significantly thinner than it was, unevenly distributed, and oriented inward toward strong local bonds rather than outward toward civic connection. What exists is real, durable, and worth building on: the church network, the community development organizations, the violence interrupters, the neighborhood advocates, the journalists at Capital B Gary, the informal mutual aid that holds families together.

The Society does not arrive to replace any of this. It arrives as an offer: here is a structure for the productive capacity that already exists in this community — the skills, the care, the mutual obligation — that gives it economic form, connects it across neighborhoods, and makes it visible and reciprocal rather than invisible and one-directional. Whether that offer is accepted depends entirely on whether the Society is actually that, demonstrated over time, rather than the latest institution to come through Gary with plans.

Gary has seen enough of the alternative to know the difference quickly. That is the condition the Society must meet. It is also, if met, the Society's most powerful credential: that it was here, that it stayed, and that it delivered.
