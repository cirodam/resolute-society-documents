# Gary, Indiana — Education

## Numbers

- **Students enrolled in Gary Community School Corporation:** 4,076 across 11 schools
- **Math proficiency:** 7% of students at grade level
- **Reading proficiency:** 12% of students at grade level
- **Indiana state averages for comparison:** math ~47%, reading ~51%
- **District operating debt at time of state takeover (2017):** $21.5 million
- **Duration of state takeover:** seven years (2017–2024)
- **Higher education nearby:** Indiana University Northwest (Gary campus), Purdue Northwest (Hammond)

---

## Gary Community School Corporation: What the Numbers Mean

Seven percent proficient in math. Twelve percent in reading. These are not numbers that require careful interpretation. They mean that in a given Gary classroom, roughly one student in fourteen can do grade-level math, and roughly one in eight can read at grade level. The rest cannot — not because they are incapable, but because they are attending schools that are under-resourced, under-staffed, and operating in conditions that would challenge any educational institution.

The district has 4,076 students today. At its peak, Gary's schools served over 40,000. The building stock, the administrative structure, and the cost base were designed for a city many times its current size. The population collapse left the district maintaining facilities it cannot fill, in a fiscal situation it could not sustain, serving a community whose poverty concentration makes every aspect of the educational mission harder. A child who is hungry, who lives in a home without heat, who witnessed violence the night before, who has asthma from the steel plant's emissions — that child is not starting the school day from the same place as the district average. Gary's schools are full of those children.

## The State Takeover and What It Produced

In 2017, Indiana placed Gary Community School Corporation under state emergency management — the first school district in Indiana to be taken over by the state. The trigger was financial: $21.5 million in operating debt, finances the incoming emergency manager described as in "total disarray." The elected school board was replaced by a state-appointed manager. Democratic control over the community's schools was removed.

The state managed the district for seven years. It stabilized the finances. It did not close the academic gap. Critics noted from early in the takeover that the state's intervention was structured around fiscal management, not educational recovery — that the emergency manager had tools to cut costs and restructure debt but no clear mandate or mechanism to address the underlying conditions producing the academic crisis. By the time local control returned in July 2024, the district was no longer technically "distressed" by the state's financial definition. The proficiency rates had not meaningfully moved.

The return to local control came with a significant caveat: residents cannot yet vote on their school board. The community regained its school system before regaining its voice over how that system is run. The democratic accountability that was removed in 2017 has not been fully restored.

What seven years of state management demonstrated is that the problems of Gary's schools are not primarily problems of management. They are problems of concentrated poverty, environmental burden, disinvestment, and the generational consequences of a city that has been systematically failed. A competent emergency manager cannot fix those things. Neither can a test score intervention or a curriculum reform. The schools reflect the city. Until the city's conditions change, the schools will too.

## What the Schools Are Competing With

Gary's schools are not competing with other schools for their students' attention and energy. They are competing with everything that poverty and violence and environmental stress produce in a child's life before they walk through the door.

A student whose family's housing is unstable moves between schools and loses continuity. A student whose parent works two jobs has no help with homework and comes home to an empty apartment. A student who has been exposed to lead or chronic air pollution may face cognitive effects that no remediation will fully reverse. A student who has witnessed violence — in the neighborhood, in the home — carries a neurological and psychological load that shapes how they sit in a classroom and absorb information. These are not excuses. They are conditions. And they are the conditions under which Gary's teachers work every day, largely without the support structures that would make those conditions manageable.

The school is also competing with the economy. In a community where the formal credential economy has demonstrably failed — where a Gary high school diploma does not reliably lead to stable employment — the argument for staying in school is harder to make and harder to sustain. The students who stay are not wrong to wonder what they are staying for.

## Higher Education Access

Indiana University Northwest maintains a campus in Gary, and Purdue Northwest is nearby in Hammond. These institutions provide real higher education access — particularly for community members who cannot relocate or attend residential campuses — but cost remains a significant barrier in a community where median household income is $36,000 and student debt creates the coercive bind described in Axiom XV. The students who do access higher education often leave Gary for employment afterward. The credential economy exports Gary's most educated residents.

## What Exists Outside the Formal System

The Black church in Gary has always been an educational institution alongside everything else it is. Sunday schools, after-school programs, tutoring, the transmission of history and moral formation and practical knowledge — these have operated continuously in Gary's churches through every crisis the formal school system has faced. They are not a substitute for adequate schools. They are what the community built when adequate schools were not available, and they represent a genuine educational infrastructure that operates largely without recognition or resources.

Practical knowledge — mechanical skills, construction, cooking, preservation, care — circulates informally in Gary's households and networks in the same way it does in Warren. The grandmother who teaches her granddaughter to cook from scratch. The mechanic who trains his nephew. The home health aide who explains medication management to the family members she works with. None of this shows up in graduation rates or proficiency scores. All of it is real education.

## What This Means for the Society

The Society's curriculum — its courses in practical literacy, civic reasoning, food production, healthcare, construction, governance — is education in a form Gary's community already recognizes and values. It is knowing-how rather than knowing-that. It is knowledge that produces immediate, visible results in daily life. It does not require a credential to be useful. It does not require leaving Gary to be worth having.

For the adults in Gary who have been failed by the formal education system — who left school early, whose credentials did not lead where they were supposed to, who carry skills the credential economy refuses to count — the Society's courses offer something the school system never did: education that takes seriously what they already know, builds on it, and gives it a form the community can recognize and reward. That is not a consolation prize for people who couldn't access the real thing. It is a different account of what education is for.
