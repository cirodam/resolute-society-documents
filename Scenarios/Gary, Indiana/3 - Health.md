# Gary, Indiana — Health

## Numbers

- **Life expectancy:** Lake County falls well below state average; Indiana itself ranks among the worst states in the country for health outcomes
- **Asthma:** Gary is in the top 10% of US communities for asthma attributable to environmental exposure
- **Cancer risk:** Communities near the Gary coke plants have cancer rates 26% above the national average; residents living near steel production face a 12–26% elevated cancer risk
- **Premature deaths attributed to Gary Works emissions:** estimated 57–114 per year
- **Asthma attacks attributed to Gary Works emissions:** estimated 32,000 per year
- **School and work loss days attributed to Gary Works emissions:** more than 11,000 per year
- **Poverty-linked chronic disease** — diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease — runs well above state and national averages

## The Environmental Fact

The most important health fact about Gary is not a disease rate or a provider shortage. It is that the air is poisoned.

US Steel Gary Works emitted 182 tons of 24 different hazardous air pollutants in 2022. The three mills operating in the Gary area released 25 million pounds of toxins in 2023 — more industrial pollution than four comparable mills in other states combined. The pollutants include benzene, arsenic, and chromium. These are not trace amounts. These are ongoing, daily emissions from a plant that has been polluting the community it sits in for more than a century, under permits that environmental advocates have repeatedly argued are insufficient to protect public health.

The Indiana state environmental agency has been criticized for failing to hold US Steel to basic clean air standards. Recent EPA rollbacks have widened the enforcement gap further. A three-year enforcement lapse is estimated to result in an additional 120 tons of hazardous emissions across steel communities in Indiana, Michigan, Ohio, and Pennsylvania — with Gary bearing the largest share.

Gary residents breathe this. They have always breathed it. The generation that worked in the mill breathed it inside and outside. Their children breathed it. Their grandchildren breathe it now. The health consequences are measurable and have been measured. The political will to stop it has been intermittent and so far insufficient.

This is not a historical injustice. It is a present one.

## Healthcare Access

Indiana designates over 70% of its counties as medically underserved. Gary and Lake County are in that category. The healthcare providers that do exist in Gary skew toward hospital emergency rooms, federally qualified health centers, and the Medicaid-dependent care that a population where 40%+ is on Medicaid requires. Preventive care, mental health services, dental care, and specialist access are all significantly constrained.

The largest employment sector in Gary is healthcare and social assistance — which sounds like an asset but is partly a symptom. Healthcare employment in Gary is heavily concentrated in home health aides, nursing assistants, and social service workers: people providing care to a population made sick by poverty and environmental exposure, for wages that keep those caregivers themselves in poverty. The system produces the workers it needs while underpaying them and underserving the community.

The ER is the primary care provider for a significant portion of Gary's population — not because people prefer emergency rooms but because they have no realistic alternative for conditions that should never reach an emergency.

## Mental Health

The mental health burden in Gary is substantial and largely unaddressed by formal services. It is produced by overlapping conditions that each independently generate psychological harm and that in Gary operate simultaneously: chronic poverty, community violence, environmental contamination, the multigenerational grief of a city that was dismantled, and the specific trauma of watching institutions fail people over and over.

Mental health services in Gary are limited. The providers that exist are stretched thin, often inaccessible without transportation, and frequently culturally disconnected from the community they're meant to serve. The informal mental health infrastructure — the pastor who talks someone through a crisis, the grandmother who holds the family together, the neighbor who checks in — carries an enormous weight that is not counted in any dataset and not compensated in any budget.

## Substance Use

Lake County and the broader northwest Indiana corridor have been significantly affected by the opioid crisis that has devastated communities across the Rust Belt. Gary's history with substance use predates the opioid wave — crack cocaine reshaped parts of the community in the 1980s and 1990s in ways whose consequences are still present — but the current crisis layer is real and adds to an already heavy burden. Treatment access is limited. Recovery support is limited. The informal networks of care that address mental health also absorb much of what formal substance use treatment does not reach.

## What the Community Has Built for Itself

The Black church network in Gary has historically been the most durable health infrastructure in the city — not clinically, but in the ways that matter for daily survival. Churches have organized food distribution, coordinated transportation to medical appointments, provided the social support that mitigates the health effects of isolation, and served as the first point of contact for families in crisis. This is not a substitute for adequate healthcare. It is what fills the gap where adequate healthcare does not exist.

Community health advocacy has been active and increasingly effective. Capital B Gary, a nonprofit news organization, has done sustained reporting on the environmental health crisis that has pressured both US Steel and state regulators. Environmental justice organizations have pursued litigation and regulatory action. These efforts represent a community that has not accepted its situation as inevitable — that has continued to name what is being done to it and demand otherwise.

## What This Means for the Society

The Community Health Worker model the Society has developed elsewhere is directly applicable in Gary and more urgently needed here than in most contexts. A CHW embedded in the community — someone who knows the members, tracks their conditions, connects them to care, identifies problems before they become emergencies — is precisely what the formal healthcare system fails to provide and what the informal networks are already attempting to do without structure or compensation.

The environmental health crisis is a collective problem that requires collective voice. The Society is not an environmental advocacy organization, but a community that has developed genuine political capacity through its governance structures is a community that can show up when it matters — to comment periods, to city council meetings, to the legal processes that determine what US Steel is permitted to release into the air that every member breathes. That is not outside the Society's purpose. It is a direct expression of the covenant: what harms one member harms all, and the community responds accordingly.
