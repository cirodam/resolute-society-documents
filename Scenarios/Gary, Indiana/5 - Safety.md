# Gary, Indiana — Safety

## Numbers

- **Violent crime rate:** 898 per 100,000 — roughly 2.4 times the national average
- **Homicides, 2023:** 52
- **Homicides, 2024:** 40 (down 23%)
- **Homicides, 2025:** 28 (down 30% — lowest since 1970)
- **Non-fatal shootings, 2023:** 147; **2024:** 133; **2025:** 101
- **Combined decline, 2023–2025:** homicides down 46%, non-fatal shootings down 31%
- **Homicide closure rate, 2025:** 82%

---

## The History and the Trend

Gary has carried a reputation as one of the most dangerous cities in America for decades, and for much of that time the reputation was earned. At its worst, Gary's per-capita homicide rate placed it among the highest in the country. Violence became part of the city's identity in the national imagination — a shorthand for urban failure, used by people who had no other interest in Gary.

The honest account requires holding two things simultaneously. The first is that Gary has been and remains a genuinely dangerous place, with violent crime rates far above the national average. The second is that Gary has made real and significant progress. Twenty-eight homicides in 2025, the lowest figure in fifty-five years, is not a statistical artifact. It is the result of sustained work by the police department, federal partners, and — critically — community members who have been building anti-violence infrastructure for years. The trend is real. It does not erase the history, but it is not nothing.

The community that is being asked to understand here is not a community in freefall. It is a community that has been fighting its way back, with limited resources, from a very deep hole.

---

## The Character of Violence in Gary

Gary's violence is not random. It is concentrated geographically — in specific neighborhoods, on specific blocks — and it is concentrated demographically — among young Black men, typically between the ages of 18 and 34, in social networks where disputes escalate through a logic that outside institutions have historically been unable to interrupt.

The conditions that produce this violence are not mysterious. Deindustrialization removed the stable employment that had structured working-class life and given young men a legitimate pathway to status and income. The drug economy moved into the vacuum — not as a moral failure of individuals but as a rational response to a labor market that had nothing to offer. The drug economy brings its own enforcement mechanisms, its own dispute resolution, its own hierarchies of status and power. Violence is the price of operating within it, and it is paid disproportionately by the young men inside it and the community around it.

The people most likely to be killed in Gary are not random victims. They are people embedded in social networks where violence is an ongoing risk, often with prior contact with the criminal justice system, often in economic situations where legitimate alternatives are genuinely scarce. This is not a justification. It is a diagnosis. You cannot address violence by addressing its symptoms without addressing its conditions.

## Policing

The Gary Police Department has invested in strengthening its investigative capacity, expanded its partnership with federal law enforcement — the ATF and US Attorney's office — and participated in a four-year federal program through the Department of Justice's National Public Safety Partnership. The homicide closure rate of 82% in 2025 reflects real investigative improvement. The department added twelve new officers in 2024.

Gary is also a pilot site for the National Initiative for Building Community Trust and Justice, which works to improve relationships between police and the communities they serve through procedural justice, implicit bias training, and reconciliation practices. The existence of this initiative reflects a recognition — from both the department and the community — that policing alone does not produce safety, and that the relationship between a majority-Black community and law enforcement carries a specific weight that has to be addressed directly.

The relationship between Black communities and police is not a Gary-specific problem. It is the national context in which every interaction between a Gary officer and a Gary resident takes place. What the department is attempting to build is trust in an environment where the historical grounds for distrust are well-documented and the stakes of getting it wrong are high.

## The Community Response

The decline in Gary's violence is not primarily a policing story. Community members — activists, pastors, violence interrupters, organizers — have been doing sustained anti-violence work for years, some of it predating the recent federal partnerships. Anti-violence basketball games and community forums sound modest. They represent something more: community members choosing to be visible, to be present in public space, to assert that the streets belong to the community and not to the violence that has occupied them.

Violence interrupter programs — which place credible messengers in the communities most affected by violence, people who know the social networks and can intervene before disputes escalate — have been part of Gary's recent progress. These programs work where policing cannot, because they operate through relationships and trust rather than authority. They represent exactly the kind of community capacity the Society is built to honor and support.

The Black church network has been part of the anti-violence work in Gary for decades. Funerals happen in these churches. Grieving families come to these pastors. The community holds its loss in these spaces and continues to organize from within them.

## What Safety Actually Means Here

Safety is not only the absence of violence. It is the presence of conditions in which people can be fully present in their community — can walk to a store, let their children play outside, attend a meeting at night, know their neighbors, participate in public life without the calculation of whether it is safe to do so.

By that broader definition, Gary has not been fully safe for a long time, and the recent declines in homicides and shootings — while genuine and significant — have not yet restored those conditions across the whole city. The community member who stays indoors, who does not attend evening meetings, who organizes their life around avoiding certain blocks — that person is experiencing an ongoing safety deficit that the crime statistics do not fully capture.

This matters for the Society because the Society requires presence. The covenant is lived in showing up. Members who cannot safely show up — or who have organized their lives around not showing up — are members whose participation the Society cannot assume. The Society in Gary has to be honest about this: building genuine community in conditions of concentrated violence requires directly engaging with safety as a community project, not treating it as background context that the police will handle.

---

## The Relationship Between Safety and Everything Else

Violence in Gary is not separable from the other conditions documented in this landscape series. It is downstream of the economic conditions that removed legitimate pathways to income and status. It is downstream of the educational conditions that left young people without credentials or skills the formal economy recognizes. It is downstream of the health conditions — including the mental health burden of poverty and trauma — that are inadequately addressed by the formal healthcare system. It is downstream of the political conditions that have left Gary without the institutional resources to address any of these things adequately.

You cannot solve Gary's safety problem without addressing Gary's conditions. But you also cannot wait for Gary's conditions to be fully addressed before beginning to build community. The Society operates in the conditions as they are — acknowledging them, designing around them, working to change them through the collective capacity that genuine community produces. Safety is not the precondition for the Society. Safety is part of what the Society is trying to build.
