# Gary, Indiana — The Narrative

## The City That Was Built to Be Used

Gary, Indiana is not a city that grew around an industry. It is a city a corporation built from nothing to serve its purposes, and whose story since has been the story of what happens when the corporation decides it is done.

---

## Origin: A Company Builds a City (1906)

In 1906, United States Steel Corporation quietly purchased roughly 10,000 acres of Indiana swampland on the southern shore of Lake Michigan. It named the city it built there after its own chairman, Elbert Henry Gary, who never lived in it. The Gary Works — the largest steel mill in the world at the time of its construction — was the reason for everything. The streets, the schools, the housing, the water system: all of it existed to put workers within reach of the furnaces. Gary was incorporated as a city in 1906. By 1910 it had 16,000 people. By 1930 it had 100,000. The mill needed them, so they came.

---

## The Rise: Immigrants, Labor, and the Promise of Wages

The early workforce was Eastern European — Polish, Croatian, Hungarian, Slovak — drawn by the same promise that drew immigrant labor to every American industrial city in the early twentieth century: wages, however hard won, that exceeded anything available at home. The union came later. The United Steelworkers organized Gary's mill workers through the 1930s and 1940s, and for a generation the combination of industrial wages and union power produced something close to a working-class prosperity. The city built itself — schools, churches, a real downtown with department stores and movie theaters, neighborhoods with enough stability to pass between generations.

---

## The Great Migration: A Black City Takes Shape

Black workers began arriving during World War I, when European immigration collapsed and the mill needed labor. They kept coming through the 1920s, 1930s, and 1940s — families from Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia, chasing the same promise the Eastern Europeans had chased. They got the dirtier jobs. They were blocked from promotion. They were confined by residential segregation to specific neighborhoods — the "Patch" south of the main rail line, and later others — while the white ethnic communities held the better housing closer to downtown. The city was officially integrated; the lived reality was not. And yet the Black community built itself within those constraints: churches, mutual aid organizations, social clubs, a neighborhood life of genuine depth. By 1960, when the city hit its peak population of 178,320, Gary was one of the largest Black urban communities in the Midwest. The Jackson family — Joe, Katherine, and their children — lived on Jackson Street on the west side. Michael Jackson was born in Gary in 1958.

---

## The Decline: Gradual, Then Total

Gary did not have a single Black Monday. The decline was slower and therefore in some ways harder to name and resist. US Steel began contracting in the 1960s as foreign competition increased and domestic demand shifted. The plant and its allied facilities employed over 30,000 workers in the early 1970s. By 1990 that number was 6,000. Today it is 2,246. White residents left for the suburbs — Merrillville, Portage, Chesterton — taking the tax base with them. Downtown hollowed out. The department stores closed. The movie theaters closed. The population fell: 178,000 in 1960, 116,000 in 1980, 80,000 in 2000, 67,000 today. Gary has lost 62% of its peak population. Much of what was built for 178,000 people still stands, vacant and deteriorating, in a city of 67,000.

---

## Political Self-Determination: The Hatcher Election (1967)

In 1967, Gary elected Richard Hatcher as mayor — one of the first Black mayors of a major American city, elected the same year Carl Stokes won in Cleveland. The white-controlled Democratic Party machine fought his candidacy. He won anyway. He served five terms, through 1987. He brought federal money into the city, built public housing, paved streets, kept basic services functioning through a fiscal crisis that would have broken a less determined administration. He could not stop US Steel from leaving. He could not stop white flight. He could not reverse a deindustrialization that was happening across every industrial city in America simultaneously and that no municipal government had the power to arrest. He had been handed, as one account put it, a city already in economic freefall. Political control without economic control turned out to be insufficient — not because Hatcher failed, but because the problem was larger than any mayor.

---

## The Shadow of Chicago

Gary sits 25 miles from downtown Chicago. On a clear day you can see the skyline from the Gary lakefront. The proximity is the cruelest fact about the city's situation. Gary is not remote. It is not isolated. It is adjacent to one of the great economic engines of the Midwest, close enough to commute from, and it has been excluded from Chicago's prosperity with remarkable consistency. Many Gary residents work in Chicago. The wages they earn largely circulate elsewhere. The economic activity that might have built Gary instead built the suburbs between it and Chicago. The city is in the Chicago metropolitan area in every geographical sense and excluded from it in every economic one.

---

## What Remains

The Gary Works is still there — the largest integrated steel mill in North America, still operating, still visible from most of the city, employing a fraction of what it once did. The lakefront is genuinely beautiful, adjacent to the Indiana Dunes National Park, largely inaccessible to the residents who live nearest to it because the industrial corridor sits between the city and the water. The Black church network remains the most durable institutional fabric in the city. Community development organizations work in the vacancy. The population of 67,000 — 76% Black, 32% in poverty, median household income of $38,731 — is the population that stayed, or couldn't leave, or chose not to. They are the city now.

What Gary has never had is an institution built specifically for the people who remain — accountable to them, sustained by them, serving their purposes rather than extracting from them. Every institution that has come through Gary has come from outside. The mill. The federal programs. The nonprofit initiatives. The state interventions in the schools. All of it done to Gary rather than by Gary. That is the specific gap the Society is built to fill.
