# Abortion

---

## Opening

- The abortion debate is almost entirely conducted in bad faith — not because the people in it are dishonest as individuals, but because it has been politically captured so thoroughly that predetermined conclusions are reached before reasoning begins
- Both sides deploy arguments backward: conclusion first, justification second
- What the Society's framework requires: follow the logic where it leads, accept uncomfortable conclusions, name manipulation when it occurs
- This essay begins with two claims and works out what follows from them:
  1. A pregnancy is a moral tragedy — a genuine conflict between real values, happening inside a specific person's body, with no outcome that fully honors everything that matters
  2. No person or group of people can commandeer another person's body for their purposes — not even purposes they believe are good, not even when life is at stake
- What this essay will not do: tell you whether abortion is morally permissible. That turns partly on a question that is honestly unresolvable. But it will tell you who decides, and why, and what the community owes.

---

## I. Why This Is a Tragedy

*The personhood question*

- The politically charged version: "Is the fetus alive?" — biologically, yes, trivially and from fertilization. This is not the hard question.
- The actually hard question: when does morally significant life begin? When does a developing human being acquire the kind of moral status that generates claims on others?
- Why this is genuinely unresolved:
  - **Fertilization** — genetic uniqueness established. But: every somatic cell is genetically unique. Genetic novelty alone is not an account of moral status. And: roughly half of fertilized eggs fail to implant naturally — a fact that sits oddly with full moral status from fertilization
  - **Implantation** — a significant biological event, a necessary threshold for development. But: not obviously morally decisive in itself
  - **Viability** — the capacity to survive outside the womb. Problems: (a) entirely dependent on available technology — the threshold shifts as neonatology improves, (b) varies by location and medical access, (c) a being's moral status cannot coherently depend on the state of medical technology
  - **Sentience / capacity for suffering** — neurological development capable of pain perception is a meaningful moral threshold. But: development is gradual, not a single moment. And: unconscious human beings who cannot currently feel pain retain full moral status — so sentience alone can't be the complete account
  - **Birth** — a significant legal, social, and relational transition. But: it's not obvious what metaphysically changes at the moment of passage through the birth canal
- The honest conclusion: the question of when morally significant life begins is genuinely unresolved. This is not relativism. It is epistemic honesty about a question that depends on contested philosophical commitments that are not themselves resolvable by evidence.
- What this means for the tragedy: because the personhood question is unresolved, we cannot know with certainty what is on the other side of the conflict. The fetus's moral status may be full, partial, or developing — we do not know. That uncertainty does not dissolve the tragedy. It is part of what makes it a tragedy: a real conflict whose full weight cannot be precisely measured.
- The tragedy is not resolved by greater certainty on the personhood question. It is resolved by the second claim — which holds regardless of how the personhood question is answered.

## II. The Commandeering Prohibition

*What follows even if we grant full fetal personhood*

- Grant the most demanding premise: full moral personhood from the moment of fertilization. The fetus has a genuine right to life. The conflict is now between two full moral persons, both with legitimate claims grounded in the same foundational commitment to human dignity.
- **The axiom beneath the prohibition**: the commandeering prohibition is not a separate foundational commitment — it derives from dignity. Dignity means persons cannot be instrumentalized: used as mere means to someone else's ends. The chain is: dignity → non-instrumentalization → no one can direct another person's body toward their purposes without consent, whatever is at stake. What follows is an application of that principle:
- The key philosophical distinction: **the right to life is not the same as the right to use another person's body**
  - You can believe murder is wrong and also believe no one can compel you to donate a kidney to save a dying person
  - The dying person's right to life does not generate a right to your kidney — even though your kidney would save them and withholding it means they die
  - This is not because their life doesn't matter. It is because their right to continued existence does not include a right to use your body without your consent.
  - This logic holds even under full fetal personhood — the fetus's right to life does not automatically include the right to use the mother's body
- **The asymmetry that is morally decisive**: the mother is not merely affected by the situation — she *is* the situation
  - The pregnancy happens inside her body
  - She bears all the physical cost — nine months of biological labor, physiological transformation, medical risk
  - Pregnancy and childbirth carry real morbidity and mortality
  - She is the only party who lives inside the particular facts of the situation in the way that grounds legitimate judgment
  - Both parties created the situation in most cases. Only one party *is* the situation.
- **The responsibility objection** — the most serious challenge: if she voluntarily engaged in the act that created the dependency, the kidney analogy may not apply. You didn't choose to be connected to the violinist; she chose to have sex knowing pregnancy could result. Has that prior act created an obligation that makes continuation compellable?
  - Consent to sex is not consent to pregnancy. These are distinct acts. We do not treat consent to any action as consent to all foreseeable consequences — and treating it that way here would require doing so nowhere else
  - Even granting some version of implicit obligation from a prior act: we do not allow prior acts to permanently surrender bodily autonomy. Contracts can be exited. Consent can be revoked. No past decision binds a person to nine months of ongoing physical labor in their own body.
  - Even granting full responsibility for creating the situation: responsibility generates proportionate obligation — not bodily commandeering specifically. A father who creates a pregnancy has genuine obligations: financial, relational, parental. Those obligations are real and enforceable. They do not require physical labor in his body because they cannot. The question is not whether her responsibility generates obligations — it does. The question is whether those obligations must take the specific form of nine months of physical labor in her body. That form is not derived from the responsibility itself. It is assumed. And that assumption is exactly what the commandeering prohibition denies.
  - The objection also establishes nothing about rape and incest cases, where there is no prior consensual act
  - What the objection does, at most: deepen the tragedy in consensual cases by adding a dimension of responsibility. It does not establish that continuation is compellable.
- **The conclusion**: no person or group can commandeer another person's body — not the state, not a court, not a partner, not a community acting on sincerely held moral beliefs. The fetus's right to life, whatever its full moral weight, does not include a right to use her body. Continuation cannot be externally compelled.

## III. Who Decides

- The two claims together determine the answer
- A moral tragedy is happening inside her body. No external actor can commandeer her body to resolve it. Therefore: she navigates it.
- **The decision-maker**: the pregnant person, together with her physician
  - Not because outsiders lack information — though they do
  - Not because her judgment is infallible — it isn't
  - Because the commandeering prohibition means no external actor has legitimate authority to substitute their judgment for hers about what happens inside her body
- **What the community's role is not**: to make the decision
- **What the community's role is**: to ensure the conditions under which a genuine decision can be made — economic support, accurate information, absence of coercion, absence of criminal threat. Developed in Section VII.
- **A note on late pregnancy**: the commandeering prohibition does not change with gestational age — it licenses ending the pregnancy throughout. But there is a distinction that matters as development progresses: the prohibition grounds the right to end the bodily connection, not necessarily the right to terminate the fetus specifically. Early in pregnancy these are the same act. Once a fetus is viable, they are not. The framework answers who decides the same way at every stage. What changes is what the decision involves — developed in Section IV.

## IV. The Cases

*The framework applied — each case shows how the two claims operate in specific circumstances*

### A. Life of the Mother

- The moral tragedy partially resolves here — not cleanly, not without grief, but with greater clarity about what dignity requires
- The life already present, already a full member of the human community, facing imminent death, takes precedence
- This is a different philosophical category — closer to necessity (*anangke*): the thing that must be done regardless of what it costs. Necessity carries its own weight — the weight of being required to do something genuinely terrible to prevent something worse.
- **The hard middle — genuine medical uncertainty**:
  - When it isn't definitively clear that the mother's life is in danger, we are in genuine medical and moral uncertainty
  - The commandeering prohibition applies: uncertainty does not create a new opening for external authority. It compounds the tragedy. The physician and patient together remain the only legitimate decision-makers.
- **The political reality that must be named**: laws with no life-of-mother exception — or with exceptions so narrow they require waiting until a patient is demonstrably dying — produce documented, predictable harms. Physicians forced to wait until a patient is "sick enough" to legally intervene watch people deteriorate toward death. This is not a contested empirical claim. It has killed people.

### B. Rape

- Requiring a rape victim to continue a pregnancy is requiring her to bear — in her own body, for nine months — the ongoing material cost of a crime committed against her
- That requirement cannot be derived from any coherent account of dignity. It compounds violation with obligation. It makes the victim responsible for the consequences of what was done to her.
- The responsibility objection from Section II does not apply — there is no prior consensual act. The commandeering prohibition applies in its clearest form.
- **The fetal innocence argument** — the most serious counter: the fetus bears no culpability for the circumstances of its conception
  - Correctly establishes that termination is not punishment of the fetus
  - But establishing fetal innocence doesn't establish that continuation can be compelled. The commandeering prohibition holds regardless of the fetus's culpability.
  - What fetal innocence does: deepen the tragedy. Now both parties are innocent. The fetus didn't choose to exist in these circumstances. The mother didn't choose the violation. The only party who bears culpability is absent from the consequences entirely.
- The framework's conclusion: external judgment is illegitimate. The community's obligation is support, not determination.

### C. Incest

- The rape analysis applies in full
- Additional dimensions compound it:
  - The original violation is often deeper — betrayal of a protective relationship
  - The victim is frequently a minor, adding developmental harm beyond the physical
  - Elevated genetic risk for the resulting child adds a layer of potential suffering that doesn't exist in other cases
- The framework's conclusion is identical: external judgment is illegitimate. The tragedy is deeper, not different in kind.

### D. Nonviable Pregnancy

- The moral tragedy structure shifts here — what is actually on the other side of the conflict?
  - Not a life that will be saved
  - Not a dignity that will be honored
  - The biological process continuing to its inevitable conclusion
- Requiring continuation:
  - Cannot protect a life — there is no life to protect
  - Requires a person to continue bearing the full physical and psychological cost of a pregnancy that will not produce a living child
  - For no purpose the framework can identify as legitimate
- The commandeering prohibition applies with full force and with nothing on the other side to create even the structure of a tragedy
- The political reality: legally contested in multiple states. Physicians have delayed treatment for ectopic pregnancies and incomplete miscarriages out of legal fear. People have died.

### E. Late Pregnancy

- The hardest case the framework faces, and the one that requires the most honest engagement
- The commandeering prohibition licenses ending the pregnancy throughout — that right does not diminish with gestational age
- But "ending the pregnancy" can mean two different things:
  - **Termination**: the fetus does not survive
  - **Delivery**: the fetus is born and, if viable, survives separately
- Early in pregnancy these are effectively the same act. Once a fetus is viable outside the womb, they are not.
- **What the commandeering prohibition licenses**: ending the bodily connection — separation. That right is clear.
- **What the commandeering prohibition does not automatically license**: termination of the fetus specifically, when separation without termination is possible. Those are distinct acts, and the prohibition grounds one more directly than the other.
- **What resolves the choice between termination and delivery**: in practice, this is a medical judgment. In the circumstances where late-term procedures actually occur — severe fetal anomaly incompatible with life, serious maternal health emergency — termination is frequently medically safer than delivery, and in some cases delivery is not a meaningful clinical option. The pregnant person and her physician make that determination. In the hypothetical case of a healthy viable fetus with no medical emergency, the commandeering prohibition grounds the right to end the connection; delivery accomplishes that. Whether termination rather than delivery is additionally justified in that case is where the framework's answer is most demanding and most honest: the prohibition alone does not supply that justification. The decision remains hers — but the framework does not pretend the weight of what is being decided is what it was at six weeks.
- **Why such cases are nearly nonexistent in practice — Principle 34**: the rarity of late-term termination in healthy viable cases is not produced by legal restriction. It is produced by innate moral reasoning. People feel the weight of the decision deepening as pregnancy progresses and act on that without external compulsion. The law claims credit for an outcome that the person's own moral sense was already producing — and in claiming that credit, destroys the space for the genuine exceptions where late intervention is medically necessary.
- **The distinction between late-term abortion and infanticide**: this is where the framework produces its most honest and least comfortable answer
  - The distinction is real — but it is narrower than typically claimed, and it is not located where most people assume
  - It is not a difference in the moral status of the fetus versus the infant. That status does not change at the moment of birth. A fetus at 38 weeks and a newborn are not meaningfully different beings.
  - The distinction is in the nature of the claim. The fetus makes a claim on her body specifically — the commandeering prohibition applies. The infant, once born, makes a claim on the community's care, not on anyone's body in particular. Birth matters because it ends the bodily connection and therefore ends the commandeering prohibition — not because something changes in the being itself.
- **What this means for the community**: if the commandeering prohibition means the community cannot compel her to continue the pregnancy, the community's obligation intensifies at viability. It must provide genuine alternative care for the child that can exist separately. A position that opposes late-term termination without providing that care has not followed its own logic.
- **The political reality**: late-term abortions are rare and occur almost exclusively in circumstances of severe fetal abnormality incompatible with life, or serious maternal health emergencies. The cases where the philosophical distinction between termination and delivery actually matters clinically are a small fraction of already rare procedures. The political debate treats late-term abortion as a casual choice. It is almost never that.
- The framework's honest conclusion: the commandeering prohibition answers who decides throughout. At viability, what is being decided becomes more complex — the moral weight is at its heaviest, and the community's obligation to provide genuine alternatives is at its most demanding.

### F. The "Normal" Cases

- The moral tragedy of Section I is real and is not dissolved by any of the above
- The responsibility objection has the most purchase here — in consensual pregnancy, there is a prior act, and the framework takes that seriously as part of the weight of the decision, not as grounds for external compulsion
- What the framework does and does not say:
  - Does not say: abortion is morally fine. The personhood question is unresolved; the tragedy is real; the responsibility dimension in consensual cases is real.
  - Does say: the commandeering prohibition holds. The pregnant person is the legitimate decision-maker.
- This is not permission language. It is not the community saying "we don't care." It is the community saying: we recognize that you are navigating a genuine moral tragedy, that the commandeering prohibition means no one else can navigate it for you, and that our role is to ensure the conditions under which you can make a genuine decision — not to make it for you.

## V. The Father's Interest

- His interest in the outcome is real. He has a genuine stake. This deserves acknowledgment rather than dismissal.
- He does not have a claim on the process by which the outcome is reached — because that process happens inside another person's body, and the commandeering prohibition applies regardless of relationship
- **What the framework says he is owed**:
  - Genuine participation in deliberation — his voice, his perspective, his stake deserve to be heard and taken seriously
  - Honest speech applies within the relationship as much as anywhere else
  - The community should support genuine communication between the parties
- **What he is not owed**: veto power. Veto is commandeering by another name.
- **The hardest case**: the father who desperately wants the child, whose values hold that a person exists from fertilization, who has no mechanism to prevent the termination of what he understands to be his child
  - That is also a moral tragedy. His loss is real. His grief is legitimate. The framework doesn't dissolve that — it names it honestly.
  - His tragedy generates a claim on the community's compassion and support. Not a claim on her body. Those are different things.

## VI. Honest and Dishonest Argument

*What the commitment to honest speech requires when applied to this debate*

### A. The Heartbeat Case — A Study in Deliberate Manipulation

- At six weeks of gestation: there is no heart. There is no cardiac organ. What exists is a cluster of electrically active cells that will eventually differentiate into cardiac tissue.
- Those cells produce electrical impulses that, when detected by ultrasound equipment and converted to audio, produce a sound.
- That sound is not a heartbeat in any medically or embryologically meaningful sense. It is the sonification of electrical activity in undifferentiated cells.
- The medical and embryological communities are clear on this. This is not ambiguous.
- "Fetal heartbeat" at six weeks is not a lay simplification of a complex concept. It is not an approximation. It is a fabrication — specifically designed to produce an emotional response that substitutes for genuine reasoning.
- The framework can say something the political debate almost never says directly: **the people deploying this language are not engaging in good faith.** Argument that deliberately misrepresents biological reality to produce emotional substitutes for reasoning fails the Society's standard for honest speech completely.

### B. Dishonest Argument on the Other Side

- The symmetry commitment requires naming this — and requires honesty about where the symmetry holds and where it doesn't
- **"It's just a clump of cells"** — the mechanism here is different from the heartbeat case, and the difference matters. The heartbeat claim is a biological fabrication: a sound that doesn't exist is said to exist, and the medical community is unambiguous about this. The "clump of cells" claim is a philosophical conclusion dressed as a biological description. The cells are real. What's being claimed is that their moral status is obviously nil — that the question of when morally significant life begins has a clear answer and that answer is "not yet." But no such clear answer exists. The essay has shown why. A genuinely contested philosophical position is being presented as though it were a factual observation. That is epistemic dishonesty, even if it is less precisely fabricated than the heartbeat case.
- **"My body, my choice" as a conversation-stopper** — the commandeering prohibition is the right principle. But deployed as a slogan rather than an argument, it becomes something different: a way of foreclosing engagement with what is actually being navigated rather than enabling it. The pregnant person is navigating a moral tragedy in which something real is at stake on both sides. Invoking bodily sovereignty as a phrase that ends the conversation rather than as a philosophical position that requires engagement with the tragedy performs the same move as the heartbeat case in reverse — substituting rhetorical closure for genuine reasoning. The Society holds the commandeering prohibition firmly. It does not hold that invoking it dissolves the tragedy or eliminates what makes the situation heavy.
- **"No restrictions, ever"** — treating a late-term abortion and an early one as morally identical requires dismissing the personhood question entirely rather than honestly engaging it. The commandeering prohibition holds in both cases. The weight of what is being navigated is not the same, and an argument that pretends otherwise is not reasoning honestly about what it is endorsing.
- **The honest accounting of asymmetry**: the dishonesty on the pro-choice side operates primarily through a different mechanism than the heartbeat case — correct principles deployed as slogans, philosophical conclusions presented as biological facts, genuine complexity collapsed into language that makes engagement feel unnecessary. The heartbeat case is an outright fabrication. These cases are philosophical pretense. Both fail the honest speech standard. The Society names both, and names the difference, because pretending the failures are identical in kind would itself be a failure of honesty.

### C. What Good-Faith Pro-Life Argument Actually Looks Like

- Genuine engagement with the personhood question, including acknowledgment of the genuine philosophical difficulty
- Direct engagement with the commandeering prohibition — not dismissal but real philosophical attempt to answer why the fetus's right to life includes a right to use the mother's body
- Engagement with the limits of the responsibility objection — acknowledging that it cannot carry the full weight placed on it, and cannot apply at all in rape and incest cases
- Acceptance of at minimum the life-of-mother exception, or if not, a genuine philosophical account of why a dead mother is the required outcome
- Fidelity to biological and medical fact — including what six-week electrical activity is and what delayed care produces
- Good-faith pro-life argument exists. It deserves engagement. The heartbeat legislation and the no-exceptions bans are not it.

**The most serious philosophical version — and what honest engagement produces**

- Don Marquis's "future like ours" argument is the strongest philosophical pro-life argument precisely because it bypasses the personhood question entirely. It does not claim the fetus is a person now. It claims that what makes killing wrong is deprivation — taking from a being the valuable future it would have had. A fetus has such a future. Therefore killing it is wrong for the same reason killing any person is wrong.
- This is genuinely more sophisticated than most political pro-life argument. It deserves a real response.
- **The first problem — who judges value, and from whose perspective?**
  - Marquis asserts the fetus's future is valuable because it would contain experiences, projects, and relationships. But value is being assessed from the outside — by someone imagining what that future would contain
  - The mother's future is also valuable. It also contains experiences, projects, and relationships — ones that pregnancy and childbirth impose real costs on. Marquis's own framework, applied consistently, generates a conflict between two valuable futures. He does not resolve this conflict. He barely acknowledges it.
  - His argument does not establish that the fetus's future is more valuable than the mother's. It establishes that both futures have value — which is exactly the structure of the moral tragedy the framework already names.
- **The second problem — the argument proves too much**
  - The "future like ours" framing applies equally to contraception and to decisions not to conceive. A possible person who is never conceived also never receives their valuable future. Marquis wants the argument to stop at fertilization, but his logic gives him no principled reason to stop there.
  - An argument that proves contraception is wrong on the same grounds as abortion has not clarified the moral landscape. It has revealed that its premises are broader than its author intended.
- **The third problem — it does not survive the commandeering prohibition**
  - Even granting that the fetus has a valuable future, that future does not generate a right to use the mother's body to reach it. A dying person also has a valuable future — one your kidney could preserve. That future does not generate a right to your kidney.
  - Marquis's argument establishes that something of genuine value is lost in abortion. The framework already grants this — it is part of what makes the situation a tragedy. What Marquis's argument cannot establish is that the loss can be prevented through commandeering another person's body. Those are separate questions, and the commandeering prohibition answers the second one regardless of how the first is resolved.

## VII. Where the Society Stands

*Separating the personal moral question from the political question*

- **What the Society does not say**: abortion is always morally permissible. The personhood question is genuinely unresolved. In cases where fetal personhood is most plausible, the moral tragedy is real and its weight is serious. The framework takes that seriously.
- **What the Society does not say**: abortion is always morally impermissible. The commandeering prohibition holds even under full fetal personhood. Epistemic humility about personhood means certainty in this direction is not warranted either.
- **What the Society says**: the decision belongs to the pregnant person and her physician, made under conditions of genuine freedom, with accurate information, without economic coercion, without criminal threat
- **The precaution principle applied — and why it doesn't resolve as expected**:
  - A perceptive challenge from within the framework: Principle 6 holds that where action is irreversible and outcomes uncertain, the burden of justification falls on action, not restraint. Abortion is irreversible. Fetal moral status is genuinely uncertain. Does precaution therefore demand not aborting?
  - The challenge fails for a specific reason: the precaution principle assumes restraint is the low-cost neutral option. Here it is not. Continuing a pregnancy is also irreversible — nine months of physical transformation, medical risk, life trajectory changes that cannot be undone. There is no neutral waiting position when a pregnancy is in progress. Both paths involve irreversible consequences for real persons with genuine moral standing. The precaution principle cannot adjudicate between two irreversible options. It selects between action and genuine restraint. Genuine restraint does not exist here.
  - The deeper problem with the challenge: the commandeering prohibition is prior to the precaution principle. Precaution tells you to be careful when you act. It does not determine who has legitimate authority to act. The commandeering prohibition already answers that question. Precaution invoked by an external party as grounds to override that authority has misapplied the principle — using a tool about *how* to act as though it were a tool about *who* may act.
- **The pro-life member who supports choice**:
  - I hold a personal moral position about when life begins and what that means
  - I also recognize that the commandeering prohibition means my certainty about that position does not give me the right to impose it on someone else's body — not because I'm uncertain about my beliefs, but because bodily sovereignty is a principle that applies even when others disagree with how it is exercised
  - These are two separate questions. I can hold both simultaneously.
  - This is not moral cowardice or relativism. It is the correct application of a principle that holds in every other domain.
- **The community's positive obligation** — more demanding than legal permission, and derived from the framework rather than appended to it:
  - **Accurate medical information, free from political distortion** — the commandeering prohibition is only meaningful if the decision is genuine. A genuine decision requires accurate information. Political distortion of medical fact forecloses genuine decision-making as surely as legal compulsion does. Epistemic manipulation is commandeering by another route.
  - **Real economic support for either path** — the commandeering prohibition prohibits legal compulsion. It does not automatically prohibit economic compulsion. A community that says "she decides" while creating conditions where only one path is financially feasible has not honored the prohibition — it has outsourced the commandeering to economics. Real freedom of decision requires real freedom of means.
  - **No coercion toward either decision** — the same logic extends to social pressure. A decision made under duress — whether financial, relational, or social — is not a genuine navigation of the tragedy. It is imposition by softer means. The community's obligation is to the conditions of genuine choice, not to any particular outcome.
  - **Absence of stigma for either choice** — the tragedy framing requires this. If the situation is genuinely tragic, both paths involve real loss and neither path is a moral failure. Stigmatizing either choice treats the navigation of a genuine tragedy as a character defect. That is the category error Principle 18 names directly.
  - **Support for children and families** — this follows from the commandeering prohibition's own logic. The community cannot make the decision; it must therefore bear the consequences of whatever decision is made. A community that opposes termination while refusing to support the children born into difficult circumstances has demanded a specific outcome while refusing its costs. That is not a coherent position. It is the commandeering prohibition applied selectively — invoking dignity when it serves a preferred outcome, withdrawing it when the bill arrives.

## VIII. What Just Law Would Look Like

*Applying the framework to current legal reality*

- **What this framework is not**: Roe v. Wade — a privacy right derived from the penumbra of constitutional structure. Clever. Fragile. Dependent on a jurisprudential move that could be and was undone.
- **What this framework is**: a positive obligation derived from dignity, the commandeering prohibition, and the limits of legitimate external judgment. A stronger and more coherent foundation — one that produces more demanding community obligations alongside the protection of individual decision-making.
- **The structural problem with abortion law — Principle 34**: the Society's framework identifies a specific kind of legal failure — law that codifies what innate moral reasoning already produces. Abortion law is a direct instance. Late-term abortions are rare across every legal regime, not because law prohibits them but because people feel the increasing moral weight of pregnancy as it progresses and act on that without external compulsion. The law is not changing behavior — it is codifying what innate reasoning already produces. What it adds is not behavior change. It is rigidity. And rigidity's cost is the exceptional case: the woman with the ectopic pregnancy, the fetal abnormality incompatible with life, the maternal health emergency. Those are exactly the cases innate reasoning can see and law cannot. The law is most harmful here precisely because it is most redundant.
- **What just law would say**: the decision belongs to the person inside the situation, made with her physician, under conditions of genuine freedom, with accurate information, without economic coercion, without criminal threat. The community's role is to ensure those conditions exist — not to make the decision.
- **What current law produces instead**:
  - Substitutes institutional certainty for epistemic humility about a genuinely unresolved question
  - Transfers judgment from those with proximity and visibility to those without either
  - Commandeers the bodies of the poor in ways it cannot commandeer the bodies of the wealthy — the wealthy person in a ban state has options the poor person does not
  - Criminalizes navigation of moral tragedy — turns people facing the hardest situations into criminals for making the only decision they could actually see from where they stood
  - In its most extreme forms actively increases harm to people the framework obligates the community to protect — delayed ectopic treatment, withheld miscarriage care, prosecuted physicians
- **The physician criminalization problem**: laws that expose physicians to criminal liability for exercising medical judgment produce the most predictable and documented harm — delayed care while physicians wait for legal cover that never arrives. A legal framework whose enforcement mechanism produces preventable mortality has failed on its own terms.
