# Data Centers and the Extractive Grid

## I. Introduction
- The invisible infrastructure of digital life
- Data centers as physical objects with physical consequences
- Thesis: the energy and resource demands of data centers represent a new form of extraction that concentrates costs on land and communities while concentrating benefits elsewhere

## II. What Data Centers Actually Require
- Power: scale of consumption, how it compares to cities, industrial users
- Water: cooling systems and the volumes involved
- Land: footprint, location patterns, displacement
- The gap between how digital infrastructure is perceived and what it physically demands

## III. Who Bears the Cost
- Siting patterns: rural areas, regions with cheap power, often near water sources
- Local grid strain and what it means for existing residents
- Water table and supply competition with agriculture and households
- Tax incentives and what communities give up to attract them

## IV. Who Captures the Benefit
- Revenue, jobs, and where they actually flow
- The mismatch between local cost and distributed benefit
- Comparison to historical extractive industries

## V. The AI Acceleration
- How the current buildout differs in scale from prior waves
- Projected demand curves and what they imply for resources
- The feedback loop: more AI requires more infrastructure requires more energy

## VI. Implications for Resilience
- Communities structurally dependent on infrastructure they don't control
- Grid fragility introduced by large variable loads
- What it means to build resilience against this backdrop

## VII. Conclusion
- The material reality behind the digital economy
- What a community would need to see clearly before consenting to this exchange
