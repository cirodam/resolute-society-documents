# Mass Incarceration: A Judgment

## Outline

**I. What we have built**
- The numbers in plain language: scale, racial arithmetic, the before/after
- The comparison that lands: we incarcerate at higher rates than authoritarian states we regard as cautionary tales
- The opening thesis: this is not a system that got out of hand — it is a system that works exactly as designed, toward ends that cannot survive honest examination

**II. How it was built**
- The political turn: 1970s to 1994, the deliberate construction of the system (Nixon's admission; mandatory minimums; three strikes; the 1994 Crime Bill)
- The prosecutorial turn: plea bargaining and the 97% ea rate — the adversarial system has been largely replaced by administrative coercion
- The private interest: prison industry, guard unions, rural economic dependence — concentrated interests with structural stake in the system's continuation

**III. Axiom III: engineered invisibility**
- The system works precisely because those who sustain it politically never encounter what it involves
- The prison is located far from the people whose votes maintain it; the suffering inside is invisible by institutional design
- This is not an accident of geography — it is the condition of the system's political sustainability

**IV. The racial architecture**
- Alexander's argument stated cleanly: this is not a system with racial disparities; it is a racial system
- The historical continuity: convict leasing, the Black Codes, each successive form finding new legal container
- What the disparity looks like at every decision point: policing, charging, plea offers, sentencing, parole

**V. Axiom XI and IX: the premises the system runs on**
- Every structural feature — mandatory minimums, permanent records, collateral consequences, disenfranchisement — presupposes that persons are identical to their worst moments
- The evidence says otherwise; the system is built on a false premise about human nature
- Legitimacy test: does it flow from genuine consent, serve those subject to it, remain accountable to them? Each condition fails

**VI. What it actually produces**
- On its own terms: recidivism rates, what research says actually reduces crime
- What incarceration produces instead: severed families, destroyed communities, concentrated poverty, trauma that compounds across generations
- The political economy of failure: the system fails its stated purpose while succeeding at its actual function

- The closing judgment applied directly from the axiom framework
- Not reform vs. abolition as the frame — the prior question is whether the system's premises are honest, and they are not
