# Immigration

## Where the Debate Actually Is

The immigration debate most Americans remember — about border security, pathways to citizenship, comprehensive reform, the proper balance between enforcement and integration — is not the debate being conducted now. What is being conducted now is something different in kind, and treating it as a hotter version of the old argument misses what has happened.

The argument being advanced today, at the highest levels of American government, is not that illegal immigration is a problem requiring policy solutions. It is that immigration itself — the movement of non-Western, non-white people into the United States — is an invasion. That the people coming are barbarians at the gate of a civilization they are incapable of sustaining. That the demographic character of America is being deliberately replaced, and that this replacement is an existential threat requiring emergency response.

This is not an immigration policy position. It is a racial ideology. It has a specific intellectual history, specific authors, specific institutional homes — and it has been carried from those homes into the mainstream of American politics by specific people over the course of several decades. Understanding what is happening requires understanding that history rather than treating the current moment as a policy debate that has simply gotten heated.

## The Ideology and Where It Comes From

The specific ideas animating the current moment have names and authors.

**The Great Replacement** is a theory developed by French author Renaud Camus, published in 2011, arguing that white European populations are being deliberately replaced by non-white immigrants through a coordinated elite project. The theory is not a policy critique. It is a conspiracy framework asserting that immigration is a weapon being used against white civilization by forces — sometimes named as Jewish elites, sometimes left vague — seeking the destruction of Western identity. It is straightforwardly antisemitic in its original form and has been adopted wholesale by American white nationalist movements.

**The Camp of the Saints** is a 1973 French novel depicting a flotilla of brown-skinned immigrants overwhelming and destroying white European civilization while liberal elites applaud. It is graphic, explicit about its racial framework, and has been a touchstone of white nationalist literature for decades. It was referenced enthusiastically by Steve Bannon in multiple interviews. Its framing — the non-Western world as a civilizational threat, white liberals as collaborators in civilizational suicide — is the direct ancestor of the invasion rhetoric now standard in mainstream Republican politics.

**The Fall of Rome** narrative is the claim that Rome's decline was caused by immigration and demographic replacement, and that the same process is underway in contemporary America. This is historically disputed at best and ideologically motivated at worst — Roman historians do not generally attribute Rome's fall primarily to immigration — but it serves its function, which is to frame immigration as civilizational catastrophe rather than human movement.

**"Poisoning the blood"** is a phrase Donald Trump used repeatedly beginning in late 2023 to describe immigration's effects on America. It is a phrase from Mein Kampf. Its appearance in mainstream American political speech was not accidental and was not condemned by the mainstream of the party in which it appeared.

These are not the ideas of an immigration debate. They are the ideas of a racial movement that has succeeded, over several decades, in moving from explicit white nationalist publications into the daily language of American political life.

## The People Who Did This

This mainstreaming did not happen organically. It was a project, and it had architects.

Stephen Miller has been the most consequential of them. His career traces the path from explicit white nationalist intellectual infrastructure to official government power with unusual clarity. As a student at Duke, he formed an alliance with Richard Spencer — later the most visible white nationalist in America — and with the white nationalist publication VDARE, which has described itself as defending white America against demographic replacement. Emails published by the Southern Poverty Law Center showed Miller, while a senior White House official, directing reporters to VDARE and American Renaissance — another explicitly white nationalist publication — for story ideas, and coordinating immigration coverage with those outlets.

As the primary architect of Trump administration immigration policy, Miller translated this ideology into governing action. Family separation — deliberately designed to deter immigration by inflicting suffering on children — was his project. The travel bans targeting Muslim-majority countries. The "zero tolerance" prosecution policy. The near-elimination of refugee admissions. The systematic dismantling of legal immigration pathways. The use of emergency powers framing to describe immigration as an invasion requiring military response.

What Miller understood, and what the project required, was that the ideology had to be laundered. The explicit racial framework — white replacement, civilizational invasion — could not be stated plainly in mainstream political speech, at least not initially. So it was translated: invasion without the racial theory stated explicitly. Barbarians without the theory of civilizational hierarchy stated explicitly. Emergency without the Great Replacement stated explicitly. The translation was thin enough that the original ideology remained visible to those who recognized it, while providing deniability to those who preferred not to.

That deniability has eroded substantially. "Poisoning the blood" requires no translation. Describing immigrants as "not people" and "animals" requires no translation. The language has converged toward the ideology it was always drawing from.

## The Legitimate Grievance

None of what follows denies that the people who have absorbed this narrative have real grievances. They do. And a response to this moment that dismisses those grievances as mere racism — that treats everyone who worries about immigration as a white nationalist — will fail, because it misunderstands the mechanism.

The working class of the United States has experienced something real over the past fifty years. Manufacturing employment collapsed. The communities built around it hollowed out. Wages stagnated for decades while productivity and wealth accumulated upward. The institutions that once gave working-class life structure — unions, civic organizations, stable employment, affordable housing in functional communities — were systematically weakened. Opioids moved in where purpose had been. Deaths of despair increased. Life expectancy declined for the first time in generations.

These are not imagined grievances. They are documented, measurable, and severe. The people experiencing them are right to be angry. The question is not whether their anger is legitimate. It is what their anger is being aimed at and whether that aim was chosen or manufactured.

## What the Narrative Does

The scapegoat mechanism has a consistent structure in American history. A period of genuine economic distress produces real grievance. Political actors redirect that grievance toward the most vulnerable and least powerful population available. The redirection serves the interests of those who actually produced the conditions causing the distress, because it protects them from the accountability they would otherwise face.

The working-class economic catastrophe of the past fifty years was produced by specific decisions made by specific people. The offshoring of manufacturing was a policy choice, not a natural phenomenon. The destruction of union power required sustained legislative and legal effort. The financialization of the economy — the shift from producing things to extracting value from productive activity — was the result of specific regulatory choices made by people who benefited from them. The trade agreements that made offshoring profitable were negotiated by identifiable actors with identifiable interests.

The people who made those decisions are not the targets of the immigration narrative. The people at the absolute bottom of the economic order — who had no power to produce those outcomes, who are frequently victims of the same concentrated power that crushed the working class, who came here because their own communities had been destabilized by American economic and foreign policy — are.

That is the scapegoat mechanism operating exactly as it always has. Take genuine pain. Give it a target that cannot fight back, that cannot vote, that can be acted upon without political cost, that has been pre-dehumanized to make action feel like defense rather than cruelty. And protect the actual sources of the grievance from examination.

## The Mechanism in Action

Abstract analysis of dehumanization is less useful than watching it happen. The mechanism has been operating in full public view, in documented and undeniable form, for years. Here is what it looks like.

In September 2024, at a presidential debate watched by approximately 70 million people, Donald Trump claimed that Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio were stealing and eating residents' pets — cats and dogs. The claim was false. Springfield city officials stated publicly that there was no evidence of this. Local residents confirmed it had not happened. It was a fabrication, traceable to a single unverified Facebook post that had circulated in far-right media until it reached the presidential debate stage.

JD Vance, who had helped spread the claim, was asked about it afterward. He acknowledged he had no evidence it was true. He said he had spread it anyway because he wanted to "draw attention" to immigration issues in Springfield. He was asked, in other words, whether he had knowingly spread a lie about a specific immigrant community. His answer was yes.

The consequences were immediate and specific. Haitian residents of Springfield received death threats. Bomb threats were called in to schools, causing them to close. Community centers frequented by Haitian immigrants were targeted. Children were afraid to go to school. A community of people who had come to Springfield legally — many recruited by employers who needed workers, who had rebuilt their lives there, who were contributing to a city that had been economically struggling — spent days in fear because the Republican nominee for president had repeated a story about them eating pets that he and his running mate knew to be false.

Trump did not stop. He repeated the claim at the debate and continued repeating it at rallies afterward. The function of the claim was not to be true. Its function was to attach a specific image — savage, barbaric, beyond the pale of civilization — to a specific population of Black immigrants. The image does not require the claim to be factual to do its work. It requires only repetition.

This is the mechanism. It is not subtle. It is not a policy dispute. It is the deliberate attachment of dehumanizing imagery to a specific community of people by the most powerful political figures in the country, with the explicit acknowledgment that the specific claims do not need to be true to serve their purpose.

The Springfield example is not an exception. It is characteristic. "They're not people, they're animals." "Poisoning the blood of our country" — a phrase from Mein Kampf, used at rallies, printed on merchandise, never walked back or explained. The framing of immigration as an "invasion" requiring military response and emergency powers. Each carries the same logic: immigrants are not persons in the full sense, they are a threat to be neutralized, and the normal moral weight that persons carry does not apply to them.

That is dehumanization. It is not a rhetorical escalation in a policy debate. It is a specific and documentable process with a specific function, conducted by specific people, with specific consequences for specific human beings.

## What the Evidence Shows

The invasion narrative requires specific empirical claims to be true. Most of them are not.

Immigrants — documented and undocumented — commit crimes at lower rates than native-born citizens. This finding is robust across studies, methodologies, and immigrant populations. The claim that immigration produces crime is not a misreading of the evidence. It is a fabrication.

The economic effects of immigration are more complex than either side of the debate typically acknowledges, but they do not support the replacement narrative. Immigration increases economic output. Immigrants start businesses at higher rates than native-born citizens. The net fiscal impact of immigration is positive over the medium and long term. There is genuine evidence that low-wage immigration creates wage pressure for specific categories of low-wage native workers — this is the most legitimate economic concern in the debate — but it is a marginal and contested effect, and it does not survive as an explanation for the scale of working-class economic distress, which was well underway before recent immigration waves and which is primarily attributable to the forces named above.

The generational integration pattern — first generation maintains native language, second generation is bilingual, third generation is predominantly English-speaking — has been consistent across every immigrant wave in American history and holds for current immigrants. The claim that contemporary immigrants are failing to integrate in ways that previous waves did not is not supported by the evidence.

## What This Has Cost

The ideology, translated into policy, has produced specific outcomes that deserve to be named plainly.

Children were separated from parents at the border as a deliberate policy mechanism — not as an enforcement necessity but as a deterrent, designed to inflict sufficient suffering on families that others would be discouraged from coming. This was stated explicitly by the architects of the policy. Children were held in conditions that violated basic standards of care. Some were not reunited with their parents. The suffering was the point.

People who have lived in the United States for decades, who have raised American children, who have built businesses and relationships and community ties, have been removed to countries they left as children or have never lived in. People have been deported to El Salvador, South Sudan, and other countries with which they have no meaningful connection, in conditions that amount to disappearance. Due process protections that the legal system had long treated as baseline have been stripped from proceedings affecting people's lives and safety.

ICE operations have been conducted at churches, schools, and hospitals — the specific spaces that had historically been treated as off-limits precisely because their function depends on people being able to enter them without fear. Immigrant communities have been made into populations living in permanent fear, unable to report crimes, unable to access medical care, unable to participate in community life. This does not serve any community integrity function. It produces the opposite of integration.

## The Historical Pattern

The United States has run this cycle before. Irish immigrants were depicted as a criminal underclass whose Catholic loyalties made them incompatible with American democracy. Italian immigrants were racialized as non-white and associated with crime and disorder. Chinese immigrants were described as an invasion of an alien civilization, leading to exclusion acts that remained law for decades. Japanese Americans were interned as enemy agents on the basis of their ancestry. Eastern European Jewish immigrants were depicted as carriers of Bolshevism and racial degradation.

In every case, the scapegoated group was later recognized as having been essential to the communities that rejected them. In every case, the hostility was later identified as a combination of genuine economic anxiety and deliberate manipulation by interests that benefited from the redirection. In every case, the specific empirical claims made against the group — their criminality, their unassimilability, their threat to wages and culture — were contradicted by the evidence.

The current moment is that cycle, accelerated and intensified, powered by communication infrastructure that did not exist in previous iterations and deployed by people who understand the mechanism and are using it deliberately.

## What Is Being Protected

The honest conclusion of this analysis is not comfortable.

The working class of the United States has been devastated by fifty years of economic decisions made by people with concentrated power over economic and political life. Those decisions produced the genuine grievance that the immigration narrative is exploiting. The people who made those decisions — and who continue to benefit from the economic arrangements they produced — are substantially the same people who have funded and promoted the immigration narrative, or who have been its political beneficiaries.

This is not a coincidence. It is the mechanism. You cannot sustain an economic order that extracts value from working people while exposing them to the full force of market risk without giving them something else to be angry at. The immigration narrative is that something else. It is extraordinarily well-designed for its purpose: it captures genuine grievance, it provides a target that cannot fight back, it produces political energy that can be harvested, and it protects the actual sources of the problem from the accountability that genuine anger would otherwise demand.

The people who have absorbed this narrative are not, for the most part, racial ideologues. They are people with real grievances who have been offered a real-sounding explanation for their situation. The explanation is false. The grievances are real. And the false explanation is serving the interests of the people who produced the conditions they are angry about.

That is what this is. That is what it has been designed to do. Naming it clearly is not a political act. It is the basic requirement of seeing what is actually happening.
