# Vaccine Mandates

---

## Opening

- The vaccine mandate debate is not primarily a philosophical dispute about bodily sovereignty. It is a crisis of institutional trust that was misread as a crisis of public compliance — and responded to accordingly, making everything worse.
- Almost no one who resisted COVID vaccine mandates was disputing the basic science of how vaccines work. They were disputing whether these specific institutions, at this specific moment, with this specific product, had earned the authority to compel them.
- That is a different question. And the Society's framework has a clear answer to it — not a politician's answer, not a comfortable one, but an honest one.
- What this essay will do: name what the debate was actually about, apply the framework to it honestly, and follow the logic where it leads
- The conclusions:
  1. Vaccines are good. The evidence for them should be followed honestly and communicated clearly.
  2. The commandeering prohibition applies. No one may be compelled to undergo a medical procedure without genuine consent — not by the state, not by an employer.
  3. Employment conditions that remove genuine consent are economic coercion. The framework opposes them here for the same reason it opposes them in the abortion context.
  4. The obligation falls entirely on institutions: convince, don't compel. Compulsion is not a substitute for the persuasion the institutions failed to achieve.

## I. What the Debate Was Actually About

*Institutional trust, not vaccine science*

- The politically charged framing: "anti-vaxxers vs. science." This framing is dishonest and was deployed strategically to avoid a more uncomfortable conversation.
- What the resistance was actually about: a specific set of institutions — federal health agencies, pharmaceutical companies, employers, media — had damaged their credibility in specific and documentable ways, and were now demanding compliance with an urgent directive on a compressed timeline
- The conditions that produced distrust:
  - Emergency use authorizations rather than standard approval timelines — a genuine departure from established process, presented as routine
  - Rapidly changing guidance, including on masking and transmission, that institutions presented as settled when it was not
  - Prior institutional failures — the opioid crisis produced by FDA-approved medications, the documented history of pharmaceutical companies suppressing unfavorable trial data, specific CDC failures that were matters of public record
  - The conflation of policy disagreement with scientific illiteracy — critics of mandate policy were labeled "anti-science" regardless of the specificity or legitimacy of their concerns
  - Financial relationships between regulatory agencies and the companies they regulate that were not hidden but were not prominently disclosed
- **People intuitively distinguish between vaccines — and that intuition tracks something real**: the "anti-vaxxer vs. science" framing implied that vaccine hesitancy was categorical — that people who questioned COVID vaccines rejected all vaccines equally. This was not true, and the institutions knew it. Most people who hesitated about COVID vaccines had no objection to polio or MMR. They were making distinctions the institutions refused to recognize.
  - Smallpox killed 30% of those infected, left survivors disfigured, and was visibly catastrophic for everyone regardless of age or health status. The vaccine had a decades-long track record and eradicated the disease. The case is clear, stable, and has been clear and stable for generations.
  - COVID was genuinely different in ways that matter: severity was heavily concentrated in the elderly and immunocompromised — a 25-year-old and a 75-year-old faced meaningfully different situations. The vaccine was developed in under a year on novel mRNA technology with emergency authorization rather than standard timelines. And guidance changed repeatedly in ways smallpox guidance never did — masks don't work, then masks are mandatory; two doses and you're protected, then you need a booster, then multiple boosters. Each reversal was presented with the same institutional confidence as the position it replaced.
  - The person who accepts polio vaccination without hesitation and is skeptical about a novel emergency-authorized vaccine whose guidance kept changing is not being inconsistent. They are tracking real differences in track record, disease severity, institutional certainty, and the stability of the evidence. That intuition is sound.
  - The institutional error was collapsing all of this into a single binary and labeling anyone who made distinctions as "anti-science." That move both misread the nature of the hesitancy and made genuine engagement impossible — because you cannot address a concern you have refused to hear. It also confirmed the core suspicion: that the institutions were managing compliance rather than communicating honestly.
- **A distinction the essay must draw clearly**: the legitimate critique of institutional behavior in this essay must not be read as validating fabricated claims about vaccines — and the essay will not allow that conflation to stand
  - The COVID vaccines were genuinely effective at reducing severe illness and death, particularly for vulnerable populations. The evidence for this is real and the Society follows it.
  - Claims that COVID vaccines contained microchips, caused widespread fertility damage, or were instruments of government control were fabricated. They had no evidentiary basis. People who refused vaccination on the basis of those claims were not reasoning well — they were doing exactly what Principle 1 prohibits: reaching a conclusion regardless of what the evidence shows.
  - The Society holds both things simultaneously, because both are true: **the vaccines worked, and the institutions behaved badly.** These are separable claims. The critique in this essay is specifically of institutional behavior around mandates and communication — not of vaccine science. Nothing here should be read as supporting the fabricated information ecosystem that grew alongside legitimate hesitancy.
  - That fabricated ecosystem is itself a product of institutional failure. When official sources lose credibility through dishonesty, financial capture, and repeated reversals, people fill the void with whatever is available. The conspiracy content that spread during COVID is not separate from the institutional behavior problem — it is its direct product. Institutions that issued mandates rather than earning trust created the conditions in which fabricated alternatives could flourish, and then blamed the population for seeking them. That too is a cost of the mandate approach that was not accounted for in the policy calculation.
- The honest acknowledgment: some distrust was miscalibrated even where it was structurally rational. The structure of the concern — "I don't trust these specific institutions making this specific claim under these specific conditions" — is not irrational. But the conclusion some people drew — that the vaccines themselves were dangerous or malicious — went beyond what the evidence supported and into territory the evidence directly contradicted.
- **The ivermectin case — what the whole dynamic looks like in practice**: ivermectin is not a fringe substance. It is a legitimate antiparasitic drug with a decades-long track record, widely used in developing countries, developed by researchers who won the Nobel Prize. During COVID, early studies — small, poorly controlled, but not fabricated — suggested possible antiviral properties. People who were frightened and looking for treatment options found this and pursued it. What happened next illustrates everything wrong with how the institutions handled the crisis:
  - The early evidence was genuinely ambiguous. Not fabricated — preliminary and inadequately designed, but a real evidentiary question that deserved honest engagement
  - Some of the most-cited supporting studies were later found to have fabricated data. The Elgazzar study, widely shared, was retracted for data fabrication. This is part of why the final evidence picture — larger, better-designed trials finding ivermectin ineffective against COVID — should be trusted: the research that eventually settled the question was of a different quality than what initially circulated
  - The Andrew Hill case documented something the essay has already named as structural: a researcher's meta-analysis supporting ivermectin was found to have been shaped by a pharmaceutical company with financial interests pointing in a different direction. This is not conspiracy. It is documented. It is exactly the kind of research environment corruption that makes institutional trust a genuine question rather than a failure of public understanding.
  - The FDA's institutional response was the "you are not a horse" campaign — mocking people who were asking a legitimate question about an ambiguous early evidence base, in the middle of a crisis, when they felt abandoned by institutions that weren't offering them much. That response did not engage with evidence. It performed contempt for the people asking.
  - The result: legitimate early inquiry, fabricated supporting evidence, documented institutional corruption, and institutional contempt for the people asking — all mixed together in a way that made it nearly impossible for the average person to know what to believe or who to trust
- What the institutions did in response to legitimate skepticism: issue mandates. Which is exactly what you do when you have stopped trying to convince people and decided to compel them instead.

## II. What the Commandeering Prohibition Says

- The prohibition derives from dignity: persons cannot be instrumentalized — used as means to someone else's ends. A mandated injection is a bodily intrusion. The prohibition applies.
- The prohibition does not have a public-good exception. A bodily intrusion that serves the community rather than a specific individual is still a bodily intrusion. The beneficiary of the commandeering does not determine whether it is commandeering.
- **Employment conditions are commandeering by another route**: the most common form of COVID vaccine mandate was not state compulsion but employment conditions — take the vaccine or lose your job. The abortion essay already named this mechanism: economic coercion substitutes for genuine consent and produces the same result as physical compulsion. Whether the coercion is physical or economic, it is still coercion. A decision made under threat of losing one's livelihood is not a genuine exercise of the sovereignty the commandeering prohibition protects.
- The honest acknowledgment: some will argue that employment conditions are different — that employers have legitimate authority over workplace policies. The framework's response is Principle 15 (Non-Domination): when a person must accept a condition because the alternative is destitution, and when they have no genuine exit from that condition, the consent is formal rather than real. That is domination through constructed dependency, not voluntary agreement.

## III. The Obligation on Institutions

*Before compulsion is on the table, convince*

- The Society's commitment to honest speech and fidelity to evidence (Principle 1) applies to institutions as much as to individuals. An institution that claims authority to compel compliance must first earn the trust that makes voluntary compliance possible.
- What earning trust requires:
  - Honest communication about what the evidence shows — including what it doesn't show, what is uncertain, and what is genuinely new
  - Transparent acknowledgment of the compressed timeline and what that means for long-term data
  - Genuine engagement with specific concerns rather than dismissal of all hesitancy as ignorance or malice
  - Disclosure of financial relationships and institutional interests that bear on the claim being made
  - Acknowledgment of prior failures rather than presentation of institutional authority as self-justifying
- What COVID institutions frequently did instead:
  - Presented emergency authorizations as equivalent to standard approval without explaining the difference
  - Dismissed concerns about long-term effects as misinformation when long-term data genuinely did not yet exist
  - Characterized all hesitancy as anti-science rather than distinguishing legitimate concern from fabricated conspiracy
  - Cited institutional authority — "trust the CDC" — rather than engaging transparently with the evidence
- An institution that has lost trust through prior dishonesty or institutional capture cannot demand compliance as a substitute for the persuasion it failed to achieve. The path back to compliance runs through restored trust, not through compulsion that confirms every suspicion the institution was trying to overcome.
- Principle 34 is directly applicable: law is structurally suspect where innate moral reasoning already produces adequate outcomes. Most people, in communities with functional institutional trust, voluntarily vaccinate when evidence is clear and honestly communicated. Mandates were applied precisely where that trust had broken down — at the moment when the mechanism was least appropriate and most counterproductive.

## IV. Why Mandates Fail When Trust Has Broken Down

- A mandate issued to a population that distrusts the mandating institution does not produce compliance — it produces resentment, entrenchment, and deeper distrust.
- The person who already suspects the institution reads the mandate as confirmation: "they know they can't convince us, so they're forcing us." This reading is not entirely wrong. It accurately describes what happened.
- Mandates also consume institutional credibility as a resource. The credibility that could have been rebuilt through transparent, honest engagement was instead spent on enforcement — leaving less available for the next public health crisis, and the one after that.
- The practical evidence: COVID vaccine mandates produced significant backlash, fueled political polarization around public health, and strengthened rather than weakened anti-institutional movements. The population most resistant to mandates did not become more compliant — it became more organized. The net effect on public health through the erosion of institutional credibility is a genuine cost that was not accounted for in the policy calculation.
- This is the mechanism Principle 34 names at the institutional level: compulsion applied where persuasion should have done the work destroys the capacity for persuasion to do that work in the future.

## V. What Rebuilding Trust Would Actually Require

*Better messaging is not the answer*

- The standard institutional response to vaccine hesitancy is a communication problem diagnosis: people don't understand the science, so the fix is clearer explanation, more accessible language, trusted messengers. This diagnosis is wrong, and the prescription fails because of it.
- People who distrust healthcare institutions are not primarily confused about germ theory. They are correctly perceiving that the system has misaligned incentives that do not reliably place their wellbeing first. Clearer messaging about vaccine safety does not address why they suspect the messenger.
- **The structural features that have earned distrust**:
  - Fee-for-service medicine creates financial incentives toward procedures and prescriptions regardless of patient benefit. The doctor who prescribes more earns more. Patients sense this even when they cannot fully articulate it.
  - Pharmaceutical industry influence over medical education and practice is documented, pervasive, and rarely disclosed transparently. Continuing medical education is routinely sponsored by companies with financial interests in the prescribing decisions being educated. The research literature itself is shaped by publication bias — industry-funded studies with unfavorable results are less likely to be published.
  - The opioid crisis is not ancient history. FDA-approved medications, aggressively promoted by pharmaceutical companies, prescribed by physicians operating within a system that rewarded volume, killed hundreds of thousands of Americans within the professional lifetime of currently practicing doctors. The institutional response was settlements, not reckoning. The financial penalties were treated as a cost of doing business. No fundamental structural change followed.
  - Regulatory agencies have documented financial relationships with the industries they regulate — through the revolving door between agency employment and industry positions, through user fees paid by pharmaceutical companies to fund FDA review, through the career incentives that shape agency decision-making. This is not conspiracy. It is publicly documented and structurally predictable.
  - The patient experience of the healthcare encounter: rushed, dismissed, treated as a compliance target rather than an agent. The person who feels their concerns are not genuinely heard by their physician is not wrong about what is happening.
- **What rebuilding trust would actually require**:
  - Structural separation of financial incentives from clinical recommendations — payment systems that do not reward volume and prescription
  - Genuine transparency about pharmaceutical influence: which research is industry-funded, which physicians are paid speakers, which guidelines were developed by panels with industry relationships
  - Real institutional accountability for the opioid crisis — not financial settlements that leave the structure intact, but genuine reckoning with what the system produced and why
  - Patient-centered care that treats the person in the room as a genuine agent with legitimate concerns, not a compliance problem to be managed
  - Honest communication about uncertainty — what is known, what isn't, what the evidence shows and what it doesn't — rather than projecting institutional confidence that the evidence doesn't support
- **How far current institutions are from this**: the same institutions that produced the opioid crisis, that have documented financial relationships with pharmaceutical companies, that rushed emergency authorizations and presented them as routine — those institutions then issued mandates when the population they had not earned trust with declined to comply. The mandates confirmed every suspicion rather than addressing any of them.
- **What the Society models instead**: the Society builds health knowledge and practice in its members rather than cultivating dependency on institutional authority. A member who understands how vaccines work, why they work, and what the evidence actually shows — and who has received that knowledge from people with no financial interest in the answer — is in a fundamentally different position than a member who has been told to trust the CDC. The path to genuine public health is not mandates. It is a population capable of evaluating evidence, in relationship with institutions that have earned the right to make recommendations.

## VII. Where the Society Stands

- **Vaccines are good.** The evidence for them is real and should be followed honestly. The Society encourages its members to take vaccines to the extent the evidence indicates is appropriate — and that qualifier is not a throwaway. It means the Society follows the evidence, not the mandate. Where evidence is strong, the recommendation is strong. Where evidence is uncertain, the Society says so.
- **The commandeering prohibition applies.** No member may be compelled to undergo a medical procedure without genuine consent. This is not a qualified right subject to override by sufficiently important public health objectives. It is a direct expression of what dignity means.
- **Economic coercion is coercion.** Employment conditions that remove genuine consent — take this or lose your livelihood — are not voluntary. The Society opposes them here for the same reason it opposes them in the abortion context: because genuine consent requires genuine freedom, and coercion by economic means is still coercion.
- **The obligation falls on institutions.** The Society's role in public health is to build the conditions under which genuine informed decisions become possible: accurate information honestly communicated, genuine engagement with uncertainty, transparency about institutional interests, and the trust that makes voluntary compliance possible. The Society does not substitute compulsion for persuasion it failed to achieve.
- **Institutional credibility is a public health resource.** A community that burns its members' trust through dishonest communication or coercive policy has damaged something it will need in the next crisis. The Society treats the maintenance of genuine trust as an obligation — not because trust feels good but because it is the only mechanism through which informed voluntary action becomes possible at scale.

---

## VIII. What Just Policy Would Look Like

- Honest, transparent communication about evidence — including uncertainty, timeline limitations, and what is and isn't yet known
- No employment conditions that remove genuine consent
- Voluntary programs with genuine incentives rather than coercive conditions
- Medical exemptions that are real rather than nominal
- Genuine engagement with specific concerns rather than dismissal of all hesitancy as a single phenomenon
- Institutional transparency about financial relationships and prior failures
- The recognition that institutional credibility is built slowly and destroyed quickly — and that every coercive measure accelerates the destruction
- The understanding that a community in which members genuinely trust the institutions advising them on health, and in which those institutions have earned that trust through honest conduct, will achieve higher vaccination rates than any mandate — without commandeering a single body
