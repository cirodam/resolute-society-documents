# Scientific Studies and Public Discourse

---

## Opening

- Public debate increasingly centers on scientific studies — cited as irrefutable evidence, deployed as rhetorical weapons, and rarely read by the people invoking them
- This creates a specific epistemological problem: the authority of science is invoked while the actual practice of science — tentative, cumulative, self-correcting — is ignored
- A single study is not irrefutable evidence. It is a data point. The essay will explain what that means and why it matters.
- What this essay will do: build the practical toolkit for evaluating scientific claims honestly — not to dismiss science, but to understand what science actually is and how knowledge is actually built
- What the Society requires of its members: not blind deference to institutional authority, and not reflexive dismissal of expert consensus — but the capacity to evaluate evidence with genuine understanding of what it shows and what it doesn't

---

## I. What a Study Actually Is

*To be developed*

- A study is a structured attempt to answer a specific question under specific conditions — it is not a statement of universal truth
- The hypothesis it tests, the population it studies, the conditions it imposes — all of these limit what it can actually tell you
- A study that finds X in population Y under conditions Z tells you something about X in Y under Z — and often less than the headline suggests

## II. The Hierarchy of Evidence

*To be developed*

- Not all studies are equal: the difference between a single observational study and a replicated randomized controlled trial
- Anecdote → case report → observational study → RCT → replication → systematic review → meta-analysis → consensus
- Why consensus is not the same as any single study — and why attacking a single study does not undermine consensus
- Why a single supporting study is not the same as established evidence

## III. Quality Markers — What Media Coverage Almost Never Tells You

*To be developed*

- Sample size and statistical power
- Randomization and control groups
- Blinding
- Pre-registration — why it matters and what p-hacking is
- Peer review — what it does and doesn't guarantee
- Replication — whether independent researchers got the same result
- Funding source — and the documented effect of industry funding on results

---

## IV. The Replication Crisis

*To be developed*

- A substantial fraction of published studies — including landmark ones — have failed to replicate
- Why this happens: publication bias (positive results get published, null results don't), incentive structures in academic publishing, p-hacking, small sample sizes
- What the replication crisis means for how we should hold scientific findings — with more tentativeness than public discourse allows
- What it does not mean: that science doesn't work, or that all findings are equally uncertain

---

## V. Funding Effects

*To be developed*

- Industry-funded studies consistently produce results favorable to the funder at rates that cannot be explained by chance
- Documented across pharmaceutical research, tobacco research, food science, and others
- The mechanism: not necessarily fraud, but study design choices, selective publication, and the framing of results
- What this means for evaluating studies: funding source is a quality marker, not a conspiracy indicator

## VI. How Media Coverage Distorts Science

*To be developed*

- "A study found" — the phrase that ends all questions while conveying almost no information
- The systematic distortion: preliminary findings presented as settled, relative risk presented as absolute risk, correlation presented as causation
- The coffee cycle: why the same substance seems to cause and prevent cancer in alternating headlines
- What honest science journalism would look like vs. what exists

## VII. What "The Science Says" Actually Means

*To be developed*

- The difference between scientific consensus and individual studies
- How consensus forms — slowly, through convergence of independent evidence
- Where genuine scientific uncertainty exists vs. where it is manufactured
- The manufactured doubt playbook: tobacco, climate, and how industries have used single studies and outlier researchers to create the appearance of controversy where consensus exists
- How to distinguish genuine uncertainty from manufactured uncertainty

## VIII. What the Society Requires

*To be developed*

- The practical epistemological toolkit: how members should approach a scientific claim
- The questions to ask: who funded it, was it replicated, what kind of study was it, what does the broader literature say
- The posture: neither deference nor dismissal — genuine engagement with what the evidence actually shows
- How this connects to the curriculum: Practical Epistemology, Foundational Literacy, and the Society's commitment to fidelity to evidence as a living practice rather than a slogan
