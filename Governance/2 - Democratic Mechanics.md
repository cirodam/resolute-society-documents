## The Annual Referendum

### The Referendum

Each November, the Society shall hold an annual referendum. All members — provisional and full — are eligible to participate. The referendum is the direct expression of the Society's sovereign will and shall be treated as such by the General Assembly.

### Agenda

The General Assembly sets the agenda of the referendum, subject to the petition provisions above. Questions placed on the referendum by petition carry the same standing as questions placed by the Assembly.

### Sequence

The referendum shall be held before the sortition draw for the incoming General Assembly. The results of the referendum shall be among the first matters considered by the incoming Assembly.

## Institutional Maintenance

### The Obligation

The present crisis is a case study in deferred institutional maintenance. The separation of wealth from political power was not destroyed in a single event — it eroded through a thousand individually rational accommodations across decades, each too small to compel response, the accumulated result catastrophic. The practical capacity of persons to grow food, heal, govern themselves, and resolve conflict without institutional intermediaries was not eliminated by decree — it atrophied through substitution, each substitution convenient, each generation less capable than the last until the dependency became total and extraction became possible. The epistemic commons — the shared infrastructure of journalism, public education, and legitimate expertise through which a democratic society maintains contact with reality — was not dismantled overnight but defunded, consolidated, and delegitimized incrementally, until the conditions for shared factual ground ceased to exist. The living systems on which civilization depends were not destroyed by malice but depleted by rational extraction, the maintenance costs deferred to generations not yet present to object. The social fabric was not torn but dissolved — relationship by relationship, institution by institution, third place by third place — through forces operating exactly as designed.

None of this required anyone to intend it. It required only that deferral remain the path of least resistance — that no institution held a standing obligation to ask, on a regular schedule, what was drifting, what had been captured, what was no longer paying its way, what harm was accumulating unseen. The absence of that obligation is not an oversight. It is the condition that makes the long, quiet failure of institutions possible.

The Society's axioms describe not just what the Society believes but how institutions fail. Power concentrates. Capacity atrophies. Complexity accumulates. Moral blindness sets in. Market logic infiltrates through ordinary decisions. These are not hypothetical risks — they are documented patterns, operating now at civilizational scale, named in the Society's founding documents because they operate predictably and invisibly. What makes them dangerous is that they do not announce themselves. By the time they are visible they are typically advanced.

The Society therefore holds institutional maintenance as a standing obligation — not a response to crisis but a scheduled practice, conducted on a regular cadence, before deterioration becomes visible. Maintenance is what an institution does when it takes its own failure modes seriously. It is the practical expression of Axiom I applied to the Society itself: we may be wrong, we may have drifted, we may be causing harm we cannot currently see — and we are obligated to look. We build this obligation into our structure because we know what happens when it is absent.

### The Maintenance Committee

Each June, the General Assembly shall constitute a Maintenance Committee by sortition from the full membership. The Maintenance Committee is distinct from all other committees and may not be combined with any standing body. Its independence from the General Assembly's ordinary operations is essential to its function — a body that reviews the Society's institutional health must not be composed of the people currently exercising the authority under review.

The Maintenance Committee holds three authorities. It may review any aspect of the Society's institutional life against the commitments of this Definition and the Society's principles. It may advise the General Assembly on findings and courses of action. And it may place questions directly on the annual November referendum without requiring General Assembly approval. This last authority is the critical one: it ensures that the membership can hear what the Maintenance Committee has found regardless of whether the General Assembly finds the findings convenient.

The Committee conducts its work between June and October, reporting findings to the General Assembly and to the full membership before the referendum. The incoming General Assembly shall take up the Maintenance Committee's findings as among its first obligations of the new year.

### Maintenance Categories

The Maintenance Committee reviews the following conditions each year:

**Power concentration.** Whether any member, officer, body, or informal network is accumulating influence beyond its formal role. Whether sortition is genuinely distributing governance or whether the same individuals are cycling through roles in ways that produce de facto continuity of power. Whether petition thresholds are practically accessible. Whether recall mechanisms remain operable in practice, not just in principle.

**Capacity and dependency.** Whether members who have been part of the Society for five or more years are more capable or more dependent than when they joined. Whether the Society's divisions are building capacity in members or delivering services to passive recipients. Whether core functions of the Society could still be performed if any given division were dissolved or disrupted.

**Moral blindness.** What concerns are being raised by members with the least institutional standing — the newest, the quietest, those who bear costs the majority does not. What populations or beings the Society's conduct affects that are not represented in its deliberations. What harms the Society may be causing or enabling that current members are not positioned to see. This review shall specifically seek testimony from members who have joined within the past two years.

**Commercial integrity.** Whether market logic has entered any division's operations through ordinary decisions, each individually rational but collectively drift. Whether dependencies on commercial infrastructure are accumulating. Whether member relationships within the Society are becoming increasingly transactional.

**Equal standing.** Whether some members are consistently heard less than others in deliberation. Whether participation in governance is broadly distributed or concentrated. Whether the quietest members' concerns are reaching the General Assembly through the petition system or going unheard.

**Ecological stewardship.** Whether the land and living systems the Society inhabits are being stewarded consistent with the Society's principles. Whether any ecological thresholds are approaching that require attention. Whether the Society's ecological footprint is moving in the direction its commitments require.

**Institutional complexity.** Reviewed in depth every five years, and assessed briefly every year: whether all existing divisions, committees, and governance mechanisms are still paying their way. Whether any structures have been maintained past the point where the problem they were built to address still exists. Whether a new member can understand how the Society governs itself within a reasonable period.

**Founding rationale.** Reviewed in depth every five years: whether the rationale for the Society's major governance choices remains sound in light of what has been learned since founding. Whether any provisions of this Definition, reviewed against the Society's accumulated experience, warrant amendment proposals to the General Assembly.

### Findings and Response

The Maintenance Committee's findings are reports, not recommendations. The Committee identifies conditions; the General Assembly and the membership determine response. Findings shall be entered into the Society's permanent record.

The General Assembly is obligated to address every finding that identifies a condition inconsistent with this Definition or the Society's principles. An addressed finding requires a documented response — either a course of action or an explicit determination, with rationale, that no action is warranted.

The Maintenance Committee may place any finding directly on the November referendum. It does not require General Assembly approval to do so. This authority exists because the findings most important for the membership to see are often the ones the General Assembly is least inclined to surface.

A finding that the Society cannot bring itself to address is itself information — about where power actually sits, what the Society is willing to see, and what it has decided to stop seeing. The Maintenance Committee shall note unaddressed findings from prior years in each subsequent annual review.

## Committees

### Nature and Creation

A Committee is a body created by the General Assembly to exercise delegated authority for a specific purpose. Committees are not standing features of the Society's governance but instruments of the Assembly's will, constituted when needed and dissolved when their purpose is fulfilled or the Assembly determines otherwise.

### Selection by Sortition

Committee members are selected by sortition. The General Assembly determines from which pool each Committee is drawn: the full membership of the Society, or the membership of a designated College. Sortition ensures that Committee authority does not accumulate in self-selected persons and that the experience of governance is distributed broadly through the membership over time.

### Scope and Accountability

Each Committee operates within the scope defined by the General Assembly at its creation. Committees report to the Assembly and may not exceed the authority delegated to them. The Assembly may modify, expand, or dissolve any Committee at any time.

## Executive Divisions

### Nature and Purpose

An Executive Division is a body responsible for the delivery of services in a single domain of community life — food, housing, health, and similar — and for the operational capacity to fulfill that responsibility. Divisions are the Society's executive arm: they do not deliberate policy but implement it, and do not make decisions reserved to the General Assembly but exercise genuine autonomy within their domain.

### Creation

Executive Divisions are created by the General Assembly. The Assembly defines the domain of each Division at its creation. The Society's constitution shall identify the Divisions constituted at founding.

### Division Officers and the Officer Corps

Each Executive Division is led by a Division Officer elected by the members of the Division. Division Officers are collectively known as the Officer Corps. The Officer Corps may meet collectively or in part as they deem appropriate to coordinate the Society's operational life. The Officer Corps holds no governing authority as a body — authority remains with the General Assembly and the individual Divisions.

### Autonomy and Emergency Authority

Executive Divisions exercise genuine autonomy in their day-to-day operations within the scope established by the General Assembly. In emergencies, Divisions are expected to act independently and decisively in their domain without waiting for Assembly direction. The capacity to function without instruction is a design requirement, not an exception.

### Accountability

Executive Divisions are accountable to the General Assembly. The Assembly may direct, modify, or dissolve any Division except as provided for the Judicial Division below.

### The Judicial Division

There shall be a Judicial Division responsible for the resolution of injuries and contract disputes between members and between members and associations of the Society. The Judicial Division is constitutionally permanent and may not be dissolved by the General Assembly. Its permanence is essential to its independence, and its independence is essential to its function.

The Judicial Division shall be governed by a Committee drawn by sortition from the full membership. A Division Officer, selected by or accountable to that Committee, shall manage its day-to-day operations. The Committee governing the Judicial Division has the authority to review and set aside decisions of the Division Officer where the integrity of a proceeding requires it.

### Obligated Divisions

Seven Executive Divisions are obligated by the principles of this Definition and are constitutionally permanent. They may not be dissolved by the General Assembly. Their permanence is not a feature of their design but a consequence of the obligations the Society has accepted. A Society that has guaranteed food, water, shelter, sanitation, healthcare, safety, and education to its members cannot dissolve the institutions through which those guarantees are fulfilled without breaking the covenant that membership requires.

**The Division of Food** is the community organized around food production — an instrument of collective action, not a food provider. It is responsible for the food security of every member. Its domain encompasses agriculture, foraging, preservation, storage, and distribution. Its success is measured not by what the Division can do, but by what the community can grow, preserve, and provide for itself. It is derived from Principle 14.

The Division of Food does not treat industrial animal suffering as an acceptable foundation for the Society's food security. The moral standing of beings capable of suffering generates obligations that extend into how the Society produces food, not only whether members are fed. The Division's mandate therefore includes the ongoing development of food systems — through growing, foraging, hunting, and husbandry practices — that the Society's own principles can honestly endorse. This is a direction, not a deadline. The Society does not require perfection before membership or impose purity standards on individuals. It requires that the Division, as an institution, move the Society's food systems progressively into alignment with what the axioms hold to be true about suffering and moral standing. It is derived from Principle 14 and from Axiom VIII and Principles 8 and 9.

**The Division of Water** is the community organized around water security — an instrument of collective action, not a water provider. It is responsible for the water security of every member. Its domain encompasses source protection, treatment, distribution, storage, and the knowledge and capacity to maintain water safety under both ordinary and emergency conditions. Its success is measured not by what the Division can do, but by what the community can access, test, treat, and sustain through its own knowledge and infrastructure. It is derived from Principle 14.

**The Division of Housing** is the community organized around shelter — an instrument of collective action, not a housing provider. It is responsible for the shelter security of every member. Its domain encompasses construction, maintenance, repair, and the allocation of dwelling within the Society's commons. Its success is measured not by what the Division can do, but by how broadly the community holds the practical capacity to house itself. It is derived from Principle 14.

**The Division of Health** is the community organized around health knowledge and care — an instrument of collective action, not a healthcare provider. It is responsible for the healthcare of every member. Its domain encompasses preventive care, community health work, emergency response, and palliative care. Its success is measured not by what the Division can do, but by what the community can diagnose, treat, and prevent through health knowledge distributed broadly across its membership. It is derived from Principle 14.

**The Division of Sanitation** is the community organized around sanitation — an instrument of collective action, not a sanitation service. It is responsible for the sanitation security of every member. Its domain encompasses waste management, composting, hygiene infrastructure, and the knowledge and capacity to maintain safe, dignified sanitation under both ordinary and emergency conditions. Its success is measured not by what the Division can do, but by what the community can design, build, and maintain in sanitation systems through its own knowledge and labor. It is derived from Principle 14.

**The Division of the Watch** is the community organized around its own safety — an instrument of collective action, not a security force. It is responsible for the safety of every member. Its domain encompasses the prevention of and response to immediate threats to members and the Society's common life. Its success is measured not by what the Division can do, but by how broadly the capacity to recognize, prevent, and respond to threats is held throughout the membership. The Watch does not adjudicate harm — that function belongs to the Judicial Division. When the Watch responds to harm between members, it acts to ensure safety and transfers accountability to the Judicial Division. The Watch is the protective arm of the restorative justice framework, not a punitive institution in its own right. It is derived from Principle 14.

**The Division of Education** is the community organized around its own formation — an instrument of collective action, not a school. It is responsible for the cultivation of every member. Its domain encompasses the full breadth of the Society's formation work: the curriculum, apprenticeships, the Compendium, and the practical support of the College structure. Its success is measured not by what the Division can do, but by what its members know, can teach, and can transmit to those who come after them. Genuine education is a condition of dignity, not a commodity, and this Division is the institution through which that commitment is honored. It is derived from Principle 20.

Each obligated Division maintains active communication and mutual aid relationships with its counterpart Divisions in every sister Society within the federation. This inter-Society relationship is constitutive of what each Division is — not an optional extension of its function but an expression of the covenant that Article XXI establishes between Societies. The Division of Food communicates directly with sister Divisions of Food. The Division of Water with sister Divisions of Water. The Division of Health with sister Divisions of Health. The Division of Sanitation with sister Divisions of Sanitation. And so on for each obligated Division. These relationships are established at founding and maintained as a standing obligation regardless of whether a crisis requires them. The infrastructure for mutual aid exists before it is needed, or it does not exist when it is needed.

## Colleges

### Nature and Purpose

A College is a body of peers organized around a shared domain of knowledge and practice. Colleges exist to preserve and transmit knowledge, to establish standards of practice within their domain, and to organize the Society's knowledge-holders into a community of mutual accountability and ongoing formation.

### Creation and Dissolution

Colleges are created and dissolved by the General Assembly. The Assembly defines the domain of each College at its creation.

### Self-Governance

Each College establishes its own rules, standards, and entrance criteria within its domain. Colleges govern their internal life without Assembly direction in ordinary circumstances. They are accountable to the General Assembly, which retains the authority to dissolve a College or to direct it where the Society's interests require.

### Colleges and Committees

The General Assembly may direct that a Committee be drawn by sortition from the membership of a College when the subject matter of the Committee's work falls within that College's domain. A Committee so drawn exercises the General Assembly's authority, not the College's, and reports to the Assembly accordingly.

## Conciliation and the Resolution of Harm

### Orientation

When harm is done within this community, the Society asks not what punishment is deserved but what repair is possible. The needs of those who have been harmed are the primary concern of any proceeding. The Judicial Division builds its processes around this commitment.

The punitive system the Society is rejecting is not a natural response to harm. It is a constructed arrangement — built through specific political choices, serving specific interests, producing specific outcomes for specific populations with a consistency that cannot be explained by anything other than design. Permanent exclusion from civic life, the elimination of future possibility, the use of suffering as the measure of consequence — these are not the inevitable results of taking harm seriously. They are choices, presented as natural in exactly the way Axiom XIV describes all constructed arrangements. The Society names this construction honestly and refuses it.

The Society's approach is grounded in what persons actually are. Persons are not fixed. The capacity for genuine change is an observable feature of human nature, not a moral preference. The person who caused serious harm is not identical to the person who caused it if genuine work has been done — and a justice system built on the premise that persons are permanently defined by their worst moments is making a factual error as much as a moral one. Accountability is real: harm requires acknowledgment, repair where possible, and demonstrated change over time. Safety is real: the community holds an obligation to protect its members. Permanent exclusion is itself a serious harm, chosen only when safety genuinely requires it and never as punishment alone. Genuine accountability is more demanding than punishment. The Society does not mistake the absence of suffering for the absence of consequence.

### Jurisdiction

The Judicial Division has jurisdiction over injuries caused by the actions of a member or association, and over disputes arising from agreements and obligations between members or between members and associations of the Society.

### Mediators

A Mediator is assigned to each matter brought before the Judicial Division. The Mediator is the primary responsible party for the matter, takes ownership of its resolution, and has the authority to resolve straightforward cases without further escalation. The Mediator exercises genuine practical judgment — not merely procedural management — in the pursuit of resolution.

### Juries

In cases of sufficient gravity, a Jury shall be convened. Juries are selected by sortition from the full membership of the Society. The Mediator remains involved in Jury proceedings but does not hold decision authority over the questions committed to the Jury.

The Jury's function is not to render a simple verdict but to exercise full practical judgment in response to a structured set of questions specific to the type of matter before them. These questions shall be developed and maintained by the Judicial Division and shall vary by the type of harm or dispute. Jurors are expected to deliberate genuinely, to bring their full wisdom to each question, and to reach conclusions that can guide meaningful repair.

### Moral Tragedy

Some matters brought before the Judicial Division are not problems. They are tragedies. The distinction is not procedural — it is a difference in kind that changes what the Division is obligated to do.

A problem has a solution. Careful process, adequate information, and practical judgment can identify what went wrong, what repair is possible, and what demonstrated change looks like going forward. Most matters before the Division are problems in this sense, and the Division's processes are built for them.

A tragedy is a situation in which the conditions themselves are wrong — in which every available path involves genuine harm, irreversible loss, or the violation of something that deserved to be whole. The person navigating a tragedy did not create those conditions and cannot escape them. They must choose anyway. There is no resolution because the situation does not admit of one. To judge a person's navigation of a tragedy against a standard the situation made unreachable is not accountability. It is cruelty wearing the language of accountability. The Society refuses it.

When the Division determines that a matter is a tragedy rather than a problem, the Division's obligation shifts. It does not seek resolution. It asks what this person needs — what information, what support, what presence, what care will help them navigate something that should not have to be navigated alone. The Division stands with people in tragedy. It does not evaluate them afterward. Mediators in such matters are not pursuing correct outcomes but ensuring that no member faces impossible conditions without the community beside them.

### Moral Particularity

Every matter before the Division is irreducibly particular. The categories the Division uses — types of harm, classes of dispute, applicable precedent — are tools for organizing thought. They are not substitutes for the reality of the specific situation.

Two matters in the same category may be entirely different moral realities depending on what brought the parties there, what constraints they are navigating, what relationships are implicated, and what consequences they will bear. To process both identically because they share a category is to judge neither honestly. It is to judge the category and call it justice.

The Mediator's obligation is genuine engagement with what this situation actually is — not a template applied to a type. This requires proximity: real access to the specific facts that determine what a situation is. It requires humility: acknowledgment that the full moral weight of a situation may not be visible from outside it. And it requires the discipline to resist the comfort of precedent when precedent is being applied to a situation it cannot adequately contain.

This is not a rejection of consistency. It is a rejection of false consistency — the application of identical process to situations that differ in ways that matter morally. The Division builds processes that are consistent in their obligations and particular in their application. The hardest work is not the application of process to clear cases. It is honest engagement with situations where process, applied mechanically, would produce outcomes that dignity cannot endorse.
