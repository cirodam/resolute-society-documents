## Society Credits


### Purpose

Society credits are the internal medium of exchange of a Resolute Society. They are not a substitute for external currency. They do not represent a claim on any external asset and have no value outside the community that sustains them. They exist to facilitate exchange, labor, and obligation within the Society without importing the accumulation dynamics and commercial logic the Society was built to resist. Every design feature of the credit system — the fixed supply, the demurrage, the treasury — follows from this purpose.

### The Credit Supply

The total supply of society credits in circulation at any time is determined entirely by membership. For each member in good standing, sixty thousand (60,000) credits exist within the Society's economy. No credits exist beyond this amount. No Society may mint credits for any purpose other than member issuance, and no Society may allow total outstanding credits to exceed sixty thousand times current membership. The credit supply is a direct and continuous expression of the community's size.

### The Treasury

The treasury is the Society's central credit pool. It is the beating heart of the Society's internal economy — the collective reserve from which common expenditure flows and to which membership and demurrage return. The treasury is not the property of any member, officer, or division. It belongs to the membership as a whole and is governed by the Society's democratic mechanisms.

The treasury receives:

- Sixty thousand (60,000) credits upon the admission of each new member
- Monthly demurrage collected from all individual member balances
- The remaining credit balance of any departing member, after the burn obligation is satisfied

The treasury distributes credits through:

- Monthly allowances to members, if established by the Society
- Payment for labor, goods, and services rendered in furtherance of collective Society purposes
- Emergency support for members in material need, as authorized by governance
- Any other expenditure authorized through the Society's normal democratic process

The treasury balance is a matter of public record within the Society. No officer or division may hold treasury credits on behalf of the Society outside the treasury without explicit authorization.

### Member Issuance

When a person is admitted to full membership, the Society mints sixty thousand (60,000) society credits on their behalf. These credits enter the treasury immediately and are not issued directly to the member's individual balance. They represent the member's contribution to the Society's collective economic capacity — the liquidity their membership adds to the community's internal economy. The member's individual balance begins at zero and grows through participation, labor, allowance, and exchange.

### Individual Balances

Each member maintains an individual credit balance. This balance reflects the credits a member holds from exchange, labor, and allowance. Members may use their individual balances to pay for goods, services, and labor within the Society; to contribute to collective projects; and to meet any credit obligations established by the Society. Individual balances are personal but not private — the Society maintains transparent records accessible to all members, consistent with the Society's commitments to accountability and the absence of concealment in its internal economy.

No member may hold an individual balance exceeding sixty thousand (60,000) credits. Credits that would cause a balance to exceed this ceiling are redirected to the treasury. This ceiling exists to prevent the accumulation dynamics that the credit system was designed to avoid.

### Demurrage

Society credits carry a demurrage charge — a monthly holding cost applied to all individual member balances. Demurrage is the Society's dues mechanism. It is not a penalty. It is the designed cost of holding credits rather than circulating them, and it is the primary means by which the treasury is continuously replenished from the activity of the membership.

Demurrage is applied to individual member balances at the close of each month. The charge is calculated as a percentage of the balance held. The percentage collected flows directly to the treasury.

Each Society shall set its own demurrage rate. The rate may be adjusted through the Society's normal governance process as conditions change. The rate may not be set below two percent (2%) of the individual balance per month. A Society that fails to maintain a demurrage rate at or above this floor is in breach of this governance standard.

Demurrage does not apply to the treasury. The treasury is the collective pool of the membership and is not subject to decay.

### Monthly Allowance

A Society may establish a monthly credit allowance distributed from the treasury to all members in good standing. The allowance, if established, shall be equal for all members. The amount, and whether an allowance is offered at all, is determined by the Society through its normal democratic process and may be revised at any time.

The allowance is not a wage. It does not vary with contribution, rank, tenure, or any other individual characteristic. If offered, it is offered to every member equally as a basic participation floor — a guarantee that every member enters each month with some capacity for exchange within the internal economy, regardless of their activity in the prior month.

The treasury must be capable of sustaining the monthly allowance without depletion. A Society may not authorize an allowance that would reduce the treasury below a reserve floor established in its constitution. If the treasury falls below this floor, the allowance is suspended until the floor is restored.

### Member Departure

When a member departs the Society — whether through voluntary resignation, removal, or any other cause — sixty thousand (60,000) credits are retired from the total supply. Credits are not transferable outside the Society and may not be redeemed for external currency.

The departure process proceeds as follows:

- If the departing member's individual balance equals or exceeds sixty thousand (60,000) credits: sixty thousand credits are burned from their balance, retiring them from the total supply. Any remaining balance above sixty thousand is transferred to the treasury.
- If the departing member's individual balance is less than sixty thousand (60,000) credits: their entire individual balance is burned. The shortfall — the difference between sixty thousand and the burned amount — is collected by running a one-time demurrage charge against all remaining member balances, proportional to each member's balance at the time of departure. The amount collected is then burned to complete the retirement. If this one-time charge would reduce any member's balance below zero, that member's balance is reduced to zero and their share of the shortfall is absorbed collectively.

The departure demurrage charge is applied once and is separate from the regular monthly demurrage cycle. Members are notified of any departure charge applied to their accounts.

### Restrictions on Use

Society credits may not be:

- Converted into external currency of any kind, at any exchange rate, under any circumstances
- Transferred to any person who is not a current member in good standing
- Used to satisfy any obligation outside the Society
- Inherited, bequeathed, or transferred upon a member's death; upon death, the member's individual balance is treated as a voluntary departure and the departure process applies
- Held by any entity other than individual members and the treasury; no division, working group, or officer account may hold society credits except as the treasury

No member may offer to exchange society credits for external currency, nor may any member accept such an offer. Violation of this restriction is a serious breach of the Society's internal economy and shall be treated as such by the Society's accountability processes.

### Administration and Transparency

The Society shall designate a body — a treasurer, a finance working group, or equivalent — responsible for maintaining accurate records of the treasury balance, all individual member balances, all minting and burning events, all demurrage charges, and all allowance distributions. This body acts as steward of the record, not as governor of the economy; decisions about treasury expenditure remain with the membership through normal governance.

All credit records shall be accessible to all members. No member's balance may be concealed from the membership. The Society shall publish a summary of the credit economy — total supply, treasury balance, total individual balances outstanding, monthly demurrage collected, and allowance distributed — at regular intervals no less frequent than quarterly.

Disputes arising from credit transactions between members are subject to the Society's normal dispute resolution process. Disputes about the accuracy of recorded balances are resolved by the administrative body, subject to appeal to the membership.

## Federation Credits

### Purpose

Federation credits are the medium of exchange for the federation of Resolute Societies. They facilitate exchange between Societies — for labor, goods, hospitality, and mutual aid — and may also be used for exchange within a Society. They are distinct from society credits and may not be converted into society credits, external currency, or any other instrument. Like society credits, they exist to facilitate obligation and exchange without importing accumulation dynamics. Unlike society credits, they are governed at the federation level and their integrity depends on coordination across multiple independent Societies.

### The Credit Supply

The total supply of federation credits across the entire federation is determined by total membership. For each member in good standing across all Societies, sixty thousand (60,000) federation credits exist within the federation economy. The total supply at any time is sixty thousand multiplied by the total number of current members federation-wide. No credits may be minted for any purpose other than member issuance. No credits may exist beyond this amount.

### Member Issuance

When a person is admitted to full membership in any Resolute Society, sixty thousand (60,000) federation credits are minted on their behalf. These credits enter the issuing Society's federation treasury. They are distinct from and additional to the sixty thousand society credits minted at the same time. A new member therefore represents sixty thousand credits entering the Society's internal economy and sixty thousand credits entering the federation economy simultaneously.

### The Distributed Ledger

The federation does not maintain a single central ledger. Instead, each Society maintains the segment of the federation ledger that is relevant to it. A Society's ledger segment contains every federation credit entry in which that Society is a party — every transaction in which it sent or received credits, every balance it holds, and every obligation it has incurred.

Because every transaction has two legs — an outgoing entry and an incoming entry — a transaction between two Societies is recorded on both ledgers: the outgoing leg on the sender's ledger segment, the incoming leg on the receiver's ledger segment. A transaction within a single Society is recorded entirely on that Society's own ledger segment, which holds both legs.

No Society's ledger segment is authoritative for any entry outside its own participation. Each Society is the authoritative record-keeper for the entries on its own segment.

### Transaction Structure

Every federation credit transaction consists of exactly two parts:

- An **outgoing entry**: a debit recorded on the sending Society's ledger segment, reducing the sender's balance
- An **incoming entry**: a credit recorded on the receiving Society's ledger segment, increasing the receiver's balance

A transaction is valid if and only if both entries are valid. An outgoing entry is valid if the sending Society holds sufficient federation credits to cover it. An incoming entry is valid if it corresponds to a valid outgoing entry of equal amount. Neither entry alone constitutes a completed transaction. A transaction in which one leg is valid and the other is not does not settle and does not alter either Society's balance.

When both Societies are the same — for intra-society use of federation credits — the Society holds both legs of the transaction on its own ledger segment. The validity requirements are the same.

### Federation Treasuries

Each Society maintains a federation treasury alongside its society credit treasury. The federation treasury holds the Society's unallocated federation credits — credits not currently committed to any pending transaction or individual member balance. Federation credits minted upon member admission enter this treasury. Federation credits received from other Societies in completed transactions may enter this treasury or be distributed to individual member balances as the Society determines.

The federation treasury is a matter of public record. See Transparency and Public Records below.

### Annual Reconciliation

The total supply of federation credits is adjusted once per year through an annual reconciliation. The reconciliation corrects for the net change in federation membership over the preceding year.

The reconciliation proceeds as follows:

**Step one — calculate the required supply.** The federation determines the current total membership across all Societies. The required supply is sixty thousand multiplied by that total.

**Step two — calculate the excess.** The federation compares required supply to actual outstanding credits. If actual credits exceed required supply, the difference is the burn amount. If actual credits are below required supply, the federation mints the difference and distributes it to Societies proportional to their current membership.

**Step three — apply the progressive burn.** If a burn is required, the burn amount is distributed across Societies using a progressive formula. The formula has three properties that are guaranteed by its design and may not be waived:

- The poorest Society — the Society holding the fewest federation credits — pays an obligation approaching zero
- The wealthiest Society — the Society holding the most — bears a disproportionately larger share than its proportional holdings would suggest
- No Society's obligation exceeds its total holdings; a Society cannot be made insolvent by the reconciliation process

The formula works as follows. Each Society's burn obligation is:

> **obligation = B × H^α / Σ(H^α)**

where B is the total burn required, H is the Society's total holdings, and the sum in the denominator is taken over all Societies. The exponent α governs the steepness of the progressive curve. When α equals one, every Society burns the same percentage of its holdings. As α increases above one, the burden concentrates more heavily on wealthier Societies and approaches zero for the poorest.

The exponent α is not fixed. It scales with the pressure ratio — the degree to which actual outstanding credits exceed the desired supply:

> **r = B / D** (where D is the desired supply: 60,000 × total members)
>
> **α = 1 + r × (α_max − 1)**

When the excess is small, α is close to one and the distribution is gentle. When the excess is large, α approaches α_max and the curve steepens significantly. The parameter α_max is set by the federation's technical standard and may be adjusted through federation-level governance.

The full mathematical specification, with defined symbols, worked examples, edge cases, and the no-insolvency proof, is maintained by the federation as a published technical standard. Any Society may apply the formula to publicly available holdings data at any time to compute the current theoretical burn distribution.

**Step four — execute the burn.** Each Society burns its assigned obligation from its own ledger segment. The order of burning within a Society's ledger is: individual member balances first, applied proportionally across all member balances; federation treasury last. No individual member balance may be reduced below zero by the burn; any per-member shortfall is absorbed by the federation treasury. The federation treasury absorbs what member balances cannot cover. Because the formula guarantees no Society's total obligation exceeds its total holdings, the federation treasury will always be sufficient to cover any remainder after member balances are applied.

### Transparency and Public Records

Each Society shall make the following information publicly available at all times, updated no less frequently than weekly:

- **Total federation credits outstanding**: the net sum of all federation credit entries on the Society's ledger segment, including credits held in its federation treasury, credits held in individual member balances, and any credits committed to pending unsettled transactions
- **Credits currently in account**: the balance held in the Society's federation treasury alone, not including individual member balances or pending transactions

These two figures are the inputs any Society or observer needs to apply the federation's progressive burn formula and calculate what this Society's burn obligation would be if reconciliation occurred at this moment. The federation publishes the formula as a standing technical standard. Any Society, member, or observer may at any time take the publicly available figures from all Societies, apply the formula, and verify the expected distribution of any burn.

This transparency is not optional. A Society that fails to publish current figures, or that publishes figures that do not reconcile with its ledger segment upon inspection, is in breach of federation standards and subject to federation-level accountability processes.

### Relationship to Society Credits

Federation credits and society credits are separate instruments. They may not be converted into each other at any ratio. They may not be netted against each other. A debt denominated in society credits cannot be satisfied in federation credits, and vice versa. The two systems operate in parallel: society credits govern exchange within a Society; federation credits govern exchange between Societies and provide a second medium for intra-society exchange where the Society chooses to use them.

### Restrictions on Use

Federation credits may not be:

- Converted into external currency of any kind, at any exchange rate, under any circumstances
- Converted into society credits at any ratio
- Transferred to any person or entity that is not a member in good standing of a Resolute Society or a recognized federation body
- Used to satisfy any obligation outside the federation
- Accumulated without limit by any single Society; accumulation limits and their enforcement are defined in the federation's technical standard

No Society may offer to exchange federation credits for external currency or society credits, nor may any member transact on behalf of a Society in a manner that violates these restrictions.

### Administration

Each Society designates a body responsible for maintaining its federation ledger segment, publishing the required public figures, participating in annual reconciliation, and resolving internal disputes about federation credit balances. This body coordinates with counterpart bodies in other Societies to settle inter-society transactions and resolve discrepancies between ledger segments.

Disputes between Societies about the validity of a transaction — where one Society's ledger segment records an entry that the other's does not, or where the two records conflict — are escalated to the federation's dispute resolution process. Neither Society's record is presumed correct; the federation process examines both segments and the underlying transaction record to determine the authoritative entry.
